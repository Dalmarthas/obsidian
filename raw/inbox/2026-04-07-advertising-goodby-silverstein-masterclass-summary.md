📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass is taught by Jeff Goodby and Rich Silverstein, the legendary advertising duo behind iconic campaigns like "Got Milk?" and the Budweiser Lizards. The course provides an insider's view into their creative philosophy, agency culture, and practical approaches to advertising that transcends traditional marketing. Rather than teaching "how to sell anything to anyone," they focus on creating meaningful work that people actually want to see—work that's human, funny, beautiful, and true.

Jeff Goodby came from journalism with an English degree from Harvard, while Rich Silverstein is a dyslexic designer from Parsons School of Design with an exceptional photographic memory for imagery. Their partnership, initially arranged by Hal Riney, represents a perfect yin-yang of writing and visual thinking. Together, they built Goodby Silverstein & Partners in San Francisco, an agency that values humanity over corporate culture, makers over employees, and popular culture creation over mere advertising.

The course covers everything from foundational philosophy to practical execution: understanding what advertising really is, working with brands, storytelling in 30 seconds, managing creative teams, pitching new business, and building agency culture. Throughout, they emphasize listening to the world around you, staying true to yourself, respecting your audience, and never being satisfied with work that's merely "good enough."


🎯 CORE PHILOSOPHY & THEMES

ADVERTISING AS HUMAN STORYTELLING
• Advertising isn't about selling—it's about creating things people want to see
• "People read what interests them. Sometimes it's advertising" (Howard Gossage)
• The goal is to be remembered for things that are human, funny, and beautiful—not numbers
• Advertising is "art serving capitalism" and "a corporate-funded study of what people love and hate"
• Always make advertising that doesn't look like advertising
• The best work is timeless—you could see it five years later and still like it

MASS INTIMACY
• Create messages for millions that resonate intimately with individuals
• The cell phone represents this perfectly—one-to-one communication reaching millions
• Make people feel like the message is talking directly to them: "Wow, these guys understand me"
• Never talk down to consumers—speak to people like they're your wife, your friends
• Respect that you're entering someone's home, pocket, or computer

TRUTH & AUTHENTICITY
• Start from truth, no matter how loony it seems—people can smell when you're faking
• Inside should reflect outside: diverse staff, half the partners are women
• Only take brands you'd want in your house, drive, eat, or work with
• Don't make things up—look at culture to give you ideas
• Honesty over manipulation in every message


📊 KEY FRAMEWORKS & CONCEPTS

THE VANDALISM RULES (Rules for Creativity)

1. DON'T COPY, STEAL
   - Amateurs copy, artists steal (Picasso)
   - Be aware of things around you—good ideas come from anywhere
   - Listen to cab drivers, kids, people at games, the world
   - Example: "Just Do It" came from hearing a murderer's last words
   - Example: "I Love This Game" came from someone at an NBA game

2. PART OF YOUR JOB IS NOT TO DO YOUR JOB
   - David Ogilvy's advice: Write for 2 hours, spend the rest looking for things to write about
   - Be alert all the time for material you can use
   - Turns you into an appreciator of life
   - Makes you better at living, not just advertising

3. RUN TOWARD FIRE
   - Take chances and look for provocative ideas
   - The worst thing is doing the right thing but having nobody notice
   - Make sure people see your work—that's where the money goes
   - Example: Police shootings pro bono work

4. SEEK OUT FAME
   - Care whether people notice your work
   - Get to know business reporters and press
   - This isn't vanity—it's professional necessity
   - If great work doesn't get noticed, something is wrong

5. SHARE THE CMO'S FOXHOLE
   - Understand the Chief Marketing Officer's problems
   - What keeps them up at night? What's their job like?
   - Speak their language about their problems
   - Bonds you to them, makes them more forgiving
   - Allows you to actually solve their problems

STRATEGY AS FOUNDATION
• Strategy is your friend—use it to sell ideas
• "For the bold" strategy (Doritos) enables bold creative work
• When clients agree to a strategy, hold them to it
• Every project starts with: What does the brand stand for? Why should I buy it?
• Absence can sell presence (Got Milk deprivation strategy)
• Find the insight that changes everything

BRANDS AS LIVING ORGANISMS
• Brands have brain, heart, and soul
• They're inarticulate giants that need a voice
• Your job: form the "river of knowledge" into something simpler, more powerful
• Heritage has value—don't be afraid to go back to it
• Brought back "The Ultimate Driving Machine" for BMW
• Brought back "There Is No Substitute" for Porsche
• Sometimes you need to kill bad heritage that holds brands back


✍️ THE CREATIVE PROCESS

STARTING WITH NOTHING
• When you have nothing, you have to be intuitive: "If it feels right, it will be right"
• Make things out of nothing using friends, real people, scrappy production
• Example: Mill Valley Film Festival—real people acting like cinema experts
• Example: Chevy's Fresh Mex—shoot commercials each morning, throw them out daily
• Naivete works when starting something—you don't know what you don't know
• Trouble comes when you overthink or when you get money

30-SECOND STORYTELLING
• Great filmmakers aren't always best commercial directors—different mindset
• Need instant gratification, traditional structure: beginning, middle, end
• ALWAYS start with the ending—if you don't know where you're going, forget it
• Never present an idea by telling its title—the title isn't in the commercial
• Test in casting: if it's not funny in casting, it won't be funny with money
• Details make things great

HAL RINEY'S TIMING CHARTS
• Create diagrams: timeline from 0-30 seconds
• Above line: sounds, dialogue, music
• Below line: visuals, how long each cut lasts
• Helps you know what you want when talking to directors
• Modern version: Record yourself reading the script with timing
• Draw bad sketches of each scene, photograph them
• Put pictures above soundtrack in iMovie—now you have the commercial

THE MOBILE DEVICE METHOD
1. Take your script
2. Describe and read everything that happens with timing
3. Do funny voices for each character (be the dog, the announcer)
4. Make it exactly 30 seconds—you have your soundtrack
5. Draw really bad drawings of each scene
6. Photograph the drawings
7. Put them above the soundtrack in editing software
8. You now have a timed-out version of your commercial


🎨 WORKING WITH IDEAS & CLIENTS

HOW TO SELL CRAZY IDEAS
• Don't think of them as crazy—make sense out of them
• Strategy is your friend: "You asked for bold, this is bold"
• Never tell people how to feel ("this is funny")—let them feel it
• Let ideas roll downhill with everyone participating
• Good ideas keep rolling and people won't let them go
• People make suggestions and feel part of it

TOOLS FOR SELLING IDEAS
• Mood boards with movement, GIFs, music
• Music sets tone—use unaffordable music for the feeling
• "Idea videos" create living, breathing brand concepts
• Can be dangerous—clients want to just produce that
• Film yourself presenting (selfies)—shows you're cool and shows work
• How you present is as important as the idea itself

READING THE ROOM
• Study the client and people across the table
• Some need explanation, some are visual, some like stories
• Present differently to different people
• Know what they're liking/not liking and lose what's not working
• Every meeting could be your last—treat it that way
• Germans want you standing, buttoned up, formal presentation
• Don't talk over your partner—looks like you don't trust each other

WORKING WITH CREATIVE DIRECTORS
• Most fearful thing is presenting to creative director, not client
• Don't try to make everyone happy with decisions
• Find tough love: be honest but keep them coming back
• Don't compete with your team—edit them
• Make suggestions on the spot, not the next day with finished layouts
• Tell people gently, make it their idea
• Know who can take direct feedback vs. who needs gentleness
• Work with people before ideas are fully baked—easier to course-correct
• Don't kill ideas in front of groups—demoralizing
• Meet one-on-one in small spaces, not boardrooms

THE CLIENT AS THERAPIST PARTNER
• Be the client's therapist—listen to their real problems
• Requires trust so they'll tell you what's really going on
• Maintain open dialogue: "That doesn't feel right" → "Why? We can fix that"
• Don't present arrogantly like you know best
• Sometimes clients know more about product than you
• Sometimes they know too much—help them see what matters
• Know the big boss—champions at the top make everything easier
• Example: Brian Roberts (Comcast CEO) got Robert Redford for voiceover


🎬 PRODUCTION & CRAFT

IMPORTANCE OF CRAFT
• Never rest—projects are never done until they're forced out the door
• "Could be made better, just a little" is always true
• Don't rest on laurels, just do the next ad
• Details matter: casting, location, timing, music, editing
• Michael Bay added so much to Aaron Burr spot (special glass, wide angles, details)
• Everybody adds something as projects move along

TYPOGRAPHY & VOICE
• Rich: "I see typography as voices"
• Got Milk needed an identity beyond Helvetica
• Set quirky type, had Danny Delk record audio: "Got Milk?"
• Visual + audio identity created lasting power

DIRECTORS & PRODUCTION PARTNERS
• Always meet people who will actually work on the business (no bait-and-switch)
• Sometimes Jeff directs when appropriate
• Work with makers: editors, animators, photographers, sound engineers
• Everyone feels part of the company, everyone contributes
• Learn to do things you don't know how to do (VR for Dalí Museum)


📱 MODERN ADVERTISING LANDSCAPE

BRANDS NEED SOCIAL CONSCIENCE
• Mad Men days are gone
• Brands should stand for something beyond product

ADVERTISING IS EVERYTHING AND NOTHING
• The rules are off—there are no rules anymore
• Acts matter more than commercials sometimes

• Examples: Cheetos Museum, Cheetos selling on eBay

DIGITAL & HUMANITY

🏆 CAMPAIGN CASE STUDIES

GOT MILK? (California Milk Processors)

Strategy Foundation:
• Milk was boring, "Milk does a body good" wasn't working
• Planner John Steele's insight: "Only time I notice milk is if I run out"
• Absence of product could sell the product (deprivation strategy)
• Wouldn't you be afraid to run out?

Creation of Tagline:
• Jeff suggested "Got Milk?" for foam core board
• Planner wanted "Got Enough Milk?"
• Jeff insisted on shorter version
• Rich initially thought it was stupid: "That's it? That's the stupidest thing"
• Rich realized it needed visual/audio voice identity
• Set quirky type, recorded "Got Milk?" audio

The Pitch:
• Video in office fridge—people opening door, no milk, frustration
• Put milk mustaches on everyone in leave-behind book
• John Steele suggested campaign of famous people with mustaches
• Jeff said it would be "first level"—turned it down
• (National Milk Board later did milk mustache campaign successfully)

Aaron Burr Commercial:
• First spot produced, still the best
• Michael Bay directed (before Transformers fame)
• Guy's an expert on Aaron Burr, gets call for $10,000 question
• Mouth full of peanut butter sandwich, no milk
• Bay added: car in loft, actual gun, giant glass, wide-angle details
• Jeff looked at Tom Weldoun's edit: "It's done. It's perfect."
• Nothing was changed—only time that ever happened

Cultural Impact:
• Woman at school cake sale: "Can we say 'Got Cakes?'"
• "Got Jesus" shirts, lactating mothers' "Got Milk" shirts
• Jeff Manning (client) had no money for lawyers, let people use it
• Became part of popular culture beyond advertising
• Letting go of ownership was the best decision
• Still seen on truck mudflaps, used everywhere

BUDWEISER LIZARDS

Origin Story:
• Budweiser had Frogs campaign (bud-weis-er)
• Running out of ideas, August Busch IV said they'd kill the frogs
• Jeff on plane back from St. Louis got excited about killing frogs idea
• Suggested raccoons, team said lizards (cold-blooded, mean)
• First cast as Scorsese goodfellas types
• Changed to Jewish Catskill comedians—funny, won't actually kill frogs
• Personalities: Louis gets pissed off, Frank calms him down

Success:
• Only planned few spots for Super Bowl
• Ran four spots, four of five top-rated that year
• Kept doing them for years
• On hold music when calling brewery
• "That's a campaign"

BMW: THE ULTIMATE DRIVING MACHINE

First Attempt Lost:
• Pitched Porsche twice, lost first time
• Guy Day (Chiat Day) explained why: Germans want formal presentation
• Can't be slobs, roll up sleeves, sit down
• Must leave coat on, buttoned up, stand when talking
• Never talk over each other—looks like you don't trust each other

Winning the Pitch:
• Applied all lessons learned
• Process of many meetings over months
• Treated every meeting as if it were the last
• Always surprised them with work, passion—even when they didn't ask
• Made "Man and Machine" video
• Voiceover of man and woman speaking about man and machine
• Very sexy, resonated deeply
• Arrived at "The Ultimate Driving Machine"
• Every touch point was a quality meeting

Regaining Business Years Later:
• Friend said BMW changed media agency, might change creative too
• Jeff went to Pebble Beach car release with press pass
• Met CEO, talked about Germany and Porsche
• Called Meredith (PR person) for photos of marketing people
• Found global marketing head, used bad German to introduce himself
• She said "send me work"
• Made website showing auto work
• Got invited into review
• Moral: "If I can do that as chairman, you can too"

SATURN: IT'S DIFFERENT IN A SATURN

Strategy:
• "Different kind of car, different kind of company"—too many words
• Shortened to "It's different in a Saturn"
• DNA was people first, not cars
• They cared about people inside, cars served the people

Execution:
• Jamie Barrett's idea: What if there were no cars, just people?
• Bold idea for car company to not show cars
• People walking as if they were cars
• Director taught people to walk like cars (car waddle)
• People in traffic, with flashlights as headlights
• Kids trailing behind school bus
• "When we make our cars, we don't see sheet metal, we see people"

E-TRADE SUPER BOWL

Strategy:
• Super Bowl spots are very expensive ($2M then, $4-5M+ now)
• Make that part of the message

Execution:
• Monkey dancing with two guys in garage, off-beat
• "We just wasted $2 million. What are you going to do with your money?"
• Perfect for the product
• Lawyers at party scratching heads during commercial
• When line hit, they went nuts
• Subsequent years: "Money coming out of the wazoo" emergency room doctors

CHEVY SUPER BOWL: END OF THE WORLD

Setup:
• Mayan calendar predicted end of world
• Guy driving around in Chevy truck after apocalypse, with dog
• Meets three other guys in Chevy trucks
• "Where's Dave?"
• "Dave didn't drive the longest lasting most dependable truck on the road"

Legal Crisis:
• Day before Super Bowl, GM lawyers called client
• Said Ford would sue—can't say their truck wouldn't survive
• Joel Iwanik (client) called Jeff worried
• Jeff: "What kind of argument is that? Ford can't make it through end of world?"
• "That's not a real argument. I'd like to hear them make that case"
• Client agreed, ran it
• Moral: Be sane, recognize absurdity, laugh a little

DORITOS & CHEETOS SUPER BOWL WORK

📚 BECOMING BETTER AT THE CRAFT

FOR COPYWRITERS

Study Language:
• Actually study the language, not just slang
• Proofreaders constantly ignored—listen to them
• Better words, better sentence construction matters
• Read widely—"Joseph Stalin read 500 pages a day, you can read a lot too"
• Reading is raw material for writing and teaches you about your audience

Don't Have a Style:
• "A lot of people are like 'that's my style, I always write stoned'"
• That's not a style, that's something you do once
• Great writers can do many different things
• Write funny, write serious, write beautiful
• Don't be the poet all the time—be the idiot, then the poet
• Learn how to do all of it

Take Liberties With Reason:
• If you take liberty with language, have a reason
• Got Milk is grammatically correct (some think it isn't)
• Breaking rules requires understanding rules first

FOR DESIGNERS

Observe Everything:
• Look at past, present, future
• Open your eyes to the world
• Cars, architecture, electronics, clothing—design is everything
• Where you sit in your room is important
• Environment is your design

Find Great Design:
• Go to record stores, look at album art
• Find avenues where great design happens
• Don't sit at home looking at computer—go touch it, experience it
• Be curious: "How did that happen? What's going on over there?"
• Watch buildings being built, look at clothes in windows
• Take images constantly yourself

Practice Constantly:
• "How do you get to Carnegie Hall? Practice, practice, practice"
• Do work yourself even if you don't show anybody
• Rich writes story every morning for 15 years
• Rewrites constantly, overcomes dyslexia through practice

FOR CREATIVE DIRECTORS

No One Is Born To Do It:
• Trial and error, takes time
• Study it deliberately to get better
• Force yourself to study: How are people reacting? What are they thinking?
• Have I been straightforward enough? Dampened their enthusiasm?
• Ask yourself these questions constantly

Balance Honesty & Encouragement:
• First instinct: try to make everybody happy (mistake)
• Must find way to say you're not happy and still have them come back
• "Tough love"—stop being friend, be honest partner
• Don't scare them—they must come back and make it better
• Job is to be editor, not competitor

Don't Do It For Them:
• Mistake: try to compete, prove you can write/art direct better
• Better to work with them and edit
• On-the-spot suggestions work
• Coming back next day with finished layout doesn't work
• It's always their idea (even when it's yours)

Know Your People:
• Scope out personalities
• Some can take direct feedback, jokes, being told it sucks
• Others will "jump off the bridge" if you stop them mid-script
• Treat different people differently
• Watch body language—creative people sensitive to every word

Work Process:
• Don't wait for Holy Grail answer
• Work with people before ideas fully fleshed out
• Talk about ideas in rough form
• Easier to kill things before weekend spent making them beautiful
• Fine line between half-baked and crazy-but-worth-discussing
• Invite the latter


🎓 GETTING STARTED & BREAKING IN

PORTFOLIO BUILDING

Charles Martell's Advice (McCann):
• Pick three campaigns you think are good—write more ads as if you worked there
• Pick three you hate—write new campaigns for them
• Pick three companies—invent three new products for each
• Write one-page autobiography (funny, not resume)
• Make it entertaining so people know you'd be fun to work with

Jeff's Encyclopedia Entry:
• Wrote as if encyclopedia entry about himself
• All true up to interview date
• Changed agency name to match interview
• Rest was total baloney (crown prince of advertising, awards, ideas became movies)
• Made fun of himself, showed he wouldn't take himself too seriously
• Important to get across

Pro Bono for Portfolio:
• Don't make thing up then pitch organization
• Go to organization first: "I want to help. What are your problems?"
• When you come in with solution, usually don't know their real problems
• Example: Golden Gate Recreation Area needed renaming, new nomenclature
• Create things for portfolio that show who you are and what you care about

INTERVIEWING & PERSISTENCE

Keep at It:
• Jeff interviewed 15+ times at places before getting job
• "I was fearless but really insecure"
• Showed what he had everywhere possible
• Make your own luck by persistence
• Like actor going to castings—keep going until it hits

Ask for Feedback:
• If you want to be designer, interview with designers
• Ask why they don't like it or why they do
• Use feedback to improve

Change If It Doesn't Feel Right:
• No one knows exactly where they're going
• If it doesn't feel right, change it
• But if it does feel right, keep working at it
• Something will happen if you want it and believe in it

Act Like You Know:
• "You have to act like you know what you're doing even if you don't"
• Everyone is insecure—even jerks are most insecure
• If we're all insecure, then what we have is honesty
• Speak how you feel, be yourself
• If they don't like you, that's okay
• Believe in yourself—when you do, you don't have to fake it
• "You are you is good enough"

LISTENING & LEARNING

Listen to Everything:
• Good ideas come from anywhere: clients, kids, cab drivers
• Resist tendency to believe only your ideas are good
• "I Love This Game" came from fan at NBA game
• "Sega!" scream came from animator at animation house
• World gives you answers if you listen

Be Students of the World:
• Music, theater, art, food, architecture
• Appreciate everything and communicate that appreciation
• Be of the now, be popular culture machines
• Students of popular culture get the best insights

Don't Need Advertising Background:
• Don't have to be advertising or film student
• Sometimes advertising students "poisoned with history, know too much"
• Like improv: be fast on feet, emote sense of what you're doing
• Nobody really knows what they're doing anyway

Opportunity to Remake Everything:

Emperor's New Clothes:

Strategic Decisions:
• What are you going to pitch?

PITCHING TACTICS

Every Meeting Is a Meeting to Lose:
• Not a meeting to win—meeting to lose
• Always have people who will really work on business present
• Never bait and switch
• Jeff and Rich don't do all pitches

Quality Every Time:
• Every single touch point is quality meeting
• Treat every meeting as if it's the last
• Work brains out, work all weekend
• Surprise them with work and passion they didn't ask for
• BMW: months of meetings, always treated as last meeting

Read the Room:
• Sensitive to what they're liking/not liking
• Lose stuff they're not liking in next meeting
• That's hard to do but necessary

Make It Memorable:
• SEGA: Everyone in company learned different game
• Put up bleachers around room
• Every game has specialist—ask any question

Be Scrappy & Personal:
• Jeff went to BMW car release at Pebble Beach

🌟 GIVING BACK & SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY

WHY GIVE BACK

Built-In Guilt:
• Advertising people have "built-in feeling of guilt" about daily work
• Designer quote: "Designers think advertising people are whores, advertising people think designers are airheads"
• People want to do something explicitly good
• Less of a client, more of a heart
• "Put talent to heart—it's lovely"

✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS & ACTION ITEMS

MINDSET SHIFTS

1. You're Not Selling, You're Creating What People Want to See
   - Stop thinking about "how to sell anything to anyone"
   - Think about creating things you'd want to experience
   - Respect that you're entering someone's personal space

2. Be Yourself—It's Good Enough
   - Everyone is insecure (even jerks most insecure)
   - Honesty over faking it
   - Speak how you feel
   - If they don't like you, that's okay
   - Believe in yourself = don't have to fake it

3. Listen More Than You Create
   - Good ideas come from everywhere
   - Cab drivers, kids, clients, people at games
   - Be alert all the time for material
   - Study the world around you
   - Part of your job is NOT doing your job—looking for things to do

4. Start With Truth
   - People smell phoniness
   - Come from truth no matter how loony
   - Observe culture rather than making things up
   - Use real insights into how people think and feel

PRACTICAL ACTIONS

Building Your Foundation:
□ Create portfolio using Charles Martell's method
□ Write one-page autobiography (funny, shows personality)
□ Do pro bono work—but ask organization their problems first
□ Interview everywhere—15+ places if needed
□ Ask for feedback: why they don't like it, why they do
□ Study language and read widely (500 pages/day if Stalin could)
□ Don't have a style—practice writing many ways
□ Observe design everywhere: cars, architecture, clothes, buildings
□ Take images constantly yourself
□ Practice daily even if you don't show anyone

Creating Work:
□ Always start with the ending—know where you're going
□ Never present an idea by its title
□ Create timing charts for commercials (above line: sound, below: visuals)
□ Modern method: record yourself with timing, draw scenes, edit in iMovie
□ Test in casting—if not funny there, won't be funny with money
□ Use mood boards with movement, GIFs, music
□ Make "idea videos" to sell concepts
□ Film yourself presenting (selfies)

Working With Others:
□ Read the room—study people across table
□ Present differently to different people
□ Never talk over your partner
□ Quality at every touchpoint, every meeting
□ Lose what they're not liking in next meeting
□ Work with people before ideas fully baked
□ Give tough love: honest but encouraging
□ Know who can take direct feedback vs. needs gentleness
□ Meet one-on-one, not in boardrooms
□ Make suggestions on the spot, not next day with layouts

Selling Ideas:
□ Don't call them crazy—make sense of them first
□ Strategy is your friend—use it to sell ideas
□ Never tell people how to feel—let them feel it
□ Let ideas roll downhill with everyone participating
□ Be persistent—come back with better materials, research
□ Know the big boss if possible
□ Understand CMO's problems (share their foxhole)
□ Study the client and people across table over time

Career Development:
□ Act like you know what you're doing even when you don't
□ If it doesn't feel right, change it
□ But if it does feel right, keep working at it
□ Make your own luck through persistence
□ Get to know business reporters and press (carefully)
□ Seek out fame—care whether work gets noticed
□ Run toward fire—take chances, be provocative
□ Change jobs if needed—interview at 15 places like Jeff did
