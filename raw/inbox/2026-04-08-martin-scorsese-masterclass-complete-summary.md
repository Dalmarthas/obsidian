MARTIN SCORSESE MASTERCLASS: COMPLETE SUMMARY
===============================================

📋 OVERVIEW

Martin Scorsese's masterclass is a deeply personal exploration of filmmaking as both craft and calling. Speaking from Alice Tully Hall at Lincoln Center—where he first showed Mean Streets in 1973—Scorsese emphasizes that this course is not for those intrigued by moviemaking as a career, but for those who feel they must make films. Throughout 20 lessons, he shares insights from over five decades of filmmaking, covering everything from his formative experiences in New York's Lower East Side to technical aspects of production, always returning to the central theme: filmmaking as an obsession and a need to tell stories.

The masterclass is organized around Scorsese's belief that there are no shortcuts or universal processes in filmmaking. Instead, he advocates for developing your individual voice, learning from cinema history while finding your own path, and maintaining the "spark"—that essential creative impulse that drives you to make films. He emphasizes the importance of passion over technical perfection, collaboration built on trust, and the willingness to let films have their own life during production. Drawing from his own experiences making films like Taxi Driver, Raging Bull, Goodfellas, and many others, Scorsese provides both practical wisdom and philosophical guidance for aspiring filmmakers.

🎯 CORE PHILOSOPHICAL THEMES

THE CALLING OF FILMMAKING
• Filmmaking is a vocation, not just a career—you must feel compelled to tell stories
• The transition from one calling (priesthood) to another (cinema) requires honesty with yourself
• You must do what you're called to do within yourself, not what you think you should do
• Cinema, like the church, informed Scorsese's worldview through visual impact and ritual
• The moral questions that emerge from faith—doubt, good and evil, living morally in a tough world—became central to his storytelling

THE INDIVIDUAL VOICE
• Guard your unique perspective—it's what's precious and irreplaceable
• Don't be conscious of developing a style; let it reveal itself naturally
• Your background, experiences, and observations form the basis of authentic storytelling
• The essential question: "What do you see as cinematic within your way of life?"
• Everything in life is cinematic; it depends on how you perceive it

CINEMA AS LANGUAGE AND LITERACY
• Moving images are not inferior to written words—both are modes of communication of equal value
• Visual literacy is simply literacy, period
• Ancient writing shows words and images are almost indistinguishable—they share a common source
• Not all images are meant to be consumed like fast food—some pursue something mysterious
• Understanding the difference between images that sell, entertain, inform, and tell stories is crucial

THE VALUE OF CINEMA HISTORY
• Know the old masters not to copy them, but to see if they speak to you
• Be aware of what came before, even if you ultimately reject it
• You need to know what you're rejecting to make informed creative choices
• The 100+ years of cinema history is essential context for understanding the medium
• Early filmmakers created a new language and art form—we're building on their foundation


🎬 FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS

THE NATURE OF THE MOVING IMAGE
• Cinema is about sequences of images, not single images
• When you place one image next to another, a third "phantom event" happens in the mind's eye
• Adding or removing a single frame changes this mental image—each frame is crucial
• The power of film happens despite theoretical ideas, in the actual experience
• A shot has its own value independent of what you initially intended it for

THE SCREENPLAY AS STARTING POINT
• Scripts aren't written, they're rewritten (Alexander McKendrick)
• The screenplay is not the final product—the film is
• One draft is rarely enough; the process is about discovery
• Reading something and shooting it often reveals that screen time is longer than expected
• The script allows you to open up character and world, but you must stay true to the plot when necessary

RESEARCH AND ACCURACY
• Research provides the bedrock of your picture—the rules of the world reveal themselves
• But don't let research lead you away from what sparked the film initially
• "There's nothing more boring than accuracy on a film"—transform what you learn
• Historical details can dictate framing, sound, and actor movement in unexpected ways
• Balance accuracy with the story you're trying to tell

PASSION PROJECTS VS. COMMERCIAL WORK
• Some films you must make because you can't rest until you tell that particular story
• Other projects may intrigue you for different reasons—early aviation, recreation of history
• The key element is always a desire and need to express these thoughts on film
• Sometimes you're drawn to material that recalls films you loved (film noir, for example)
• Trust your instincts about which projects deserve your complete commitment


🎥 PRE-PRODUCTION PROCESS

FINDING YOUR STORY

Sources of Material:
• Personal experience and observation from your own life and world
• Stories that come solely from your interests or concerns
• Other writers' scripts that speak to you philosophically or thematically
• Adaptations of novels or nonfiction material that resonate deeply
• Moral questions you're compelled to explore: sin, redemption, good and evil

Evaluating Material:
• Does it have a point of view that's very close to you?
• Does it explore themes you're constantly drawn to?
• Can you bring something personal to someone else's script?
• Are you willing to go through the arduous process of bringing it to life?
• Does it attract you because of inherent moral themes and choices?

WORKING WITH THE SCRIPT

Development Process:
• Expect multiple drafts—it's just the beginning after the first draft
• Each draft sparks something new as you see it in your mind
• For nonfiction, distill the source material down to film size
• Research can lead in fascinating directions but may threaten to overwhelm
• Find the structural concept—visual and narrative—that makes it work

Screenplay to Screen:
• Design much of the film on paper: drawings, diagrams, notes, edited sequences
• Some come to fruition, some don't, but mostly they do
• The script allows you to envision editing patterns before shooting
• Rehearsals become writing sessions—actors help develop character truth
• Be prepared to rewrite on set and again in the editing room

Opening Up Scenes:
• Strong scripts allow you to improvise or expand specific moments
• Work within character—if something develops differently, it must fit who they are
• Some films (Goodfellas, Casino, Wolf of Wall Street) need life to come through overtly
• Trust between you, the writer, and actors enables exploration
• Know when to return to the plot versus when to let character breathe

CASTING

The Casting Process:
• Casting is 85-90% of the picture
• Don't settle for "close enough" or second best—there are no shortcuts
• Meet people, spend time with them, talk with them extensively
• Put actors together with each other to see how they respond
• Your casting director must know you want to work with actors making the same film

Finding the Right Actor:
• Look for actors who come from the world/culture you're depicting
• Mix non-actors with actors when appropriate—it can reduce artificiality
• The person must already be there in some way—you can't teach someone to lean against a car in 1955
• Watch for moments when the actor inhabits the character so completely you forget who they are
• Some actors transform through finding posture, manner, use of language (Ben Kingsley as Georges Méliès)

Building Relationships:
• Work with people who share similar life experience and worldview
• Trust is essential—you must all be making the same film
• Long-term collaborations (De Niro in 8 movies, DiCaprio in 5) build on shared understanding
• The ability to take chances without fear comes from trust and comfort
• Be prepared for actors who can't perform due to various issues—have contingency plans


🎨 PRODUCTION DESIGN & VISUAL ELEMENTS

PRODUCTION DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

Truthfulness to Time and Place:
• Draw on memory, heightened or otherwise
• Be as truthful as possible to the period while serving the story
• Some films require accuracy (Age of Innocence, Gangs of New York)
• Others allow more stylization (Casino, New York New York)
• The design should capture the spirit of the time and place

Creating Authentic Worlds:
• Use actual locations when possible for truthfulness
• Build sets that feel lived in, not like costumes or stage sets
• For period pieces, research actual families and base details on historical fact
• Make production design decisions that reflect character and story theme
• Sometimes take license—gangs dressed differently, you can push this further

Stylization vs. Naturalism:
• New York, New York intentionally used codified studio musical look from 1940s-50s
• Knew the rear projections and high curbs weren't "real" but accepted as real
• Placed improvisatory acting style against artificial backgrounds as experiment
• Casino went for spectacle—glitz, theatrical, like a Vegas nightclub act
• Gangs of New York blended naturalistic setting with science fiction in reverse

COSTUME DESIGN

Period vs. Contemporary:
• Period costume design involves accuracy plus artistic flourish (Piero Tosi, House of Tirelli)
• Contemporary design requires understanding the world and character deeply
• Know where characters shop, inherit clothes, what colors work for them
• Understand contemporary expression in dressing—style changes over time
• Costumes must always come from character—what would she/he actually wear?

Finding Character Through Costume:
• De Niro finding the hat for Mean Streets showed he understood the culture
• The black jacket with white top in Raging Bull made a character statement
• Rupert Pupkin's complete look (seersucker suit, blue shirt, yellow tie) came from a mannequin in a window
• Test extensively—20-30 different noses for Raging Bull's makeup
• Costume comes from the actual environment the story takes place in, not imposed upon it

CINEMATOGRAPHY

Understanding Light:
• Scorsese admits he never fully understood light and still doesn't
• Growing up in tenements: daylight and night were the main distinctions
• Work closely with directors of photography who add the element of light
• Learn a little each time, even if you forget afterwards—it shouldn't stop you
• Intensity of light relates directly to depth of focus (f-stop, small opening, more light needed)

Working with Cinematographers:
• Michael Ballhaus brought freedom with camera and light, especially on After Hours and Last Temptation
• Freddie Francis taught about transforming images—one eye instead of two creates mystery
• The camera operator on Moby Dick learned: it's not just "a shot of a whale"—needs interpretation
• Different DP relationships serve different purposes—old prose style vs. newer approaches
• Trust your DP to understand your vision and execute it technically

Camera Movement and Lenses:
• Design camera moves that mean something, not arbitrary motion
• Steadicam example: technology won't direct the film for you—you must still design shots
• Specific lenses create specific effects (Freddie Francis's five lenses for deep focus)
• The equipment is a tool—art dictates technology, not the other way around
• Physical limitations of equipment can affect shooting time (Raging Bull fights took 10 weeks instead of 5)

COLOR

Emotional Response to Color:
• Loved vibrant three-strip Technicolor from childhood (Duel in the Sun)
• The moment in Wizard of Oz when sepia becomes Technicolor was magical
• Two-color films felt especially magical
• Color process changes affected how color appeared on screen
• Black and white is also color—it's a different aesthetic, not inferior

Using Color in Storytelling:
• When a picture is made in color, you must design in color
• Color means something, even when you don't want it to
• Be careful of colors you allow in the frame
• Use of primary colors is important for impact
• Desaturated color (Moby Dick style) can create specific moods

The Aviator's Color Journey:
• Represented color as available in whatever Technicolor process existed at that time
• Started with two-color look from 1920s
• Slowly slipped into saturated three-strip Technicolor
• Ended with colder color
• Required costume designer to design for specific color processes
• Digital intermediate made this obsession possible

COLOR IN POST-PRODUCTION

Digital Intermediate Possibilities:
• DI increased possibilities of color dramatically
• Strange because there is no limit—so many possibilities
• Must force yourself to limit those possibilities
• Don't let technology take over
• Just because you can do something doesn't mean you should


📍 PRODUCTION

LOCATIONS

Location Scouting:
• A good location scout knows exactly what you want (if you know)
• They should bring back 4-6 different options showing various looks
• Photos and video of locations are deceptive—always better to go in person
• Scorsese personally doesn't like scouting anymore due to traffic and time
• Sometimes you trust images and regret not visiting—go when possible

Understanding Space:
• You may think you have more room than you actually do based on overhead diagrams
• Doors, walls, and immovable objects affect camera placement
• Example: Shutter Island spiral staircase needed to be turned for camera movement
• Always best to see locations and spend time there if not building a set
• Limitations can become advantages if you work with them creatively

What Matters Most:
• Only important thing is what it looks like on screen
• Actors may find it difficult if their perception of reality differs from camera view
• On film it might look right even if it feels wrong in person
• Visit locations at the right time of day if shooting day scenes
• Multiple locations must come together as one coherent world (Age of Innocence)

SHOOTING LOW-BUDGET FILMS

Learning Efficiency:
• After Hours: Learning to make pictures faster, cheaper, with energy intact
• Shot in 40 nights, averaging 25-26 setups per night
• Michael Ballhaus reinstilled excitement and love of the process
• Designed all shots beforehand in drawings and diagrams on script edges
• The excitement: "Tonight we do the shot that begins on the chair, tracks up, around face..."

Last Temptation of Christ:
• Very low budget, shot very quickly in Morocco
• Every movement, every setup was hard to do—just accept it
• The arduousness became part of the experience: "This film has to be made this way"
• Became more than making a film—became a life experience
• One company move only; had to shoot crucifixion in middle instead of at end

Structured Efficiency:
• For 75-shot crucifixion scene, planned 25 shots per day
• Cut down to 50 shots over 2 days due to schedule
• Timed each shot: 10 minutes for simple, 15 for more complex, 45 for key moment
• "Quality time"—intent on getting what was needed within time limit
• Shot extra takes when possible, deciding which to use in editing

WORKING WITH CREW

Core Team Philosophy:
• Really good to have people you trust—basic element in filmmaking
• If it's a passion project and everyone's invested, that's rewarding
• Feel you've gone through an experience together
• On bigger pictures, small core is your lifeline
• This core should share desire to make the film, not overdo their role

The Director's Challenge:
• Stanley Kubrick: hardest part of directing is getting out of the car in the morning
• Once out, you're besieged with questions and problems
• From "Have you got your right shoes?" to actors disagreeing about entire character arc
• Goal: eliminate unnecessary questions during the day
• Cut through equipment and complications to get back to your original idea

Dealing with the Machine:
• Don't be intimidated by enormity of the machine
• Technology of today shouldn't stop you from saying something
• If you can't do something, don't let them tell you that you can't
• Knowledgeable people who like working with you will find ways to help
• Core group of 3-4 people deals with trust and excitement about what you're doing

Extreme Conditions:
• Silence in Taiwan: Shooting in tail end of typhoon
• AD and DP soaked, beaten, but ready to shoot
• Crew was set, actors (playing martyrs) were already in the scene
• Everyone committed despite difficult conditions
• Weather and commitment dictated rapid shooting

DIRECTING ACTORS

Understanding Acting:
• Important for directors to know something about acting
• Good to get in front of camera and fail miserably, then succeed a little
• Understand challenges: hitting light, being there for other actor, walking across room naturally
• Know the fears actors face and techniques they use
• Helps find the connection that sparks between you

The Director's Role:
• Term "director" can be misleading when it comes to actors
• Orient actors because you're always shooting out of sequence
• They need to know where they are in story and emotionally
• Let actors explore—tell them to try anything, it's OK to fail
• Freedom to fail, especially in rehearsal, is essential

Working Method:
• Have time to fail as much as possible on set if time permits
• Shape through discussion if something goes in different direction
• Sometimes cover scenes you know won't work—it's part of actor's process
• Stopping certain actors from trying something can throw them off
• Best to let them work it out, shoot it, then use it or not

Building Long-Term Relationships:
• De Niro (8 movies), DiCaprio (5 movies) based on similar life experience
• De Niro knew the neighborhood, codes, behavior, culture
• DiCaprio's instincts and curiosity similar, 30 years later
• Ability to take chances without fear comes from trust
• Can open character and enrich within context of who character is

Trust and Exploration:
• With solid story, actors can take scene in new directions
• Go with them, see what it is, decide in discussion and rehearsal
• May do it and not use it—doesn't mean you intended not to use it
• Sometimes things that seem wrong in the moment work in context of cut
• Some actors (like Harvey Keitel) understood the work and world deeply

Practical Challenges:
• Sometimes actors are incapacitated (chemicals or other issues)
• Get them to do just enough, cover in such a way
• Takes time and patience
• May need to recast if problems arise early
• Choose actors who can stay on track no matter what's happening internally


✂️ EDITING

EDITING AS SACRED SPACE

The Heart of Cinema:
• The editing room is as sacred as the set
• This is where the film really comes alive
• Every time you enter, you're struck by the wonder of it all
• Sometimes it's rote, hard physical work, but something always happens
• The third phantom event—the image in the mind's eye—is the essence of cinema

The Power of a Single Frame:
• Take one frame away or add two frames—the mind's eye image changes
• Old prints with jump cuts or missing pieces made scenes different by accident
• Everything depends on that one frame—it is storytelling
• Each frame is important, not just for technical reasons but for meaning
• This is filmmaking, not just "succession of beautifully composed rendering of script pages"

David Lean's Approach:
• Lean came from film editing background
• Would rehearse scene with actors and time it (e.g., 2:30)
• Then do it in 2:20, then 2:00, then 1:40—picking up pace
• What he was pointing out: things appear longer on film
• Has to do with how brain perceives 24 frames per second
• Always found himself trying to pick up pace as much as possible

WORKING WITH THELMA SCHOONMAKER

The Relationship:
• Began collaboration on Raging Bull after difficult previous experiences
• Trust is paramount—won't walk in to find scene re-edited without you
• Same mind, like-mindedness, not part of industry machinery
• Loyalty is to the film and director, not studio or financiers
• She memorizes footage and remembers different takes for specific needs

The Process:
• Look at rushes daily when possible (harder with remote locations)
• Make selects in order of preference during dailies
• Pull everything that might be useful, even if unsure
• During recutting, often use stuff you thought you wouldn't use
• Director directs the cutting of the scene, especially if not pre-designed

Technical Precision:
• Sometimes it's about two frames too long in the middle shot
• "Add a frame on the third. Still off. Take one off the second, put two on the third."
• She knows how to achieve what he intends technically
• She knows certain patterns and what he likes
• No arguing about trying different approaches—understands the vision

EDITING STRATEGIES

Iterative Editing for Long Content:
• For short content (<100 lines), create complete file in one attempt
• For long content (>100 lines), use iterative editing
• Build file across multiple tool calls: outline, add sections, refine
• Start with structure, add content section by section
• Review and refine throughout process

Designed Sequences vs. Open Scenes:
• Scenes with mathematical design (1A to 1B to 1C) go together more fluidly
• Mean Streets, Taxi Driver, Raging Bull fights were well laid out and drawn
• Open scenes without specific shot design require more collaboration
• Work on scene, editor assembles, come back and refine
• Sometimes work done at night must be completely redone next day

Film Structure and Pacing:
• After Hours: First cut was 2:35, needed to be 90 minutes
• Reading something and shooting it takes longer on screen
• Had to find ways to trim story down through testing with audiences
• Rewriting and reshooting for four days helped achieve right pace
• Painful to cut two or three favorite scenes, but necessary

The Assembly Process:
• George Lucas: "If you like your first cut, you're in trouble"
• Should get physically ill seeing first assembly—if not, something's wrong
• It's never what you wanted; things misfire and don't happen as planned
• Use edited patterns but they're not working everywhere
• May have to do middle or end first due to special effects or actor availability

SCREENING AND REVISION

Private Screenings:
• First screenings are just for yourselves
• Then screen for trusted colleagues (Scorsese showed After Hours to Gilliam, Powell, Spielberg)
• Get suggestions but maintain your vision
• Some things you thought were clear in shooting are unclear
• Find different ways together to clarify what should be clarified

Audience Testing:
• Very important for certain kinds of films
• Get feedback on whether people understand things
• Decide together what needs clarification
• At certain point, realize what you don't want clarified
• Some will get it, some won't—that's acceptable

Final Decisions:
• Some directors give same scene to three editors—different valid approaches
• Ultimately must look at it when think it's finished
• It's never finished—there's always more that could be done
• The work in progress continues sometimes into final mix
• Even color correction can change tone of picture

Computer vs. Film Editing:
• Immediate changes possible with computer editing (2-3 minutes vs. 25-30 minutes)
• Lost the valuable process of waiting and absorbing changes
• Threw Scorsese for a while—had to learn different approach
• Too many choices possible now—must steel yourself against them
• Need to stay focused on what you want to say, cut away extraneous
• Everything at fingertips means you must know what you're doing even more


🎼 POST-PRODUCTION ELEMENTS

SOUND AND MUSIC

Sound Design:
• Frank Warner on Raging Bull: exhausted all ways to express sounds of punches
• Decided to take sound OUT for one big moment—worked perfectly
• Created in the mix, all working together
• Sound effects in ring scenes were impressions of how Jake saw it
• Everything twisted and turned to represent perception after being punched

Music Usage:
• Wolf of Wall Street rehearsals: told actors to take it to a point, stop, save for cameras
• Casino: "Sunshine of Your Love" guitar riff hit right when De Niro's eyes flared
• Combination of planning (36 fps camera move in) and spontaneous editing with music
• Music can be part of structural concept from beginning
• Sometimes music becomes the editing—rhythm and pace

VOICEOVER

Natural Element in Storytelling:
• For Scorsese, voiceover was always a natural element in movies
• Not a literary device but one of the cinematic elements
• Inspired by Kind Hearts and Coronets (restrained humor, irony)
• Also Shoot the Piano Player and Jules and Jim (Truffaut)
• Barry Lyndon's use of language in voiceover influenced Age of Innocence

Goodfellas Approach:
• Whole movie structured around voiceover
• Not just exposition in a dull, didactic way
• Expresses joy, warmth, love between characters
• Freewheeling aspect of life they're living
• Ironic commentary rather than just explanation

Age of Innocence:
• Used Edith Wharton's language directly from novel
• Some questioned "Who's talking?"—the storyteller, the person living story
• Took advantage of literary quality while making it cinematic
• Part of visual storytelling, not separate from it
• Used Barry Lyndon's language approach as inspiration

TECHNOLOGY AND INNOVATION

Steadicam Lesson:
• Used for Raging Bull fight scene initially
• Realized after a day: technology wasn't going to direct the film
• Scrapped it all and reshot with proper design
• Eventually understood nature of Steadicam for long takes (Goodfellas Copa scene)
• Technology is tool—must still direct equipment and know what it's capable of

Pre-visualization:
• The Aviator: first time using pre-vis extensively
• Taking notes and drawings, transferring to Rob Legato's pre-vis
• Storyboards came alive in new way
• New way of making pictures for action sequences (flying scenes)
• Exciting but required learning as going along

Digital Tools in Editing:
• Can see different elements almost immediately
• React immediately to them
• But immediate nature is a drawback—lost contemplation time
• Need to learn to do without valuable process of waiting
• Must tame the infinite choices available
• Stay committed to essence of what you're doing


🎓 EDUCATION AND LEARNING

FORMAL FILM EDUCATION

NYU Experience:
• Enrolled in Washington Square College 1960—quite small compared to today
• Haig Manoogian taught History of Motion Pictures with drill instructor speed
• Weeded out students who weren't serious
• Second year: Sight and Sound class, learned basic 16mm filmmaking
• By end of year, could make 3-4 minute film

Manoogian's Philosophy:
• Focused on individual voice and stories you had to tell
• Wouldn't let anyone direct unless they wrote the film themselves
• Hated melodrama—wanted authentic personal expression
• Encouraged protecting the spark in yourself
• Understanding the potential of moving image and the cut

The Value of a Shot:
• Manoogian became excited when students used shots differently than planned
• "Now you understand the value of a shot"
• Shot has value in itself, no matter what you shot it for
• May not matter ultimately—takes on own life, intention, essence
• Can't be taught—you have to just do it

What School Can Provide:
• Can't learn to make film in school—you learn it yourself
• School gives opportunity to make a film
• Greatest thing teacher can give: confidence and inspiration
• If you have something passionate, school helps you think maybe you can do it
• Inspiration is more important than technical instruction

LEARNING FROM CINEMA HISTORY

Absorbing Films:
• Don't study while watching for first time—let film work on you
• If hit by certain things, go back and try to find them
• Often memory of scene is different from actual shots
• Why? Use of sound, cut, imperceptible camera move
• Almost photographic memory of images and editing sequences

Working from Memory:
• Before home video, had to remember films or find them in theaters
• Would draw own versions from memory
• Specific shots became key references (High Noon marshal's badge, Ben-Hur chariot race)
• The Small Back Room: traffic light and windshield in rain influenced Taxi Driver
• Northwest Passage: tracking shot with rifle influenced gun scene in Taxi Driver

Making References:
• Generation in '60s-'70s made references to "entertainment" films
• French filmmakers (Godard, Truffaut) referenced Bogart, Hawks, Hitchcock
• Who's That Knocking: used The Searchers as starting point for conversations
• The Searchers reflected America they were experiencing
• Using westerns in Lower East Side film created intentional contrast

INFLUENCES AND INSPIRATION

Italian Influences:
• Rossellini, Fellini, Pasolini taught doing much with little
• Pasolini's Medea: Maria Callas's necklaces made of wood
• Sense of authenticity without being encumbered by production design
• Fellini Satyricon: felt like science fiction in reverse
• Inspired approach to Gangs of New York—naturalistic with sci-fi in reverse element

British Cinema:
• Powell and Pressburger films (The Small Back Room, Tales of Hoffmann)
• Maybe liked British cinematographers because of overcast in their country
• Different approach to light than Hollywood productions
• Alec Guinness, Olivier, James Mason—different world but inspiring performances

American Independent:
• Cassavetes's Shadows proved you could make narrative film without studio system
• Lightweight equipment enabled true independence
• No more excuses—if they could do it, anyone could
• But must have spark, desire, passion to say something utilizing film
• Scenes with people gave sense of authenticity that felt alive beyond screen


📚 KEY LESSONS BY TOPIC

LESSON 1: INTRODUCTION
• Filmmaking as necessity vs. career interest
• Speaking from Lincoln Center, showing Mean Streets there in 1973
• No blueprints or shortcuts—must find your own way
• Practical advice combined with personal philosophy
• Guard what's precious: your unique voice

LESSON 2: BEGINNINGS
• Relationship between priesthood calling and filmmaking calling
• Church and cinema both made sense through visual impact
• Growing up in Lower East Side informed storytelling
• Moral questions from faith became central to films
• Early exposure to movies created distant dream of filmmaking

LESSON 3: MARTIN'S EDUCATION
• NYU film department in early 1960s—small, intense
• Haig Manoogian's teaching: individual voice, write your own scripts
• Understanding value of a shot through doing
• Can't learn filmmaking in school—you learn it yourself
• School provides opportunity, confidence, and inspiration

LESSON 4: DISCOVERING YOUR PROCESS
• No universal process that covers all bases
• Different filmmakers work in completely different ways
• Common sense preparation: talk to costume, DP, locations people before shooting
• Film must have its own life—stay open to what happens
• Goodfellas examples of unexpected moments adding value

LESSON 5: CHANNELING YOUR INFLUENCES
• Absorbing films through repeated viewing, not initial study
• Working from memory when films weren't easily accessible
• Making references to classic films in your own work
• The Searchers as starting point for conversations in Who's That Knocking
• Understanding why certain shots stayed with you

LESSON 6: DEVELOPING YOUR STYLE
• Don't be conscious of developing style—let it reveal itself
• Style will be something else than what you think—more essential
• Verbal and visual literacy are equal modes of communication
• Not all images meant to be consumed—some pursue mystery
• Documentary work informs narrative structure

LESSON 7: DIRECTING & TECHNOLOGY
• Technology is tool—art dictates technology, not reverse
• Steadicam lesson: equipment won't direct film for you
• Computer editing: immediate changes vs. valuable waiting process
• Must know what you're doing even more with infinite choices
• Commitment to essence of what you're doing is crucial

LESSON 8: FINDING THE STORY
• Everything is cinematic depending on how you perceive it
• Stories come from personal experience or passionate interest
• Moral choice is inherent in everything we do and observe
• Sometimes intrigued by something and enjoy the process
• Never set out to make film with specific moral theme—themes attract you

LESSON 9: WORKING WITH THE SCRIPT
• Screenplay is step to get to film, not final product
• Scripts are rewritten, not written—it's process of discovery
• Research provides bedrock but shouldn't overwhelm story
• Rehearsals become writing sessions with actors
• Rewriting continues on set and in editing room

LESSON 10: CASTING ACTORS
• Casting is 85-90% of the picture
• Don't settle—spend time meeting people, talking with them
• Put actors together to see how they respond
• All must be making the same film—that's the key
• Mix non-actors with actors when appropriate for authenticity

LESSON 11: DIRECTING ACTORS
• Important to know something about acting—get in front of camera yourself
• Director orients actors since shooting out of sequence
• Give freedom to explore and fail, especially in rehearsal
• Trust built over time enables taking chances
• Sometimes must work with incapacitated actors—requires patience

LESSON 12: LOCATIONS
• Always better to visit location in person—photos are deceptive
• Only thing that matters is what it looks like on screen
• May have less room than diagrams suggest
• Visit at right time of day for lighting needs
• Multiple locations must come together as coherent world

LESSON 13: PRODUCTION DESIGN
• Be truthful to time and place while serving story
• Draw on memory, heightened or otherwise
• Some films allow more stylization than others
• Design should capture spirit of time and place
• Limitations can become advantages with creativity

LESSON 14: COSTUME DESIGN
• Period costume involves accuracy plus artistic flourish
• Contemporary design requires deep understanding of world and character
• Costumes always come from character—what would they actually wear
• Finding character through costume (examples from Mean Streets, Raging Bull)
• Test extensively and trust instincts

LESSON 15: UNDERSTANDING CINEMATOGRAPHY
• Don't be cowed by not knowing everything about light
• Work closely with DP who adds element of light
• Learn a little each time even if you forget
• Different DPs serve different purposes in your career
• Intensity of light relates to depth of focus

LESSON 16: SHOOTING LOW-BUDGET FILMS
• Learn to make pictures faster, cheaper, with energy intact
• After Hours: 40 nights, 25-26 setups per night
• Design all shots beforehand in drawings and diagrams
• Time each shot and stick to schedule
• Arduousness can become part of the experience

LESSON 17: WORKING WITH CREW
• Trust is basic element in filmmaking
• Passion projects where everyone's invested are rewarding
• Core team of 3-4 people is your lifeline
• Hardest part is getting out of car in morning
• Don't let machine intimidate you or tell you what you can't do

LESSON 18: EDITING PART 1
• Editing room is sacred space where film comes alive
• Third phantom event happens in mind's eye
• Single frame matters—adding or removing changes everything
• David Lean's approach: rehearse at different paces
• Trust with editor essential—Thelma Schoonmaker collaboration

LESSON 19: EDITING PART 2
• Envision much of film in terms of editing on page
• Editor knows what you intend, understands patterns
• Working with precision: sometimes two frames matter
• Screenings important for feedback and clarification
• Film never finished—continuous work in progress

LESSON 20: COLOR
• Loved vibrant three-strip Technicolor from childhood
• When picture made in color, must design in color
• Be careful of colors allowed in frame
• The Aviator represented color as available in each Technicolor period
• Digital intermediate increased possibilities but must limit them


✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

ESSENTIAL TRUTHS ABOUT FILMMAKING:
1. Filmmaking must be a need, not just an interest—you can't rest until you tell your story
2. There are no shortcuts or universal blueprints—you must find your own way
3. Guard your unique voice and perspective—that's what's precious and irreplaceable
4. Cinema history provides context, not rules—know it to understand what you're building on or rejecting
5. Every person on your set plays a role—you need their dedication and expertise

THE CREATIVE PROCESS:
6. Let films have their own life during production—stay open to what happens in front of you
7. The screenplay is a starting point, not the final product—rewriting happens at every stage
8. Trust is fundamental—between you, your collaborators, and your actors
9. Casting is 85-90% of the picture—never settle for close enough
10. Design extensively on paper but be ready to adapt when circumstances change

TECHNICAL AND CRAFT WISDOM:
11. Technology is a tool—art dictates technology, not the other way around
12. Each frame matters—adding or removing one frame changes the image in the mind's eye
13. Things appear longer on screen than in reality—pick up the pace
14. What matters most is what it looks like on screen, not what it feels like in person
15. Computer editing offers infinite choices—you must discipline yourself to focus on essence

COLLABORATION AND RELATIONSHIPS:
16. Work with people who share your life experience, worldview, and passion for the project
17. Long-term collaborations build on trust and shared understanding
18. Give actors freedom to explore and fail—it's essential for finding truth
19. Your core team of 3-4 trusted people is your lifeline on any production
20. Loyalty must be to the film and director, not to studio or financiers

PHILOSOPHY AND APPROACH:
21. Moral questions of good and evil, sin and redemption are inherent in storytelling
22. Everything in life is cinematic—it depends on how you perceive it
23. Research provides bedrock but shouldn't lead you away from what sparked the film
24. Some things should remain unclear—not everything needs explanation
25. The process is as important as the product—filmmaking becomes a life experience

FINAL WISDOM:
Making films is about pursuing and discovering something surprising and mysterious. It's not about mass manufacturing but about individual artistic expression. The most important thing you can learn is that you must be honest with yourself about what you're called to do. Every decision—from casting to camera movement to color timing—should serve the story you're burning to tell. And remember: if you really thought about making a picture step by step, you'd never do it. You have to have the passion, bravery, and yes, ignorance, to just do it. That's what's essential. That's what endures.
