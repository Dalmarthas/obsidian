📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass represents Aaron Sorkin's comprehensive approach to screenwriting, covering everything from the fundamental mechanics of drama to the nuanced craft of dialogue and character development. Sorkin, the Oscar-winning writer behind The West Wing, The Social Network, A Few Good Men, and Steve Jobs, shares his process for creating intelligent, fast-paced, dialogue-driven stories that engage audiences intellectually and emotionally.

The course emphasizes that great screenwriting is built on a foundation of clear intention and obstacle—characters who want something badly and face formidable opposition in getting it. From this structural core, Sorkin demonstrates how to develop authentic characters, conduct meaningful research, write captivating dialogue, and craft scenes that propel stories forward. Throughout the lessons, he stresses that writing is less about natural talent and more about understanding the rules of drama, practicing relentlessly, and learning to diagnose why stories work or fail.

Sorkin's philosophy centers on treating the audience as intelligent collaborators in the storytelling event, never talking down to them but also never confusing them. He advocates for writing characters smarter than yourself, finding conflict in ideas rather than just physical action, and approaching every script with the discipline and rigor of a craftsperson rather than waiting for inspiration.

🎯 CORE THEMES & PRINCIPLES

THE FOUNDATION: INTENTION AND OBSTACLE
• The absolute bedrock of every story: somebody wants something, something is standing in their way
• The want must be strong and urgent—not casual or easily dismissed
• The obstacle must be formidable—if it can be overcome easily, it's not worth writing about
• Test both by "pressing on them"—if $20 solves the problem, explain why they don't have $20
• This creates the "driveshaft" of the story—everything else hangs on this clothesline
• Without clear intention and obstacle, you have journalism, not drama
• The protagonist doesn't have to win, but they must try—effort is what matters

THE AUDIENCE AS COLLABORATOR
• The audience is a component in the storytelling event, not passive observers
• Like a pointillist painting, the viewer's mind completes the work
• Audiences want to put things together themselves—treat them as intelligent
• Most satisfying when they don't see reversals coming despite paying attention
• Give them the same clues as your detective/hero—let them try to solve it
• Never confuse the audience—even slight confusion creates apprehension
• But never tell them something they already know—it kills momentum
• The storyteller must have the audience by the hand, leading them confidently

CONFLICT AS THE ENGINE
• Drama requires conflict—no conflict means journalism, not drama
• The conflicts Sorkin prefers are conflicts of ideas, not just physical obstacles
• Ideally, competing ideas should have equal weight and validity
• Characters should believe passionately in their positions
• When writing antagonists, write them as if they're making their case to God
• Never judge your characters—find what's common between you and them
• Even villains must be written from a place of understanding, not condemnation

THE RULES OF DRAMA
• Rules don't restrict art—they make it beautiful (like rules make sports beautiful)
• All rules come from Aristotle's Poetics—2,500 years old and still accurate
• Parts of drama: exposition, inciting action, action, reversal, climax, denouement
• Stories involve motion—you must travel from Point A to Point B
• If you introduce something in Act 1, use it in Act 3 (Chekhov's gun)
• If you use something in Act 3, introduce it in Act 1
• The last 15 minutes of a movie are most important for audience satisfaction
• The first 15 pages of a screenplay are most important for getting it made

🎬 THE COMPLETE SORKIN SCREENWRITING PROCESS

PHASE 1: FINDING AND DEVELOPING IDEAS

What Constitutes an Idea:
• "I want to write about surfing" is NOT an idea—it's just a location
• An idea requires "but, except, and then"—story elements that create conflict
• You don't have an idea until something disrupts normalcy
• Not every interesting subject has dramatic potential (example: Harry Houdini—interesting but lacking conflict in his life)
• Look for intention and obstacle embedded in the premise

Testing Ideas:
• First question: Is there drama? Is there conflict?
• Can you see intention and obstacle clearly?
• Are competing ideas equally strong?
• Is this "your pitch"—something you can write authentically?
• Baseball metaphor: batting .333 (succeeding 1 in 3 times) gets you to the Hall of Fame
• You can swing at a good pitch and still miss—failure is normal

Types of Projects:
• If it can be a play, make it a play (Sorkin's most comfortable form)
• If it's about a place you're attracted to → TV series
• If the characters metaphorically "die" at the end (no more story after) → feature film
• If you have many short stories about a place → TV series
• If you're attracted to a specific moment in time → feature film

Starting Without a Clear Idea:
• Sometimes you say yes to a "blind date" project
• Believe you can fall in love with it through the process
• Start with enough elements that conflict can be found
• Example: Steve Jobs—knew it was there, just had to find it
• Example: The American President—started with casting idea (Redford as widowed president)
• If you're a new writer, consider adapting a short story where plot is already solved

PHASE 2: RESEARCH

Two Types of Research:

1. Hard Research (Nuts and Bolts):
   • Specific, factual, non-subjective information
   • How many nuts and bolts in the Golden Gate Bridge
   • How does a computer work
   • What does the Secret Service do when they hear gunfire
   • Can be delegated to research assistants
   • Can be done by email or phone after initial meeting

2. Soft Research (Finding the Movie):
   • When you don't know what you're looking for yet
   • Trying to discover points of friction, conflict, story
   • Must be done in person—you need to hear it yourself
   • Talk to as many people as possible who are close to the subject
   • Hope that cool stories emerge in passing
   • Example: West Wing—learned about presidential motorcades leaving aides behind
   • Example: Steve Jobs—meetings with Lisa Jobs to understand their relationship

Research Process:
• Start somewhere—it's a standing jump
• Use prominent people in books or known associates as entry points
• They'll refer you to others who know more specific details
• Expect 90% of research not to make it into the final script—but you need it anyway
• High-profile projects make research easier (people come to you)
• For sensitive subjects, build trust gradually—don't ask tough questions in first meeting
• An hour is usually sufficient for most interviews
• Continue via email/phone after initial meeting

What to Look For:
• Cool patois—language specific to a vocation
• Makes audience feel these characters know what they're doing
• Sometimes you want audience NOT to understand a line—shows expertise
• Anecdotes and stories that reveal character or create obstacles
• Technical accuracy (but know you'll bend truth for dramatic purposes)
• The human element—what makes powerful people relatable

Research Sorkin Finds Meaningless:
• Filling garbage cans with period-appropriate trash the camera won't see
• Actors spending weeks watching surgeons when they won't actually operate
• Anything that doesn't help actors or isn't visible to camera
• Over-emphasis on physical authenticity vs. emotional truth

PHASE 3: INCORPORATING RESEARCH INTO WRITING

When Research Becomes Story:
• Sometimes research gives you the entire episode (presidential motorcade → West Wing episode)
• Sometimes it reveals an inherent problem with dramatic potential (census episode)
• Sometimes it provides authentic language that creates credibility
• President's daily diary revealed human moments—naps between meetings with kings

The Inner Moral Compass for True Stories:
• Take the Hippocratic oath: First, do no harm
• Don't do something that would hurt someone
• Don't change fundamental truth of what happened
• You WILL lie constantly—it's necessary for drama
• People's lives don't play out in scenes that form narratives—you create that
• Judge what the "more important truth" is

Examples of Acceptable vs. Unacceptable Changes:
• The Social Network: Having Zuckerberg drink a screwdriver instead of beer—acceptable
  (More visually interesting, shows drinking to get drunk, doesn't change fundamental truth)
• David Fincher disagreed, wanted accuracy—he won, but both had valid points
• Having him do cocaine—unacceptable (fundamentally different behavior)
• Steve Jobs: Mac refusing to say hello happened weeks before launch, not minutes before
  (Changed timing for dramatic purposes—acceptable)

Technical Accuracy in Dialogue:
• Example from Steve Jobs: "It's an unimplemented trap, but the error code is wrong. It's a system error."
• Sorkin admits he still doesn't know what this means
• What matters: it's correct, it happened, it sounds authentic
• Creates feeling that these engineers know more than we do
• Sometimes exact accuracy matters less than emotional truth

PHASE 4: DEVELOPING CHARACTERS

The Anti-Biography Approach:
• Don't write long character biographies with childhood details irrelevant to your story
• The character wasn't five years old—they were born at the age they are when lights come up
• They only get to be five if they say "When I was five, I saw my father kill himself"
• Don't write down they like creamy peanut butter unless it serves the story
• This is magical thinking that won't help you write
• You're doing it because you feel like you're supposed to, not because it works

Characters Born From Intention and Obstacle:
• Character emerges from what they want and what stands in their way
• The tactics they use to overcome obstacles define who they are
• Example: Mark Zuckerberg in The Social Network
  - Wants to be accepted, wants social life
  - Builds virtual world where he's in control
  - Tactics: intelligence, coding, revenge, obsession
• Example: Toby Ziegler in The West Wing
  - Wants president to fulfill his potential
  - Tactics: bluntness, honesty, dressing down the president directly

Finding Yourself in Characters:
• Must have something in common with every character you write
• Cannot judge them at all—even antagonists
• Write them as if they're making their case to God for entry to heaven
• Example: Colonel Jessep in A Few Good Men
  - The "You can't handle the truth" speech
  - Had to believe his argument about Santiago's death saving lives
  - If you don't believe it while writing, character becomes mustache-twirling villain
  - Many people find his argument compelling—that's the point

Characters Different From Yourself:
• Sorkin is a straight white Jewish male
• When writing characters unlike himself, steers clear of writing what it's "like" to be them
• Contribution to equality: making differences irrelevant to the story
• Fills writers' rooms with diverse people who bring different perspectives
• Uses their input to create authentic arguments between characters
• Lets those arguments have equal weight

The Role of Actors in Character Development:
• Actors bring inner life that writers can't fully prescribe
• Example: Richard Schiff as Toby wearing wedding ring without it being scripted
  - "So you can figure out why I'm wearing it"
  - Became: divorced but still in love, doesn't want to take ring off
• Casting informs character
  - John Spencer's hardscrabble street sense shaped Leo McGarry
  - Contrasted with Bartlet's "to the manner born" quality
  - Their friendship became more interesting because of this contrast

Character Tactics Define Character:
• How each character overcomes obstacles reveals who they are
• Toby: direct, blunt, honest, willing to dress down the president
• Leo: manipulates with humor, runs the place, inspires fear and respect
• Ainsley Hayes: nervous energy that manifests as rapid-fire talking
• C.J. Craig: uses humor and deflection
• Josh Lyman: competitive, often shoots from the hip

Never Use Character Traits as Christmas Ornaments:
• Understudy story: Marine who changed voice and body to "play Marine man"
• He didn't believe he was as strong as the character
• Started adding decorations instead of just saying the words
• Your job: write the character's intentions and obstacles clearly
• Actor's job: bring the humanity

Properties of Characters vs. Properties of People:
• These have very little to do with each other
• Goal isn't to make characters as human as possible
• "Human moments" are inferred by audience, not prescribed by writer
• Photo-realism in art is impressive but not the goal
• Would you rather hear a singer sound like a flute, or just listen to a flute?
• Don't confuse documentary realism with drama

PHASE 5: STRUCTURING THE STORY

Three-Act Structure:
• Act 1: Chase your hero up a tree
• Act 2: Throw rocks at them
• Act 3: Get them down (or not—dying in the tree is okay if they tried)

The Escape Must Be Introduced in Act 1:
• Can't use a gun in Act 3 unless introduced in Act 1
• Conversely, don't introduce gun in Act 1 unless used in Act 3
• Whatever escape mechanism exists must be visible early
• Can't suddenly reveal "magic rope" in Act 3
• This is Chekhov's gun principle

Page Numbers as Road Signs:
• Standard screenplay: 100-125 pages
• Sorkin's scripts: much longer due to dialogue (180-201 pages)
• Dialogue takes more space on page, less time on screen
• Action takes less space on page, more time on screen
• If you're on page 20-25 without introducing protagonist or inciting action—trouble
• First 15 pages are most important for getting movie made
• Last 15 minutes are most important for audience satisfaction
• Must round the bases even if you hit a home run

Story vs. Drama:
• Fact: "The Queen died"
• Story: "The Queen died and then the King died of a broken heart"
• Drama: Add conflict—King must rule alone, people trying to remove him, he was never the smart one, etc.

Exposition:
• Must tell audience what they need to know before inciting action
• Need at least one character who knows as little as audience does
• Avoid starting sentences with "As you know..."
• Example: Steve Jobs film—Chrisann doesn't understand what's special about Mac
  Steve explaining to her = explaining to audience
• Courtroom dramas perfect for exposition—jury knows nothing, like audience
• Don't present exposition as bald information
• Hide it in character conflict or objectives

Inciting Action:
• The moment when the drama actually begins
• Hamlet example: Ghost tells Hamlet who killed whom—NOW the story starts
• But need exposition first (Hamlet is prince, mother is queen, uncle is king, father died)
• Everything before inciting action is setup
• Everything after is consequence

Stakes:
• Want stakes as high as possible so audience sits forward
• Sometimes automatic (A Few Good Men: murder trial)
• Sometimes must be established (Steve Jobs: why perfectionism matters so much)
• Higher stakes = more audience investment

Film Story Arc Variations:
• You'll rarely end up with what you planned at the beginning
• Example: Molly's Game—thought Act 1 was LA, Acts 2-3 were NYC
  Actually: first half LA, second half NYC
• Acts don't have to be equal length
• Story finds its own shape as you write

PHASE 6: WRITING SCENES

Core Scene Principles:
• If you're not moving story forward, you're standing still
• Audience won't hang out while you stand still
• Must travel from Point A to Point B—even if B ends up east instead of north
• At end of scene, must be at least one step further than before
• Every scene needs intention and obstacle, even if small-scale

Launching From Scene to Scene:
• Next scene can answer question asked in previous scene
• Example: "I wonder what the sharks are doing?" → Cut to sharks
• Every scene should end with mini-climax or propulsion into next
• Danny Strong's Recount: every scene launched perfectly into next
  "No way court will rule this way" → Court rules that way
• Connected like Lego blocks

Scene Endings:
• Don't need to end with crash or exclamation point
• But should end with audience eager to continue
• Avoid feeling of spinning wheels or losing traction
• Must be clear what audience should pay attention to

Opening Scenes:
• Some lay out theme without audience realizing
  - Butch Cassidy: bank security scene = end of Wild West theme
  - Three lines of dialogue establish entire movie's meaning
• Some grab you by starting mid-conversation
  - Social Network and Steve Jobs both start this way
  - Forces audience to sit forward and catch up
• Some are traditional (crime committed, then main titles)
• Could you cut the first scene? If yes, you might not need it
  - But don't if it's establishing mood/tone (like overture in musical)

Character Introduction Scenes:
• Show what character wants, don't tell who they are
• Avoid: "Well, I am, as you know, chairman of this company..."
• If character doesn't want anything, probably shouldn't be in script
• Social Network: Mark wants acceptance into final clubs—clear "I want" scene
• American President: Sidney wants environmental legislation, faces obstacle of Chief of Staff
  Sets up conflict while establishing character
  Bonus: President standing behind her as she criticizes him

Comedy in Scenes:
• Try to tell serious stories funny when possible
• Smart, dedicated people slipping on banana peels is fun
• Don't know many rules for comedy, but:
  - Odd numbers funnier than even ("told you 17 times" vs. "16 times")
  - Words with K and M are funny
  - Never use physical appearance as source of comedy
• Best jokes are setups that pay off late
  - West Wing example: Josh's missing chair established early
  - Payoff: Charlie talking about working with smartest people while Josh falls because no chair
• Don't use one character's embarrassment unless earned

Courtroom Drama Preference:
• Sorkin tries to turn everything into courtroom drama
• Intention and obstacle incredibly clear
• Stakes self-evident
• Jury = audience surrogate
• Social Network deposition scenes most exciting to write

Physical Description in Scenes:
• Write minimum description necessary for reader to understand
• Don't write what character feels internally—actor handles that
• Because Sorkin writes little description, when he does, it's taken seriously
• Usually hearing scenes, not visualizing them
• Visualization stays out of focus
• Exception: movie he's directing—visualized everything from ice chips to poker chips

PHASE 7: WRITING DIALOGUE

The Least Teachable Part:
• Most personal aspect of writing
• Everyone crosses finish line differently
• What follows is Sorkin's method—not the only way

Dialogue as Music:
• First experience: "Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf?" at age nine
  Didn't understand content, but dialogue sounded like music
• David Mamet: virtuoso at conversations between people who can't communicate
  Glengarry Glen Ross, Speed the Plow, American Buffalo = sharp violin duets
• When speaking for performance (stage, pulpit, campaign), all rules of music apply
  - Cadence, tone, volume
  - Time signatures and measures
  - Beats per measure
• What words sound like is as important as what they mean

Rhythm and Musicality:
• It's about rhythm first
• Actors know if they've dropped or added a syllable
• Like music—can't have 5 beats in a 4/4 measure
• Whole piece has properties of symphony or opera
  - Solos and duets
  - Allegros and Adagios  
  - Arias (good and bad)

Not About Realism:
• Goal isn't imitating how people actually talk
• People don't rhyme—Shakespeare didn't care
• Most "realistic" dialogue comes from improvisation (Apatow, McKay, Phillips)
• Never actual writing—it's people actually talking
• Sorkin's goal: not imitate real speech or TV speech—sound like how he writes
• Properties of dialogue and properties of actual speech have little to do with each other

Musical Techniques in Dialogue:
• Alliteration: "People who like baseball can't like books"
• Repetition with variation: "People who like X can't like Y"
• Rhythm through repeated structure
• Taking someone's words and "punching them in the face with it"
• Dual/triple dialogue (actors speaking simultaneously)
• Timed within an inch of its life during rehearsal

Idiosyncrasies and Hiccups:
• False starts of sentences
• Stopping and restarting: "that kind of thing"
• Interrupting themselves  
• Every sound actors make gets written down
• Makes dialogue feel alive and spontaneous
• Example: "We were hung up in traffic" vs. "We were, we were hung up in traffic"
  Second "we were" sounds more natural

Network TV Limitations:
• Can't take Lord's name in vain on network TV
• Can say "motherfucker" before you can say "goddamnit"
• Limits emotional authenticity
• Example: Bartlett finding out about ambush
  Wanted "goddamnit"—can only say "damn it"
  No one in real life starts sentence with "damn it"

Physical Writing Process:
• Very physical when writing
• Talks out loud, plays all parts
• Gets up from desk, walks around
• Sometimes walks blocks away from office/house while performing scene
• Once broke nose being too physical
• Eventually sits down to type what's been worked out
• Performs scenes to test if words are speakable
• Reads aloud to catch clunky writing
• Adds idiosyncrasies and hiccups in revision
• Looks for places that go a line or two too far

The Rule of Never Repeat Information:
• Don't tell audience something they already know
• "I love you" the second time is meaningless
• This is a critical rule
• Dropping ball on playground—turns to lead, hard to pick up again

Testing Dialogue Through Performance:
• Read scenes out loud
• Play every part
• Check if words are speakable
• Or does it sound like clunky writing?
• Avoid: "Oh Denise, the sunrise..." (stilted, novelistic)

Musicality Example - West Wing Bartlet/Richie Scene:
• Begins stilted intentionally—formal, uncomfortable
• Music "turns on" when Richie hits Bartlet's button about "ordinary Americans"
• Then employs musical techniques:
  - Alliteration: baseball/books
  - Repetition: "people who like X can't like Y"
  - Percussion: "Crime. Boy, I don't know." (empty, thudding)
  - Richie takes Bartlet's words and punches him with them
• "Is all" = musical beat at end of thought
• "If it appears at times..." / "If it appears from time to time..." = musical mirroring
• Unexpected reversal: Richie climbs on top rhetorically—"game on"

PHASE 8: REWRITING

The Reality Check:
• Screenplay isn't as good as you think when you finish
• Feels great in your head—emotional, sharp
• Probably haven't gotten all of it onto page yet
• Discover this when reading through
• This is normal and expected

Why Rewriting is Easier Than Writing:
• You have a problem to solve
• No longer staring at empty page with flashing cursor
• Can attack specific problems
• Much more satisfying than starting from nothing

Get to the End First:
• Resist urge to rewrite as you go
• Even if on page 25 and haven't introduced protagonist
• Get all the way to page 400 if needed
• Create your "block of marble"
• You'll know more about it at the end than at beginning

The Sculptor Metaphor:
• Famous sculptor asked how he made statue of David
• "I looked at block of marble and took away everything that wasn't David"
• Get to end = create your block of marble
• Then chip away everything that isn't your story
• Example: American President—385 pages down to love story
• Example: Tony Kushner's Lincoln—500 pages down to 13th Amendment story

Kill Your Darlings:
• If thing works without it, it shouldn't be there
• If you can cut it, you should cut it
• Hardest thing: cutting something you love
• Great joke, snappy line, fantastic scene—but unnecessary
• Went off on detour, need to return to freeway
• This is ego-driven difficulty

Exceptions - Earning Digressive Moments:
• Coen Brothers' Fargo: lunch with old classmate
  - Nothing to do with main plot
  - Tonally perfect, eerie, feels right
  - Coens powerful enough not to be overruled
• Paul Thomas Anderson's Magnolia: 3 hours 20 minutes
  - Gary Ross: "needs to cut 30 minutes, but don't know which 30"
  - Fear of accidentally cutting genius
• Can earn moments to breathe if you're good enough
• Not everything has to be plot-plot-plot
• But these moments must be earned

The Tough Test:
• Does it work without this element?
• If yes, cut it
• Brutal but necessary

Rewriting Process with Sorkin:

First Draft Delivery:
• Sends to producer (often Scott Rudin)
• Gets call few hours later: "good job" (which means it needs work)
• "Why don't you fly here tomorrow, we'll start turning pages"
• Pack big suitcase—"couple days" means three weeks

Page-by-Page Sessions:
• Every morning at producer's office
• Line by line through entire script
• Sometimes large note: whole storyline doesn't work
• Sometimes small note: just needs adjustment
• Walking out, wants to kill himself
• Realizes doesn't know how to fix what was asked
• Hates that it wasn't perfect first time

Hotel Work:
• Notes written on paper
• Starts with first note (hopefully easy one)
• Likes crossing off notes as completed
• Sometimes sees solution immediately
• Sometimes takes until next day

Types of Notes Received:

From Scott Rudin:
• Very specific notes
• Not: "needs to be 33.5% funnier"
• Usually: something not quite landing hard enough
• Bringing conflict into relief
• Knows what Sorkin was going for
• Can tell if it's clear or not

From David Fincher:
• Won't candy-coat anything
• Usually: "This described thing—we should see it"
• Response: "God, of course. If we see it, it'll really land."

From Tommy Schlamme:
• Longtime collaborator (Sports Night, West Wing, Studio 60)
• Best director in television
• Understood each other's work
• Respected boundaries of each other's jobs
• Bad writing version: "I've always loved you, John. Don't you know that?"
• Assignment: Make that land, but write it better

General Note Pattern:
• Usually about central conflict
• Bringing something into relief that's not landing hard enough

The "Wet" Note:
• Sorkin lives in area that's almost melodrama
• Writes romantically, idealistically
• Unchecked = melodrama
• "This scene is kind of wet" = went too far
• Too much hugging, "I love you," "we did it"
• Characters performing emotion you want audience to feel
• Maria Callas story: student crying while singing
  - Callas slapped her: "I'm supposed to be crying, not you"
  - Don't recommend slapping, but good lesson

Most Important Notes:
• "I don't buy the obstacle"
• "I don't buy the intention"
• Why does she need to do this?
• Shore up the necessity
• Make obstacle truly formidable

Retyping as Hedge Against Laziness:
• At some point, retype entire script
• Even without notes, without specific goals
• Knows there's "first drafty" stuff still in there
• Won't fix it unless forced to retype
• When reaching bad line, automatically fixes it
• Won't retype bad line
• Does this twice:
  1. With screenplay next to him (fixing weak lines)
  2. From memory (finding new, better ways)

Learning Exercise for New Writers:
• Take really good screenplay (Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid)
• Put it next to you and type it word for word
• Hope William Goldman osmoses into you
• Action of typing great dialogue can be instructive
• Novelists do this with Mark Twain
• Screenwriters do this with masters

Finding Your Script Editors:
• Takes time to collect trusted people
• Need people who:
  - You like talking about work with
  - Speak in vocabulary you understand
  - You trust their taste
  - Understand the way you write
  - Won't try to write your script their way

Sorkin's Key People:
• William Goldman (mentor, taught him everything)
• Tommy Schlamme (director/producer—Sports Night, West Wing, Studio 60)
• Scott Rudin (producer—Social Network, Moneyball)
• Lauren Lohman (assistant—can tell when she genuinely likes something vs. being polite)

What You Don't Want in Script Editor:
• Someone trying to write your script their way
• "Awful lot of talking—cut it and add action"
• Find someone who understands YOUR voice
• Like dating—probably won't marry first person
• But keep searching, and keep them when found

The Doctor Analogy:
• If arm hurts, tell doctor "it hurts when I do that"
• That's the only useful information you have
• Don't diagnose yourself or prescribe treatment
• Doctor is expert after that point
• Same with script notes: audience/reader tells you where it hurts
• You (the writer) diagnose and treat

Example - Two Women at A Few Good Men:
• Out of town tryout at Kennedy Center
• Woman said: "didn't think second act was really structured"
• She doesn't know about structure
• But something stopped her from enjoying second act
• Do you want to address that, or was she an anomaly?
• Take the symptom seriously, not the diagnosis

Be Polite to Everyone:
• Listen to everybody (or pretend to)
• Thank them
• But be careful who you actually listen to
• If lucky, talking to someone smart who understands:
  - Scripts in general
  - Way you write specifically
  - What you're trying to do

First Question After Test Screening:
• Not "was it good?"
• "Was it comprehensible?"
• "Did you follow the story?"
• "Did you understand it?"
• Deliver the package unharmed—that's first goal

Joke Example - A Few Good Men:
• "How do you feel about that theory?"
• Worked on stage, not in movie
• Worked in rehearsals
• In movie: single shot of Kevin Pollak
• Can't tell who he's talking to = confusion
• Even tiniest confusion makes audience apprehensive
• Put confusion in mix = lose engagement

The Balancing Act:
• Audience needs storyteller to have them by the hand
• But antidote for confusion is dangerous too
• Worst crime: telling audience something they already know
• Balance on edge of razor:
  - Not confused
  - Not being told things they know

PHASE 9: UNDERSTANDING YOUR AUDIENCE

The Storyteller's Handshake:
• Audience needs to feel you have them by the hand
• You're leading them confidently through story
• Any wobbling in that confidence = audience anxiety
• They need to trust you know where you're going

Audience Intelligence:
• Treat them like they're smart—because they are
• They don't want to just sit back and observe
• They want to put things together themselves
• Satisfying when they don't see reversal coming
• Satisfying when they had same clues as hero but couldn't solve it

The Pointillist Painting Metaphor:
• George Seurat's "Sunday on the Island of La Grande Jatte"
• Painted with dots—red, blue, red, blue, very close together
• Viewer stands back, mind mixes colors into violet
• More vibrant than mixing on palette
• Painting is different when no one looks vs. when someone looks

📚 KEY CONCEPTS & FRAMEWORKS

INTENTION AND OBSTACLE:
The foundational framework for all drama. Every scene, every story must have:
• Clear intention (what character wants)
• Formidable obstacle (what stands in their way)
• High stakes (why it matters)
• Strong tactics (how they try to overcome obstacle)
Without this, you have journalism, not drama.

THE DRIVESHAFT:
Metaphor for intention and obstacle as the mechanism that makes story move forward. Once this is sturdy, everything else—comedy, themes, social commentary—can hang on it like a clothesline.

PRESSING ON IT:
Technique for testing whether intention is strong enough and obstacle is formidable enough. Ask: Can this be easily overcome? Why not? Plug every escape hatch.

THE TAUGHT CLOTHESLINE:
Once you have solid conflict between protagonist and antagonist, it becomes a taught line on which you can hang everything else you want in the story—humor, romance, ideas, themes.

CHARACTERS BORN FROM CONFLICT:
Characters aren't created through biography or trait lists. They emerge from intention and obstacle, defined by the tactics they use to overcome obstacles.

HARD VS. SOFT RESEARCH:
Hard research = specific facts and technical information. Soft research = exploratory meetings to find the movie/story, where you don't yet know what you're looking for.

THE INNER MORAL COMPASS:
When writing true stories, you'll lie constantly (because drama requires it), but maintain moral compass: don't hurt anyone, don't change fundamental truth. Judge what the "more important truth" is.

COOL PATOIS:
Vocabulary specific to a vocation that makes characters sound expert and authentic. Makes audience feel these people know what they're doing.

DIALOGUE AS MUSIC:
All rules of music apply to performance speech: cadence, tone, volume, rhythm, time signature. What words sound like is as important as what they mean.

REWARDING PATIENCE:
Sometimes structure requires audience to stick with you until payoff at end. Must make journey interesting enough that patience is rewarded.

KILLING YOUR DARLINGS:
If it works without it, cut it. Great writing that's unnecessary is still unnecessary. Hardest part of rewriting.

THE SCULPTOR'S APPROACH:
Get to the end, creating your block of marble. Then take away everything that isn't your story. Don't start over until you have the full rough draft.

WET SCENES:
Scenes that have gone into melodrama, where characters are performing the emotion you want audience to feel. The Maria Callas principle: "I'm supposed to be crying, not you."

THE DIAGNOSTICIAN:
Writer must learn to diagnose why things work or don't work, not just react emotionally. Study failures to understand structure.

RULES MAKE ART BEAUTIFUL:
Like sports, art requires rules to function. Rules don't restrict—they create the framework that allows beauty. All rules come from Aristotle.

THE AUDIENCE AS COMPONENT:
The audience completes the work of art. Like a pointillist painting, their minds mix the colors. They want to participate, not just observe.

✅ STEP-BY-STEP METHODOLOGY

STEP 1: IDENTIFYING A VIABLE IDEA
□ Ensure you can use "but, except, and then" to describe it
□ Identify the intention (what character wants)
□ Identify the obstacle (what stands in their way)
□ Press on both to ensure they're strong and formidable
□ Verify there's genuine conflict, not just an interesting subject
□ Check if competing ideas have equal strength
□ Determine if this is "your pitch"—something you can authentically write

STEP 2: RESEARCH PHASE
□ Identify what type of research needed (hard facts vs. exploratory)
□ For hard research: delegate to assistants, verify facts
□ For soft research: meet people in person
□ Start with prominent figures, get referrals to others
□ Conduct initial hour-long meetings
□ For sensitive topics, build trust gradually over multiple meetings
□ Continue follow-up via email/phone
□ Listen for cool patois, anecdotes, points of friction
□ Accept that 90% won't make it into script—do it anyway
□ Look for the human element in powerful/technical subjects

STEP 3: PLANNING AND PREPARATION (The Long Phase)
□ Live with the idea for months
□ Bang head against wall (this is normal)
□ Let ideas percolate while doing other things
□ Drive and listen to music for inspiration
□ Don't write yet—let it build up
□ Identify intention and obstacle of first scene at minimum
□ Accept that most days you won't write (this is the process)
□ Wait until you feel ready to type first scene
□ Know you're ready when it comes out easily, not like ketchup from bottle

STEP 4: BUILDING THE STRUCTURE
□ Use index cards on cork board to visualize scenes
□ Start with what you know (first few scenes)
□ It's like walking in dark with flashlight—see five scenes ahead
□ Put those up, take it from there
□ Don't worry if you can't see the end yet
□ Track page numbers as road signs
□ Ensure protagonist introduced by page 20-25
□ Ensure inciting action happens relatively early
□ Check that you're moving from Point A to Point B
□ Accept that Point B might end up different than planned

STEP 5: WRITING SCENES
□ Ensure scene has clear intention and obstacle (even if small)
□ Verify scene moves story forward (traveling, not standing still)
□ End scene with launch into next scene
□ Check that scene ends with you wanting to continue
□ Test if scene works without certain elements (if yes, cut them)
□ For character introduction: show what they want
□ For exposition: use character who knows as little as audience
□ Avoid "as you know..." constructions
□ Hide exposition in conflict or character objectives

STEP 6: WRITING DIALOGUE
□ Perform the scene physically—play all parts
□ Speak lines out loud to test if they're speakable
□ Listen for rhythm and musicality
□ Check for clunky writing or stilted language
□ Add idiosyncrasies: false starts, hiccups, interruptions
□ Look for opportunities for overlapping dialogue
□ Cut lines that tell audience what they already know
□ Use alliteration, repetition, and variation for music
□ Remove lines that go one or two lines too far
□ Ensure words sound good, not just mean the right thing

STEP 7: COMPLETING FIRST DRAFT
□ Get all the way to the end before rewriting
□ Don't stop at page 25 because protagonist not introduced
□ Create your full "block of marble"
□ Accept it will be clunky, unwieldy, too long
□ Trust that you'll know more about it at the end
□ This might be 385 pages—that's okay
□ Once finished, identify what the story really lives in
□ Recognize what can be cut away

STEP 8: REWRITING PROCESS
□ Read through entire draft
□ Accept it's not as good as in your head (this is normal)
□ Identify what didn't make it from head to page
□ Make list of problems to solve
□ Share with trusted script editor(s)
□ Receive notes—try not to panic
□ Start with easiest note first
□ Cross off notes as completed
□ Address "wet" scenes (too melodramatic)
□ Strengthen intention and obstacle where needed
□ Kill darlings (cut beloved but unnecessary material)
□ Fix scenes where conflict isn't clear
□ Retype entire script as hedge against laziness
□ Retype again from memory to find new solutions

STEP 9: TESTING AND REFINING
□ Read aloud to check comprehensibility
□ First question: Do people understand it?
□ Not: Do they like it? But: Do they follow it?
□ Identify where confusion exists
□ Plug holes where audience could solve problems too easily
□ Ensure you're not telling them what they already know
□ Balance between not confusing and not over-explaining
□ Check that first 15 pages are strong (for getting movie made)
□ Check that last 15 minutes are strong (for audience satisfaction)
□ Verify you've introduced everything in Act 1 that's used in Act 3
□ Verify you've used everything in Act 3 that was introduced in Act 1

STEP 10: FINAL POLISH
□ Retype sections that still feel first-drafty
□ Strengthen weak jokes or cut them
□ Tighten any scenes where you're standing still
□ Verify every scene ends with forward motion
□ Check that character tactics are clear and consistent
□ Ensure dialogue has musicality
□ Remove redundant information
□ Cross off final items on note list
□ Accept you'll wish you could rewrite it again (always true)
□ Deliver the script

💡 PRACTICAL EXERCISES & ACTION ITEMS

FOR DEVELOPING IDEAS:
□ Take subject you're interested in and press on it: Where's the conflict?
□ Practice using "but, except, and then" to turn subjects into stories
□ List 5 interesting subjects; identify which have inherent conflict
□ For cross-country trip idea: What must happen in LA on specific day? What stops them?
□ Take favorite movie: identify intention and obstacle in every scene

FOR RESEARCH:
□ If writing about vocation, interview someone in that field for one hour
□ Ask: "Tell me something I don't know about [subject]"
□ Listen for cool patois—write down vocabulary specific to profession
□ Record anecdotes that reveal character or obstacles
□ Identify what research is "hard" (factual) vs. "soft" (exploratory)
□ Accept you'll only use 10% of what you learn

FOR STRUCTURE:
□ Buy index cards and cork board
□ Card out first five scenes you know
□ For each card, write: intention, obstacle, outcome
□ Check each scene moves story forward
□ Verify scenes launch into each other
□ Track page numbers—protagonist by 20-25, inciting action by 30

FOR CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT:
□ Don't write character biography
□ Instead write: What does character want? What stands in way? What tactics will they use?
□ List three tactics specific to this character's personality
□ Write scene where character wants something badly—focus on tactics
□ Find something in common between you and character (even antagonist)

FOR WRITING DIALOGUE:
□ Perform scene physically—play all parts out loud
□ Record yourself to hear rhythm
□ Check if words are speakable
□ Read published screenplay while watching movie—see how dialogue looks on page
□ Take favorite dialogue scene: identify musical elements (alliteration, repetition, rhythm)
□ Practice adding idiosyncrasies (false starts, hiccups)
□ Remove any line where character tells audience what they already know

FOR SCENES:
□ Write scene with clear intention and obstacle
□ End scene with question that launches into next scene
□ Test: Does scene work without certain elements? If yes, cut them
□ Write character introduction showing what they want (not who they are)
□ Take exposition-heavy scene: hide information in conflict

FOR REWRITING:
□ Read draft all the way through without stopping to fix
□ Make list of all problems (large and small)
□ Start with easiest problem first
□ Cross off each problem as solved
□ Identify any "wet" scenes—cut emotional performance
□ Find trusted friend to read and give notes
□ Ask only: "Did you understand it? Could you follow the story?"
□ Retype entire script once (with original next to you)
□ Retype sections from memory to find new solutions

FOR BECOMING A DIAGNOSTICIAN:
□ Watch movie that doesn't work—diagnose why (don't just mock it)
□ Watch movie that breaks rules but works—figure out how
□ Read screenplay for favorite movie—see how it looks on page
□ Take apart favorite screenplay like car engine—lay pieces on floor
□ Read Aristotle's Poetics (64 pages)
□ Watch five episodes of TV show—identify intention and obstacle in each scene

FOR DEALING WITH WRITER'S BLOCK:
□ Drive and listen to music (especially music from high school)
□ Find long straight road so you don't have to think about turning
□ Let scene play out in head while driving
□ Eventually return to desk and type it
□ Accept that most days you won't write (this is normal)
□ On days you do write well, feel like you can fly (enjoy this feeling)
□ Crossing items off list helps emotionally—do it
□ Accept you're in constant state of writer's block—breakthrough is exception

LESSON 21: WRITING CAPTIVATING DIALOGUE
• Least teachable part
• Most personal part

• Glengarry Glen Ross like sharp violin duets

• Speaking for performance: all rules of music apply
• What words sound like = what words mean
• Rhythm most important
• Actors know if dropped syllable

• "That's not how people really talk" — who cares?

🎓 KEY TAKEAWAYS

ON CRAFT:
1. Intention and obstacle is everything—without it, you have journalism, not drama
2. Rules don't restrict art; they make it beautiful—learn Aristotle's Poetics
3. Dialogue is music—rhythm and sound matter as much as meaning
4. Characters emerge from conflict, not from biography or trait lists
5. The audience is intelligent—make them a collaborator in the storytelling

ON PROCESS:
6. Most of writing time is spent not writing—this is normal and necessary
7. Get to the end of your draft before rewriting—create your block of marble first
8. Research extensively, knowing 90% won't make the final script
9. Find trusted script editors who understand your voice—it takes time to collect them
10. Rewrite by solving specific problems, not by hoping for inspiration

ON CHARACTER:
11. Never judge your characters—even antagonists are making their case to God
12. Find what you have in common with every character you write
13. Show what characters want, don't tell who they are
14. The tactics characters use to overcome obstacles define who they are
15. Actors will bring inner life—your job is clear intention and obstacle

ON STORYTELLING:
16. The first 15 pages sell the movie; the last 15 minutes satisfy the audience
17. Every scene must move the story forward—if standing still, you're in trouble
18. Set up in Act 1 everything you'll use in Act 3 (and vice versa)
19. Treat yourself to moments that breathe, but they must be earned
20. When writing true stories, don't harm anyone and don't change fundamental truth

ON OVERCOMING CHALLENGES:
21. Writer's block is the default state—breakthrough is the exception
22. Drive and listen to music when stuck—let ideas percolate unconsciously
23. You'll always wish you could rewrite again—accept this and move on
24. Be a diagnostician—figure out why things work or don't work in others' scripts
25. Write for yourself and people you know, not for what you think "they" want

THE SORKIN PHILOSOPHY IN ONE SENTENCE:
Write intelligent characters in clear conflict, treat the audience as smart collaborators, respect the rules of drama, and never stop learning from the work of others and your own mistakes.
