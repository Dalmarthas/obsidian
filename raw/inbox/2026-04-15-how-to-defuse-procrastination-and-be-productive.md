## 📝 Executive Summary

The video explains procrastination through the lens of **working** memory and shows why relying on willpower and “just get started” often backfires. It proposes a practical approach drawn from motivational interviewing and addiction psychiatry: stop forcing the ideal behavior, create intermediate options, and consistently do “the next best thing” while occasionally resetting working memory by physically changing environments.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

- Procrastination is not a simple “study vs. play games” conflict; your working memory locks onto a very narrow 3‑on‑3 battle between a few dreaded tasks and a few favorite distractions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- Working memory can only hold a limited number of cravings and resistances, which means most reasonable middle-ground options never even appear in your mind.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- The common advice to “just get started” often fails because tiny actions that obviously do not solve the problem feel pointless and create internal resistance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 💡 Core Concepts

## 1. Working memory and procrastination

The speaker frames procrastination as a function of working memory—the system that temporarily holds what you are actively thinking about, craving, or resisting. In practice, when you “should” study but “want” to play games, your working memory only juggles a few tasks you’re avoiding (study, take out trash, update resume) and a few distractions (games, scrolling, porn), rather than the full range of available actions. Because craving requires an object (“I crave X”) and resistance attaches to specific objects, your emotional struggle is limited to whatever items happen to be in working memory at that moment.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

This narrow focus means your brain doesn’t even consider many reasonable alternatives like doing 10 push‑ups, going for a walk, or handling a tiny but meaningful piece of the work. The model comes from addiction psychiatry, where leveraging working memory helps patients resist highly compelling urges like drugs or alcohol by shifting what is represented in the mind.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 2. Spectrum of behavior instead of two poles

The video criticizes the binary frame “to drink or not drink” (or “study vs play games”) and replaces it with a spectrum of possible behaviors between the two extremes. At one end is the “ideal” behavior (e.g., fully abstain from alcohol, perfectly prepare for the exam), which tends to invite maximum resistance and the highest risk of failure. At the other end is the easiest, lowest‑cost behavior (keep drinking, keep playing), which requires zero willpower.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

Motivational interviewing suggests you should not start by pushing people (or yourself) to the ideal end of the spectrum. Instead, you locate a realistic middle point: for example, “not drink during the week” or “study one worksheet tonight,” which still have meaningful impact but generate far less resistance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 3. Why “just get started” often fails

The popular idea “just get started” is reframed as psychologically flawed in many cases. If you tell yourself “Just read one line” or “Just put one cup in the trash,” your brain quickly evaluates whether that action will solve the larger problem (passing the final, cleaning the room). If your working memory computes that this tiny action has almost zero impact on the outcome, it feels like “the biggest waste of time,” and resistance increases.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

Additionally, the real effort is often not in the micro‑action itself but in the activation cost (e.g., taking out the textbook). Reading one line versus five lines involves almost identical setup, but one has much more perceived benefit, which increases motivation via a better cost–benefit ratio in dopaminergic systems like the nucleus accumbens. Therefore, the problem is not “starting too big,” but often starting too small, in ways that your brain correctly judges as meaningless.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 4. “Do the next best thing” and “What CAN you do?”

From motivational interviewing, the core principle is: “Do the next best thing.” Instead of insisting on the full ideal (“study all evening, be perfectly sober forever”), you ask: “If I can’t do the ideal, what is the next best thing I actually can do?” You might move one or two steps down from the ideal, but not all the way down to the smallest, token effort.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

The crucial mental move is shifting from “What should I do?” (which triggers shame, perfectionism, and resistance) to “What can I do?” (which targets realistic capability and leverages your autonomy). This question populates your working memory with mid‑range tasks—like “avoid drinking at the barbecue,” “clear five cups from the desk,” or “finish this worksheet before gaming”—that are both doable and impactful.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 5. The doorway effect and resetting working memory

The “doorway effect” is a psychological phenomenon where walking through a doorway causes you to forget what you were going to do because your working memory resets when you change rooms. The video suggests intentionally using this effect to break out of entrenched internal conflicts around procrastination or addiction.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

Taking a short walk through several doorways (out of your room, out of the house, around the block) can clear the cluttered, conflicted contents of working memory. When you return, the internal battle “study vs games vs scrolling” often feels less intense, and you can more easily generate and choose from new, middle‑ground options.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 🔨 Actionable Tips

- When stuck procrastinating, explicitly ask: “What CAN I do right now that is the next best thing?” and write down 2–3 concrete, mid‑sized actions (e.g., “review one section,” “reply to two important emails,” “clear five items from the desk”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- Avoid token “micro‑actions” that clearly will not move the needle (like reading a single line) and instead choose small but meaningful units of work (a page, a problem set, a 15‑minute block) so your brain perceives real progress.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- If you feel locked in a mental tug‑of‑war (e.g., hovering over the “Play” button), stand up and walk through multiple doorways or around the block to forcibly reset your working memory before deciding again.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- For habit change (like reducing drinking, screen time, or gaming), set intermediate behavioral rules such as “no drinking during weekdays” or “no games until I complete one defined task,” not all‑or‑nothing commitments.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- Before starting work, specify the task in concrete terms in your working memory (“study chapter 3 for 30 minutes,” “finish this worksheet”), rather than holding a vague, overwhelming concept like “I have to study.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)

## 🔍 Additional Insights

- The same working‑memory‑based method used to help people reduce or stop methamphetamine, cocaine, alcohol, or marijuana use can scale down to everyday procrastination with tasks like studying or chores.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- Procrastination is framed as “insane” in the sense that we often operate not on what we are actually capable of, but on wishes about what we “should” do, ignoring realistic capacity and intermediate options.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- There is a negotiation analogy: just as you can move a child from “broccoli vs gummy bears” to “apple with peanut butter,” you can negotiate with yourself to accept an option between “ideal discipline” and “pure indulgence.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- A subtle but important point is that once your brain locks onto one desired outcome and one avoided task, pushing directly against that conflict mostly burns willpower; designing new options outside that internal battlefield is usually more efficient.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
    
- Ambiguity warning: The video implies that “just getting started” is one of the worst strategies, but this is context‑dependent; the critique applies when the “start” is trivial and obviously inadequate, not when initial steps are meaningfully scoped.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VBifDZwPiI4&list=WL&index=8)
