📝 Executive Summary  
The video explains how to use body, hands, eye contact, and movement to project confidence and credibility in public speaking, including seated and online contexts. It offers specific physical guidelines to reduce visible nervousness and strengthen connection with the audience.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Nonverbal delivery often influences audience impressions more than verbal content, especially judgments of credibility and confidence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- A balanced, tall stance with feet parallel under the shoulders prevents nervous swaying and signals stability.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Purposeful, open hand gestures above the waist and beyond the shoulders reinforce messages and convey confidence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Eye contact should be distributed across the audience in a nonpatterned way to create genuine connections without darting or staring.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- The same body language principles apply when standing, sitting in meetings, and presenting online, with small adjustments for context.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Importance of nonverbal behavior  
    The audience evaluates both what you say and how you say it, and delivery can matter more than content in forming impressions. Viewers make rapid judgments about whether a speaker is credible and confident based largely on nonverbal cues.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
2. Fundamental body stance  
    The **body** should appear balanced and tall, taking up space rather than shrinking. The recommended stance: head straight, shoulders and hips square, feet facing forward under the shoulders as if on train tracks, which minimizes swaying and rocking (self-soothing behaviors that signal discomfort).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
3. Hand and gesture mechanics  
    Hands are tools to demonstrate and emphasize ideas through gestures directed toward the audience, similar to offering a handshake. Effective gestures: arms out and extended, above the waist, breaking the line of the shoulders; ineffective behavior includes tensing hands, pinning elbows, and keeping gestures close, which produces a “T‑rex dinosaur” appearance. Resting positions include arms hanging gently at the sides (tailor’s posture) or hands lightly clasped in front of the belly button, returning to these between gestures.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
4. Eye contact for connection  
    Eye contact is culturally expected (in North American norms) and is key to audience connection. The speaker should spread eye contact across individuals, holding brief connections then moving on in a nonpatterned way to avoid darting eyes or over-focusing on one person.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
5. Movement strategy  
    Movement should be intentional, not constant pacing. Three recommended moments for movement: stepping toward the audience at the start to welcome them, stepping toward question askers when taking questions, and moving during transitions between points while always landing back in the balanced stance. Confident speakers move toward the audience; nervous speakers tend to retreat.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
6. Seated and online adaptations  
    When seated (meetings or sit-down presentations), the speaker should sit balanced with feet flat or crossed at the ankles, lean slightly forward to show engagement, keep hands on the table (fingers clasped or one hand on the other), stay open, and remove distracting objects from in front of them. For online/video presentations, head balance and stillness are critical, shoulders should remain square, and the speaker should look directly at the camera rather than the screen to avoid appearing as if looking down.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

- Stand tall and balanced: head straight, shoulders and hips square, feet parallel under shoulders like train tracks; avoid side-angled feet that invite swaying.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Use open gestures: extend arms toward the audience, keep gestures above the waist and beyond the shoulders, then return hands to relaxed sides or lightly clasped at the belly button.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Relax the upper body: consciously release tension in hands and shoulders to prevent hunched posture and constricted, short-range gestures.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Manage eye contact: briefly lock eyes with one person, finish a phrase or idea, then shift to another person in a nonrepetitive pattern.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Move with purpose: step toward the audience at the start and when answering questions; during transitions between points, move to a new spot and re-establish the balanced stance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- In meetings: lean slightly forward, keep feet grounded, place hands calmly on the table, and clear the area of objects you might fidget with.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Online: keep your head level and relatively still, shoulders square, and look directly into the camera lens rather than the image on screen.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- Swaying and rocking are framed as self-soothing behaviors analogous to a child sucking their thumb, which may comfort the speaker but undermines audience perception of confidence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- The “T‑rex dinosaur” metaphor captures how tight elbows and constrained gestures visually signal nervousness and should be avoided.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- The tailor’s fitting posture (arms relaxed at the sides) is highlighted as visually strong despite feeling exposed, pointing to a frequent mismatch between internal comfort and external appearance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Leaning back when seated can be misinterpreted as disengagement or nervousness, even if the speaker feels more relaxed in that position.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
    
- Ambiguity note: Cultural references to eye contact norms are explicitly tied to North American expectations and may not generalize globally without adaptation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=m7SSj5Z5kTo&list=WL&index=12)
