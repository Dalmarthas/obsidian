- Title of Video: “Focusing is about saying no” – Steve Jobs (WWDC ’97)[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
Steve Jobs argues that true **focus** in a company means deliberately killing many parallel, even interesting, initiatives so that limited talent and resources align behind a few coherent directions. He emphasizes that this requires strong leadership willing to endure internal frustration, negative press, and personal unpopularity in order to produce products where “the total is much greater than the sum of the parts.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Focus is primarily about saying “no” to many projects, not about saying “yes” to everything that seems interesting or promising.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Good engineers can still produce poor overall results if management allows work to scatter across too many unrelated directions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Leadership must make hard cancellation decisions (“put a bullet in the head”) on projects that don’t fit the company’s coherent strategy, even if they are locally sensible.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Saying “no” inevitably angers people and attracts negative media, and leaders must accept these “lumps” without publicly blaming individuals.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
- The payoff of disciplined focus is a small set of great products where the integrated whole exceeds the sum of individually good parts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Focus as systematic refusal  
    Jobs reframes focus as a disciplined process of refusal rather than enthusiastic agreement. When organizations equate focus with saying “yes” to many good ideas, they fragment effort and lose strategic clarity. Real focus is the repeated act of saying “no” to initiatives that are interesting in isolation but misaligned at the system level.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
2. The “farm of animals” and sub-additive total  
    He describes Apple’s situation as a “farm” with many different “animals” (projects) all moving in different directions. Each effort was “arguably interesting,” but collectively they produced an outcome where the total was less than the sum of the parts. This captures the idea that uncoordinated innovation, however talented, can reduce overall organizational effectiveness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
3. Good engineers, bad management  
    Jobs explicitly separates engineering quality from management quality: Apple had good engineers but “lousy engineering management.” The problem wasn’t talent but lack of a strong, unifying product and platform strategy to channel that talent. This highlights that leadership’s central role is prioritization, integration, and coherent direction-setting, not just acquiring smart people.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
4. Micro vs. macro sense-making  
    He draws a distinction between “microcosmic” and “macrocosmic” logic. A project like OpenDoc could make sense within its own narrow context, for the people working on it, and even technically. But at the macro level—where you consider industry trends, ecosystem adoption, and overall company direction—it did not make sense, especially versus emerging Java-based container strategies.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
5. The cost of saying no: people and reputation  
    Jobs openly acknowledges that cancelling projects hurts those who invested time and identity in them. Saying no “pisses off people,” some of whom then criticize leadership in the press, generating “shitty articles” that the company must absorb “like an adult.” Because leaders generally cannot or should not publicly reveal personnel details, they must tolerate one-sided narratives without retaliating, which is psychologically and politically difficult.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
6. Strategic decision on OpenDoc  
    He gives OpenDoc as a concrete example: he supported “putting a bullet in the head” of the project. His reasons were that (a) he did not consider it great technology, (b) it did not align with where “the rest of the world” was going, and (c) even its own team was trying to restart it in Java, implying rework and misalignment. This illustrates how he evaluates projects against external ecosystem realities and architectural coherence, not just internal enthusiasm.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    
7. Outcome of focus: integrated, superior products  
    Jobs predicts that the result of this painful pruning will be “really great products where the total is much greater than the sum of the parts.” The emphasis is on integration: fewer, more cohesive products instead of a sprawling portfolio of disconnected technologies. This anticipates Apple’s later strategy of tight hardware–software integration and a small number of flagship product lines.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

- Enforce strategic coherence
    
    - Regularly audit all active projects against a small set of fundamental directions or pillars; kill those that do not clearly reinforce them, even if they are individually promising.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Distinguish micro from macro logic
    
    - Before greenlighting or continuing a project, ask: “Does this make sense in the broader industry and ecosystem, or only within our local team’s perspective?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Protect the organization’s maturity in public
    
    - When cancellations lead to negative press or external criticism, avoid public personal attacks or detailed HR explanations; accept the reputational “lumps” and maintain professional silence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Normalize “no” in culture
    
    - Train managers and teams that most good ideas will be rejected for the sake of focus, and that this is a feature of strategy, not a dismissal of their competence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Evaluate projects against external reality
    
    - Use market adoption, platform trends (e.g., de facto standards like Java in the clip), and ecosystem compatibility as filters, not just internal technical elegance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        

🔍 Additional Insights

- Emotional acknowledgement
    
    - Jobs explicitly says “I apologize” and “I feel your pain” to those whose work was cancelled, which recognizes emotional cost without retreating from the decision. This models a leadership stance that combines empathy with firmness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Media asymmetry
    
    - He notes that employees who are angry can freely tell their side to journalists, while leadership cannot fully respond due to confidentiality and professionalism constraints. This creates an inherent narrative asymmetry that leaders must anticipate and absorb.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Leadership maturity framing
    
    - Jobs frames Apple’s behavior as “taking [the lumps] like an adult,” signaling that enduring unfair criticism without lashing out is part of organizational maturity and long-term credibility.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
- Ambiguity / unclear content (flagged)
    
    - The clip references “the last six months” and specific press outlets (e.g., San Jose Mercury) without detailed context on each cancelled project or individual, leaving some causal links implicit.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
        
    - His offhand remark that he “didn’t think [OpenDoc] is great technology” is not technically substantiated in the clip; the underlying technical evaluation criteria remain unclear.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H8eP99neOVs&list=WL&index=7)
