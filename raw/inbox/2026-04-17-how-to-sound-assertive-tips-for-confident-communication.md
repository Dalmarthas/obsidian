## 📝 Executive Summary

The video explains three practical strategies to sound more assertive: stop over-apologizing, stop downplaying your words, and treat “no” as a complete sentence. By shifting from automatic apologies to gratitude, removing self-undermining phrases, and saying “no” without overexplaining, you project confidence while still respecting others.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)

---

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

- Over-apologizing makes you sound like an inconvenience and erodes your perceived worth; replace unnecessary “sorry” with expressions of gratitude such as “thank you for your patience.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Downplaying your words (“this might be a dumb question,” “I could be wrong but…”) signals insecurity and makes you sound less confident than you actually are.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “No” is a complete sentence; adding lengthy justifications weakens your assertiveness and suggests you need permission to hold boundaries.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Assertive language creates a positive feedback loop: sounding assertive increases confidence, and greater confidence makes it easier to speak assertively.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Mental reframes (“I’m not sorry, I’m Daniel,” “I am my own attorney”) help you detach from habitual apologizing and advocate clearly for your own needs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)

## 💡 Core Concepts

1. **Assertiveness as honest, direct respect**  
    Assertiveness is framed as being honest and direct about your needs while respecting the other person, rather than being aggressive or passive. Lacking an assertive voice leads to being “walked over,” diminished confidence, and a poorer trajectory in life, especially in contexts where you are new and feel compelled to defer to everyone.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
2. **The problem of over-apologizing**  
    Saying “I’m sorry” or “so sorry” in almost every interaction (e.g., walking in a minute late, starting an email reply, or getting someone’s attention) becomes a verbal tic rather than a meaningful apology. This pattern effectively turns into apologizing for your existence, time, and priorities, sending the message that you are an inconvenience.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
3. **Gratitude instead of apology**  
    A central reframe is to replace unnecessary apologies with gratitude statements like “thank you for your patience” or “I appreciate your time.” In minor situations (e.g., 30 seconds or 1 minute late), gratitude is both sufficient and more assertive, whereas a genuine apology is still appropriate when you are clearly wasting someone’s time (e.g., 5 minutes late).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
4. **Downplaying your words and self-sabotage**  
    Phrases such as “I hate to bother you but…,” “this might be a dumb question but…,” or “you probably know better than me but…” down-grade your contribution before you even make it. These fillers create needless side-conversations (e.g., “oh, you’re not bothering me”) and highlight insecurity that may not actually exist, undermining your credibility.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
5. **Replacing hedging with additive language**  
    Instead of hedging, you’re encouraged to add to the conversation with phrases like “can I build on that?,” “can I take that further?,” or “there’s something I’d like to add to that.” This shift positions you as someone who is contributing value rather than seeking permission to speak.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
6. **“No” as a complete sentence**  
    Overexplaining after “no” (for example, listing all your commitments, excuses, or justifications) dilutes your assertiveness and makes it sound like you need to defend your decision. The more words you add after “no,” the more it implies you owe an explanation, while a simple “no” communicates clear boundaries and self-respect.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
7. **Identity and mindset reframes**  
    The creator proposes identity-based tricks, such as mentally saying “I’m not sorry, I’m Daniel” before an unnecessary apology, to remind yourself you are not defined by your apologies but by your needs and presence in the conversation. Another metaphor is to imagine yourself as an attorney representing “you” as the client, then converting “my client needs…” into “I need…,” which makes your language far more assertive.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
8. **Reputational and career impact**  
    How you sound shapes others’ perceptions of who gets chosen for opportunities, promotions, and responsibilities at work. Being known as someone who “says what you mean and means what you say” with an assertive tone builds credibility and changes your professional trajectory.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    

Ambiguity/unclear: The video does not give precise “rules” for when apology vs gratitude is mandatory outside the lateness example, so applying this distinction in more sensitive contexts (e.g., serious mistakes) requires personal judgment and situational nuance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)

## 🔨 Actionable Tips

- Replace “sorry” with gratitude in low-stakes situations
    
    - Instead of “sorry I’m late,” say “thank you for your patience” when you are just slightly late (e.g., ~1 minute).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Instead of “sorry for the late reply,” write “thank you for your patience” or “I appreciate your understanding” in emails.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - When you need someone’s attention, switch “sorry, do you have a second?” to “when you have a moment, I’d appreciate some of your time.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Erase or rewire hedging phrases
    
    - Catch and remove openers like “I hate to bother you,” “this might be a dumb question,” or “you probably know better than me.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Replace them with additive language: “can I build on that?,” “can I take that further?,” or “there’s something I’d like to add.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Treat “no” as a full answer
    
    - Practice saying “no” without immediately following it with “because…” unless there is a strong ethical or relational reason to elaborate.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Notice when you start stacking excuses (dog, schedule, obligations) and deliberately stop at “no” or offer a brief, neutral explanation if absolutely necessary.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Use identity-based cues to break habits
    
    - When you feel an unnecessary “sorry” coming, tell yourself internally “I’m not sorry, I’m [your name],” as a reminder your role is to express your needs, not pre-emptively apologize for them.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - In situations where you would say “I could be wrong but…,” reframe as your own advocate: “I need to understand what’s going on here” or “I need to feel understood before we end this conversation.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Build the assertiveness–confidence loop
    
    - Use one assertive phrase per day (e.g., “thank you for your patience,” “I’d like to add something,” “no”) to create small wins and reinforce a more confident self-image.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Reflect after conversations on when you sounded most assertive and how that affected the interaction, to strengthen the feedback loop.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)

## 🔍 Additional Insights

- Assertiveness often breaks down most when you are new in an environment (new school, new firm), where the urge to be deferential at the expense of your boundaries is strongest.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Cultural norms can amplify over-apologizing (Canada is mentioned humorously), so some of these patterns are socialized rather than purely individual flaws, which may require extra self-awareness to counteract.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The “attorney for yourself” visualization is deliberately theatrical: imagining a courtroom and a “client” version of you helps externalize your needs so you feel more justified advocating for them, then internalize that advocacy by switching from “my client needs” to “I need.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The host emphasizes that these changes are not about becoming harsh or inconsiderate but about aligning your language with your actual value and priorities so people take your time and presence seriously.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    

Potentially debatable points:

- The advice to avoid apologizing for being slightly late may conflict with norms in very punctual or hierarchical cultures where even small delays warrant explicit apology.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Relying on a bare “no” without any context might be perceived as abrupt or rude in some relationships, so balancing brevity with relational sensitivity is important.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HuF6cMePYTA&list=WL&index=21)
