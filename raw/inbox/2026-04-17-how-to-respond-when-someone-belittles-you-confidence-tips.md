📝 Executive Summary  
The video presents a three-step framework to respond to belittling comments in a way that protects your integrity, avoids escalation, and subtly shifts power back to you. The speaker emphasizes staying calm, making the other person confront their own behavior, and using silence strategically when necessary, illustrated with courtroom and workplace examples.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- There are three core strategies: make them repeat the belittling comment, ask questions of intent, and, if needed, respond with calm silence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Asking someone to repeat a hurtful remark often disrupts their “dopamine hit,” exposes their behavior, and makes them less likely to double down.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Questions of intent (“Did you say that to embarrass me?”) flip the spotlight from your reaction to their motives, frequently prompting backtracking or apology.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- If a person openly admits harmful intent, that signals they are unsafe to be around, and silence plus distance is often the healthiest response.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Laughing off or minimizing belittling comments reinforces the behavior; calm, direct responses communicate clear boundaries and self-respect.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Psychological goal of belittling  
    Belittling comments are used to trigger an emotional reaction and give the belittler a sense of control or a “dopamine spike.” They often aim to damage credibility, embarrass, or provoke, especially in public or high-stakes settings (e.g., depositions, team meetings). Understanding that provocation is the goal helps you focus on not “taking the bait.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
2. Strategy 1 – Make them say it again  
    The first move is to calmly ask the person to repeat what they said: “I need you to say that again” or “Can you repeat that for me?” This is counterintuitive, but it denies them the expected emotional payoff and forces them to hear their own words in a more exposed, self-conscious way. Often they will refuse, soften, or rephrase, which already reduces the aggression and reveals that they know the comment was inappropriate.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
3. Strategy 2 – Questions of intent  
    Once the comment is clear, you ask a direct question about intent: “Did you intend to embarrass me?” “Did you say that to make me feel small?” “Did you say that to dismiss my ideas?” This shifts the spotlight from your reaction to their behavior, functioning like a mirror that forces them to examine why they said what they said. In many cases, people backtrack (“Oh no, I didn’t mean it that way”), apologize, or move on, and they learn you’re not an easy target.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
4. Strategy 3 – The power of silence  
    If the person confirms harmful intent (“Yeah, I did mean to embarrass you”) or doubles down, silence becomes the response. Calm, steady silence communicates that you do not feel compelled to engage with disrespect and that their words “have nothing to stick to,” making their insults ring hollow. This silence is framed not as passivity, but as a powerful assertion of standards: you do not respond below a threshold of respect.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
5. Integrity and emotional maturity  
    The underlying theme is maintaining **integrity**—protecting your values and self-respect rather than “winning” the exchange. The emotionally mature person is the one who does not feel obligated to respond to every insult, especially in front of others who are observing your composure and boundaries. Over time, these responses establish you as someone who can “stand your ground” without being drawn into ugliness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
6. Case examples (courtroom and workplace)
    

- Courtroom/deposition example: A client trained to respond to a snide comment by asking, “Did you say that to embarrass me?” led the opposing attorney to immediately backtrack and deny any harmful intent, effectively neutralizing the attack.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Team meeting example: When someone says “Nobody asked you” after your suggestion, the recommended sequence is: ask them to repeat it, then ask an intent question like “Did you say that to make me feel small or to dismiss my ideas?” and, if they double down, let silence do the rest.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

1. Gendered/bias-laden put-downs  
    In a listener question, a woman describes a male coworker making “Not bad for an old lady”–type remarks that linger and hurt later at home. The advice is not to laugh it off but to stay calm and either ask him to repeat the line (possibly “slower,” forcing him to hear how harsh it sounds) or ask, “Did you say that to embarrass me?” or “Did you say that because you thought it was funny?” These responses expose the behavior, remove the “joke” shield, and signal that she will not play along with belittlement.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Use a scripted three-step sequence
    

- Step 1: “Can you repeat that?” / “Say that again for me.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Step 2: “Did you intend to embarrass me?” / “Did you say that to make me feel less?” / “Did you say that to dismiss my ideas?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Step 3: If they double down or admit harmful intent, say nothing, maintain eye contact if appropriate, and let the silence stand.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

1. Workplace meeting example (ready-made script)
    

- When someone says “Nobody asked you” after your input:
    
    - You: “Can you repeat that for me?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
        
    - If they repeat: “Did you say that to make me feel small or to dismiss my ideas?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
        
    - If they apologize, you can choose whether to accept; if they double down, remain silent and composed.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
        

1. Handling “jokes” disguised as insults
    

- Do not laugh, minimize, or wave it off; that implicitly endorses the behavior.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Respond calmly with: “Did you say that to embarrass me?” or “Did you say that because you thought it was funny?” and then let the question hang without nervous filler.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

1. Boundaries with persistently harmful people  
    If someone openly confirms they intended to hurt or embarrass you, treat that as data: they are not safe or respectful. Where possible, reduce contact, avoid relying on them for emotional safety, and keep your responses minimal, calm, and professional.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
2. Internal mindset shift  
    Remind yourself in the moment: “They want a reaction; my calm is my control.” Focus on preserving your integrity and emotional maturity rather than trying to come up with a clever comeback.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- The strategies are adapted from high-conflict legal contexts (cross-examination, depositions) where witnesses are deliberately provoked, then generalized to everyday life.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Asking someone to repeat themselves or clarify intent does not require aggression; it can be delivered in a neutral, even slightly curious tone, which increases your credibility.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Silence is framed as an active communication tool, not a lack of response; it signals that you hold a clear respect threshold and will not participate below it.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- The speaker stresses that consistently laughing off or minimizing hurtful comments trains others to continue, whereas calm confrontation and questions of intent often stop the behavior long term.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
    
- Ambiguity: in some hierarchical or culturally sensitive contexts, directly asking about intent could have social or career risks; the video does not deeply address nuance around power imbalances or unsafe environments, so additional judgment may be needed in those cases.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l686kTH2Rlg&list=WL&index=22)
