**Executive Summary**

This interview with Bong Joon-Ho explores the creative process behind _Parasite_, focusing on how real-life experiences, dynamic character interactions, and layered storytelling contribute to the film’s unique narrative. Bong discusses why nuance trumps stereotypical portrayals of good and evil, and how deeply local themes can paradoxically heighten universal resonance. The discussion provides practical writing insights for constructing stories that are rich in both psychological and social complexity.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **Character Development:** Bong Joon-Ho prioritizes developing characters through their actions and interactions, rather than isolated backstories.
- **Nuanced Morality:** The film avoids clear-cut villains and heroes, instead presenting characters in morally ambiguous, relatable circumstances.
- **Family as a Core Unit:** Bong’s stories often begin with incomplete or troubled families, driving them into extreme and revealing situations.
- **Physical Setting as Narrative Device:** The architecture of the house in _Parasite_ is intentionally tied to narrative escalation, making spatial understanding integral to the plot.
- **Universality Through Specificity:** Deeply Korean details amplify the film’s universal appeal, illustrating how local context can create global resonance.

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

- **Characterization Through Interaction:**
    
    Bong starts his writing by plotting actions and situations, letting characters emerge from their responses to one another. He views actions as core to character, dismissing rigid consistency in favor of authenticity within group interactions. True character is revealed in how they treat others—demonstrated in _Parasite_ when each family member's distinct reaction to peripheral characters elucidates their personalities.
    
- **Rejecting Black-and-White Morality:**
    
    _Parasite_ intentionally avoids painting any group as wholly good or bad. Even the “con artist” family elicits empathy, as their justifications and motivations are understandable. Bong emphasizes reality’s complexities, reflecting how most people exist in ambiguous moral “grey zones.” Rich characters are not portrayed as inherently evil; instead, the film explores their complicity and obliviousness in social structures.
    
- **Family and Extreme Situations:**
    
    Many of Bong’s films start with fractured or incomplete families thrust into crisis (_The Host_, _Memories of Murder_). In _Parasite_, the unity and eventual fragmentation of the family underline emotional consequences, with their final separation acting as emotional punishment.
    
- **Space as a Narrative Catalyst:**
    
    60% of _Parasite_ unfolds in the wealthy family’s house. The film’s first hour methodically educates viewers on the home’s architecture, which becomes pivotal for the dramatic unraveling in the second half. The house is both a literal and metaphorical structure, embodying class divisions and hosting the escalating conflict.
    
- **Layers of Parasitism:**
    
    All three families depicted—rich, poor, and those in hiding—exhibit parasitic behaviors. The wealthy “leech” off the labor of the working class, while the poor manipulate access and resources. Yet, the real tragedy is the conflict among the disadvantaged themselves, set against the indifference of the elite.
    

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- **Build Characters Through Action:**
    
    Start with scenarios and see how characters react, rather than meticulously designing their traits first. Let their behaviors define who they are.
    
- **Show, Don’t Tell, with Relationships:**
    
    Reveal personalities through interpersonal exchanges, not exposition or narration.
    
- **Exploit Setting for Story and Tension:**
    
    Design physical spaces that inform and constrain your plot; walk the audience through the geography early to build suspense later.
    
- **Draw Universality from Local Detail:**
    
    Don’t shy away from culturally specific details; these can create authenticity and deepen global appeal.
    
- **Avoid Simplistic Morality:**
    
    Strive for multidimensional characters whose actions may be questionable, but who remain comprehensible and evocative to the viewer.
    

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- **Origin of Story:**
    
    Bong’s initial inspiration came from personal experience as a tutor for a rich family, directly paralleling _Parasite_’s premise.
    
- **Writing Process:**
    
    He wrote a 15-page treatment before the story’s more iconic elements (the bunker, secret basement) emerged, which materialized late in the writing via bursts of inspiration.
    
- **Empathy Through Participation:**
    
    Bong describes “rolling in the mud” with his characters—fully inhabiting their perspective, rather than manipulating them detachedly.
    
- **Use of Symbols:**
    
    Native American imagery in _Parasite_ is intended to reveal the superficiality of the wealthy family’s tastes and their ignorance toward the deeper cultural significance, demonstrating how symbols can critique class and cultural appropriation.
    
- **Message of Tragedy:**
    
    Although there are no overt villains, the narrative ends in tragedy, prompting reflection on systemic social forces and the unintended consequences of morally ambiguous actions.
