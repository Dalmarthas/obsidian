📝 Executive Summary  
Jeremy Utley argues that most professionals dramatically underuse AI’s creative potential because they treat it as a passive tool rather than an active **teammate** that can question, coach, and collaborate with them. By shifting mindset, learning to have AI interview you about your work, and cultivating disciplined “inspiration” inputs, non‑technical people can 10x their creative output and build surprisingly powerful workflows. The definition of creativity—going beyond the first “good enough” idea—does not change in the age of AI, but AI makes it easier than ever to stop too early if you do not set higher objectives and demand volume and variation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Treat AI as a teammate, not a tool: collaborate, give feedback, and ask it to ask you questions to unlock far better outcomes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Use AI to “use AI”: ask it how to ask better questions and where it can best augment your specific workflows, KPIs, and responsibilities.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Focus AI on the parts of work you dread; small, non‑technical experiments can create huge productivity gains across entire organizations.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)

💡 Core Concepts

1. AI as a contextual creative assistant  
    Utley opens with the Churchill-in-the-bathtub metaphor to illustrate that we now have access to an assistant that knows our context, voice, and intent at a level once reserved for heads of state. Even an “ordinary” person can dictate complex work—like speeches or documents—to AI from anywhere, because the model can hold, recall, and adapt to rich personal context. This reframes AI from a generic answering machine to a deeply contextual partner embedded in your thinking and writing process.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
2. Don’t ask AI; let it ask you  
    A key shift is moving from, “I type a question; AI gives an answer” to “AI interviews me so it can help design the best questions and solutions.” Utley highlights that traditional tools (Excel, PowerPoint, email) cannot teach you how to use themselves, but AI uniquely can, if you explicitly ask it to. By having AI ask one‑by‑one questions about your workflows, responsibilities, and objectives, it can propose both obvious and non‑obvious ways to integrate itself into your work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
3. From tool to teammate  
    Utley’s research shows a productivity and creativity “realization gap”: although AI can make people faster and improve quality, fewer than 10% of professionals capture meaningful gains. Underperformers treat AI as a one‑shot tool; outperformers treat it like a teammate that they coach, critique, and co‑create with. When AI produces mediocre work, a “tool” mindset leads to abandonment, whereas a “teammate” mindset leads to feedback loops—asking what it needs to know, iterating, and exploring alternative approaches.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
4. Non‑technical creativity with AI  
    Utley emphasizes that non‑technical professionals can build high‑impact solutions with only natural language. The Glen Canyon ranger example shows a backcountry ranger, frustrated by multi‑day paperwork for simple tasks, who built a natural-language workflow in 45 minutes that automates statements of work and saves thousands of human days across the National Park Service. This illustrates that AI-driven creativity is not limited to coders; it is available to anyone willing to experiment on “dreaded” parts of their job.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
5. Inspiration as a discipline  
    Drawing on his teaching at Stanford and a collaboration with hip hop artist Lecrae, Utley argues that inspiration is not a random spark but a disciplined practice of cultivating inputs. The most creative people he knows deliberately expose themselves to diverse sources, experiences, and perspectives, knowing these inputs shape the quality of their outputs. In the AI context, everyone has access to the same model, so the differentiator becomes the user’s technique plus their unique backlog of experiences and inspirations that they bring into the prompts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
6. Creativity as “more than the first thing you think of”  
    Utley cites a seventh grader’s definition: “Creativity is doing more than the first thing you think of,” linking it to cognitive biases like functional fixedness, the Einstellung effect, and Herbert Simon’s concept of satisficing. Humans tend to latch onto early solutions and stop once something seems “good enough,” and AI accelerates this tendency by making “good enough” trivially easy to obtain. Therefore, in the AI era, creative practitioners must explicitly prompt for volume and variation, and then invest time in sorting and processing multiple options.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
7. The unchanged definition of creativity in the AI age  
    Utley argues that AI does not change what creativity is, but it changes how easily we can either reach or avoid creative states. If your objective is merely adequacy, AI will happily deliver average solutions instantly; if your objective is world‑class work, you must continuously push for more, better, and different ideas. The human’s stated or unstated goals—and their willingness to iterate with AI—determine whether AI is a creativity amplifier or a creativity suppressant.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
8. Identity shift: “I don’t use AI; I work with it”  
    Utley closes with a strong identity-level reframe: creative people should not “use” AI like a tool but “work with” it like a collaborator. He insists that creators should not fear AI; they should lean in, because properly framed collaboration will unleash human creativity at an unprecedented scale. This line flags a subtle but important philosophical stance: human agency remains central; AI is a force multiplier rather than a replacement.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Run the “AI expert consultation” prompt
    

- Ask your preferred LLM: “You’re an AI expert. I’d love your help to figure out where I can best leverage AI in my work and life. Please ask me questions, one at a time, until you understand my workflows, responsibilities, KPIs, and objectives well enough to make two obvious and two non‑obvious recommendations for how I could leverage AI in my work.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Answer honestly and in detail, and then implement at least one obvious and one non‑obvious suggestion the model produces.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    

1. Treat AI like a coached teammate
    

- When AI’s output is mediocre, do not discard it; instead, give it specific feedback (what is missing, what is wrong, what constraints changed) and ask it to try again.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Regularly ask, “What do you need to know from me to do better?” or “What 10 questions should I be asking about this problem?” to surface blind spots in your own thinking.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    

1. Target “dreaded tasks” first
    

- Make a short list of recurring tasks you dislike or that feel bureaucratic (reports, documentation, email drafts, summaries, routine analyses).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- For each, prototype an AI-assisted workflow where you describe the task in natural language and iteratively refine the system prompt until it reliably produces usable drafts or templates.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    

1. Build an inspiration routine around AI
    

- Use AI to generate reading lists, case examples, or perspective shifts related to your field, then feed your reflections back into the model when you work on creative tasks.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Before serious creative work (strategy, design, writing), prompt the model to show you 10–20 unconventional angles, metaphors, or analogies, then selectively incorporate or adapt them instead of starting from a blank page.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    

1. Force yourself past “good enough”
    

- When you get a satisfactory idea or draft from AI, require at least 5–10 more variations before you decide.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Ask the model explicitly for “wild,” “counterintuitive,” or “edge-case” alternatives and then evaluate them using your own judgment rather than defaulting to the first coherent answer.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    

1. Use AI for interpersonal “drills”
    

- For difficult conversations, have AI interview you about the other person, create a psychological profile, role‑play the conversation from their perspective, and then critique your approach.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Treat this as a rehearsal environment to experiment with phrasing and strategies in low‑risk conditions before the real interaction.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)

🔍 Additional Insights

- Human agency and framing are central: Utley repeatedly emphasizes that AI’s impact depends less on technical prowess and more on how humans choose to relate to it—as a tool, a partner, or a mirror of their own thinking habits.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- There is a cultural and linguistic gap: many organizations want “AI transformation” but lack even a basic shared vocabulary around how to work with AI day to day, which forces experts like Utley to start with fundamentals rather than advanced strategy.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Students and non‑experts are often leading in inventive use cases: Utley notes that his students routinely discover applications he never imagined, implying that openness and experimentation may matter more than seniority or domain authority for AI‑driven innovation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- The talk subtly critiques passive creativity myths: by redefining inspiration and creativity as disciplines and behaviors, it pushes against the romantic idea of the lone genius, positioning AI as a scaffolding that makes disciplined creativity more accessible.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
    
- The overall stance is optimistic but not naïve: while Utley does not dwell on risks or ethics in this short talk, his focus on human objectives, agency, and mindset implicitly acknowledges that AI can either dampen or amplify creativity depending on how it is integrated.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wv779vmyPVY&list=WL&index=25)
