- Title of Video: 3 Techniques for Managing Speaking Anxiety[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
The video presents three research-backed techniques to manage, rather than eliminate, public speaking anxiety: greeting anxiety mindfully, reframing speaking as a conversation, and shifting attention to the present moment. These methods aim to make both speaker and audience more **comfortable**, enabling clearer communication even in high‑stakes situations.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Public speaking anxiety is extremely common and should be managed, not “overcome,” because a certain level of arousal is functional.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Mindfully acknowledging anxiety (“this is me feeling nervous”) prevents a negative spiral of worry before speaking.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Reframing speaking from “performance” to “conversation” reduces perceived pressure and supports more natural delivery.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Using questions and inclusive, conversational language makes the interaction feel two‑way and lowers anxiety.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Bringing attention into the present moment (through physical activity, music, counting, or tongue twisters) decreases focus on feared future outcomes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Nature and function of speaking anxiety
    

- Around 85% of people report being nervous about public speaking, and it ranks among the top fears in surveys of Americans.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The speaker argues anxiety is useful: it provides energy, focus, and signals importance, so the goal is to **manage** it, not eradicate it.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- A communicator’s core responsibility is to help the audience feel comfortable enough to receive the message, even if the content is challenging.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

1. Technique 1 – Mindful greeting of anxiety
    

- Many speakers experience early physical symptoms (stomach discomfort, shaking legs, perspiration) and then catastrophize (“they’ll see I’m nervous; this will go badly”), which amplifies anxiety.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Research on mindful attention suggests that simply noticing and naming the state (“this is me feeling anxious; I’m about to do something important”) interrupts the escalation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- This does not necessarily reduce baseline arousal but prevents the cognitive spiral that often makes anxiety unmanageable.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

1. Technique 2 – Reframing from performance to conversation
    

- In performance contexts (acting, singing, dancing), people perceive strict “right” and “wrong” ways to execute, making errors feel catastrophic.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Presentations do not have a single “right” way; they exist on a spectrum from better to worse, so treating them as performances adds unnecessary pressure.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The speaker recommends viewing presentations as conversations, even with large audiences, and operationalizing this through specific behaviors.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11) a) Use of questions
    
    - Questions are inherently dialogic and help create a sense of two‑way interaction.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    - The speaker models this by asking the audience to count letters or respond to prompts, creating participation and reducing his own sense of performing “at” them.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    - He also structures his own notes as a sequence of questions he will answer, which anchors him in a conversational mindset.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    
    b) Conversational language and pronouns
    
    - Nervous speakers often distance themselves physically (backing away from the audience) and linguistically (formal, impersonal phrasing).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    - Examples of distancing language: “one must consider the ramifications,” “today we will cover step one, step two, step three.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    - More conversational alternatives include inclusive pronouns and direct address: “this is important to you,” “we all need to be concerned,” “first, what we need to do is…”.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        
    - This linguistic shift supports a more **relational** stance and helps reduce anxiety by making the situation feel like mutual engagement rather than judgment.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
        

1. Technique 3 – Present‑orientation and time perspective
    

- Drawing on work with Philip Zimbardo on time orientation, the speaker explains that worrying about future consequences (grades, funding, approval, laughs) heightens anxiety.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- By bringing attention into the present moment, speakers can lessen concern about hypothetical negative outcomes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Various methods to induce present focus are given: intense but brief physical activity (e.g., push‑ups), walking before speaking, listening to a focused music playlist, mentally challenging counting tasks (e.g., backwards from 100 by 17), and tongue twisters.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Tongue twisters have the added benefit of warming up the voice while forcing concentration on articulation, as illustrated by the “I slit a sheet…” exercise used with the audience.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Before speaking:
    

- When you notice physical symptoms of anxiety, pause, breathe, and explicitly label the experience (“this is me feeling anxious because this matters”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Prepare your outline as a list of questions you will answer rather than bullet points of topics; this primes a conversational mindset.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Do a brief present‑focusing activity: walk around the building, perform a short physical routine within your limits, or listen to a specific song that centers you.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

1. While designing and delivering the talk:
    

- Intentionally build in questions—rhetorical, show‑of‑hands polls, or quick checks—to create a sense of dialogue.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Use inclusive pronouns (“we,” “you”) and concrete language instead of abstract, formal phrases that distance you from the audience.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Position yourself in a way that feels physically engaged rather than withdrawn from the audience to reinforce psychological connection.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

1. Immediately before going on stage:
    

- Use a cognitive focusing task such as counting backwards by an awkward interval or repeating a tongue twister to force attention into the present.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Combine tongue twisters with vocal warm‑ups so your voice and attention are both **prepared**.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- The speaker emphasizes that audiences often feel uncomfortable watching a visibly nervous presenter and either over‑encourage (nodding) or disengage, which paradoxically can increase the speaker’s distress.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Making the audience comfortable is framed as an ethical and practical obligation of the communicator, independent of whether the message is pleasant.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The techniques are explicitly grounded in academic research (mindfulness, time perspective theory, and empirical work on reframing), enhancing perceived credibility.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- A potentially debatable point is the strong claim that presentations are never “performances”; some speaking contexts (e.g., keynotes, TED‑style talks) blur this boundary and may still benefit from performance techniques, though his argument is that over‑identifying with performance increases anxiety.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
    
- No major ambiguous content appears, but the video only highlights three tools while briefly mentioning that many other anxiety‑management strategies exist without detailing them, leaving scope for further exploration.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5naThX63pF0&list=WL&index=11)
