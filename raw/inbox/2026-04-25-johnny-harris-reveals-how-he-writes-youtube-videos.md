- Title of Video: Johnny Harris Reveals How He Writes YouTube Videos[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)

📝 Executive Summary  
Johnny Harris describes a four‑month, highly structured yet emotionally driven process for making YouTube documentaries that are both visually beautiful and widely watchable, centered on rigorous research, visual-first scripting, and obsessive empathy for the viewer. He explains how constraints, marketing promises (title/thumbnail), classic “who did what to whom” prose, and bespoke art direction work together to turn almost any topic into an addictive visual story while preserving depth and factual rigor. Underneath the craft, he frames the channel’s mission as modeling curiosity and breathing life into seemingly mundane subjects through enthusiasm, human detail, and poetic reflection.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)

---

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Making a single “premium” video is a four‑month pipeline: idea → research dossier → visual-first script → shame draft → multiple cuts → final polish, all under tight time constraints that sharpen the work but must be managed for sustainability.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Writing is explicitly **video‑first**: every line of prose is tied to specific visuals, animations, and sound, using active, concrete language and a “classic style” focused on agents doing things rather than abstract concepts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Marketing is decided early: titles and thumbnails embody a clear promise (“why/ how X”) that must be reinforced and ultimately fulfilled, while also acting as an entry point to a much broader web of ideas.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The team builds a bespoke art direction and mood board for each video—maps, type, set design, animation style—treating every piece like its own mini film rather than conforming to a rigid channel brand.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Craftsmanship and authentic enthusiasm take precedence over pure analytics; Johnny optimizes selectively but deliberately resists over‑gamifying retention and output to preserve the “human soul” of the work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)

💡 Core Concepts

1. End‑to‑end production workflow
    

- Each video typically takes about four months, starting from a pool of pitches the team has been collecting.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Once greenlit in Slack, Johnny writes a “reporting brief” full of questions; a researcher then spends 3–4 weeks creating a 60–80 page “info doc” that surfaces facts, visuals, and context.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Johnny uses that dossier to wrestle with the material, outline, and then enters “scripting week”: 3–4 days of intense, isolated writing where he simultaneously directs visuals and locks the story.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- After the script, post kicks off: assets are collected, facts are checked, Johnny films, and the post team builds a “shame cut” (radio edit + crude visuals), then rough cut, fine cut(s), audio lock, and picture lock, with dense notes at each stage.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

2. Constraints and scheduling
    

- Historically they tried four videos a month, working in three‑day sprints, which rapidly improved skills but made the process unsustainably exhausting.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Now they aim for about 30 videos a year, using 4‑day scripting blocks and experimenting with cycles like three weeks of writing then a week of catch‑up, preserving room to extend a project if the story isn’t “cracked” yet.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Johnny’s life is built around sacred 9am–2pm writing blocks, three to four days a week, with zero meetings or Slack; he treats them like game day, heavily ritualized with coffee, music, and a high‑focus, almost manic flow state.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

3. Field reporting and note‑taking
    

- In the field, he records long spoken notes into his phone and relies heavily on transcription; this helps him process chaotic human interviews where each conversation can radically shift his understanding.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Over his career, he swung from “show up and let the story come to you” to heavily pre‑engineered shoots with pre‑planned shots and soundbites, then back toward a looser approach where field time is for experiencing and reacting, and structured explanation happens later in VO.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He distinguishes between knowledge gathering (reading, experts) and storytelling, which is the curation and linear arrangement of that chaos into an emotionally coherent narrative.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

4. Marketing, promise, and retention
    

- Even when he’s open‑ended about the story, he decides on the “packaging” early: a title and thumbnail that answer “why should someone click?” often framed as “how” or “why” questions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The thumbnail/title represent a simple, straight promise (“why Saudi Arabia is building a 1 trillion dollar city in the desert”) that he must eventually answer, but he uses that entry point to smuggle in more complex topics (resource curse, petro‑states, urban planning, etc.).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He believes you can defer the actual answer until the end as long as you quickly reinforce the promise within the first minute and continually foreshadow that “there’s a lot more going on here.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

5. Visual‑first scriptwriting and “coding”
    

- Johnny writes in a two‑column script: prose on the left, visuals on the right, with each sentence or two mapped to specific shots, animations, and sound.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He “codes” the script using macros and color‑coding to indicate asset collection, animation nesting, and references for future animators; every line of narration has a paired visual action or motion.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- History videos benefit from built‑in chronology, which acts as a structural backbone so he can focus on what to curate and how to explain it in a fresh way.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He favors openings that drop the viewer straight into action and emotion (“it started with the murder of their prophet in Illinois”) rather than expository “I want to tell you about…” setups.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

6. Writing style: classic, active, concrete
    

- Influenced by Steven Pinker’s “classic style,” he focuses on clear agents and actions: “they fled” instead of “the migration of 6,000 Mormons,” “we send Coca‑Cola to Thailand” instead of the abstraction “globalization.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He constructs stories through “icons” on maps (e.g., Mormons vs. federal government) and “loads” them with motives and fears, so viewers track their intentions and spatial relationships over time.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- A rigorous fact‑checking layer sits atop this: every assertion is highlighted, checked, annotated with citations (often multiple sources), and then published as a source document so viewers can scrutinize the evidence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

7. Dual voices: explainer vs poetic
    

- Most of a video runs in an active “explainer” voice advancing the narrative, but near the end he often pivots into a more contemplative, emotional register with softer, less precise language that invites reflection.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- In these concluding sections he juxtaposes praise and critique in the same sentence (e.g., beauty of a valley alongside “sugarcoated” history and exploitation), resisting one‑sided condemnation and foregrounding complexity.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He deliberately uses vivid, surprising phrases (“undetonated bombs and abandoned bones”) and concrete human details inspired in part by outlets like The Economist, which weave rigorous analysis with small, evocative scenes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

8. Viewer empathy and tone
    

- Johnny constantly asks how different segments of the audience (e.g., believing Mormons) will react to each word, balancing the need to hook the majority with respect for sensitive subgroups.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- His on‑camera persona is peer‑to‑peer rather than distant expert: “let me show you this map” positions him as a curious friend inviting the viewer to learn alongside him.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He believes many journalists write to impress peers rather than audiences, overestimating context and underestimating intelligence; instead he tries to supply context while talking to viewers as smart equals in plain language.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

9. Art direction and team structure
    

- The company is primarily a production team of editors, animators, visual producers, researchers, and business staff, many of them self‑taught and “scrappy” rather than from traditional TV.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Every video gets its own “art ingredients” doc and mood board: references for archival treatment, set design vibes, animation styles (e.g., “Chernobyl fuzz” inspired by HBO’s Chernobyl titles), map aesthetics, and symbol systems.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The channel avoids a single rigid brand package; instead, each story drives its own bespoke visual language, within a broad culture of high standards but non‑precious experimentation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

10. Passion vs scalability and AI
    

- When they scaled to a video a week with 17 projects in flight, the machine worked but Johnny’s passion faded; they intentionally accepted him as a bottleneck because his obsession is what gives videos their “magic.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The company’s long‑term strategy is to launch more creator‑led channels where individual journalists have distinctive voices, rather than optimizing a faceless content factory.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He argues that in an AI world, human enthusiasm and storytelling remain a key differentiator, analogous to people still wanting to watch humans play chess even after computers became stronger.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Design a repeatable research pipeline
    

- For each big piece, write a clear reporting brief of what you want to know and the questions you must answer before you touch the script.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Build a habit of turning raw research into an “info doc” (even if you’re solo): longform notes with key facts, quotes, visuals, and competing perspectives you can later curate into a narrative.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

2. Write for the screen, not the page
    

- Use a two‑column script: left for words, right for explicit visual, motion, and sound directions; force yourself to pair every line of narration with a specific visual change.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- As you draft, prefer active sentences with clear agents (“X did Y to Z”) and concrete images over conceptual nouns and abstractions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

3. Lock sacred deep‑work blocks
    

- Schedule recurring 3–4 hour writing blocks at the same time of day, protected from all communication; treat them like non‑negotiable production time, not “if I have time” windows.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Build a pre‑block ritual (music, coffee, workspace setup) that trains your brain to enter a focused, game‑day state quickly.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

4. Decide the promise early
    

- Before diving into structure, draft 5–7 possible titles starting with “How…” or “Why…” to clarify what question the story promises to answer.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Revisit that promise at outline and draft stages to ensure the story actually satisfies it by the end, while still making room for richer side‑topics.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

5. Balance rigor with accessibility
    

- Mark every factual assertion in your script and attach at least one source, even if it’s just for your internal confidence; if possible, keep a source doc that viewers or clients can inspect.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- When tempted to use jargon, ask: “How would I explain this to a smart friend with zero context?” Then write that version, using specific scenes or details whenever possible.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

6. Build bespoke “art ingredients” per project
    

- Create a one‑pager of reference images and written vibes for each project: color palette, typography, map style, set design adjectives, animation references.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Share that document with collaborators before production so everyone has the same vocabulary and mental images for the look and feel.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

7. Use dual voices in your scripts
    

- Structure scripts so that most of the runtime is in a crisp, active explainer voice, but reserve space near the end for a slower, more reflective passage that acknowledges ambiguity and emotion.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- In those contemplative sections, deliberately juxtapose admiration and criticism in the same paragraphs to signal nuance and avoid didacticism.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)

🔍 Additional Insights

- Johnny’s own learning struggles and dyslexia shaped his style; he writes the way he wishes things were explained to him—verbally, visually, and without pretension, often dictating notes instead of typing.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He consciously avoids purely “anti‑” postures: even when critiquing institutions that harmed him, he embeds authentic praise to capture the true mixed experience and keep viewers with differing identities engaged.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Surprise is treated as an act of language, not just plot: he actively searches for non‑default phrasing—climbing out of the “canal” of cliché—to make familiar subject matter feel newly uncanny or beautiful.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- He sees simplification not as betrayal but as access: there will always be deeper, more complex versions of any story, but without simplification and storytelling, most people would never engage with them at all.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- Looking forward, he views deeper integration of on‑screen characters and other people’s lived experiences as the next frontier in his craft, complementing his already strong explanatory and poetic voices.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    

Ambiguities / potential issues:

- Some time estimates (e.g., “four‑month process,” “30 videos a year”) are described informally, so actual durations may vary between projects.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
    
- The balance between pre‑planned marketing (titles/thumbnails) and editorial independence is framed aspirationally and could be more complex in practice.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zq4b96m1AvM&list=WL&index=11)
