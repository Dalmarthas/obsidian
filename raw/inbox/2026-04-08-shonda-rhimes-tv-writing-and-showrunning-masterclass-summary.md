SHONDA RHIMES MASTERCLASS: TV WRITING & SHOWRUNNING
COMPREHENSIVE MASTER SUMMARY

================================================================================
📋 OVERVIEW
================================================================================

This masterclass offers an insider's look into television writing and showrunning from Shonda Rhimes, creator of Grey's Anatomy, Scandal, and How to Get Away with Murder. The course covers the complete journey from finding initial ideas through production, breaking into the industry, and managing a writers' room. Shonda shares her personal writing process, emphasizing that television is a writer's medium where what's in the writer's head becomes what's on screen—a fundamental difference from film, where directors hold creative control.

The masterclass reveals both the creative and practical aspects of TV writing. Shonda demonstrates how to develop concepts into premises, research effectively, create memorable characters, and structure compelling scripts. She also addresses the business realities of television, including working with networks, managing production constraints, and navigating the fast-paced demands of episodic television where scripts must be delivered every eight days. The course emphasizes authenticity, originality, and character-driven storytelling while acknowledging that television is both an art form and a business.

Throughout the lessons, Shonda stresses the importance of discipline, preparation, and understanding both the creative rules and when to break them. She shares candid insights about her own challenges, mistakes, and evolution as a writer, making this not just instructional content but a mentor-like guide for aspiring television writers.

🎯 CORE THEMES
================================================================================

THE WRITER'S MEDIUM
• Television is fundamentally different from film: in TV, the writer's vision becomes what's on screen
• Writers have creative control—they hire and fire directors, not the other way around
• Television offers intimacy with audiences who spend more time with characters than their own family members
• Characters can go on endless journeys across multiple seasons, unlike film's contained three-act structure
• The medium allows for deeper character development and evolution over time

CHARACTER-DRIVEN STORYTELLING
• Everything must come from character—plot without character motivation is meaningless
• Characters should drive the story, not serve as devices to deliver plot points
• Audiences will follow any story if it feels emotionally honest to the characters
• Character motivation is essential—without it, there is no story
• Think of characters as actual people with needs, not as plot devices

AUTHENTICITY & ORIGINALITY
• Write in your own voice, not in imitation of others
• Create original work that hasn't been seen before
• Avoid clichés—if you've heard dialogue before, it's already a cliché
• Listen to real conversations and observe authentic human behavior
• Trust your unique perspective and experiences

DISCIPLINE & PROCESS
• Writing is like running—muscle memory develops through daily practice
• The more you write, the faster you access creative flow
• Develop rituals that signal your brain to enter writing mode
• Deadlines are essential for productivity
• Consistency matters more than perfect conditions

📊 KEY CONCEPTS & FRAMEWORKS
================================================================================

IDEAS VS. PREMISES
• Idea: "A show about gangsters"
• Premise: "A show about modern-day New Jersey gangsters where the lead goes to therapy weekly" (The Sopranos)
• The more specific the idea becomes, the more it transforms into a premise
• Apply journalism's 5 W's: Who, What, Why, When, Where, and How
• You should be able to explain your premise in two sentences clearly

IDENTIFYING TV IDEAS VS. FILM IDEAS
• Film ideas have endings—they're close-ended stories
• TV ideas generate endless stories with no clear endpoint
• Test: Can you imagine 400 episodes? That's a TV series
• TV ideas should make you see infinite storytelling possibilities
• When an idea won't leave your head and characters start forming around it, it's real

THE "WHITE HAT" PRINCIPLE
• Protagonists should be fundamentally good people or have redeeming qualities
• Even flawed characters need to be someone audiences want to spend time with
• Olivia Pope wears a "white hat"—she's fundamentally trying to do good
• Characters can be complex and make mistakes but need core likability
• This principle keeps audiences invested over many seasons

TEXT VS. SUBTEXT
• Text: What characters actually say
• Subtext: What they really mean
• Human beings rarely say exactly what they mean
• Never write subtext as dialogue—it becomes painful exposition
• Allow actors to convey emotional subtext through performance
• Avoid "therapy-speak" where characters explain their emotions explicitly

PLANTING & PAYOFF
• If you plant something in your story, it must pay off eventually
• "If there's a gun on the wall, it must go off"
• This can happen in the same episode or seasons later
• Every element should exist for a purpose
• "Kill the puppy"—if you introduce a puppy, that puppy must serve the story
• Planting and payoff create satisfying story arcs and give structure

THE PROMISE OF YOUR SHOW
• Every pilot makes a promise to the audience about what kind of show it will be
• Episode 2 is essentially Episode 1 repeated in a new way
• Don't veer off course or suddenly change what the show is about
• Fulfill the promise every week, or consciously evolve it over time
• Breaking the promise disappoints and confuses your audience
• Check every episode: Are we delivering what we promised?

THE "NEW PERSON" DEVICE
• Include a character who's new to the world (Quinn in Scandal, interns in Grey's)
• This person represents the audience—they don't know what's happening
• Allows natural exposition through explaining things to the new person
• Makes the audience feel smart and included on the journey
• Avoids awkward "as you know" dialogue


================================================================================
✍️ THE COMPLETE WRITING PROCESS
================================================================================

PHASE 1: FINDING & DEVELOPING IDEAS

Finding Ideas:
• Ideas come from everywhere: conversations, observations, news, obituaries
• Keep a journal or use phone notes to capture ideas, dialogue fragments, thoughts
• Wait for ideas that won't go away—they become "songs stuck in your head"
• When characters start wrapping themselves around an idea, it's becoming real
• Pitch ideas to friends/family—their questions reveal if the idea has merit

Developing from Idea to Premise:
• Start broad, then get increasingly specific
• Apply the 5 W's: Who, What, Why, When, Where, How
• Define central conflicts and character needs
• Ensure you can state the premise in 2-3 sentences
• Test if you can see endless story possibilities

Market Considerations:
• Understand what the marketplace wants but don't be limited by it
• Consider the cultural/political moment—optimistic times support cynical shows (Scandal), pessimistic times support optimistic shows (West Wing)
• Television is both art and business—be aware of both sides
• Use market demands as creative challenges: "Can I write the best version of what they want?"
• Know what's safe so you can consciously choose not to be

PHASE 2: RESEARCH

Why Research Matters:
• Adds authenticity and credibility to your work
• Provides unexpected story ideas and details
• Makes fictional worlds feel lived-in and real
• Gives you confidence in your material

Research Methods:
• Primary sources: Interview people who do the job you're writing about
• Site visits: Go to locations and observe environments
• Read extensively: Books, articles, case studies
• Watch documentaries and relevant media
• Study procedural details of professions you're depicting
• Continue research throughout the writing process, not just at the beginning

What to Research:
• Professional procedures and terminology
• Physical environments and settings
• Emotional truths of experiences
• Historical or technical accuracy
• Cultural specifics and nuances

PHASE 3: CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

Creating Characters:
• Start with what makes them unique and specific
• Define their needs (what they must have) vs. wants (what they think they need)
• Understand their backstory, even if it never appears on screen
• Give them contradictions and complexity
• Make them feel like real people, not archetypes

Character Voices:
• Each character should sound distinct
• Word choice and speech patterns reflect personality
• Some characters are verbose, others hesitant
• Christina Yang: precise, sarcastic, cutting
• George O'Malley: nervous, apologetic, awkward
• Dr. Bailey: commanding, with "arias" of authority
• Let character personality dictate dialogue style

Character Flaws & Growth:
• Characters should evolve over seasons, not remain static
• They should change based on experiences
• Flaws make characters relatable and interesting
• Show character growth through changing behavior patterns
• Static characters become boring over time

PHASE 4: STRUCTURE & OUTLINING

Five-Act Structure:
• Act 1: Introduce characters, set up world, present problem (visual, intriguing)
• Act 2: Escalate situations, expand the world, meet more characters
• Act 3: Peak tension—"hold onto your seats" moment, often with a surprising turn
• Act 4: Ticking clock or character revelation, secrets emerge
• Act 5: Resolution or victory moment

Act Endings:
• Each act should end with something that turns the story
• Create "oh" or "wow" moments that make audiences want to continue
• Even in streaming, emotional beats and story turns create engagement
• Something must happen to change direction at each act break

Story Lines:
• A Story: Main storyline involving your protagonist (at least one scene per act)
• B Story: Secondary storyline, possibly equally large or slightly smaller
• C Story: "Runner"—smaller storyline, maybe 3 scenes total
• Smart writing intersects all storylines in scenes that service multiple stories
• Ensure you're continuously servicing your A story—don't let it disappear

Beat Sheets & Outlines:
• Beat Sheet: One or two words/sentences per story beat
• Identifies all important moments before detailed outlining
• Helps visualize the complete story arc
• Can be done on paper, whiteboard, or note cards for flexibility

Outline Options:
• Detailed outlines (25-30 pages) make script writing easier
• Brief outlines (5 pages, one per act) require more improvisation during scripting
• Outlines ensure everyone understands the story direction
• Shonda doesn't personally write outlines but recommends them for collaboration
• The more detailed your outline, the easier the script writing

Creating Story Bibles:
• Document with pages on each character (who they are, where they're going)
• Episode descriptions including themes, cases, character arcs
• Provides template for your show's format
• Useful for defining your show before production begins
• Shonda's Grey's Bible had 12 episode ideas for Season 1

PHASE 5: WRITING THE SCRIPT

Starting with the Opening:
• The opening must pull audiences in immediately
• Attention spans are short—make them want to stay
• Consider multiple approaches before committing
• Shonda wrote 3 different openings for Scandal over a year
• The opening defines how you'll introduce your protagonist and world

The Pilot Specifically:
• Needs compelling characters, story, dialogue
• Requires a great opening and an ending suggesting interesting things to come
• Don't kill main characters in pilots (unless money isn't an issue)
• Avoid expensive sets/locations that can't be sustained in series
• No single "right" way to write a pilot anymore—experimentation is possible
• The pilot makes a promise about what the show will be

Scene Writing:
• Every scene must resonate and move the story forward
• Remove unnecessary stage directions about how actors should act
• Focus stage directions on location and spatial information
• Make sure scenes are set in the right place
• Keep dialogue spare, even in lengthy monologues

Dialogue Principles:
• Say all dialogue out loud while writing to test naturalness
• Write what sounds like real conversation, not "TV talk"
• People don't speak in complete sentences
• People use wrong words, talk over each other, drop out mid-thought
• Avoid having characters explain their emotions explicitly
• Character word choice and patterns create distinct voices

Writing with Headphones:
• Shonda writes wearing noise-canceling headphones with loud music
• Music occupies the part of the brain that would otherwise distract
• Creates a Pavlovian trigger—headphones = writing mode
• Allows writing anywhere: parks, airports, soundstages, hallways
• Develop your own ritual for entering the creative headspace

Daily Writing Habit:
• Write in order from beginning to end
• Can't move to the next scene until the current one is right
• If stuck, switch to another project to unstick your brain
• The "hallway to the door" metaphor: daily practice shortens the path to creativity
• Writing muscles develop memory like physical muscles
• Schedule writing time and tasks to be completed

Using the Right Tools:
• Shonda uses Movie Magic Screenwriter (most people use Final Draft)
• Works on a MacBook for portability
• Can transfer work between desktop and laptop seamlessly
• Choose whatever screenwriting software works for you

PHASE 6: EDITING & REWRITING

Shonda's Editing Method:
• Write 10-15 pages, then perfect them before moving forward
• Never moves ahead until current pages are perfect
• Reaches "Fade Out" with minimal additional editing needed
• Prevents losing the thread of earlier work
• Keeps everything fresh in memory

What to Edit For:
• Ensure scenes resonate emotionally
• Make dialogue as original as possible
• Remove overly verbose dialogue—keep it spare
• Eliminate stage directions suggesting how to act
• Check that all necessary story beats are in place
• Verify no unanswered questions remain

Hearing Your Script:
• Say dialogue out loud while writing (even with headphones on)
• Helps identify whether dialogue sounds natural
• Tests pacing and emotional truth
• Identifies if fights feel authentic or monologues are emotional
• Acts out the entire script to feel your way through

Writers' Table Reads:
• Writers sit around and read scripts aloud, playing different parts
• Helps identify plot problems that aren't apparent on the page
• Reveals where scenes need to be cut or moved
• Three-dimensional experience makes issues obvious
• More helpful for Shonda than reading scripts silently

Actor Table Reads:
• Third layer of verification after writing and writers' table read
• Professional actors bring the script fully to life
• Final check before production begins
• Helps catch any remaining issues with character or story

Writer's Block:
• Shonda doesn't believe in writer's block—denying its existence helps combat it
• The solution: write something else to unstick your brain
• Switch between projects when stuck
• Having multiple shows allows this flexibility
• The act of writing about anything helps unblock the specific problem


================================================================================
🎬 BEYOND THE PILOT: SERIES WRITING
================================================================================

EPISODE 2 & EARLY EPISODES
• Episode 2 is essentially Episode 1 repeated differently
• Don't make a completely different show
• Reiterate the pilot's promise in a new way
• Build audience trust by delivering consistency
• Only deviate consciously and over time

SEASONAL PLANNING
• Think of each season as a new show with the same characters
• Change visual style, music, pacing, tone
• Character arcs should be completely different each season
• Use all your ideas in each season—end with no plan for the next
• Don't save ideas thinking you might not get renewed
• Walking away from a season with no clear next direction is good

LONG-TERM SERIES MANAGEMENT
• Characters must evolve and change over time
• Static characters become boring
• People want to watch character evolution
• Each season should feel visually and tonally different
• Maintain versatility and willingness to make adjustments

BALANCING PROCEDURAL & SERIALIZED ELEMENTS
• Procedural: Case-of-the-week format (Grey's Anatomy medical cases)
• Serialized: Ongoing character and plot arcs
• The balance can shift over seasons based on story needs
• Scandal started with cases but evolved to pure serialization
• Modern TV allows more flexibility than traditional network requirements
• Sometimes the case is just "Olivia" or "The White House"

PLANTING & PAYING OFF ACROSS SEASONS
• Plant story elements that pay off episodes or seasons later
• "Sweet baby" in Scandal Pilot paid off later with backstory
• Creates satisfying full-circle moments for dedicated viewers
• Builds loyalty and rewards audience investment
• Plan some long-term story threads intentionally

👥 CREATING MEMORABLE CHARACTERS
================================================================================

THE FUNDAMENTALS
• Characters need compelling dilemmas, not just interesting jobs
• They should be people you'd want to spend years with
• Give them contradictions that make them human
• Understand their needs (what they must have) vs. wants (what they think they need)
• Every character should have a secret or vulnerability

DEVELOPING CHARACTER VOICES
• Each character needs a distinct way of speaking
• Consider: are they verbose or economical with words?
• What's their emotional state? (nervous, confident, cutting)
• Word choice reveals personality
• Some characters have "arias" (long speeches), others are sparse
• Watch how your character's voice changes over time (Bailey as resident vs. attending)

WRITING FOR YOUR ACTORS
• Once cast, Shonda writes with actors' voices in mind
• Hearing their actual voices enriches the writing
• Never tells actors how to act—let them bring interpretation
• Actors often do the opposite of what she imagined, but it's always better
• The collaboration between writer and actor is intimate and special

CHARACTER BACKSTORY
• Know your character's history even if it never appears on screen
• Backstory informs present behavior and choices
• Understanding where they came from explains where they're going
• Creates depth that audiences can sense even without explicit exposition

CHARACTER CASTING
• Look for actors who spark imagination
• It's not about specific looks or types—any actor can audition
• Find someone you feel you can write for
• The "vetting process": find three people who can confirm the actor is a good person
• You'll work with them for years—they need to be good humans
• Check that they're excited to be there and fun to work with
• The actors' temperament affects the entire set

💼 BREAKING INTO THE INDUSTRY
================================================================================

EDUCATION & TRAINING
• Film school is valuable but not essential
• USC helped Shonda with contacts and production knowledge
• Consider the financial burden—student loans can be crushing
• Alternative: Get a PA job and write at night
• The key is to write, regardless of formal education
• Experience on set or in writers' offices can be more valuable than school

THE VALUE OF ORIGINAL WORK
• Write original material, not just spec scripts
• Original work proves you have your own voice
• Could be an article, play, novel, or original script
• Spec scripts only prove you can imitate someone else's voice
• Having original work is essential for getting hired

NETWORKING EFFECTIVELY
• Focus on building relationships with peers, not just established figures
• Your peers will rise together—the junior agent today becomes powerful tomorrow
• The intern you meet becomes a network president in 10 years
• Those who rise will take you with them
• Make friends with people your own age in the industry
• They're more likely to take risks on you than established executives

GETTING NOTICED
• If you get any job in the industry, make the best coffee they've ever had
• Do it with a smile and energy
• Entitled attitudes suck air from the room
• Great attitudes get noticed and lead to opportunities
• Be the first one there and the last to leave
• Shonda admits she was a terrible assistant initially—learn from her mistake

WRITERS' PROGRAMS
• Apply to network writers' programs (ABC, NBC, etc.)
• Several Scandal writers came from ABC's program
• Provides access to network executives most people never get
• Offers a way into the industry while getting paid
• Helps you determine if this career is for you
• Excellent networking opportunity

RESOURCES & OPPORTUNITIES
• Sundance, Women in Film, Black Filmmakers Foundation offer programs
• These organizations actively try to open doors
• Research what's available—resources exist if you look
• Use cheerful persistence (not annoying persistence)
• Polite inquiries and reminders work better than aggressive approaches
• Say "I understand nothing's available now, but please keep me in mind"

WHAT NOT TO DO
• Don't approach at fan events to pitch scripts or request meetings
• Legally, scripts can't be accepted at public events
• These situations can be inappropriate and scary for creators
• Use professional channels, not fan interactions
• At writers' events, ask questions—don't hand out scripts to 500 people
• Walk in a professional line

THE MENTOR MYTH
• Don't try to "find a mentor" or ask someone to be your mentor
• Mentorship develops organically over time
• Asking directly means they can't be your mentor
• Multiple people teach you things throughout your career
• Look back later to see who influenced you
• Learn by example, words, and opportunities given

THE PATH TO SUCCESS
• Get as close to a writers' room as possible
• Stand near a showrunner and absorb everything
• Work as a PA or writers' assistant
• Let showrunners notice you and read your scripts
• Write original work that showcases your voice
• The proximity to working writers accelerates learning

NOT EVERYONE NEEDS THEIR OWN SHOW
• It's perfectly wonderful to write on someone else's show
• Staff writing is a valuable career
• Learn and hone your craft without the pressure of running a show
• Watch other people's mistakes and triumphs
• Many rough writers grow into amazing ones through staff experience
• If everyone had their own show, no one would have staff writers

THE BEAUTY OF WRITING
• Writing is the only job in Hollywood you can do without being hired first
• You can demonstrate your ability before anyone gives you a chance
• Directors need equipment and actors; actors need roles
• Writers just need themselves and something to write on
• "If you can write, somebody will hire you"
• There are more writing jobs than good writers—it's a writer's market


================================================================================
✅ WORKING IN A WRITERS' ROOM
================================================================================

RULES FOR NEW WRITERS

Essential Behaviors:
1. Hold the marker and stand at the board—shows energy and engagement
2. Talk at least once a day—silence is deadly
3. Read writers' room notes from the previous night
4. Be the person who reminds everyone what was said
5. Try to be useful and helpful
6. Be kind and nice
7. Be the first one there and the last to leave

Homework & Preparation:
• Go home and do work every evening
• Read writers' room notes from the day before
• Prepare ideas for the next day's discussion
• If it's your episode, come in with new pitches and ideas
• Never show up with no new work—you must forward the story
• Write new scenes to fill the board for discussion
• Arrive prepared with thoughts, not just "what are we doing?"

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

Not Talking:
• Silence makes people forget you're there
• They start thinking you're stupid or have nothing to say
• You get a grace period for being new and nervous
• After a while, silence means you're "dead in the water"
• If you were hired, you deserve to be there—act like it

Talking Too Much:
• Hogging all the airtime prevents others from contributing
• Nobody else gets to share thoughts or ideas
• Rare but deadly when it happens
• Balance is key—contribute without dominating

Plot-Only Pitches:
• Never pitch pure plot without character motivation
• "A man wants to blow up a building" means nothing
• Must come from character: "His heart is broken, leading to emotional breakdown"
• Shonda literally says "I can't hear you" to plot-only pitches
• Everything must relate to character emotion and stakes
• What's emotional for the character? For the audience?

Being the Naysayer:
• Just shooting down others' ideas without offering solutions
• Gets you fired fastest in most writers' rooms
• Instead, build on ideas or find alternative solutions
• Constructive contribution, not just criticism

Only Performing for the Showrunner:
• Some writers only "turn on" when the showrunner is present
• Other writers notice and report this
• Be consistently engaged regardless of who's in the room
• The room shouldn't be competitive—it's collaborative

WHAT MAKES A GOOD PITCH

Coming from Character:
• Relate every pitch to character needs and emotions
• Plot events must have emotional stakes
• Explain why the character is doing something, not just what they're doing
• Make the audience feel something through character connection

Completeness:
• Pitches don't have to be fully developed A-to-D stories
• But you should have ideas, even if incomplete
• Start somewhere and let the room help build it
• Better to have partial ideas than no ideas

LEARNING THE SHOW

The Drafts Method:
• One writer read: first draft, production draft, final draft
• Then watched the cut of each episode
• Compared to see what changed and why
• Understood what gets honed and cut
• Learned to write the show better and faster than anyone
• Highly recommended for joining an existing show
• Shows you what the showrunner thinks the show should be

Understanding the Template:
• Every show has its own structure and rhythm
• Learn what the showrunner values
• Write the show they want, not the show you imagine
• That's the only show that matters

WRITERS' ROOM CULTURE

Grey's Anatomy Room:
• Like kindergarten or a nursing home day room
• Full of arts and crafts: knitting, painting by numbers, elaborate projects
• Has a treadmill for running during discussions
• Always active with multiple activities
• Very tight-knit group that never leaves
• Heavy eating component
• Everyone eats lunch together—don't be the person who doesn't

Scandal Room:
• Smaller room with fewer people
• Feet up on furniture, relaxed atmosphere
• Different vibe than Grey's

Room Etiquette:
• If everyone has assigned seats, don't take someone else's spot—it's deadly
• Follow the cultural norms (Scotch Tuesdays, bake-offs)
• Participate in group activities if that's the culture
• Understand and respect each room's unique personality

WHAT SHOWRUNNERS LOOK FOR

Original Voice:
• Writers who have unique perspectives
• Not afraid to express opinions
• Willing to argue for their ideas
• Comes from diverse backgrounds and experiences

Confidence Without Arrogance:
• Not afraid of the showrunner
• Willing to debate and defend positions
• But also collaborative and open to feedback

Diversity of Perspective:
• Different ages, backgrounds, experiences
• Creates better television through varied viewpoints
• Prevents echo chamber of agreement
• The showrunner can't always be right

Building on Ideas:
• Taking others' concepts and expanding them
• Finding solutions when you disagree
• Contributing constructively to the collaborative process

🎥 SHOWRUNNING & PRODUCTION
================================================================================

THE SHOWRUNNER'S ROLE

Creative Leadership:
• Direct story and oversee storytelling across all episodes
• Make final decisions on scripts, casting, edits
• Maintain the show's vision and voice
• Ensure consistency across episodes and seasons
• Balance multiple shows simultaneously

Production Management:
• Work with line producers on budget and feasibility
• Understand production limitations and costs
• Make decisions about what's practical to shoot
• Interface with studio and network
• Oversee all aspects from script to final cut

THE PRODUCTION REALITY

The Eight-Day Cycle:
• Every 8 days, a new script must be ready
• For 22 episodes: 22 cycles of 8 days each
• 8 days in prep, 8 days shooting, 8 days post-production
• Continuous cycle—if "the trains stop," production stops
• Money keeps getting spent while everyone waits
• Must constantly stay ahead of production

TV vs. Film Timeline:
• Movies: 364 days to think, then months to write
• TV: immediate execution—idea on Monday, table read next Monday, shooting following Monday
• Movies can take years from script to screen
• TV is terrifying and exciting in its immediacy
• No months of notes, discussion, rehearsals—it happens fast

LINE PRODUCERS

Building the Relationship:
• Sit down honestly about what you know and don't know
• Tell them what you need from them
• "I need to know when something's too expensive"
• "I need to know when I'm doing something crazy"
• "Give me the fences to play within so I know what I'm doing"
• Many writers don't do this, leaving producers uncertain

Why It Matters:
• Line producers don't know if you're clueless or just don't care about budget
• Being honest creates partnership and education
• They can walk in and say "you're going to kill me, but..."
• Invaluable feedback prevents budget disasters
• Without answers, you have no idea anything is wrong

The Writer's Production Education:
• Many writers have no production experience—this is dangerous
• Writers need to understand how a set works
• Shonda says she's "the only person not working" when on set
• Every writer who gets a show needs "line producer boot camp"
• Understanding production makes all the difference

NON-WRITING PRODUCING PARTNERS

The Value:
• Shonda highly recommends having one
• Rare in Hollywood for TV shows
• Invaluable for managing multiple shows
• Allows the showrunner to focus on story

What They Handle:
• Deal with studio and network
• Manage production side and logistics
• Handle heavy lifting on non-creative aspects
• Enable showrunner to direct story
• Make it possible to run multiple shows (Shondaland has had 6 shows on air simultaneously)

Why More People Should Do This:
• Shonda and Betsy Beers always wonder why others don't
• Starting to become more frequent
• Expands capacity for creating and overseeing shows
• Allows for both creating own shows and shepherding others' shows

WORKING WITH ACTORS

Casting Process:
• Look for actors who spark imagination
• Must feel like you can write for them
• Not about specific looks or meeting a type
• Anyone can audition for any role

The Vetting Process:
• Find three people in town who confirm the actor is a good person
• You'll work with them for years—compatibility matters
• Creative process is intimate—you're inside each other's heads
• Affects mood and temperament of the entire set
• Creates special relationships when it works
• Owe it to other actors and crew to have excited, fun people

Dialogue & Performance:
• Dialogue is text and should be performed as written
• "I am a writer. You are an actor. I'll never tell you how to act, you'll never tell me how to write"
• Let each other do their jobs
• Actors bring interpretation Shonda never imagined
• Surprises are often better and richer than original vision
• Love being surprised by what actors do with words

SEX SCENES ON NETWORK TV

The Reality:
• Network TV cannot show actual sex
• People think there's "tons of sex" on Shondaland shows
• Actually zero sex shown—only sexiness
• Pride in depicting intimacy without showing body parts or sex acts
• More creative than cable where "you can show anything"

Shondaland Rules:
• Every woman can do a sex scene "in a snow suit" if she wants
• Body covered neck to ankles—they'll make it work
• Woman on set decides how the scene goes
• Must feel 100% comfortable
• Shonda appears on set as "the human chastity belt"
• Never display pieces of women's bodies gratuitously

The Process:
• Meeting ahead of time to discuss what it will look like
• Often not specifically written: "Sex scene in whatever way actors feel comfortable. Make it sexy."
• Sometimes very specific about what to see
• Choreographed with directors who understand the rules
• Nobody should feel forced to do anything
• Sometimes scenes are hotter because you imagine what's happening

THE IMPORTANCE OF CREW

The Hierarchy Truth:
• A set is about the crew, not director or actors
• Crew runs the set—they're the experts
• Everyone else is essentially a guest
• Their work deserves respect for things to run smoothly and look good
• Many people don't understand this coming in

Episodic Television Family:
• Crew stays the same, cast stays the same, writers stay the same
• Directors and guest stars rotate
• Becomes a real family over years
• Shonda's never had a show run less than 6 seasons
• People move from show to show within Shondaland
• Currently 5 crews, creating one big Shondaland family
• Lovely to work with the same people for so long

Why It Matters:
• Crew's craft is extraordinary and often unrecognized
• Their expertise enables everything else to happen
• Understanding this creates better working relationships
• Respecting crew hierarchy makes production smooth

🎬 DIALOGUE WRITING MASTERY
================================================================================

WHY DIALOGUE MATTERS
• What characters say and don't say tells you everything
• Word choice reveals character deeply
• Lazy dialogue writing shows in the final product
• Dialogue can be the difference between good and great

LEARNING FROM THE BEST
• Breaking Bad's "I am the one who knocks" speech
• The West Wing's intelligent, rapid-fire exchanges
• The Crown's use of silence to convey everything
• Watch shows for how they use dialogue differently
• Notice when characters show instead of tell

WRITING AUTHENTIC DIALOGUE

The Eavesdropping Method:
• Listen to real conversations everywhere
• Become a professional eavesdropper—it's fun and educational
• Notice how people actually talk
• People don't speak in complete sentences
• People use wrong words, drop out mid-thought, talk over each other
• Real people say crazy stuff sometimes

What to Avoid:
• TV talk—dialogue that sounds written
• Clichés—anything you've heard before
• Perfect articulation of emotions
• Characters saying exactly what they mean
• "Here's how I feel and why I feel it and what my therapist said"
• Long explanations of inner states

The Out Loud Test:
• Say all dialogue aloud while writing
• Shonda does this with headphones on, acting every part
• Can't hear herself but can feel if it works
• Tests whether dialogue sounds natural
• Reveals pacing, emotion, humor
• If you can't say it naturally, there's a problem

Getting Others to Read:
• Have someone read dialogue aloud to you
• Hearing it in another voice reveals issues
• Catches awkward phrasing and unnatural speech
• Essential part of the revision process

TEXT VS. SUBTEXT

Understanding the Difference:
• Text: what people actually say
• Subtext: what they really mean
• Never write subtext as text
• Let actors convey emotional subtext visually and silently
• "Shakespeare can get away with it. You cannot. Neither can I."

The Therapy Speak Problem:
• Real people don't explain their emotions explicitly
• "Don't you see my heart is broken and here's why..."
• Painful to watch characters become their own therapists
• Find visual or subtle ways to convey inner states

CREATING DISTINCT CHARACTER VOICES

Matching Voice to Character:
• Some characters are verbose (Papa Pope, Cyrus Bean)
• Others are hesitant and sparse
• Consider personality when choosing words
• Christina Yang: sarcastic, cutting, precise, making sure you know she thinks you're stupid
• George O'Malley: nervous, apologetic, always backtracking
• Dr. Bailey: tough "arias" of authority
• Young Resident Bailey: mumbly, hesitant, terrified, shy

The Character Evolution:
• Voices can change over time as characters grow
• Bailey as resident vs. Bailey as attending
• Show character growth through language changes
• Maintain core personality while showing development

Word Choice Matters:
• Specific words reveal specific personalities
• Don't make every character sound like you
• Give characters their own patterns and preferences
• Match vocabulary to education, background, emotional state

WRITING WITH ACTORS IN MIND

Once Cast:
• Can only hear actors' voices after casting
• Makes writing richer and more interesting
• Hear their actual voices in your head
• Brings additional dimension to character

The Partnership:
• Writer writes, actor acts—respect boundaries
• Never tell actors how to act
• They never tell you how to write
• Actors bring unexpected interpretations
• Often the opposite of what was imagined
• Always better and more original than expected

DIALOGUE AS CHARACTER REVELATION

What Characters Don't Say:
• Silence can be more powerful than words
• Watch shows with meaningful silent scenes
• Pay attention to what's conveyed without dialogue
• Sometimes everything is communicated nonverbally

Subtext in Action:
• Characters hint at feelings rather than stating them
• Emotional truth comes through between the lines
• Audience engagement increases when they read subtext
• Trust your audience to understand implications

PRACTICAL DIALOGUE TIPS

Length and Density:
• Even characters who talk a lot should have "spare" dialogue
• Overly verbose dialogue is a problem
• "Huge monologues" should still be "spare huge monologues"
• Cut anything that doesn't serve character or story

Originality:
• Make dialogue as original as possible
• Avoid predictable responses
• Surprise yourself with what characters say
• Fresh language keeps scenes alive

Natural Flow:
• Let conversations meander like real talk
• People don't always finish thoughts
• Interruptions are realistic
• Overlapping dialogue shows authenticity

🎯 KEY TAKEAWAYS & ACTION ITEMS
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS FOR ASPIRING WRITERS

1. Start Writing Daily
   • Establish a writing routine and stick to it
   • Write every day to build muscle memory
   • Don't wait for perfect conditions
   • Use headphones or develop your own ritual
   • Schedule specific writing time blocks

2. Study Television
   • Watch pilots of shows you love and hate
   • Get scripts for those pilots
   • Read, watch, then read again
   • Dissect structure, act breaks, character introductions
   • Choose one show and study it deeply (like Shonda did with West Wing)
   • Watch classic television, not just current shows

3. Create Original Work
   • Write an original pilot, not just spec scripts
   • Develop your unique voice
   • Make sure it's something you'd want to watch
   • Don't try to copy what's already been done
   • Let your original work showcase who you are as a writer

4. Develop Your Idea
   • Turn your idea into a specific premise
   • Apply the 5 W's: Who, What, Why, When, Where, How
   • Test if you can see 400 episodes
   • Make sure it's character-driven
   • Ensure it can generate endless stories

5. Create a Beat Sheet
   • Map out the key moments in your pilot
   • Identify your A, B, and C stories
   • Plan where each act should end
   • Know your ending before you start writing
   • Use whiteboards, note cards, or paper—whatever works

6. Practice Authentic Dialogue
   • Eavesdrop on real conversations
   • Say all dialogue out loud while writing
   • Read your work aloud or have others read it
   • Remove "TV talk" and clichés
   • Give each character a distinct voice

7. Get Close to the Industry
   • Apply for network writers' programs
   • Get a PA job or writers' assistant position
   • Network with peers, not just established figures
   • Be the person with the best attitude
   • Make the best coffee anyone's ever had

8. Build Your Knowledge Base
   • Read news daily from multiple sources
   • Understand current events and cultural moments
   • Stay informed about the marketplace
   • Know what networks are looking for
   • Read books, watch documentaries, absorb everything

MINDSET SHIFTS

Stop Waiting for Permission:
• You can write without anyone hiring you
• Demonstrate your ability before getting hired
• Nobody needs to give you a chance to prove yourself
• The industry needs good writers more than writers need the industry

Embrace the Process:
• Don't believe in writer's block—deny its existence
• When stuck, write something else
• Deadlines are your friend
• The more you write, the easier it gets
• Trust your unique process

Character First, Always:
• Everything comes from character
• Plot without character motivation is meaningless
• Audiences follow emotional honesty
• Think of characters as real people, not devices

LONG-TERM DEVELOPMENT

Build Your Community:
• Develop relationships with fellow emerging writers
• These peers will rise with you
• Your intern friend becomes tomorrow's network president
• Support each other's work and growth

Keep Learning:
• Study how shows are structured
• Learn from both successes and failures
• Understand production realities
• Know the business side while maintaining artistic integrity

Stay Authentic:
• Write shows you want to watch
• Develop your unique voice
• Don't imitate others
• Trust your perspective and experiences

REMEMBER

"Writing is like running—the more you do it, the stronger those muscles get, and the faster you reach the creative door."

"If you can write, somebody will hire you. There are more jobs than good writers right now."

"The only job in Hollywood you can do without being hired first is writing. Sit in your closet and write."

"Character motivation is everything. Without it, you have no story."

"Your dialogue exists as written. Let actors bring their interpretation, but respect each other's craft."

"Every show makes a promise in its pilot. Fulfill that promise every week."

"If you plant something in your story, it must pay off. Kill the puppy."

"Text is what characters say. Subtext is what they mean. Never write subtext as dialogue."

"Be the first one there and the last to leave. Make the best coffee anyone's ever had."

"The beauty of television is that what's in the writer's head becomes what's on screen."
