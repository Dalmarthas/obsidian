📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass presents a comprehensive framework for entrepreneurial leadership based on decades of experience building Starbucks from 11 stores to a global brand with 30,000+ locations. The course challenges the false dichotomy between values and profits, demonstrating how companies can achieve both financial success and social responsibility. The speaker shares personal stories from growing up in public housing in Brooklyn to leading one of the world's most respected brands, emphasizing that success comes from authentic leadership, cultural investment, and unwavering commitment to people.

The course is structured around 13 core lessons that progress from foundational mindset (jumping in, being curious, establishing values) through operational excellence (focus, disruption, team building) to advanced leadership challenges (crisis management, self-cannibalization, and sustained growth). Each lesson combines practical frameworks with real-world examples, particularly from the 2008 Starbucks crisis and the company's expansion into China. The overarching message is clear: leadership is not about managing but about inspiring people to be part of something larger than themselves.

This isn't a theoretical business course—it's a deeply personal guide to building companies that people are proud to work for and customers are proud to support. The speaker emphasizes that entrepreneurship requires sacrifice, courage, and the willingness to learn from failure while maintaining high standards and deep humanity.

🎯 CORE THEMES & PHILOSOPHY

THE ENTREPRENEURIAL MINDSET
• Success is not an entitlement—it must be earned every day
• Entrepreneurship means jumping into the deep end without a life jacket and learning to swim
• Self-doubt about ability is natural; self-doubt about commitment is fatal
• Live without regrets—better to try and fail than never to attempt your dream
• There is no magical moment, formula, or textbook—only personal courage and conviction
• Not everyone is built to be an entrepreneur, and that's okay—find your position in life

VALUES AND PROFITS AS ALLIES
• The fragile balance between profit and responsibility is achievable
• Values-based companies must still financially perform—sequencing matters
• The price of admission to being values-based is financial performance
• The two are not mutually exclusive but complementary
• Success is best when shared—equity and benefits strengthen the entire organization
• Building a company you're proud of requires moral responsibility alongside fiduciary duty

THE STARBUCKS ORIGIN STORY
• Started with 11 stores and 100 people in 1987
• Built on a dream to create a national specialty coffee brand
• Founded by people with entrepreneurial DNA but no experience building national brands
• Rooted in personal experience: witnessing father's treatment as a blue-collar worker
• First company in America to give comprehensive health insurance to part-time workers (25 years before ACA)
• Pioneered equity through stock options because "success is best when it's shared"
• Created free college tuition program for all employees
• Now operates in 75+ countries with 30,000+ stores

🔑 KEY FRAMEWORKS & METHODOLOGIES

THE CULTURE-BUILDING FRAMEWORK

1. DEFINITION AND FOUNDATION
• Culture = behavior + values + guiding principles + core purpose
• Built through daily actions during startup/imprinting phase
• Analogous to raising children with values and self-esteem
• Measured by what employees say at dinner tables

2. MAINTENANCE MECHANISMS
• Cultural audits: 10 questions, once or twice yearly, optional anonymity
• Open forums and town halls with guaranteed no retribution
• Swift correction of inconsistent behavior
• Storytelling about company heritage and sacrifices
• Outside speakers for continuing education

3. INTEGRATION POINTS
• Brand equity must reflect company culture—fully integrated
• Trust and confidence with two constituencies: people and customers
• 100% consistency in "this is who we are"
• Performance-driven through lens of humanity

THE HIRING AND TEAM-BUILDING PROCESS

PHASE 1: PRE-RECRUITMENT (100 days before funding)
• Personal networking to identify potential hires
• Building relationships with 4-5 key people
• Ready-to-start team demonstrates seriousness to investors

PHASE 2: INTERVIEW STRATEGY
• Reduce tension and anxiety immediately
• Personal questions first: "What's the last book you read?"
• Family background and personal habits
• Demonstrate servant leadership from first interaction
• Multiple interviewers from different organizational functions

PHASE 3: COMPENSATION STRUCTURE
• More equity than cash, especially for senior hires
• Red flag if candidate only wants cash compensation
• Stock option pool established with board beforehand
• Annual equity rewards based on individual and company performance
• "Overpay" for exceptional talent

PHASE 4: ONBOARDING AND IMMERSION
• Prepare organization—no surprise announcements
• Immersion period before strategic responsibility
• No P&L authority until properly integrated
• Cultural fit monitoring in early weeks

PHASE 5: PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT
• Annual or biannual appraisals with no surprises
• Year-long buildup through regular one-on-ones
• Every meeting has purpose and outcome
• Common language about mediocrity vs. excellence
• Address problems immediately—fail fast on mistakes

THE CRISIS LEADERSHIP MODEL

PHASE 1: SELF-ASSESSMENT
• Ask: "Do I have what it takes to lead through this?"
• Answer: Yes, but you must believe it
• Recognize when hubris has entered the organization
• Take personal responsibility (even if not current CEO)

PHASE 2: TRANSPARENCY AND TRUTH-TELLING
• Fill reservoir of trust through previous cultural investments
• Stand before entire company acknowledging critical moment
• Lay out challenges with openness and complete granularity
• Share 100% of information without piecemealing
• Trust people with same data you have

PHASE 3: EXPLANATION AND CONTEXT
• Explain why difficult decisions are necessary
• Distinguish between values (unchanged) and resource allocation (adjusted)
• Make clear you're not forsaking values
• Provide financial realities and challenges

PHASE 4: EMPATHETIC ACTION
• Make necessary cuts sooner rather than later
• Demonstrate 100% empathy for those affected
• Recognize impact on remaining employees
• Consider knowledge and families of departing colleagues

PHASE 5: CULTURAL REINFORCEMENT
• Ring the bell on wrong behavior immediately
• Use storytelling about heritage and sacrifice
• Remind people success is not an entitlement
• Eradicate entitlement like a disease

THE COMPETITIVE STRATEGY FRAMEWORK

1. INTERNAL FOCUS FIRST
• Be in control of your own destiny
• Lead offensively, not reactively
• Don't let competitors dictate strategy
• Competition should inform, not determine

2. MARKETPLACE ANALYSIS
• Understand what chessboard looks like
• Study every direct and indirect competitor thoroughly
• Create guidebook of what competitors have done
• Prepare for likely competitive responses

3. DIFFERENTIATION STRATEGY
• Create enduring, sustainable competitive advantage
• Establish big wedge between yourself and competitors
• Features, benefits, price, convenience combined
• Premium positioning rather than price competition
• Entrepreneurial nimbleness large companies can't match

4. INNOVATION APPROACH
• Invest ahead of growth curve
• R&D vital for disruption innovation
• Break rules and fracture marketplace
• Create new categories, not just line extensions
• Thread innovations into brand equity guardrails

5. SELF-DISRUPTION
• Cannibalize your own business before others do
• Don't fear disrupting core business
• Balance: don't chase too many things too early
• Ensure team capacity before adding new initiatives
• Earn way into next category evolution

THE DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK

1. PRIORITIZATION (3-5 Big Goals)
• Company becomes dysfunctional chasing too many things
• Establish clear organizational priorities
• Manage against defined goals
• Personal assessment: where can I have most impact?
• Monthly calendar review for time allocation

2. THE TWO EMPTY CHAIRS TEST
• Employee chair in every leadership and board meeting
• Customer chair in every leadership and board meeting
• Shareholders represented by board (already present)
• Ask: Will this decision make people and customers proud?
• Yes = proceed; No = red flag and stop; Gray = more debate

3. OPERATIONAL ENGAGEMENT
• "Get in the mud"—hands dirty, ground-level operation
• Don't operate at 20,000 feet
• Informal conversations reveal critical information
• Financial reports should never surprise you
• Monthly P&L is report card of what you already know

4. DUAL METRICS
• Financial performance (income statement, balance sheet)
• Cultural health and strength (equally important)
• Both must be monitored continuously
• Visibility and probing questions prevent surprises

THE INVESTOR RELATIONS FRAMEWORK

1. PRE-INVESTMENT DUE DILIGENCE
• Research investor history in your space
• Talk to other entrepreneurs about their experience
• Get reference checks on fund and representative
• Set up hypotheticals about difficulties
• Ensure values alignment before signing

2. PITCH STRATEGY
• Share personal story first—WHO you are
• Explain WHY you're doing this and what you're sacrificing
• Acknowledge degree of difficulty honestly
• Demonstrate responsibility awareness
• Balance enthusiasm with healthy skepticism
• Under-promise to over-deliver later

3. EQUITY AND CONTROL
• Give up as little equity as possible
• Don't haggle excessively on early-stage price
• Control comes from performance, not ownership percentage
• 51% ownership not necessary to maintain control
• Raise more than you think you need

4. ONGOING RELATIONSHIP
• Fiduciary responsibility to meet numbers
• Provide promised rate of return
• Beat projections whenever possible
• First quarter/year critical for establishing credibility
• Transparency about challenges when they arise

💡 CRITICAL CONCEPTS & DEFINITIONS

CULTURE
More than a catch-all buzzword—understanding human behavior and elevating groups of people to believe they're part of something larger. The foundation for values, guiding principles, and core purpose that defines reason for being.

SERVANT LEADERSHIP
Leading by serving others, demonstrating from first interview that you value people's humanity, families, and well-being. Creating atmosphere where people feel valued and appreciated, not managed.

PERFORMANCE THROUGH LENS OF HUMANITY
Being financially disciplined and results-driven while maintaining deep empathy, compassion, and sensitivity to human needs. The two are not mutually exclusive.

RESERVOIR OF TRUST
Accumulated deposits into organizational culture through consistent values-based actions. When filled, allows leaders to ask difficult things during crisis. Withdrawals must be minimized; deposits continuous.

GUARDRAILS OF GROWTH
Boundaries defining what size business you're building (local/national/global) and brand equity limits preventing dilutive expansion into inappropriate categories.

IMMERSION
Period before new hire gains strategic or P&L responsibility, allowing integration into business culture and operations without immediate decision-making pressure.

UNIT ECONOMICS
Financial model for business profitability. Retail brick-and-mortar best-in-class: 2:1 sales-to-investment ratio, 20% fully-loaded operating margin.

DISRUPTIVE INNOVATION
Innovation that fractures and breaks rules, disrupting marketplace rather than incrementally improving. Creates new categories vs. line extensions.

SELF-CANNIBALIZATION
Deliberately disrupting your own core business before competitors do. Requires threading new innovations into brand equity to avoid dilution.

COMMON LANGUAGE
Shared organizational understanding of excellence vs. mediocrity, with specific examples defining expected performance standards.

CULTURAL AUDIT
Institutionalized opportunity (once or twice yearly) for employees to respond to questions about culture, values, feelings about company, environment, managers, and leadership.

IMPRINTING STAGE
Early startup phase when every daily action defines company culture permanently, analogous to raising children with values and self-esteem.

THE TWO EMPTY CHAIRS
Mental model for decision-making: visualizing employee and customer presence in leadership/board meetings to ensure decisions serve their interests.

DEGREE OF DIFFICULTY
Honest assessment of challenge level in achieving goals, shared transparently with team so they understand what they're committing to and sacrificing for.

FIDUCIARY AND MORAL RESPONSIBILITY
Dual obligation to provide shareholder returns while fulfilling ethical duties to employees, customers, and communities served.

📊 CASE STUDIES & EXAMPLES

THE 2008 STARBUCKS CRISIS

Background:
• Growth and success covered up mistakes
• Hubris entered organization like "carcinogenic disease"
• Company nearly lost entirely
• Negative comp store sales and traffic for first time in 30+ years
• 50 fewer customers per day at each store

Leadership Response:
• Day one: public apology to entire company in Seattle
• Tears because of sadness at letting people down
• Took personal culpability despite not being CEO
• New Orleans meeting with 12,000 store managers

The Transparency Decision:
• Colleagues warned against full truth-telling
• Feared scaring managers with dire situation
• Decision: trust people with 100% of information
• Shared complete granularity about degree of difficulty
• "Many people believe this is not something you should know, but we are in this together"
• Explained first-ever negative comp sales and traffic
• Asked: "What are we going to do about it?"

Outcome:
• Transformation many said was impossible
• Reinforced culture through adversity
• Established model for crisis transparency

STARBUCKS CHINA EXPANSION

Nine Years of Failure:
• Howard Schultz personally responsible
• Made same mistake twice
• Brought tenured US executives to run China
• They lacked understanding of Chinese consumer, employee, marketplace
• Attempted to change tea-drinking society behavior
• As public company, faced calls to exit China
• Persisted despite financial non-performance

The Turning Point:
• Hired Belinda Wong as CEO of Starbucks China
• Chinese person with Starbucks understanding
• Transformed business through cultural integration
• Now 3,500+ stores, opening more than one per day

The Family Meeting Innovation:
• Jack Ma (Alibaba) invited parents to company functions
• Explained one-child policy importance of family unit
• Howard "stole" the idea for Starbucks China
• Rented large auditorium (risk if not filled)
• Thousands of parents, aunts, uncles, grandparents attended
• Meeting celebrated children/employees, not company numbers
• Became annual tradition
• Led to healthcare coverage for employees' parents
• First company (American, Chinese, or otherwise) to offer this benefit
• Partnership with Chinese government and insurance company

THE XEROX EXPERIENCE

Setting:
• First job after college graduation
• Mother cried—badge of honor to tell friends
• Three-week training in Leesburg, Virginia
• Professional selling skills program

The Challenge:
• Six months as assistant to Manhattan salesperson
• 50 physical cold calls daily into office buildings
• Essentially 50 daily rejections
• Leads given to territory owner, not assistant
• Competing with peers for lead generation

Lessons Learned:
• Humility of rejection
• Understanding of perseverance
• Competitive drive to create leads
• Wouldn't want to repeat but taught so much
• Humbling experience with lasting impact

THE ALLBIRDS INVESTMENT (Maveron Fund)

Initial Assessment:
• Shoe market appeared saturated
• Large companies with great resources and innovation
• Question: Any room for another shoe company?
• Normally would advise against entering

Success Factors:
• Unique shoe design and pricing
• Distinctive fabric, quality, integrity
• Strong company ethos
• Smart social media strategy
• Emotional consumer engagement
• Management literally "walking in customers' shoes"
• Entrepreneurial nimbleness large companies lack
• Created features and benefits differentiation

Outcome:
• Broke through despite difficult market
• Grew from startup to mid-sized company
• Demonstrates niche player opportunity in large markets

THE YOUNG STARBUCKS EMPLOYEE

The Moment:
• 25-26 year old in large meeting
• Asked simple question
• Question unrelated to his supply chain role
• Asked about completely different topic
• Caught Howard's attention

Follow-up:
• Howard asked "Who is that guy?"
• Proactively approached to discuss education and experience
• Made sure other Starbucks leaders noticed him
• Employee demonstrated personal initiative
• Comfortable putting himself forward
• Lacked fear of failure

Outcome:
• Leapfrogged other people within company
• Not because Howard noticed, but because of initiative
• Demonstrates importance of showing up and making impact

EARLY STARBUCKS FUNDING CHALLENGE

The Situation:
• Business plan goal: 100 stores
• Rejected 242 times by investors
• Literally couldn't afford to reprint business plan
• Had to white-out "100" stores
• Typed "75" over it because nobody believed in 100

The Lesson:

• Dreamed big but didn't say it out loud

🔍 DETAILED TOPIC BREAKDOWNS

INTELLECTUAL CURIOSITY

Why It Matters:
• See things others don't see
• Look around corners
• Have experiences not linked to normal routine
• Meet and listen to new people
• Put yourself in uncomfortable positions

How to Develop:
• Work in organizations before starting your own
• Ask how companies make decisions
• Seek career development conversations with HR
• Create personal relationships inside organizations
• Request mentorship with clear expectations
• Study outside your immediate domain
• Never stop learning regardless of age (speaker at 65 still seeking)

Warning Signs of Lack:
• Can't answer "What's the last book you read?" quickly
• Not reading anything currently
• Staying only in comfort zone
• Avoiding uncomfortable learning situations

DISRUPTION VS. PIONEERING

Prefer Disruption Because:
• Changing consumer behavior takes too long
• Requires too many resources
• Need compelling story, form factor, and platform
• Must be willing to run long race of losing money

How to Disrupt:
• Target categories where large companies feel entitled
• Identify opportunities from complacency
• Be more innovative than established players
• Create unique features and benefits
• Leverage entrepreneurial nimbleness
• Build differentiation large companies can't match

Market Analysis:
• Not every product is national/global opportunity
• Some markets better served by niche approach
• Look for categories with emerging consumer concern
• Example: Pet food specialty/health opportunity (analog to organic/gluten-free human food)
• Find large markets where you can create complete differentiation

UNIT ECONOMICS MASTERY

Retail Brick-and-Mortar Best-in-Class:
• 2:1 sales to investment ratio
• 20% fully-loaded operating margin
• Example: $1M sales = $500K max investment + $200K profit
• If at 1:1 ratio or 10% margin, business model doesn't work

Consumer Products:
• Under tremendous margin pressure
• Hidden costs in supply chain often overlooked
• Focus on cost of getting to market, not just making product
• Supply chain aspect critical to understand thoroughly

Financial Awareness:
• Monthly/quarterly reports should be report cards, not surprises
• Constantly fed information daily/weekly
• Leaders paying attention already know results
• Surprises indicate leader not doing job

TRANSPARENCY AND TRUST

The Case For:
• If asking people to do something difficult
• If requiring sacrifice from team
• If attempting something never done before
• They deserve same information you have
• Can't piecemeal information in crisis
• "Share the rock"—give everything

The Case Against (and Refutation):
• Might scare people
• Too complex for them to understand
• Could derail your message
• REFUTATION: These concerns underestimate people and break trust

Implementation:
• Fill reservoir of trust through prior cultural investments
• Stand before entire company
• Lay out challenges with complete openness
• Share financial issues and degree of difficulty
• Explain what must be overcome
• Ask: "What are we going to do about it?"

Results:
• People rally when treated with respect
• Collective understanding drives collective action
• Transparency builds deeper trust
• Enables transformation others say is impossible

MANAGING DIFFICULT CONVERSATIONS

With Direct Reports:
• Address issues early—don't let mediocrity persist
• Have serious conversation about inconsistent performance
• Establish if person can meet expectations
• Better to know early "it's not going to work out"
• Other people watching how you handle it
• One standard for everybody—no exceptions

With People Not Fitting Culture:
• Recognize gift and privilege of working at your company
• Ensure it's two-way street—you provide tools/resources/support
• When someone is not cultural fit: "I don't think it's going to work out"
• "This is who we are and this appears to be who you are"
• Early-stage companies particularly vulnerable
• One or two wrong fits have significant negative impact

About Layoffs:
• Fiduciary and moral responsibility to make necessary cuts
• Sooner is better than later
• Emotionally difficult but preserve company
• Demonstrate 100% empathy and compassion
• Explain why decision necessary
• Understand impact on departing people AND remaining ones
• Remaining employees knew those leaving and their families

INTERNATIONAL EXPANSION

China Lessons:
• Nine years of mistakes before getting it right
• Wrong to bring US executives without Chinese understanding
• Pioneering tea-to-coffee behavior change too difficult
• Right move: Chinese CEO (Belinda Wong) with Starbucks knowledge
• Cultural integration essential for success
• Family meeting concept transformed relationship with employees

Transfer Principles:
• Can't simply impose US-centric culture
• Must adapt while maintaining core values
• Local leadership with company knowledge ideal
• Cultural sensibility to employee and consumer critical
• Look for signature events that can transform trajectory
• Innovation in benefits shows understanding of local values

WORK-LIFE BALANCE

Company Responsibility:
• Primary fundamental issue in everyone's minds
• Especially important for young people
• Must define what it means for your company
• Establish standards (not rules) for balance
• Cannot be ignored by leadership

Special Considerations:
• Deep sensitivity to women's responsibilities
• Young mothers need particular support
• Create atmosphere that's never punitive
• Deeply ingrained sensitivity to humane work environment
• Performance-driven through lens of humanity
• Two are not mutually exclusive

Implementation:
• Invest in people to create right balance
• Right atmosphere for family needs
• Never punitive toward family obligations
• Framework: performance-driven + humane

EMOTIONAL INTELLIGENCE IN LEADERSHIP

Appropriate Emotion:
• Passion about work creates emotionally charged environment
• Bringing whole self to difficult work requires emotion
• Crying when apologizing shows genuine feeling
• Emotion demonstrates investment and care

Inappropriate Emotion:
• Shame is never acceptable
• Hitting or degrading people unacceptable
• Yelling at failure destroys self-esteem
• Creating fear rather than inspiration

The Balance:
• High expectations are fine
• Demonstrating your own hard work sets standard
• Compassion and empathy as currency of trust
• Picking people up when they fall, never shaming
• Humanity in how you relate to others
• Establishing personal connection before business

⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS & HOW TO AVOID THEM

PITFALL: Hubris and Entitlement
Warning Signs:
• Success covering up mistakes
• Behavior different from startup hunger
• Acting as if success is deserved, not earned
• Small or large indicators something's wrong

Prevention:
• Ring bell immediately on wrong behavior
• Remind through storytelling about heritage
• Establish that success is not entitlement
• Combat sense of entitlement before it grows
• Cut out disease-like behavior immediately

PITFALL: Hiring Mistakes Lingering Too Long
Warning Signs:
• Six months, nine months, even a year passes
• Not admitting mistake because you made it
• Trying to change person who won't change
• Significant negative impact on company

Prevention:
• Recognize hiring mistakes quickly
• Fail fast—don't wait
• Multiple interviewers reduce initial error
• Cultural fit assessment in early weeks
• Let person go and start over sooner

PITFALL: Chasing Too Many Things
Warning Signs:
• Organization becomes dysfunctional
• Lack of clarity on priorities
• People pulled in multiple directions
• Resources spread too thin

Prevention:
• Limit to 3-5 big audacious goals
• Establish clear organizational priorities
• Manage against defined goals only
• Assess team capacity before new initiatives
• Don't chase adjacent categories too early

PITFALL: Pioneering Instead of Disrupting
Warning Signs:
• Trying to change consumer behavior
• Long timeline before profitability
• Massive resource requirements
• Established players not feeling threatened

Prevention:
• Target categories that already exist
• Find opportunities where large companies are complacent
• Look for consumer willingness to switch
• Calculate time and money for behavior change
• Consider disruption-first approach

PITFALL: Wrong Investors
Warning Signs:
• Values misalignment becomes apparent
• Investor wants different direction in crisis
• Boardroom conflict over strategy
• Pressure to compromise core principles

Prevention:
• Do due diligence before accepting money
• Talk to other entrepreneurs about investor
• Set up hypothetical difficult scenarios
• Get reference checks on fund and representative
• Ensure values alignment in advance

PITFALL: Operating at 20,000 Feet
Warning Signs:
• Surprises in monthly/quarterly reports
• Disconnect from ground-level reality
• Don't know what people think/feel
• Making decisions without full context

Prevention:
• "Get in the mud" regularly
• Have informal conversations throughout organization
• Ask probing questions about business units
• Be very visible, especially in early stages
• Engage with people outside formal settings

PITFALL: Hiding Problems
Warning Signs:
• Concerns about sharing bad news
• Fear of scaring people with truth
• Piecemealing information
• Underestimating people's capacity

Prevention:
• Share 100% truth with transparency
• Give team same information you have
• Lay out challenges with complete granularity
• Trust people to understand complexity
• Ask something of them only after full disclosure

PITFALL: Micromanaging Senior Hires
Warning Signs:
• Always in everyone's business
• Senior people frustrated
• Lack of delegation
• Entrepreneurial behavior with experienced team

Prevention:
• Recognize evolution from startup to growth
• Senior hires don't need/want micromanagement
• Find balance between input and sandbox presence
• Let people with expertise beyond yours operate
• Evolve leadership style as business evolves

PITFALL: Price-Based Competition
Warning Signs:
• Using discounts to compete
• Race to bottom on pricing
• Margin pressure increasing
• Competing with large companies on their terms

Prevention:
• Stay in premium space
• Focus on features and benefits
• Create big wedge of differentiation
• Build sustainable competitive advantage
• Price reflects quality and value, not desperation

PITFALL: Missing Self-Cannibalization Opportunity
Warning Signs:
• Competitors disrupting your core
• Defending existing business too strongly
• Fear of disrupting what works
• Innovation only around edges

Prevention:
• Disrupt own business before others do
• Don't fear cannibalizing core
• Thread innovations into brand equity
• Invest ahead of growth curve
• Break rules and fracture marketplace yourself

🚀 FINAL TAKEAWAYS

THE NON-NEGOTIABLES

1. Values and financial performance must coexist
2. Culture is not optional—it's foundational
3. Success must be earned daily, not assumed
4. People deserve transparency and complete information
5. Share success through equity and comprehensive benefits
6. Build company you and your family are proud of
7. Two constituencies matter equally: people and customers
8. Get in the mud—operate at ground level
9. Make necessary difficult decisions sooner, not later
10. Performance through lens of humanity always

THE MINDSET SHIFTS

FROM: Managing people
TO: Leading people to be part of something larger

FROM: Keeping information close
TO: Sharing 100% truth with transparency

FROM: Quarterly financial focus only
TO: Dual metrics—financial performance AND cultural health

FROM: Reacting to competitors
TO: Leading offensively, controlling own destiny

FROM: Protecting core business
TO: Self-cannibalizing before others do

FROM: Hiring when needed
TO: Building relationships 100 days before funding

FROM: Interviewing for skills only
TO: Hiring for values AND domain expertise

FROM: Individual success
TO: Team sport with shared success

FROM: Pioneering new categories
TO: Disrupting existing categories strategically

FROM: Fear of difficult conversations
TO: Addressing mediocrity immediately

THE ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS

Before Starting:
• Is entrepreneurship truly in my blood?
• Am I willing to sacrifice for this endeavor?
• Can I live without regrets if I don't try?

During Building:
• Will this decision make our people and customers proud?
• Are we chasing too many things?
• Am I getting in the mud or operating at 20,000 feet?
• What am I prioritizing and where can I have most impact?

During Crisis:
• Do I have what it takes to lead through this?
• Can I trust our people with 100% of the truth?
• Are these cuts necessary for the company's survival?
• Am I making decisions sooner rather than later?

During Success:
• Are we earning success daily or feeling entitled?
• What small behaviors indicate something's wrong?
• How do we prevent hubris from entering organization?
• Are we cannibalizing ourselves before others do?

THE ULTIMATE TEST

At the end of each day, week, quarter, and year, ask:

1. Would I want to work for this company?
2. Would my father (or those I respect most) be proud?
3. Are employees saying positive things at dinner tables?
4. Are we building something larger than ourselves?
5. Is success being shared, not hoarded?
6. Did we make people and customers proud today?
7. Are we earning success or assuming we deserve it?
8. Did we address problems immediately, not let them fester?
9. Are we operating with transparency and trust?
10. Would I want my children to work here someday?

If you can answer "yes" to these questions consistently, you're building the right kind of company—one that balances values and profits, one that people are proud to be part of, and one that will endure.
