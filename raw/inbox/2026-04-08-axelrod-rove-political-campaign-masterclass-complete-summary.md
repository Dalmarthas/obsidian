📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass provides a comprehensive guide to running successful political campaigns at all levels, from local races to presidential contests. Taught by two of America's most successful political strategists—David Axelrod (Barack Obama's chief strategist) and Karl Rove (George W. Bush's senior advisor)—the course covers every aspect of modern campaigning. The instructors share decades of combined experience, offering practical frameworks, real-world examples, and strategic insights that have shaped American politics.

The course emphasizes that successful campaigns are built on authentic messaging, thorough research, strategic planning, and disciplined execution. While technology and tactics evolve, fundamental principles remain constant: know your candidate, understand your voters, craft a compelling message, and execute relentlessly. Whether running for school board or president, campaigns must answer one critical question: "Why are you running?" The answer to this question becomes the foundation for everything else—the filter through which all decisions pass and the argument that defines the choice voters face on election day.


🎯 CORE STRATEGIC FRAMEWORK

THE FUNDAMENTAL CAMPAIGN QUESTION
Every successful campaign starts with answering: "Why are you running?" This isn't about wanting to BE something, but wanting to DO something. Candidates who know why they're running outperform those who simply want to hold office. This central purpose becomes:
- The filter for all campaign decisions
- The foundation of your message
- The core argument you present to voters
- The differentiator from your opponent

DEFINING THE CHOICE
Campaigns are battles of definition where you control what choice voters see:
- You're trying to define what the election is about
- If voters focus on your framing, you win
- If they focus on your opponent's framing, you likely lose
- The candidate who successfully frames the choice usually wins

THE MESSAGE TRINITY
Strong messages must be:
• AUTHENTIC - Reflects who the candidate truly is; people detect inauthenticity
• RELEVANT - Addresses issues voters actually care about in their lives
• CONNECTING - Speaks to voters' experiences; they see themselves in the message
• INFERENTIALLY CONTRASTIVE - Highlights qualities your candidate has that opponent lacks

UNDERSTANDING YOUR ELECTORATE
Think of two overlapping circles:
- Circle A: Your base voters (will vote for you regardless)
- Circle B: Opponent's base voters (will never vote for you)
- Overlapping space: Swing voters, weak partisans, true undecideds
- Focus resources on the overlap—these voters determine outcomes


📊 CAMPAIGN RESEARCH & ASSESSMENT

CANDIDATE ASSESSMENT
Deep understanding of your candidate is essential:

Biography & Background:
- Full life story, including struggles and obstacles overcome
- Family history and personal motivations
- Career achievements and public service record
- What drives them—especially experiences that shaped their values
- Their struggles define them (Obama's mother's battle with insurance shaped his healthcare passion)

Strengths & Vulnerabilities:
- Every candidate has both—know them completely
- Don't learn vulnerabilities from opponent's research
- Check basic facts: Do they vote regularly? (Non-voters offend the electorate)
- Review tax records, property records, business history
- Past statements, positions, votes if they've held office
- Personal issues that might emerge (mental health, divorces, arrests)

The "Front Page Test":
Ask candidates: "If this appeared on the front page of the newspaper, would you regret getting in the race?"
- Some candidates decide not to run after this reflection
- Better to know early than be surprised during the campaign
- Get issues out proactively when possible (2000 Bush DUI hurt because it surfaced Thursday before election)

Self-Opposition Research:
- Research your own candidate as thoroughly as opponent will
- Identify possible attack lines and prepare responses
- Sometimes this means releasing information early, on your terms
- Prepare responses for everything that might come up

OPPONENT RESEARCH
Ethical and systematic approach to understanding your competition:

Public Records Research:
- Previous campaigns and public statements
- Voting records if they've held office
- Tax payments, property liens, business records
- Claims they've made about themselves—verify everything
- Court records, legal issues, restraining orders

Monitoring & Tracking:
- Assign someone to monitor opponent continuously
- Look for persistent patterns, not just one-off moments
- Record public appearances and statements
- Most damaging moments come from repeated statements caught on tape
- George Allen's "Macaca" moment was rare; Romney's 47% comment was persistent

Ethical Boundaries:
- Collect information through public means only
- Never impersonate others to gain information
- Don't go over the line—it will backfire
- The manner of collection matters as much as the information itself
- Social media makes information easier to find legitimately

Attack Effectiveness:
Best attacks are those where people say "That's big" without you having to explain why:
- Use opponent's own words when possible (Kerry on Swift Boat ad)
- Have actual people dispute claims (veterans responding to Kerry)
- Don't add artificial air—let facts speak
- Attacks that seem unfair or over-the-top backfire

UNDERSTANDING THE POLITICAL LANDSCAPE
Elections don't occur in a vacuum—analyze the environment:

Historical Patterns:
- Review recent election returns in your district/state
- Identify your party's floor (worst performance) and ceiling (best performance)
- Understand where swing voters concentrate geographically
- Past voting patterns predict future behavior

Current Conditions:
- Overall mood: Right direction or wrong direction?
- Economic conditions: Prosperity or struggle?
- Major events dominating discussion (wars, crises, scandals)
- Demographic changes in the electorate
- Issue salience: What matters most to voters right now?

The Yucca Mountain Example:
In 1992 Nevada Supreme Court race, nuclear waste repository was the biggest issue despite being unlikely to reach the court. Understanding what's salient to voters—even if tangentially related—can win races.

Issue Identification:
- What are the most salient issues in your community?
- How do these issues touch people's lives?
- Which issues can you credibly address?
- Which issues favor you vs. your opponent?


✅ CAMPAIGN PLANNING & ORGANIZATION

THE CAMPAIGN PLAN
A written plan is essential for success:

Core Components:
1. Message and theme (the "why" of the campaign)
2. Target voters identified (who needs to be persuaded and mobilized)
3. Communications strategy (when and how to deliver messages)
4. Timeline with key milestones
5. Budget spread over calendar
6. Fundraising projections and reality checks
7. Resource allocation across activities

Benefits of Planning:
- Forces conscious decisions about what's necessary
- Ensures resources match ambitions
- Creates discipline and prevents reactive campaigning
- Allows you to stay on offense
- Planned campaigns have greater propensity to win
- Provides framework for monitoring and adjustment

Iterative Process:
- Plan the "what" and "when" first
- Then determine the "how" (which costs money)
- Check fundraising reality against spending needs
- Adjust and refine until plan is executable
- Can't plan final weeks 16 months out, but can set working assumptions
- Regular monitoring allows for course corrections

2004 Bush Campaign Example:
- Identified two problems from 2000: lost registration battle and weak GOTV
- Set specific targets for registration in battleground states
- Mapped out volunteer recruitment and GOTV program
- Weekly monitoring reports for 18+ months
- Could redirect resources when showing shortfalls
- Systematic approach led to improved performance

CAMPAIGN STRUCTURE & STAFFING

Essential Roles:
- Campaign Manager: Overall operation, resource allocation, shot caller
- Finance Director/Team: Fundraising is oxygen for the campaign
- Communications Director: Press relations, message discipline
- Field Director: Volunteer organization and voter contact
- Research Director: Opposition and self-research
- Digital/Social Media Director: Online presence and organizing
- Scheduler/Advance: Candidate time management and event planning
- Consultants: Media, polling, mail (but be careful not to over-hire)

Decision-Making Structure:
- Clear lines of authority essential
- Establish who makes what decisions
- Create a core group responsible for strategy
- Avoid making decisions "on the fly"
- Candidate must organize structure while focusing on candidacy
- How you run campaign is first test of leadership ability

Hiring Philosophy:
- Sometimes young and enthusiastic beats experienced
- Look for people who care about candidate, not just cash
- Personal commitment matters for key roles (campaign manager, press secretary)
- These people must be willing to tell candidate hard truths
- Trust is essential in high-pressure moments

Consultant Caution:
- Be careful about hiring too many expensive consultants
- Verify their commitment to your candidate specifically
- Don't get loaded up just because consultants are available
- Smaller races can often succeed with committed amateurs
- Experience matters less than authentic dedication

The Obama 2008 Team:
- Assembled like "Ocean's 11"—people with skills who worked well together
- Strong disagreements on tactics, but mutual regard
- Candidate questioned them, made them defend positions
- But ultimately let them do their jobs
- Cohesive team without leaking or internal sabotage
- Two-year campaign showed executive capacity


📈 TARGETING & VOTER IDENTIFICATION

THE MINIMUM WINNING COALITION
You don't need everyone—just need 50%+1:

Base + Swing Formula:
- Start with base: voters who'll support you regardless (your party's floor)
- Identify ceiling: best your party has performed (maximum potential)
- Target swing voters in between to reach 50%+1
- Concentrate efforts where swing voters cluster

Two Core Problems:
1. PERSUASION: Convincing undecided/weak voters to support you
   - These voters need more information
   - Tend to be weaker in party affiliation
   - May split tickets or switch parties
   
2. MOBILIZATION: Getting your voters to actually vote
   - Supporters who are less likely to turn out
   - Young voters vs. older voters (24-year-old less likely than 65-year-old)
   - Need reminders and assistance to vote
   - Overlap exists between persuasion and mobilization targets

Tools Available:
Both parties have sophisticated data tools:
- Republican: Data Trust
- Democratic: National/state party data systems
- Even small races can access party voter files
- Local races: Get voter file from county registrar
- Files include voting history, demographics, geographic data

MICRO-TARGETING
Advanced voter identification for larger races:

The Process:
1. Start with voter file (basic information known):
   - Name, address, age, gender
   - Voting frequency and history
   - Sometimes race (in some states)

2. Overlay commercial data:
   - Vehicle ownership and type
   - Hunting/fishing licenses
   - Internet purchases
   - Home value and property taxes
   - Utility bills
   - Magazine subscriptions
   - News sources and viewing patterns
   - Cable box viewing data

3. Conduct large survey:
   - 5,000-8,000 respondents (not 600-800)
   - Allows analysis of subgroups
   - Links attitudes to data points

4. Apply analytics:
   - Find patterns in behavior
   - Create predictive models
   - Score voters on multiple dimensions

Three Key Scores (1-100 scale):
- Turnout likelihood: How likely to vote at all
- Support likelihood: How likely to vote for you
- Issue priorities: What motivates this specific voter

Famous Patterns Discovered:
- 2004 Ohio: Pickup truck owners = Bush voters; Volvo owners = Kerry voters
- 2012: NPR listeners = Obama voters
- These patterns allow precise targeting

Tailored Messaging:
Rather than treating all targets as one group, customize by what matters:
- Education voters get education message
- Veterans get veterans issues
- Tax-conscious get fiscal message
- Strong families get social issues message
- Delivered through appropriate channels for each group

Cautions:
- Don't get too narrow—can miss persuadable voters
- Seen campaigns with universe too small, come up short
- More often problem is too small, not too large
- Data is powerful but must be used wisely
- Balance precision with reach

GERRYMANDERED DISTRICTS
Many districts favor one party:
- Doesn't mean other party can't win
- 23 Republicans represent districts Hillary Clinton won
- 17 Democrats represent districts Trump won
- Susan Collins (ME) represents mostly Democratic state
- Heidi Heitkamp (ND) represented mostly Republican state
- Possible to win tilted districts with right circumstances, campaign, and luck
- But some districts are truly impossible (Maxine Waters' LA district for Republicans; Texas Panhandle for Democrats)


🎤 CRAFTING THE MESSAGE

TYPES OF CAMPAIGN MESSAGES

1. Biographical/Introductory:
   - Explains who you are
   - Why you're running
   - Personal story and struggles
   - Authenticates your positions

2. Positive/Agenda:
   - What you want to achieve
   - Your vision and values
   - Policy positions
   - Future-focused

3. Comparative:
   - "I stand for this, they stand for that"
   - Highlights differences
   - Shows why you're better choice
   - Must be fair, relevant, and true

4. Negative/Attack:
   - Goes after opponent's record or statements
   - Most dangerous territory
   - Must follow strict rules
   - Can backfire if done wrong

Every campaign uses a mix of these—no campaign wins on biography alone.

MESSAGE DEVELOPMENT PROCESS

Information Gathering:
- Understand candidate biography deeply
- Know their record completely
- Understand opponent's strengths and weaknesses
- Analyze political landscape and voter concerns
- Identify what's authentic, relevant, and connecting

The Argument Framework:
Think of your campaign as making an argument in a trial:
- What's your opening statement?
- What evidence supports your case?
- What are the key points you must make?
- How do you address opposing arguments?
- What's your closing argument?

Bio Ads as Authenticators:
- Personal stories validate policy positions
- Axelrod client whose father sold everything for his education—made education ad powerful
- Emotional connection matters
- Struggles and obstacles make candidates relatable

THE FRAMING SPOT
Every great campaign has one big spot that crystallizes the message:

Reagan's "Morning in America" (1984):
- Incumbent satisfaction spot
- Highlighted economic progress
- Asked: "Why would we want to return to where we were?"
- Framed choice as past (failure) vs. future (success)
- Targeted specific voter segments effectively

Obama's "Our Moment" (2008):
- Candidate of change vs. Washington insiders
- "The same old Washington textbook campaigns just won't do"
- "I don't want to pit red America against blue America"
- Spoke to voter discontent with status quo
- Positioned Obama as clean break from past

Both spots succeeded because:
- They matched where voters were at that moment
- They crystallized the fundamental choice
- They were authentic to the candidate
- They clearly differentiated from opponent

NEGATIVE ADVERTISING RULES

History of Negative Campaigning:
- Not new—brutal in 19th century (just not on TV)
- Adams vs. Jackson: "Juvenile Indiscretions of General Andrew Jackson from ages 23 to 60"
- Kennedy's devastating Eisenhower ad (1960): "Can you give us an example of a major idea of Nixon's?" / "If you give me a week I might think of one"

Essential Rules for Attacks:
1. Must be FAIR: Not over the line or gratuitous
2. Must be RELEVANT: Connected to job they're seeking
3. Must be TRUE: Backed by solid evidence
4. Must be CREDIBLE: Documentation and sources

The Counter-Punch Principle:
"In politics, the counter punch is stronger than the punch itself"
- Going over the line opens you to devastating response
- Media is like radiation therapy—proper use helps, improper use harms
- Many campaigns won because opponent went overboard

Attack Ad Examples:

Swift Boat Veterans (2004):
- Used Kerry's own words about Vietnam veterans
- Had actual veterans respond on camera
- Devastating because it used his words plus credible validators
- Didn't need artificial pumping up

Romney Business Practices (2012):
- Raised questions about his economic approach
- Fit broader argument about fighting for middle class
- Weren't just random negatives—advanced core message
- Attacks should further your argument, not stand alone

Mistakes & Gaffes:
- Inevitable in campaigns—candidates are human
- Most damaging: those that play into existing stereotype
- Richard M. Daley: "You want a white mayor" (tongue-tied moment)
- Reporters who traveled with him knew his real message, minimized damage
- Obama: "Cling to guns and religion" at fundraiser
- Romney: 47% comment at fundraiser
- Clean up mistakes quickly
- Remember: There is NO off the record anymore
- Everyone is a reporter with a phone
- Someone will record and leak

Modern Reality:
- No private zone in politics
- Fundraisers are not safe spaces
- Cable news, social media multiply every mistake
- Don't get comfortable and say things you don't mean
- Context doesn't protect you


📱 COMMUNICATIONS CHANNELS

TELEVISION ADVERTISING
Still the "nuclear weapon" of politics:

Current State:
- Most powerful for governor, senator, president, many congressional races
- Costs more than ever due to diffuse viewing habits
- But smart campaigns now spend less on TV (20-25% vs. historical norm)
- More efficient targeting reduces waste

Micro-Targeting Revolution:
Traditional rule: Need 1,000 gross rating points to drive ad home
New approach: Target based on actual viewing habits, not assumptions
- Cable boxes track what people actually watch
- Overlay with micro-targeted voter file
- Buy based on individual viewing patterns, not broad demographics

Obama 2012 Example:
- Hired 54 analytics people
- On 64 cable networks vs. Romney's 9
- Advertising on "Andy of Mayberry at 3am" because data showed targets there
- News watchers often already decided—found voters elsewhere
- Cheaper AND more efficient

Cable vs. Network:
- Used to reach most voters through ABC, CBS, NBC
- Now fragmented across hundreds of channels
- Plus streaming services (Hulu, etc.)
- Can't reach entire electorate even with unlimited resources
- Must ensure you're reaching YOUR targets

Addressable Media Future:
- Customize ads for individual viewers
- Send through multiple channels
- Example: Karl Rove gets customized ad based on his viewing/interests
- Technology enabling increasingly precise delivery

RADIO
Effective niche tool:
- Drive time in major markets
- Rural areas where people spend time in cars
- Good for targeting specific constituencies
- Less expensive than television
- Can complement broader media strategy

DIRECT MAIL
"Ironically durable" old technology:

Why Mail Still Works:
1. Expensive/impossible TV markets (New York, Houston)
   - TV reaches 10+ congressional districts beyond yours
   - Mail targets your specific district

2. Household-level targeting:
   - Reach exact voters you want to persuade
   - Customize message to individual households
   - Use data to frame correct message for each recipient

3. Credible information delivery:
   - People thumb through mail
   - Provocative headlines and photos grab attention
   - Can include specific facts and footnotes
   - Space to document claims thoroughly
   - Reprint official documents inside pieces

4. Younger voters consume it too:
   - Misconception it's only for older voters
   - Obama campaign found young voters read mail

Repetition Matters:
- One piece won't convince anyone (like one TV ad)
- Need 6-10 pieces to same household over September-October
- Drive home same narrative with different elements
- Build momentum through repeated contact

Types of Mail by Purpose:
- INTRODUCTION: Letters from candidate (very personal feel)
- AGENDA: Glossy pieces with graphics showing policy positions
- ATTACKS: Must follow fairness rules but can document thoroughly
- CONTRAST: Side-by-side comparisons
- MOBILIZATION: Personalized voting information

Personalization Power:
Modern computing and variable printers allow:
- Individual household names on letters
- Nearest early voting location to their home
- Their specific precinct polling place and hours
- Pre-filled absentee ballot applications
- Phone number to call for rides to polls

The Phil Gramm Mother Letter:
- Sent to everyone 60+ in Texas
- From candidate's mother
- Said: "Phil Graham calls me every Sunday, I live on Social Security, and he won't take it away"
- Got 2,500 Christmas cards sent to special address
- Shows power of personal approach even in mass mail

Testing in Direct Mail:
- Can A/B test different letters
- Split list randomly, send different versions
- Measure which performs better
- Iterative refinement process
- Especially valuable for fundraising mail

DIGITAL & SOCIAL MEDIA

Facebook Strategy:
- Cannot run campaign today without Facebook page
- Need deliberate plan to build followers
- Buy ads to acquire followers
- Activate influencers to share with their lists
- Contact people through multiple pathways

Texas Highway Example:
- Transportation group built 175,000 Facebook followers
- Cost: $6,000 total
- Know district, location of each follower
- Can mobilize on behalf of issues
- Connect to state representatives and senators
- Unbelievable value for organizing

Required Platforms:
- Facebook (essential)
- Instagram (visual storytelling)
- Twitter (influencers, media, activists)
- Snapchat (younger voters)
- Need someone to manage all platforms
- Requires editorial oversight before posting

Social Media Content:
- Someone with candidate taking photos
- Taping candidate comments at events
- Providing raw material constantly
- Professional appearance and messaging
- Quick response capability

The Editorial Function:
Critical to have approval process:
- Prevents retweeting disreputable sources
- Stops provocative/over-the-top content
- Reviews everything before posting
- One bad post can cause major damage
- Must have someone check before "send"

Social Media for Defense:
- When attacked, your network rallies to defense
- Organic defenders more credible than campaign
- Important element of modern campaigns
- Multiplies reach of your responses

Fundraising Revolution:
- Leveled playing field for grassroots
- Small dollar donations easier to collect
- Excites grassroots = money in small amounts
- Seen across campaigns nationwide

Trust Your Network:
- Don't run command-and-control operation
- Unleash people to organize in their communities
- Monitor but don't micromanage
- Let supporters take initiative
- Obama 2008: Gave people permission to organize themselves

Obama 2008 Facebook Success:
- Started with organizational void—no money, no structure
- 80% of Wisconsin students were on Facebook
- Became essential organizing tool
- By campaign end, more friends than any entity on Facebook
- Mobilized volunteers nationwide through platform

Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez Video:
- Unknown when she started
- Created arresting biographical video
- Bound her to community and everyday people
- Shot by her own campaign
- Went viral
- Shows opportunity for low-budget campaigns

The Trump Twitter Phenomenon:
- Understood competition for attention
- Outrageous enough to always capture coverage
- Dominated Republican race by showing up everywhere
- Willing to say provocative things
- News organizations gave him platform
- Used Twitter to hijack news cycles as president
- May be sui generis (one of a kind)
- But others now emulating approach

Challenges of Social Media:
- Added coarseness to public conversation
- People say shocking things without regret
- Twitter feeds and Facebook comments can be crude and disrespectful
- May discourage people from running for office
- May discourage participation in public dialogue
- Tradeoff between empowerment and discourse quality

WEBSITES
Essential infrastructure:
- Important for donors and activists
- Data acquisition tool
- Information source for interested voters
- Journalists review for information
- Opposition researchers examine closely
- Can be used against you in ads
- Must be constantly updated
- Align with current campaign messaging
- No bait-and-switch tactics

Modern Campaign Reality:
- Multiple channels required
- No single channel reaches everyone
- Must coordinate across all platforms
- Consistent message across media
- Proper resource allocation crucial
- Do enough of each to reach targets with force


🎭 PUBLIC APPEARANCES & EVENTS

THE CAMPAIGN LAUNCH
One of few guaranteed attention moments:

Location Matters:
- Choose venue that conveys message
- Obama announced at Old State Capitol in Springfield
  * Where Lincoln prepared for presidency
  * Reference to historic nature of announcement
  * Worked in Illinois for 8 years across state
  * Bellwether representing urban, agricultural, small town, industrial

- 20,000 people in zero-degree weather
  * Message about grassroots movement
  * Showed intensity of support
  * Bought 20,000 hand warmers
  * Had heater behind platform

- For lower offices:
  * Educator: announce in school
  * Businessman: announce in plant or business
  * Insurgent: don't surround yourself with political establishment
  * Need affirmation: surround yourself with supporters and officials

People Around You:
- Convey message through attendees
- Obama Senate announcement: surrounded by elected officials (needed affirmation)
- Presidential announcement: grassroots emphasis
- Match supporters to message you're sending

Launch Speech Structure:
- Must service main message
- Venue conveys message
- People convey message
- Words explain message
- You WILL get coverage this day—make it count

THE STUMP SPEECH
Your core argument delivered repeatedly:

Basic Structure:
- Brief biography
- Fundamental message
- Policy positions
- Comparatives with opponent
- Stories of people you've met (animates speech)
- Closing about what's at stake

Consistency Requirement:
- Don't create new speeches for different areas
- Gun control in one place, gun rights in another = disaster
- You WILL be found out
- Maintain authentic positions everywhere

Freshening the Speech:
- Candidate gets tired of their own speech
- But it's new to each audience
- Add breaking news responses
- Emphasize different points in different markets
- Include local angles when relevant
- Top with timely material

Obama's "Fired Up, Ready to Go":
- Grumpy day in rural South Carolina
- Woman started chant at community center
- Became metaphor and ended every speech
- Organic moments can transform message

Managing Gaffes:
- Candidates make mistakes—inevitable
- Most damaging: play into stereotype or opponent's negative
- Rich Daley: tongue-tied "you want a white mayor"
  * Reporters knew his real message
  * Consistency of message helped
- Clean up mistakes immediately
- Acknowledge and move on

MODERN CAMPAIGN REALITIES

Everyone Is A Reporter:
- No off-the-record moments
- Fundraisers are not private
- Obama: "Cling to guns and religion" leaked
- Romney: 47% comment leaked
- Someone is always recording
- Will show up on social media
- In reporters' inboxes
- On any media outlet
- Warn candidates constantly

Multiple Media Outlets:
- Not just networks and local stations
- Social media sites covering campaigns
- Blogs, podcasts, online news
- Anyone can publish
- Must monitor all sources
- Harder to control narrative

Presidential vs. Local Speeches:
- Presidential races have moments with outsized coverage
- Local races: fewer speeches that make difference
- But stump speech still essential
- Deliver across district/county/state
- Different parts of same message


🚶 GROUND GAME & MOBILIZATION

WHAT IS THE GROUND GAME?
Mobilizing volunteers to contact voters directly:

Primary Functions:
1. Identify potential supporters
2. Generate support among likely groups
3. Persuade undecided voters
4. Motivate supporters to vote

House Parties:
- Volunteer hosts, invites friends and neighbors
- Candidate appears, gives brief talk
- Asks for support, volunteers, contributions
- Multiplies organizing capacity
- Making comeback due to online invitation tools

The Power of Personal Contact:
- Person-to-person more powerful than candidate alone
- Build relationships with voters
- Someone they might know
- Follow-up possible
- Trust built over time

FOUR PHASES OF GROUND GAME

Phase 1: Early Volunteer Recruitment (Spring)
- Target hardcore previous volunteers
- Call previous campaign volunteers
- Knock on doors of primary voters
- Ask: "Would you like to get involved?"
- Build foundation for later phases

Phase 2: Persuasion Contact (Summer/Early Fall)
- Walk programs targeting persuadable voters
- Not just introducing candidate
- Ask questions that help identify supporters
- Gather information for targeting
- Example questions about economy, key issues
- Many will say "undecided"—that's fine, opens dialogue
- Summer good timing: students available on weekends

Phase 3: Mixed Persuasion & Mobilization (September-October)
- Continue reaching persuadables
- Begin turning out identified supporters
- Focus on those less likely to vote without reminder
- Multiple contacts with same voters
- Shift emphasis as election approaches

Phase 4: Pure Mobilization (Final 1-3 Weeks)
- Forget about persuadables
- Focus ONLY on identified supporters who haven't voted
- Phone them
- Knock on their doors
- Mail them
- Provide voting information
- Arrange transportation if needed

VOLUNTEER RECRUITMENT STRATEGIES

Inventory Past Volunteers:
- Review previous campaign volunteer lists
- Contact them for your campaign
- Ask about others who might help
- Build from proven base

Make Everything a Recruitment Opportunity:
- Every event is recruitment event
- Every appearance is recruitment opportunity
- Collect names, addresses, emails, cell phones everywhere
- House parties, community events, public meetings
- Personal recruitment at each interaction

The Card Strategy:
- Candidate carries cards: "Want to volunteer? Fill this out."
- Or: "Give me your business card"
- Capture information constantly
- Follow up systematically

Self-Nominated Personal Precincts:
- Ask people to create their own precinct
- Identify people they'll personally contact
- Give them materials and information
- Empowers supporters to organize friends/family

Technology: Geofencing:
- Set virtual boundary around location
- Example: Republican women's convention with 12,000 delegates
- Anyone with certain apps crossing line gets popup
- "Come to [candidate] reception in Room X"
- "Text to volunteer"
- Captures information automatically
- Texas Railroad Commission example: $2,000 reached thousands

GOAL-SETTING & MONITORING

Set Specific Targets:
- How many households to knock on
- How many phones to ring
- How many volunteers needed
- When activities will occur
- Work backwards from election day

Monitor Progress:
- Track doors knocked vs. target
- Track calls made vs. target
- Track volunteers recruited vs. target
- Track information captured vs. target
- Identify shortfalls early
- Redirect resources as needed

Assign Responsibility:
- Someone in charge of ground game overall
- Different person for each activity type
  * Phone banks
  * Walk programs
  * GOTV activities
- Plot when each activity must begin
- Create monitoring process

ELECTION DAY OPERATIONS

Turnout Monitoring:
- Check turnout in your strong precincts
- Compare to expectations
- Identify people who haven't voted
- In some states, can check off targets as they vote
- Focus final push on non-voters

Real-Time Adjustments:
- Low turnout in strong precinct? Flood with field staff
- Send candidate or surrogate to precinct
- Target specific individuals who haven't appeared

Early Voting Changes Dynamics:
- Many states allow early voting
- Check off early voters from list
- Focus remaining efforts on non-voters
- Obama 2012: Asked Facebook friends to contact non-voters
- Powerful and helpful

The Worst Day:
Election Day is frustrating for strategists:
- Very little you can do to influence outcome
- Spend time asking people what they hear
- Usually just rumors from others
- Exception: Can monitor turnout and mobilize
- Do media reminding people to vote
- But mostly helpless feeling

Exit Polls—Ignore Them:
- Have called every race wrong for five elections
- 2004: Showed Bush losing badly
- Showed impossible demographics (75% female)
- Completely bogus but anxiety-inducing
- With modern data, can build predictive models more accurate

Analytics & Prediction:
- Obama 2012 analytics team
- 36 million voter profiles in battleground states
- Predictive model working all day
- Came within one point nationally
- Accurate state-by-state
- Much more reliable than exit polls


💰 FUNDRAISING & BUDGETING

FUNDRAISING REALITIES
"Without fundraising, none of the rest of it works"

Essential Truth:
- Money is the oxygen of campaigns
- Can't execute plan without resources
- Must be realistic about what can be raised
- Fundraising plan must align with spending plan
- Sometimes must scale back ambitions to match fundraising reality

Types of Fundraising:
1. Small dollar/grassroots (social media, email, direct mail)
2. Major donors (personal solicitation, events)
3. PACs and organizations
4. Self-funding (if candidate has resources)

Small Dollar Revolution:
- Social media enables grassroots fundraising
- Campaigns that excite people at grassroots raise money in small denominations
- Levels playing field
- Seen across campaigns nationwide
- Direct mail still effective for fundraising

Direct Mail Fundraising:
- Can test different letters
- Split list, try different approaches
- Measure results, refine approach
- Learn what works better
- Iterative improvement process

BUDGET DEVELOPMENT

Components of Budget:
- Television/cable advertising
- Radio advertising
- Digital advertising
- Direct mail
- Polling
- Opposition and self-research
- Staff salaries and overhead
- Field operations and GOTV
- Travel and events
- Legal and compliance
- Reserve for unexpected needs

Spread Over Time:
- Not uniform spending throughout
- Build toward election day
- Account for primary if necessary
- Plan for different phases
- Early: organize, introduce, define
- Middle: persuade, compare
- Late: mobilize, close

Iterative Budget Process:
1. Determine what you need to win
2. Cost out the "how" for each component
3. Check against fundraising projections
4. Adjust until realistic
5. Monitor and adapt during campaign

Common Mistake:
Loading up on multiple vehicles but not doing anything well:
- Spreading resources too thin
- Not hitting targets with enough force
- Better to do fewer things effectively
- Make sure aggregate reaches targets
- Ensure enough frequency to penetrate

THE PRIMARY CHALLENGE

Separate Campaign Within Campaign:
- Must win primary to reach general election
- Different electorate (usually smaller)
- More easily identifiable voters
- Often more ideological
- Mail particularly effective

Primary Strategy:
- Don't listen to Nixon: can't run far right/left then move center
- Nobody's paying attention? Wrong—they're watching
- Be authentic throughout
- Figure out which parts of message resonate in primary
- Don't become someone different for general election

Primary Benefits:
- Smaller universe of voters
- Voter files show who votes in primaries
- Can focus on 2 of last 4 or 3 of last 5 primary voters
- Mail reaches them effectively
- Some TV useful but digital and mail more efficient

Variable Primary Types:
- Some states allow independents
- Some have open primaries (show up and vote)
- Can have big swings in turnout
- Plan for different scenarios


🎪 DEBATES & PUBLIC FORUMS

Preparation is Critical:
- Know your positions cold
- Anticipate opponent's attacks
- Prepare responses
- Practice, practice, practice
- Study opponent's past debate performance
- Understand format and rules

Debate Strategy:
- Stay on message
- Answer the question you want to answer, not always the one asked
- Create memorable moments when possible
- Don't just defend—go on offense when appropriate
- Connect answers to broader message

Common Debate Mistakes:
- Getting pulled off message
- Appearing defensive or rattled
- Missing opportunities to contrast
- Going too negative and appearing mean
- Not speaking to camera/audience (only to opponent)

Local Race Debates:
- Often mandatory for newspaper endorsements
- Service clubs and civic organizations host
- Smaller audiences but highly engaged
- Influentials often in attendance
- Can set tone for how race is covered

Post-Debate:
- Spin room matters
- Social media reaction matters
- What journalists write matters
- Be prepared to follow up
- Capitalize on good moments
- Clean up any mistakes quickly


🎯 IDENTIFYING & LEVERAGING INFLUENCERS

WHO ARE INFLUENCERS?
People whose opinions matter to others in the community:

Formal Influencers:
- Elected officials
- Party leaders
- Union leaders (Democrats)
- Small business leaders (Republicans)
- Evangelical leaders (Republicans)
- Community organization leaders
- Ethnic/cultural community leaders

Informal Influencers:
- Respected business owners
- Long-time community members
- Religious leaders
- School board members
- Local radio personalities
- Community activists
- Neighborhood association leaders

The Palestine, Texas Story:
George W. Bush running for governor needed East Texas support:
- Democrat state representative helped (despite party difference)
- Assembled: sheriff, district judge, union leader, corrections officials, Baptist preacher, African American preacher, school board, city council
- All asked questions, heard honest answers
- County judge said: "I think we ought to get behind this boy"
- Carried Palestine because influentials spread word
- Shows power of working across party lines

Two Types of Influencers:
1. People who actually influence (priority)
2. People who think they influence but don't (can't ignore—can hurt you)

Testing Influence:
- Give them something to do
- See if they can deliver
- Verify actual influence vs. perceived influence
- Results show who really matters

ENDORSEMENTS

Do They Matter?
2016 suggests no (Jeb Bush, Scott Walker, Marco Rubio had many, all lost)
But 2016 presidential may not be generalizable:
- Local races: endorsements matter more
- Unknown candidates: matter more
- Respected community leaders: matter
- People who seem above partisan politics: matter
- Compelling personalities in ads: matter

How to Get Endorsements:
1. Ask for them directly
2. Do homework—know what matters to them
3. Find someone they know to introduce you
4. Tell them why you're running
5. Emphasize points conforming to their interests
6. Ask respectfully
7. If they want to think, let them think
8. Ask what they'll be looking for
9. Don't push too hard

Why People Endorse:
- Want to use their influence for good
- Want to help rising political stars
- Want to get things done
- Impressed by candidate personally
- Believe in candidate's message

Deployment Matters:
Do homework before accepting endorsement:
- Could they embarrass you?
- How will you use them?
- Need to script them?

South Carolina 2000 Mistake:
- Prominent veteran introduced Bush
- Wasn't properly prepared
- Spent 5 minutes attacking McCain before introduction
- Took days to recover from
- Could have been avoided with scripting

NEWSPAPER ENDORSEMENTS

Limited But Helpful Impact:
- Rarely decisive
- Sometimes small help
- Bigger help if they go "against type" (unexpected endorsement)
- Always better to have than not

Why They're Useful:
- Older voters pay attention
- Fill news hole
- Provide quotable material advancing your message
- Can share on social media
- Can reference when in that community
- Editorial boards require appearance—good discipline

Process:
- Must appear before editorial board
- Increasingly required debate or forum appearance
- Prepare thoroughly
- Advance your message during meeting
- Board will publish endorsement before election

SPECIAL INTERESTS & MONEY

The Misconception:
Accusation: Members of Congress controlled by special interests
Reality: Usually comes from people on opposite side of issue
Rather than acknowledge legitimate disagreement, they demonize:
- "You can't be against me unless someone paid you off"
- Environmental group: "If you vote against me, you're for dirty water"

The Truth About Influence:
- People support candidates who reflect their views
- NOT people they think they can bend to their views
- Highway contractor supports pro-roads candidate
- Doesn't support anti-roads candidate hoping to convert them
- Support comes from aligned views, not purchase of views

Regional Representation Is Legitimate:
- Represent steel region? Fight for steel companies
- Represent coal region? Fight for coal mines
- This is representation, not corruption
- Your constituents' interests SHOULD influence you

Setting Boundaries:
Some want things you can't/shouldn't give:
- Commitment to support specific bill you oppose
- Promise of future benefits
- Positions you don't believe in

Better to Be Straight:
- Decline requests you can't fulfill
- "I understand where you're coming from, but I can't support that"
- People admire integrity over eagerness to please
- Gain more respect being honest than seeming desperate

The Renewable Energy Example:
- Group wants you to endorse specific bill
- You believe in renewables but think bill goes too far
- Better to decline: "I understand, I'm sympathetic, but I can't support this specific bill"
- Maintain integrity while acknowledging their concerns


🎨 SLOGANS, LOGOS & BRANDING

IMPORTANCE OF SLOGANS
Often neglected but critically important:

Core Principles:
- Should express irreducible core of your message
- Must pass through message filter
- Represents basic argument you want to make
- Shows who you are authentically

Warning Sign:
Multiple slogans during campaign = campaign in distress:
- Shows you don't know who you are
- Trying to adjust to moment or opponent
- Example: Hillary Clinton added "change" when Obama moved in 2008
  * She was Washington establishment
  * Didn't reflect authentic message
  * Didn't work because inauthentic

"YES WE CAN" Story:
Obama 2004 Senate race:
- First ad: biographical about overcoming obstacles
- Ended with: "Now they say we can't change Washington. I'm Barack Obama and I approve this message to say yes, we can."
- After first take, Obama asked: "Is that too corny?"
- Axelrod gave long explanation why it wasn't
- Michelle Obama: "Not corny"
- Obama: "Okay, let's go"
- Became mantra for presidential race

Why "Yes We Can" Worked:
- Inclusive (we, not him)
- Positive and optimistic
- Spoke to people feeling defeated
- Had real meaning
- Not waste of three words
- Became movement rallying cry

Primary night with 53% (expected 38%):
- 2,000 people in hall
- Led them in "Yes We Can" chants
- Beautiful moment
- Represented spirit of people
- Authentic to campaign

LOGOS & VISUAL IDENTITY

The Obama Logo:
Inspiration from 1983:
- Harold Washington, first African American mayor of Chicago
- Blue buttons with sunrise illustration
- "New Day in Chicago"
- Every African American and many whites wore it by election day

Creating Obama Logo:
- Wanted something similar for new way, new beginning
- Sunrise at time when people hungry for different direction
- Didn't even put his name on it
- Iconic pin about movement, not just him
- Unconventional approach

Obama's Reaction: "That looks awfully corporate"
Axelrod: "Too late to change it, let's stick with it"
- Became iconic
- Had message to it
- Not wasted space
- Red, white, blue used in different, fresh, contemporary, hopeful way

Principles for Logos:
- Should convey message
- Not just decoration
- Connect to campaign themes
- Can become movement symbol
- Visual shorthand for what you represent
- Consider unconventional approaches


📡 STAYING ON TRACK

THE RACING CAR METAPHOR
Campaign is like driving a race car:
- Incoming constantly
- Events happening
- Must keep car on track
- Continually return to message
- Remind people what race is about
- Many distractions

Common Causes of Getting Off Track:
1. Reacting to opponent's attack on their turf
2. Breaking news event
3. Revelation about your candidacy
4. Getting spooked by opponent's tactics
5. Forgetting who you are

Obama 2008 Europe Trip:
- Going well, momentum building
- Europe/Middle East tour
- Ended with 250,000 in Berlin
- Overwhelming experience
- McCain used jujitsu: "international celebrity" like Paris Hilton
- Obama campaign got spooked
- Pulled down large events, did smaller events
- Then McCain chose Palin, started getting huge crowds
- Looked like they had momentum
- Adjusted back to large events
- Lesson: Don't let opponent's tactics pull you off brand

KNOWING YOUR BRAND

Paul Simon 1984 Example:
- Rock'em sock'em race against Chuck Percy
- Very acrimonious debate
- Simon: "I'll turn up my hearing aid if you turn up yours"
- Dropped 11 points in tracking
- People thought Simon was nice guy (his brand)
- Snarling attack dog eroded fundamental brand
- Solution: Went positive, direct-to-camera ad
- "I'd rather lose with principle than win by standing for nothing"
- Core message restored support
- Day by day support came back

Key Lesson:
- Never forget who you are
- Never forget fundamental message
- Don't get drawn into something that erodes fundamental assets
- Stay true to brand even under pressure

THE JEREMIAH WRIGHT CRISIS (2008)

Background:
- Obama didn't want to make speech on race
- Campaign discouraged it—didn't need emphasis there
- Then tape surfaced of pastor's incendiary comments
- Greatest hits reel of Reverend Wright's 30 years
- Created brush fire about whether black man could win
- Opponents trying to make him niche candidate
- Impute Wright's views to Obama

The Crisis Moment:
- Obama voting in Senate until 1am
- Didn't grasp intensity of interest initially
- Did cable shows, got questions in his face
- Called Axelrod and Plouffe: "I want to make speech on race. I'm not discussing it. I'm telling you."
- Friday night, wanted speech by Tuesday morning

Writing Under Pressure:
- Campaigning all day Saturday
- Shooting ads Sunday
- Leaving Monday morning for Pennsylvania
- When to write speech?
- Dictated outline to speechwriter Saturday night
- Speechwriter sent draft Sunday night
- Obama started writing at 10pm Sunday, finished at 2-3am Monday
- Campaigning all day Monday
- 9:30pm arrived in Philly
- 2-3am: finished and sent draft

The Speech:
- Powerful, thoughtful statement
- Honest about race in America
- Constitution Center in Philadelphia
- National media descended like buzzards
- Supporters nervously in audience
- Obama to Axelrod: "People will either accept it or they won't. If they don't, I won't be president. That's not the worst thing. At least I'll have said what needs to be said."

What Axelrod Learned:
1. How brilliant Obama was
2. How he operated under pressure
3. Without complaint or sleep, produced the speech
4. There were worse things than losing—not saying what needed to be said
5. That's what people sensed—saw presidential leadership

Result:
- Turned potential peril into triumph
- Looked like president when delivering
- Rose to the moment
- Presidential races are series of tests
- This showed he could be great leader

HANDLING MISTAKES

Inevitable Reality:
- If you don't believe you'll make mistakes, wrong business
- Nature of campaigns: you WILL make mistakes
- It's a long baseball season
- Ups and downs
- Playing under watchful eye of millions
- All think they know better
- Donors, activists, commentators all weigh in

2007 Iowa Time:
- 87 days in Iowa
- Inundated with people saying it's insane
- "It's a big country, why spending so much time there?"
- Answer: Knew if didn't win Iowa, couldn't win race
- But criticism constant and public

Managing Pressure:
- Mistakes happen in public, not private
- Commentators note them
- Supporters note them
- Donors feel proprietary right to comment
- Must be prepared for this reality

Keeping Even Keel:
- Good days and bad days
- Days when you think you've got it won
- Days when thrown for loop
- Days when your own mistakes create problems
- Can't get too high or too low
- Remember fundamental goals
- Remember fundamental message
- Sometimes must scramble back to fundamentals


🏁 CLOSING THE CAMPAIGN

FINAL WEEKS STRATEGY

The Complication:
- Most complicated period of campaign
- Candidate moving rapidly around district/state/nation
- GOTV operation cranking at highest speed
- Tendency to just go through motions and ask for vote
- All activities happening simultaneously

The Critical Need:
Must take step back and restate fundamental premise in new way:
- Make people say: "Aha, I get it"
- "The reason I should vote for him/her is X"
- Difficult after long process
- Feel worn out, spread thin, exhausted
- Think you've said everything already
- Can't come up with new way to say it

But Essential:
- Doesn't matter if state rep or president
- Look at last week/10 days/two weeks
- Figure out: how can we be new, different, fresh?
- Important to remember: voters tuning in for first time
- They're living their lives
- Not entirely engaged until they have to be
- Restate message, freshen it up

Presidential Tools:
- Network 30-minute show (2008—won't happen again)
- Two-minute ads
- Things out of ordinary to get attention
- New formats, new approaches

EARLY VOTING CONSIDERATIONS

Modern Reality:
- Races no longer just election day
- Early voting begins at different times in different states
- Local races: uniform rules
- But must recognize closing weeks begin earlier

Dual Timeline Challenge:
- Need program for early voters
- Need separate program for election day voters
- Texas example: half vote early, starting 20+ days before election
- Can't have "final week" in early October and then nothing
- Need fresh program for early voters
- Then NEW fresh program for final week

Strategy:
1. Aim at early voting audience (fresh and new)
2. Two weeks later: final week audience (fresh and new)
3. Different programs for each
4. Maintain freshness throughout

Media Attention:
- Smaller races may get more attention in final weeks
- When they turn to covering them
- Make most of these moments
- Set up encounters and events
- Issues speeches (though organic events often better)
- Deploy endorsements
- Show momentum

Endorsement Timing:
- What endorsements to deploy in final weeks?
- How to deploy them?
- Where are they meaningful?
- Bespeak momentum

ELECTION DAY

The Worst Day in Politics:
For message people, virtually nothing you can do:
- Can't influence outcome at this point
- Spend time on phone asking what people hear
- Generally rumors from others
- Frustrating time

Operations Side:
- Whole operation churning for GOTV
- Data people monitoring turnout
- Field people checking if your voters coming out
- Comparing to expectations
- Media reminding people to vote

Western Markets:
- Do media into western markets until end
- Remind people polls still open
- Still deliver some message
- But basically helpless feeling

Exit Polls Reality:
- Perfect record: called last five presidential races WRONG
- 2004: Told Bush was losing badly
- By time reached White House, clearly bogus
- 75% women voters (never happened in history)
- Today with data accessibility, better internal tracking possible
- Pick out individuals and precincts
- Cycle information nationwide
- More reliable than exit polls

Obama 2012 Analytics:
- Identified people who said they'd vote for them but hadn't
- Asked Facebook friends to get in touch
- Powerful and helpful

Local Race Monitoring:
- Look where turnout lower than anticipated
- In your precinct of strength
- Flood zone with field staff
- Send candidate or surrogate
- Get people out

Early Voting Impact:
- Watch people off GOTV file as they vote early
- Last 10 days/two weeks
- Only focus on people likely for you who haven't voted
- Makes Election Day more manageable

WINNING & LOSING

Winning Feels Great:
- Nothing like winning presidential contest
- But winning at any level can be really fun
- Obama Iowa caucuses: purest joy in politics
  * Improbable race few thought we'd win
  * Family and friends all working
  * Wife making last calls
  * Eyes locked across hall, both in tears
  * Part of something pure and good
  * Affirmation of why we do the work

Grant Park Moment:
- Everyone remembers Obama's victory speech
- Culmination of incredible journey
- Historic moment
- Validation of years of effort

Losing Sucks:
- It's painful and difficult
- All the effort feels wasted
- Supporters disappointed
- But part of the process

2000 Unusual Win:
- 36-day wait for resolution
- Supreme Court decision
- Karl Rove watching NBC in pajamas at hotel
- Called Bush: "NBC says you won"
- Bush watching CNN: "That's not what CNN saying"
- Both reading opinion from different ends
- Rove: "Mr. President, you're president"
- Bush: "I'm calling Jimmy Baker"
- Not roaring crowd moment
- But they took it

The Value of Bad Moments:
- Painful moments are part of process
- But wonderful moments make it worthwhile
- Feel like you've done something meaningful
- Been part of something larger than yourself
- These moments justify all the hard work
