- Title of Video: Elon Musk on Time Management[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
The video outlines how Elon Musk structures his workdays, emphasizing that the majority of his time is devoted to hands-on engineering and design rather than media or high-level business management. He highlights deep technical immersion in SpaceX and Tesla as the core of his time management approach, delegating most traditional business functions to trusted executives.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Musk splits his time primarily between SpaceX and Tesla, with a smaller recurring block allocated to OpenAI-related work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Roughly 80% of his working time is focused on engineering and design, not on media, PR, or general business activities.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- At SpaceX, he works almost entirely with engineering teams on Falcon 9, Dragon, and interplanetary (Mars) architecture.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- At Tesla, he concentrates on the Model 3, the design studio, and the engineering of both the car and the factory.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Operational business functions (legal, finance, sales) are delegated to leaders like Gwynne Shotwell at SpaceX, allowing him to focus on product and technology.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    

💡 Core Concepts

- **Engineering-centric time allocation**  
    Musk clarifies that about 80% of his time is spent on engineering and design tasks, contradicting the perception that he is mostly a media or pure business figure. This means his calendar is dominated by technical reviews, design decisions, and product development rather than meetings about marketing, PR, or high-level strategy.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- **Deep technical immersion in products**  
    The interviewer notes that Musk knows “every detail of the rocket,” underscoring an expectation of deep, granular understanding of key systems. This level of immersion serves both as credibility with engineering teams and as a way to make high-quality technical decisions quickly.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- **Clear separation of roles: technical vs operational**  
    Musk positions himself primarily as the technical and product leader, while executives like Gwynne Shotwell manage legal, finance, sales, and general operations at SpaceX. This division of labor allows a founder-CEO to protect large blocks of focus time for engineering while ensuring the business still runs effectively.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- **Focus domains at each company**  
    At SpaceX, his attention is on improving Falcon 9, Dragon, and developing a “Mars colonial architecture,” indicating long-term, mission-driven product roadmapping as a core time sink. At Tesla, he focuses on Model 3 design (aesthetics, look and feel) and the engineering of both the car and the manufacturing factory, treating the factory itself as a critical product.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- **Misconceptions about leadership work**  
    Musk flags a common misconception: people assume a CEO’s time is spent on business deals or media, but in his case it is overwhelmingly technical. This suggests an alternative leadership archetype: the **technical architect-CEO** rather than the classic manager-CEO.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

- Prioritize your true leverage zone  
    Identify the activities where your expertise provides outsized leverage (e.g., engineering, design, product) and aim to allocate a large majority of your time to those, similar to Musk’s 80% engineering focus.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Delegate operational responsibilities  
    Assign legal, finance, sales, and general admin work to capable operators so you can maintain long, uninterrupted blocks for deep work on core products or systems.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Maintain deep knowledge of your core product  
    Strive to understand key systems “down to the details” to improve decision quality, earn team trust, and spot issues early, taking Musk’s detailed knowledge of rockets and spacecraft as a model.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Treat infrastructure as a product  
    If you operate in a product or manufacturing environment, treat your production systems (factories, pipelines, internal tools) as important engineering objects worthy of direct leadership attention.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
    
- Design your calendar around build work  
    Instead of filling your days with status meetings and media, structure your schedule so that most hours are spent on creation, design, and problem-solving within your primary domain.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kYA8mxvXZos&list=WL&index=7)
