## 📝 Executive Summary

The video explains how to sound more confident in professional conversations by changing how you speak, not who you are. It focuses on removing verbal “fluff,” controlling vocal tone (especially uptalk), and tightening word choice so your sentences land as clear, firm statements rather than hesitant questions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

- Confidence is communicated largely through delivery: silence, tone, and word choice can instantly change how others perceive your certainty.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Eliminating filler words (“um,” “uh,” “you know,” “I mean”) and replacing them with brief silence makes you sound more composed and thoughtful.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Ending statements with rising tone (uptalk) and phrases like “does that make sense?” or “I just wanted to…” undermines perceived authority.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Simple wording shifts—such as “What are your thoughts?” instead of “Does that make sense?” and removing “just” and adverbs—make your communication more direct and assertive.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- When you do not know an answer, you can still sound confident by taking a pause, drawing on past experience, offering next steps, and explicitly using the word “confident” in your response.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 💡 Core Concepts

## 1. Fluff and Filler Words

The speaker defines “fluff” as filler sounds and phrases like “um,” “uh,” “you know,” “I mean,” and vague openers that create noise without adding meaning. In casual conversations with friends, such fluff is acceptable and part of natural speech, but in professional settings—presentations, meetings, high‑stakes discussions—it dilutes your message and signals uncertainty. He likens fluff to “static” or “too much ice in your drink”: it fills space and waters down the message instead of “serving your words straight.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

The psychological driver is a discomfort with silence; people unconsciously fill gaps with sound to avoid feeling exposed. This habit, however, makes you sound less prepared and less confident, especially when the first response to a question is a string of fillers before any actual content.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 2. Productive Silence and the “Breath First” Rule

The main method for removing fluff is to deliberately replace it with silence. The speaker’s rule of thumb: let your breath be the first “word” after someone asks a question. Instead of “Um, well, it depends…,” you pause, breathe, then respond with a clear sentence such as “I haven’t run across that before; I think it depends on several factors.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

This pause creates an impression of reflection and control rather than panic or scrambling, and it trains you to become comfortable with brief silence instead of reflexively filling it. Over time, this shifts your baseline response style from noisy and vague to concise and deliberate.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 3. Uptalk and Vocal Tone

“Uptalk” is described as ending sentences with a rising pitch, making everything sound like a question: “Yeah, I think we can do that by Friday?” This pattern signals uncertainty and an implicit plea for permission or validation, which undercuts your authority.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

The recommended alternative is to end statements on a lower or flat tone, similar to how you would say “Can you pass the salt.” Dropping the tone at the end makes the statement sound serious, settled, and confident rather than tentative. While the speaker notes uptalk is often more noticeable among women, he emphasizes it affects men as well and isn’t inherently tied to one gender.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 4. Word Choice That Undermines Confidence

Several specific linguistic habits weaken otherwise solid statements:[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

- Ending with “Does that make sense?”
    
    - This phrase often comes from internal insecurity—fear that you were unclear.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - It can unintentionally insult the listener by implying the content might be too complex or that they might not understand something obvious (“The sky is blue today, does that make sense?”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Overusing “just”
    
    - Phrases like “I just wanted to check in” sound hesitant, as if you are apologizing for taking space.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Removing “just” (“I wanted to check in”) projects ownership and intention.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
- Overusing adverbs (especially ending in “-ly”)
    
    - Words like “essentially,” “basically,” and “literally” are described as “comfort words” that add fluff and rarely change the meaning of the sentence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - A sentence like “So essentially, what we have is…” contributes no substance and can be cut entirely.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        

Together, these habits make your communication sound less assertive and more tentative or padded.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 5. Alternative Phrases That Invite Dialogue

Instead of “Does that make sense?”, the speaker recommends switching to invitations for input:[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

- “What are your thoughts?”
    
- “Do you have any questions?”
    
- “Do you have any feedback?”
    

These alternatives assume the listener understood you and invite collaboration rather than questioning their comprehension. They keep the conversation moving forward instead of pausing to ask if the other person is “keeping up.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 6. Handling Questions You Don’t Know How to Answer

Responding confidently when you do not know an answer is framed through a question from a salesperson who feels “deer in the headlights” when challenged. The method has three parts:[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

1. Take a beat
    
    - Resist the urge to reply instantly; pause, breathe, and signal that you are thinking.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
2. Draw on past experience
    
    - Instead of “I don’t know,” say “I haven’t run across that before” or “In my experience, that hasn’t been an issue.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - This reframes you as a seasoned professional referencing a history of situations rather than someone caught blank.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
3. Offer a plan and use the word “confident”
    
    - Follow up with a specific action: “What I’m confident we can do is X, Y, and Z,” or “I’ll confirm that with our IT team and make sure we address your concern.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Explicitly using the word “confident” shapes the listener’s perception and reinforces your own mindset.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        

The combination turns an “I don’t know” moment into a structured, assurance‑focused response.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 🔨 Actionable Tips

1. **Practice silent starts**
    
    - In your next meeting, consciously pause, take a breath, and then answer questions without saying “um” or “uh” first.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - If you catch yourself starting with fluff, stop mid‑sentence, pause, and restart with your actual point.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
2. **Record and review your speech**
    
    - Record a short mock presentation or a sales pitch and count how often you use “um,” “you know,” “just,” and adverbs like “basically.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Choose one filler (for example, “just”) to eliminate for a week, replacing it with either silence or a more concrete verb.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
3. **Train your tone at sentence endings**
    
    - Practice common work statements (“We can deliver by Friday,” “I’ll handle that,” “This is our plan”) while deliberately dropping your tone at the end.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Contrast how they sound with an uptalk version to build awareness of when you accidentally turn statements into questions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
4. **Swap insecure phrases for confident ones**
    
    - Replace “Does that make sense?” with “What are your thoughts?” or “Do you have any questions?” in emails and meetings for the next month.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Edit written communication to remove “just,” “essentially,” “basically,” and “literally” where they do not change the meaning.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
        
5. **Framework for “I don’t know” moments**
    
    - When you’re unsure, apply this template:
        
        - Pause and breathe.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - “I haven’t run across that before. In my experience, [what you have seen].”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - “What I’m confident we can do is [next steps, e.g., follow up with a team, investigate, provide an update].”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)

## 🔍 Additional Insights

- The speaker emphasizes that different standards apply in casual vs. professional contexts; you do not need to “police” your language with friends, but you should intentionally tighten it in high‑stakes situations.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Many of the weak phrases stem from an internal need for reassurance (“Am I making sense?” “Am I bothering you?”), so improving confidence is partly about tolerating the discomfort of silence and directness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The advice is designed to be non‑theoretical: simple micro‑behaviors (pause, lower tone, remove one word, swap one phrase) can be implemented immediately without complex frameworks.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The gender comment on uptalk (that it appears more among women but also among men) may be seen as sensitive; however, the speaker explicitly avoids framing it as exclusively tied to one gender and focuses on the behavior itself.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Ambiguity: the talk does not offer a quantified threshold for when adverbs or “just” are acceptable versus harmful, so some judgment is required to avoid sounding unnaturally blunt in highly relational contexts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ifImJbiLVQ&list=WL&index=21)
