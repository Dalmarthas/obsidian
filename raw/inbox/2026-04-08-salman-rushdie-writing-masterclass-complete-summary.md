SALMAN RUSHDIE MASTERCLASS: THE ART AND CRAFT OF STORYTELLING
Complete Master Summary
================================================================================

📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass represents Salman Rushdie's comprehensive guide to the craft of fiction writing, drawn from over 40 years of literary experience and 20 published books. Rushdie approaches writing as both an art form requiring deep self-knowledge and a learnable craft with specific techniques and skills. The course emphasizes that successful writing comes from understanding who you are as a writer, what story you need to tell, and how to tell it effectively.

The masterclass covers the entire spectrum of novel writing—from initial conception through character development, narrative structure, style, revision, and publication. Rushdie draws extensively on his own work (particularly "Midnight's Children," "The Satanic Verses," and "The Enchantress of Florence") as well as examples from world literature. His approach is international and inclusive, emphasizing that writers can and should write about anything, drawing from diverse cultural traditions and storytelling methods.

A central theme throughout is that writing demands both tremendous discipline and creative freedom. Rushdie warns that success doesn't come easily—he spent 12-13 years writing before achieving success—but offers that the joy of self-expression and the exhilaration of creating something good makes the struggle worthwhile. The course is designed to provide shortcuts and signposts for writers making the journey from reader to author.


🎯 CORE PRINCIPLES OF WRITING

The Foundation of Writing
• Human nature is the great constant across all literature—regardless of time period or culture, people share the same longings, flaws, and ambitions
• Writing is fundamentally an act of imagination that requires deep self-knowledge
• The unexamined life is not worth living—writers must ruthlessly examine themselves to understand what motivates, drives, hinders, and blocks them
• All writing is storytelling, and humans are "the storytelling animal"—we tell stories to understand what kind of creatures we are

The Writer's Commitment
• The only commitment needed is "the commitment of the seat of your pants to the seat of your chair"—sit down and do the work
• Don't get up until you've written something
• Writers are people who finish books—getting to the end is essential
• Write because it comes from a deep place inside you that you really need to express
• If you can find a reason not to write a book, don't write it—write the books you can't avoid writing

The Role of Conflict and Story
• Without conflict, there's no drama—"happiness writes in white ink on a white page"
• Story has always been the engine that drives literature
• The novel form traditionally follows Heraclitus: "Character is destiny"—personality determines fate
• However, modern reality is more complex—our lives are often changed by things beyond our control


📊 THE SIX ESSENTIAL QUESTIONS

Before starting any writing project, answer these six questions:

1. WHOSE STORY ARE YOU TELLING?
   - Be clear about the through line, even in books with many characters
   - It could be one person or two people, but you need clarity
   - This determines everything else about how you approach the book

2. WHAT'S THE STORY?
   - Different writers need different levels of detail before starting
   - Some need complete outlines; others discover as they go
   - Think of it as the difference between composing a symphony (fully notated) and playing jazz (improvisation within structure)
   - Rushdie himself has moved from needing extensive planning to discovering the story daily

3. WHY ARE YOU TELLING THE STORY?
   - Answer personally (something in your life motivates you)
   - Answer politically (external subject you want to explore)
   - If you don't know why, why bother telling it?

4. WHEN IS IT SET?
   - Be specific about the time period
   - The slang, commercials, billboards, daily look and sound change dramatically over time
   - Consider the rate of change—does information change daily, yearly, or remain stable?

5. WHERE DOES IT TAKE PLACE?
   - Location is absolutely crucial—"I can't get the wheels turning properly until I know what the ground is under the wheels"
   - Could be anywhere in the universe for sci-fi
   - Think of place as a character itself

6. HOW ARE YOU GOING TO TELL IT?
   - This is the hardest question—matters of form and language
   - You can mess up a good idea by getting the how question wrong
   - Consider first vs. third person, past vs. present tense, single vs. multiple narrators
   - The telling can be more important than the story itself (e.g., Joyce's "Ulysses")


✍️ STRUCTURE AND PLANNING

Approaches to Structure
• Plot and structure are like the skeleton; characters and events are the flesh
• Some writers (like F. Scott Fitzgerald) need detailed structure before writing
• Others "wing it more"—the only rule is whatever works
• The advantage of structure is knowing what you're doing and where you're going

Traditional vs. Alternative Structures
• Three-act structure exists but is not compulsory—many great works don't follow it
• A novel can exist in a single moment (Joyce's "Ulysses" is one day)
• You don't have to start at the beginning—flashbacks are now commonplace
• "Story has to have a beginning, a middle and an end but not necessarily in that order"
• The novel is an enormously fluid form—it can be anything as long as it's not random

Interconnecting Characters and Destinies
• In epic novels with large casts, individual destinies may not directly connect
• In smaller-framed novels, destinies are absolutely interlocked
• Use mirroring as a device—something happening here echoes something there
• Don't make mirrors exactly the same (too artificial), but create relationships
• Example: Two different mother-daughter conflicts in different parts of the book

Multi-Generational and Historical Novels
• Consider how generations relate to each other (e.g., Thomas Mann's "Buddenbrooks"—each generation weaker than the last)
• For historical novels like "Midnight's Children," mapping characters onto historical events requires architectural planning
• When actions link to historical events, detailed plotting becomes necessary

Finding Your Entry Point
• Most people have false starts—trying different doors until one opens to the story
• Trial and error is normal and necessary
• Keep asking yourself questions about characters and themes
• Talk to yourself on paper—interrogate what you think and why
• These notes are part of the process, not the end product


🎭 CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

The Heart of Character
• Character is traditionally destiny—the kind of personality determines the kind of life
• We all carry damage—broken hearts, feeling unloved, various wounds that shape our actions
• Understand what pushes your character in one direction or another (ambition, dreams, prejudice, need to provide for family)
• Know what they yearn for—the story often centers on whether they achieve their dream

Creating Living Characters
• There's a moment in preparation when the character feels real to you—when they're alive
• Start "listening" to them—have conversations with them in your head
• This creates characters with independent existence, not just puppets
• Readers want to feel Anna Karenina is herself, not just Tolstoy speaking through her

Character Biographies and Preparation
• You can write character biographies if it works for you—but you don't have to
• Rushdie writes haphazard notes: phrases they might use, repeated language, significant moments
• There's a point where you must put preparation aside and just imagine
• You need to feel you have the character within you, not endlessly looking back at notes

Starting with Exterior Elements
• What clothes do they wear? (Hawaiian shirt vs. Yankees shirt reveals character)
• Do they have a big nose? Are they bald?
• How do they speak? What accent?
• Work your way from exterior to interior
• Sometimes flamboyance hides insecurity; sometimes it expresses genuine extroversion

The Three Ways to Reveal Character
1. Through what they say (dialogue reveals who they are)
2. Through interior monologue (what's in their head)
3. Through action (what they do)

• Discrepancy between speech and action reveals hypocrisy (like Tartuffe in Molière's play)
• Hemingway leans heavily on action and dialogue with little interior reflection
• Ralph Ellison's "Invisible Man" is entirely interior—learning character through self-portrait

Distinctive Character Elements
• Begin with what's idiosyncratic and unusual
• Leopold Bloom in "Ulysses" starts: "ate with relish the inner organs of beasts and fowls"
• If a character has green hair, tell us that first (Isabel Allende's "House of the Spirits")
• The unusual detail intrigues readers immediately

Writing Characters Different from Yourself
• Anybody can write about anything—if not, then nobody can write about anything
• To limit writers to only their own identity categories kills the novel form
• Use techniques similar to journalism—go find out, talk to people, do research
• Steal mannerisms, gestures, ways of speaking (rarely steal a whole character)
• Look for historical models that give you permission to invent
• Characters are usually drawn from a collection of bits and pieces, glued together by imagination

Characters with Likability Problems
• Even minor characters shouldn't feel just sketched in—make everyone real
• George Eliot's Middlemarch: the main male character is "the most boring man in the world"—challenging but doable
• Nabokov's "Lolita"—the narrator is vile and knows it, yet readers stay engaged
• Sometimes bad characters are more fun to write—there's freedom in letting yourself be bad on the page
• Good people can have real problems; bad people can be interesting and engaging


🗣️ DIALOGUE AND VOICE

How Characters Speak
• Ask: How do they talk? Do they have an accent?
• Accent reveals social class, race, and region (Mississippi vs. Boston)
• Are they talkative or silent and hard to draw out?
• What vocabulary do they use? Large or limited? This reveals background
• Do they use bad language or resist it?

Dialogue Functions in Story
• Some stories are almost all dialogue (everything revealed through conversation)
• Some stories have little dialogue, but when characters speak, it's very important
• Susan Sontag's "The Way We Live Now"—entire story is people talking about someone with AIDS who never appears
• Melville's "Bartleby"—the character almost only says "I would prefer not to"—enormously condensed but tells you everything

The Differentiation Exercise
• Write a scene without saying who's talking
• Show it to someone and see if they can distinguish characters
• If readers can tell who's speaking without you telling them, you've succeeded
• Each character should speak in a way only they would speak
• Consider: one character didactic (always telling what to do), another timid
• Differentiation is a craft skill you can polish and improve

Speech Rhythms and Patterns
• Some characters speak in long flowing sentences; others in short staccato bursts
• The way they speak tells you about them
• Spoken language has rhythms—find the rhythm that suits each character

Realistic vs. Stylized Dialogue
• If writing slice-of-life realism, make characters sound as they would in real life
• If writing something more invented/fantastical, dialogue can depart from reality too
• Raymond Carver: amazing ear for accurate representation of working-class speech
• Don DeLillo: all characters speak the same way—deliberately flat, unemotional, showing withdrawal of emotional communication
• Alice Walker, Toni Morrison: influenced by biblical tradition, Old Testament, sermons—poetic heightening that people don't actually use but feels authentic

Representing Other Languages in English
• One of the hardest things: representing speech in English that's actually in another language
• Create an "ideolect"—a way of speaking that indicates to readers this is a translation
• Avoid English idioms that sound idiotic coming from non-English speakers
• Make the reader aware characters wouldn't realistically be speaking English


📖 NARRATIVE PERSPECTIVE AND STYLE

First Person vs. Third Person
• First person: reader sees character from the inside, as they see themselves
• Reader must decide if the character's self-presentation is truthful
• First person usually says "trust me, I'm your storyteller"—but the storyteller might be a liar
• Point to discrepancies between how characters describe themselves and how others see them
• First person gives the gift of writing from inside, looking out

• Third person: reader sees character from outside, gradually penetrating deeper layers
• Creates tension between how people speak about themselves vs. what they actually do
• Better for withholding secrets until dramatic moments
• Hemingway uses this with heavy reliance on action and dialogue

Multiple Perspectives
• You can have both subjective (first person) and objective (third person) sections
• Multiple first-person narrators who don't see the story the same way
• Faulkner's "As I Lay Dying": like a relay race, narrator changes almost every chapter—creates collective community view
• Experiment with all approaches to build your toolbox

First Person for Storytelling Feel
• If you want to mimic oral storytelling in print, use first person narration
• The narrator can be playful, go on tangents, tell parenthetical stories
• Can hop between moments of time and place
• Future and past illuminate the present
• This is how we naturally talk to each other
• In third person, it's harder because readers expect a different transaction

Style vs. Voice
• Style: manner in which you adhere to or break rules of classical form
• Voice: your touch as author—what makes writing your own
• Style decisions should suit the character and story
• Voice is somehow you—present in whatever you write
• If you make proper style decisions repeatedly, your voice emerges

Developing Your Voice
• Some writers know their voice from the beginning; others work toward it
• Focus on what will most clearly portray characters and tell the story
• Do this over and over, and your voice will emerge
• Don't think about style as being about you (ego intrudes)
• Think about the most effective way of telling this specific story

The Importance of Rhythm
• Issues of rhythm are absolutely central to meaning
• The way a sentence or paragraph sounds helps convey meaning subliminally
• Readers get it without knowing how—it's a kind of music
• Change word order if it lends better musicality
• Add or subtract words to improve the music in your inner ear

Reading Aloud
• Try reading what you've written aloud and listen to it
• See if it sounds nice
• If it doesn't, work to even it out and give it rhythmic quality
• Writing is a kind of music

Grammar and Sentence Structure
• You must know the rules: grammar, spelling, punctuation, sentence structure
• You don't necessarily have to keep them, but you must know them
• Style is what you decide to do about those rules—play by them, bend them, or break them, and why
• Example: Writing in the voice of a poorly educated person may require ignoring some rules

The Paragraph as Basic Unit
• The paragraph needs internal cohesion
• When a new thought comes in, start a new paragraph
• Some writers abandon paragraphing for effect (García Márquez's "Autumn of the Patriarch"—suggests dictator stops time from moving)
• Every decision needs a reason—know why you're doing it

Learning Classical Style
• Read Strunk and White's "The Elements of Style"
• Master classical style first
• Then ask from project to project if deviation will help reader's understanding

Vocabulary Spectrum
• Nabokov uses very large vocabulary—slightly show-off style displaying erudition
• Dr. Seuss used basic English—ultra-simple language but brilliant musicality
• Find the range that suits your story and characters

Distance vs. Intimacy
• Kafka maintains distance—never cozy with the author
• This distancing technique creates the "Kafkaesque"—mysterious things without explanation
• Other writers create warmth and intimacy
• Choose based on what your story requires


🌟 OPENING LINES AND BEGINNINGS

The Power of the Opening Sentence
• Most books grow out of a single sentence
• Joseph Heller said his books grew from one sentence that gave him "another couple of hundred sentences"
• It's like a key that unlocks the door—an "open sesame"
• The opening shows you how to write the book and tells readers what kind of story it is

Creating the Contract with Readers
• Very early, make a contract with readers: "I'm going to tell you this kind of story"
• If readers like that idea, they'll stick with you
• Never break the contract—it's very annoying to readers
• If the book will be surrealistic or fantastical, establish this immediately
• Don't write a realist novel and suddenly have a spaceship land on page 150 (unless deliberately for shock)
• Start as you mean to go on

Finding the Right Opening
• Think: What kind of book is this? Comedy? Horror? Thriller? Love story? Epic? Surreal?
• Whatever your novel is, the opening should express that
• It should say, "Hello, I'm this kind of story. Read me."
• Spend a lot of time getting the first sentence right

Two Purposes of the Opening
1. Personal to the author: Shows you how to write the book, opens the doorway
2. For the reader: Makes them want to read on

Examples of Powerful Openings
• Toni Morrison's "Beloved": "124 was spiteful"—tells you the house has a life of its own (ghost story)
• Tolstoy's "Anna Karenina": "All happy families are alike. Unhappy families are unhappy in their own way"—grand assertion setting up the particular unhappy family
• Dickens's "A Tale of Two Cities": "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times"—repeated contradictions evoking French Revolution mood
• Rushdie's "Haroun and the Sea of Stories": "There was once in the country of Alifbay a sad city, the saddest of cities..."—establishes tone for a book accessible to both children and adults

The "Midnight's Children" Opening
• Rushdie struggled with tone—too childish, then too adult
• Final opening: "I was born in the city of Bombay once upon a time. No, that won't do..."
• The narrator stammers, doesn't finish sentences—the thing he has to say is so big
• Eventually: "Spell it out, spell it out"—finds courage and it pours out
• The day Rushdie wrote this was "the day I became a writer"—opened the door to the whole book

You'll Know When You Have It
• There's a moment of great excitement when you find the right opening
• It shows you the voice and language of the book
• You'll feel it—the door opens and the book flows from it


🎨 STORYTELLING TRADITIONS AND TECHNIQUES

Learning from Myths and Ancient Stories
• Old stories and myths are a storehouse for learning how to tell new ones
• Myths have endured for thousands of years because they contain truth
• They're "little compressed balls of energy"—tell them in 100 words, but they explode with possibilities
• Greek myths: once religion, now wonderful narratives truthful about human beings

Using Myth as Framework
• Rushdie used Orpheus and Eurydice for "The Ground Beneath Her Feet" (about rock and roll)
• The myth creates a triangle: love, art, death
• Question: Can love, through the power of art, overcome death?
• This little story has occupied writers for hundreds of years

The Arabian Nights (One Thousand and One Nights)
• Most important collection of stories for Rushdie
• Stories originated in India (10th-11th century), moved through Persia to Arabic world, then to French and English
• Not children's stories despite Disney—full of sex and violence
• The frame story: Scheherazade volunteers to marry a king who deflowers and executes a virgin every night
• Her plan: Tell stories and never finish by morning, so the king spares her to hear the ending
• After 1,001 nights, she has civilized him through literature
• Message: A woman uses the power of story to tame a brute and save herself

Scheherazade's Techniques
• Cliffhanger endings—need to know if the person hanging off the cliff survives
• Russian doll structure—story inside story inside story
• Multiple storylines increase complexity and engagement
• These techniques keep audience sitting and interested

Oral Storytelling Traditions
• In India, oral storytelling is still very alive
• Everything the oral storyteller does is designed to keep the audience from walking away
• Strategies are opposite of Creative Writing School advice
• Don't "begin at the beginning, go on till you reach the end, then stop" (Alice in Wonderland's White Rabbit)
• Instead: juggle three or four storylines, intertwining them, with songs and dances between

Multiple Storylines
• Oral tradition shows that complexity is more engaging than simple beginning-to-end narrative
• Modern TV series (like "Game of Thrones") have retaught this lesson—many storylines can be highly engaging
• In written form, be more cautious—jumping too often can become a distraction
• Each section of each story should feel like a complete mini-story with its own arc
• This gives satisfaction like reading a short story, so readers aren't offended by the switch

Example: "The Enchantress of Florence"
• Intertwines storyline in Mughal India with storyline in Renaissance Florence
• Rushdie initially jumped back and forth too much—it was annoying
• After feedback, he revised so each section was longer with its own story arc
• Jumping became less irritating when each section felt complete

Using First Person for Storytelling Effect
• First person narrator can be playful like in real-life storytelling
• Can go on tangents, tell parenthetical stories, hop between times and places
• We understand this as how we naturally talk to each other
• In third person, it's harder—different reader expectations
• If you want to be like a storyteller, try using first person narrator


🌍 SETTING AS CHARACTER

The Importance of Place
• Some novels so relish their setting that the place is portrayed with as much love and care as any character
• The place interacts with people and becomes like a character
• For Rushdie, place is very important because his own place has changed (Bombay, London, New York)
• This gives freedom to set stories in many places but also creates worry about "the ground beneath my feet"

Place Comes First
• Location is absolutely crucial: "I can't get the wheels turning properly until I know what the ground is under the wheels"
• The migrant writer always has a question mark about belonging
• Must literally create the ground the book stands on
• Only then can characters begin moving around in it

What Place Can Be
• Doesn't have to be a city or country
• Can be a street, a café, a bedroom from childhood
• Can be a lost place (due to exile or because the place itself has changed so much)
• Writing can recreate a place that no longer exists in the world

Building the World First
• Balzac's "Eugénie Grandet": describes the town, then the neighborhood, then the house, then her room
• By the time you reach her, he's created the context of her life
• Tolkien: "In a hole in the ground lived a hobbit"—immediately sets up the kind of story
• Set up the kind of story right from the outset

Developing Place Like Character
• Think of place exactly as you think of character
• Be clear about the WHEN of the place (1970s New York is different from today)
• Specifics of the moment: buildings, commercials, billboards, slang
• If it's the future, imagine what it might become
• Things that seem normal now would feel surreal to someone from the past (smartphones, internet)

Writing About Place with Feeling
• Must have strong feeling about the place (can be love or hate)
• Rushdie hated his British boarding school—strong negative feeling could fuel a story
• If you have deep feeling, explore why—what about it makes you feel that way?
• Is it the physicality (mountains, seaside, forest)?
• Is it the people you associate with the place?

The No-Adjectives Exercise
• Choose a place that means a lot to you
• Write 300 words about it without using any adjectives
• Can't say "beautiful," "green," or "ugly"
• This forces you to tell a story about the place instead of being lazy with descriptions
• Telling a story makes readers connect and relate to the place
• Narrative is the best way to get readers to enter a place

Visual Writing About Place
• Important to write about place visually
• Film director Nicholas Roeg: "If somebody comes to see a movie and there's six pictures in their head they'll never forget, that's a good movie"
• Same applies to books—if readers remember half a dozen moments, that's a good book
• Visual is a powerful tool in creating unforgettable moments

Creating Impossible Places
• Italo Calvino's "All at One Point"—world before space and time, everyone squashed together
• An Italian mama says: "If only I had some room, how I'd like to cook noodles for you boys"
• Bang—the universe is created by the first generous impulse
• Make fantastical places as vivid as possible
• Set yourself the task: How can I make a picture in somebody's head?
• Create a world the reader wants to be in


🔮 SURREALISM AND MAGIC REALISM

Beyond Realist Consensus
• Traditional realist novel emerged when there wasn't much argument about the nature of reality
• 18th-19th century writers could assume their vision was shared by readers
• However, that consensus avoided certain things: race, poverty, women's perspectives
• Now reality is very contested—"one man's ceiling is another man's floor," truth vs. fake news

Surrealism as Another Truth
• Realism begins with the premise that we know this is all make-believe anyway
• Therefore, no reason to confine ourselves to things that would happen in real life
• Surrealism is not an escape from the real—it's another way of describing the real
• Sometimes more powerful than naturalistic description because it has more emotional force
• "If the carpet needs to fly, then let the carpet fly"—maybe that's the best way to get where you need to go

Magic Realism vs. Other Terms
• Rushdie objects slightly to the term "magic realism"
• Thinks it belongs to specific Latin American writers (late 50s to 70s) with a particular project
• Kafka could be called magic realist (people turn into bugs)
• Bulgakov's "The Master and Margarita"—devil comes to Moscow
• French surrealism, Latin American magic realism, North American fabulism—all essentially the same thing from different cultural backgrounds
• Common thread: You don't have to have imitative naturalism

When It Works Best
• Works best when it has deep roots in reality
• Author really understands the truth of the world they're writing about
• Uses surrealistic techniques to heighten and intensify that truth
• Sometimes feels more truthful than realism because of that intensification

Western vs. Indian Readings
• When "Midnight's Children" came out, Westerners praised it as fantastic novel
• Indian readers read it almost like a history book—they knew everything had roots in actual events
• Understanding what the author was trying to do is important for full appreciation

The Central Importance of Initial Conception
• If you're going to deviate from naturalistic realism, it must be part of your initial concept
• It's central to the conception because it affects how you write
• Very strange to write realistically and then suddenly change
• Make the decision at the beginning and think about implications
• How far will you go? What rules of realism will you break and why?
• This becomes your agreement with the reader

Examples of Surrealistic Openings
• "The Satanic Verses": "To be born again, first you have to die"—sung while falling from exploding plane
• Two men fall 29,002 feet and land unharmed on a beach
• If you accept this impossibility, you're ready for everything that follows
• Starts with an impossible thing as a signal

Writing Non-Human Characters
• Science fiction, fantasy, genies—what about little green men from Mars?
• Think in species terms: What characterizes their world? How does it differ from ours?
• How do they live among themselves?
• What's their view of human beings? (Do they think us feeble or envy our emotions?)

First Contact Stories
• One of the great forms of science fiction
• Story about first time humans meet alien intelligence
• "Close Encounters of the Third Kind" is a first contact story
• Brilliant story Rushdie uses: aliens look exactly like humans on TV screen but drown in a puddle when they land because they're tiny—scale is deceptive

Making the Ordinary Extraordinary
• "If you have a quiet suburban novel about family life, it becomes much more interesting if a spaceship lands on the lawn"
• Can recommend adding dramatic fantastic elements to otherwise mundane stories

The Danger of Silliness
• Something can go wrong with highly imagined writing—it can seem dumb or indulgent
• Readers are very sharp about this
• Always keep close to emotional truth of moments
• García Márquez example: Blood flows from son's suicide through town to mother's kitchen—she knows he's dead
• Completely surrealistic but emotionally true—we accept it
• When it doesn't work: whimsical without purpose, "somebody turns into a frog, so what"
• Look out for the "so what" response—if it doesn't show truth of characters, it's just done for its own sake


📝 THE WRITING PROCESS

Daily Writing Discipline
• Sit down every day and work
• Don't get up until you've written something
• The commitment is daily practice
• Some writers write standing up, but sitting down is important for most

Discovery vs. Planning
• Early in career, Rushdie needed extensive notes, architecture, plans
• Now he discovers the story more each day
• Some writers benefit from carefully worked-out schemes
• Others benefit from "winging it"—making it up as they go
• Neither is better—find which is your temperament

Starting the Work
• Most people have false starts
• The question of finding the right entry point
• Some doors lead to dead ends; others open to attractive spaces
• Keep trying until you find the door that leads through the story
• Trial and error is normal—Rushdie had 9-12 attempts before finding the right door for most books

Writer's Block and Creative Gaps
• When you finish a book, it's natural to feel you'll never think of another one
• Your head empties out and feels like there's just nothing
• Gaps can be as long as a year without knowing the next project

Reframing Writer's Block
• Don't call it writer's block—call it a very creative phase
• Allows completely free-form thinking
• Sit down each day and think: "What's in my head today?"
• Let something come out without censorship, however fragmentary
• Often the next day it's all garbage—keep going
• Through this open-minded period, something eventually sticks
• You wake up still thinking about it day after day
• Ask yourself: Why am I still thinking about that? What about it won't I throw away?
• Out of that germ, the next book often comes

The Writing State
• When alone with piece of paper or computer screen, your mind works differently
• The act of writing opens the mind, opens windows in the head
• Lets in stuff not normally available to you
• Out of that, you make your story

Moving to New Challenges
• Always look for something you haven't done before
• Don't spend your life repeating what worked once
• Each time you write, try to take a step in a direction you haven't been before
• Ask: What can I do that isn't just repeating what I've done?
• Example: Rushdie challenged himself to write "The Golden House" with a narrator unlike him in age, race, and origin

Holding the Book in Your Head
• With big books and many characters, you hold it in your head like a spider's web
• All the people connect to each other—you know who's doing what to whom
• This stays there all the time you're writing
• When finishing, at a certain moment the spider's web disappears
• At that point, it becomes dangerous to revise—like brain surgery blindfolded
• There's a moment where you think: "Whatever this book is, whatever its faults, this is what it is. I can't touch it anymore."


✏️ REVISION AND EDITING

The Central Truth
• "All writing is rewriting"—it's very rare that first attempts don't need work
• What's characteristic is that it needs work, rework, and more work
• The hardest thing is making something out of nothing—the terror of the blank page

Getting Something Down
• Important to get something down, however rough
• Once you have something, another part of your mind kicks in—your critical imagination
• Then you look at what's actually there and think: What's wrong with this?
• This becomes a process of improvement

Being Your Own Best Editor
• Look at two things when examining your text:
  1. Are there superfluous things you don't need to say? (Would be better without them)
  2. Are there things you haven't said that need to be said? (Would improve text if added)
• Use these two tests on every page for objective reading

Constant Revision
• Revise language constantly
• Sometimes revise the actual action—maybe something planned isn't satisfying
• Need to find a different way
• Characters can change—important characters become smaller, minor characters force themselves to grow
• Like when characters grow in the book, it feels like the book has organic life
• Watch language, form, and story all the time—easy for a book to go off the rails

Daily Review Process
• After a day's writing, step away (have dinner)
• Always read what you wrote that day again
• Two reasons:
  1. Maybe see something that needs fixing—try to fix it
  2. Puts the book in your head for when you wake up in the morning to go forward

The Power of Completion
• Just the act of getting to the end immediately shows you what you needed to do before
• You think: "I needed much more of this and much less of that. This is wrong, but that is right."
• Suddenly you begin to see the book because you have the whole thing
• Good case for pressing on and getting something down, then stepping back to fix

Stepping Away
• After getting something down, take a break
• Step away from the work, then come back and look at it afresh
• Distance provides fresh perspective

Perfection and Imperfection
• Randall Jarrell: "A novel is a long piece of writing that has something wrong with it"
• Probably true—if writing 80,000-200,000 words, impossible for every word to be perfect
• Perfection is a pipe dream
• Try to make it as perfect as possible while understanding there will be imperfection
• Everyone suffers from this

Attitude of Dissatisfaction
• Good to have attitude of dissatisfaction with your work
• Look at every sentence all the time
• Do I need that sentence? Is that the right word?
• Continue this long after editorial process is over, during book production
• Almost every day, read over and over trying to see tiny improvements
• Could be a comma you don't need, a paragraph to break into two, a sentence to add
• Sometimes a tiny tweak makes substantial difference to how a paragraph is read

The Final Galleys
• Process continues until you get first galleys
• Publishers say: "This is your last chance to touch the book"
• That read is very important—truly the last chance
• After that, it is what it is
• Worth going over and over and over until you can't anymore, then live with it

Knowing When It's Bad
• Should be more or less inbuilt
• Most people good at writing can see missteps and false notes
• If you can't tell when it's not good, you have a problem—because then you can't tell when it is good
• Only trick: Read a lot and know what good writing is
• Get that knowledge through reading
• Then be ruthless with yourself

Hemingway's Advice
• "Every writer needs a good shit detector"
• If you don't know when it's bad, you don't know when it's good


👥 FEEDBACK AND SHARING

The Embarrassment Test
• If someone asks to see your work and your reaction is embarrassment, it's not ready
• Embarrassment is an infallible test
• We're all trying to write things we want other people to read
• If embarrassed to show it, you know in your heart it ain't there yet
• When the embarrassment reflex disappears, the moment has come for other eyes

Different Approaches to Sharing
• Some writers show people things constantly—almost daily to partners, agents, editors
• Others hug work to themselves until much more complete
• This is a temperamental issue—work out what suits you
• John Irving's bravery: did a reading of a first draft and asked audience to tell him all the problems
• Rushdie finds this terrifyingly brave—he's too nervous for that

When to Finish Solo Work
• Rushdie takes work as far as he can by himself without showing it
• Learn to see the moment when you're not making things better—just pushing things around making them different
• At that moment, that's "finishing"
• Then become very interested in what others have to say

The Miraculous Effect of Sharing
• Something miraculous happens when the book leaves your desk
• Just knowing other people's eyes are on the book makes you look at it differently
• Before they've said anything, this alone can help you see things

What Kind of Readers to Seek
• Most important: Know who will tell you the absolute truth
• Don't need someone to pat you on the back and say you're wonderful (not helpful, even if true)
• Need people who say: "This is exactly what I felt when reading it"
• "Over here you lost my attention. Over here you got it back."
• When you're so immersed and close, difficult to have objectivity of another pair of eyes
• Doesn't have to be publisher or agent—just good readers you know who give truthful responses

How to Present Work for Feedback
• Say nothing at all—tell them nothing
• Want to see how they read the book without trying to influence them
• The book must speak for itself
• When someone buys a book, it doesn't come with the writer's explanation
• If you give the book, just let them read it and see their take
• Can't help the book by telling early readers what they should see
• Question is whether they do see what you want them to see
• If they don't, how can you improve the text so they do?

Processing Editorial Comments
• Take notes and comments seriously and really think about them
• Remember: This is your book, and you know it better than anyone
• If it doesn't feel right, don't do it
• Very few editors force changes just because they think so
• If you can explain why you don't want to do it, you can stick to your guns

Rushdie's Experience with Feedback
• For last 2-3 books, probably 75% of editor's notes led him to do something
• Sometimes small changes, sometimes larger
• About 25% he thought: "No, I don't want to do that"
• Everything that comes needs serious consideration
• But you have the right to say no

The Role of a Good Editor
• Great publishing editors put their finger on where problem points are
• Don't tell you how to fix it—just say "Here I've got a problem, see what you can do"
• Then you go away and fix it
• Usually not helpful if someone tells you exactly how to do it
• Having someone point to problems along the way can be very helpful

Not Adversarial
• Don't think of feedback as adversarial relationship
• Only thing that matters is getting the book as good as it can be
• Once published, it stays in that form for a very long time
• Best thing: Work with editor collaboratively toward common goal
• Book becomes slightly collaborative at the very end after being solitary most of its life

Being Open While Protective
• Difficult because you feel possessive about work you've made
• Feel proud and sometimes defensive
• Try to be open to conversation
• Important: The person should be basically in sympathy with what you're trying to do
• Don't want someone who wants it to be a different kind of book (not helpful)
• If you're both pulling in the same direction, trying to make the existing book as good as it can be, readings are very helpful

Example: Removing a Character
• After "Midnight's Children" was accepted, reader suggested removing a redundant character
• Salim was telling story to Padma and also a second audience figure
• Initial defensive response: "My book!"
• Two or three other people said the same thing
• Rushdie thought: "I'll try removing this character. If it's very difficult, then I'm right and they're wrong."
• The character fell out in 24 hours—showed they had been right
• Published book much better for having lost that character

Doubt and Fear
• With "Midnight's Children," Rushdie thought: "If this doesn't work, maybe I don't know what a good book is, and maybe I should do something else"
• "Hollywood or bust"—fortunately it wasn't bust
• Full of doubt all the way through writing and when finished
• Immense book (quarter million words, 600 pages) with no reputation as writer
• Previous novel hadn't done well
• Completely afraid the book would have no future
• One part pleased with it, another part saying "Who's going to want this?"
• Full of doubt about whether it would even find a publisher

The Bad Reader's Report
• First reader's report at Jonathan Cape was very negative and short
• "This novel is a fat ramble through the author's mind. He should concentrate on short stories till he has mastered the novel form."
• About as bad as you can get
• Fortunately, editor sent for second reader's report, which was very favorable
• Editors decided to go with favorable report
• Rushdie never knew about this until after publication and Booker Prize win
• Fortunate he found out later—would have added to his absolute doubt

Working with Agents
• These days, most long-term relationship is with literary agent, not publishing editor
• Publishing has become a world where people move jobs constantly
• May not have same editor for book after book (lucky if you do)
• Probably have same agent for lot of your life as writer
• Agent may be the person to have serious conversations with

When Book Doesn't Find Home Easily
• Agent and you can sit down and think about what might need to be done
• Agent might say: "There are a lot of publishers. You only need one who's going to get it. Let's keep going."
• Many writers turned down by 20-25 publishers, then found one, and book was very successful
• Harry Potter famously turned down by many—look what happened
• If you end up with one place where people get what you've done, that's all you need
• Or you might agree there's something you need to fix
• If many publishers come back saying same thing about a problem, maybe need to think about it


🔍 RESEARCH AND WORLDVIEW

Understanding Your Worldview
• All of us create a picture of the world for ourselves
• Partly created by parents, teachers, or other influences
• We think "the world is like this" and live in that picture
• That's a kind of sanity—if our picture breaks because the world changes too much, it can be crazy-making
• Try to make a literal picture of the world in your mind
• What elements would you put in it? What's in front, background, corners?

Learning from Art
• To uncover how ordinary people lived in the past, look at great art
• Often the center shows the main subject (courtly lady, wealthy patron, politician)
• But in the corners of the frame, you see everyday people
• Guy with cart selling bread, store selling vegetables
• Ordinary life is in the corners of the frame—very helpful to see

The Importance of Worldview
• Great writers have a world view out of which their work comes
• They have a way of thinking about people and societies that's their own
• Nobody else can write like García Márquez—you know from one sentence who the author is
• True of Hemingway, Fitzgerald, Toni Morrison, George Eliot
• What characterizes genius is that nobody else can do it
• They write the way they write because they are the people they are

Avoiding Generic Writing
• Writing that feels generic doesn't have distinctive worldview
• Either sounds clearly like somebody else (overly influenced)
• Or feels like 100 people could have written it
• "I Could Do That" reaction is very bad
• More personal and idiosyncratic it is, more it will feel authentically yours

Self-Knowledge as Foundation
• To be a really good writer, you need unusual amount of self-knowledge
• Really know who you are, what makes you tick, and why
• Socrates: "The unexamined life is not worth living"
• Writers deeply examine their lives—ruthlessly, not easy on themselves
• Must go very deep, like personal psychoanalysis
• Examine life until you understand what motivates, drives, hinders, blocks you
• Write from that knowledge and you'll write something that's yours and nobody else's

Write What You Know—But Expand What You Know
• "Write what you know" is good advice
• But sometimes you need to increase what you know to write well
• Means getting out of your chair, out of your apartment
• Going into rooms you don't normally go into, asking questions
• Listening to people rather than deciding how they would be
• Discovering, in other words

Learning from Newspapers
• Dostoevsky obsessively read crime pages—every book is a murder story
• Often arising from something in newspapers
• "Student murders moneylender"—germ of "Crime and Punishment"
• From that to the masterpiece is a big step, but that's the germ

Broadening Horizons Through Travel
• Grow as person and writer by broadening horizons
• Travel (doesn't have to be international)
• Journey from Manhattan to Nebraska—get out of comfort zone
• Go by yourself—if you take someone, you bring your world along
• Go alone, you're exposed to the world you're in—interaction stranger and more surprising

Research as Journey
• If historical novels attract you, find a period that interests you and dive in
• Research is a kind of journey all its own
• Can find things you'd never dream of—better than anything you could make up
• Being a writer is a long job—nobody fires you, don't have to retire
• If you want long life as writer, keep discovering
• Finding things out is very good way to have that life

Historical Novel Research: "The Enchantress of Florence"
• Most directly historical novel—deals with 400 years ago
• L.P. Hartley: "The past is another country. They do things differently there."
• People don't behave or think like in 21st century
• Research about social circumstances, political and cultural events—hard work but straightforward
• Hard bit: Imagine yourself into thought processes of people who don't think like you
• Pre-Freudian, much earlier—cast of mind very alien
• Then humanize them, give them life on page readers can identify with

Finding the Interior Truth
• Can do mechanical research—how people dressed, how court was arranged
• But finding interior truth—that's what takes work
• Emperor Akbar: began thinking in humanist way about individuals and their rights (modern thoughts)
• But also completely absolute ruler—everybody does what he says or else
• That contradiction made him interesting—that's what you're looking for

Historical Figures: Finding Your Entry Point
• When writing about major historical figure, you're not the first
• Enormous amount exists about figures like Akbar
• Must find your version—the one that speaks to you
• Willing to admit this may be more your invention than psychological reality
• But that invention gives freedom to write in a way not borrowed from earlier versions
• True whether historical novel or contemporary: How do you write your thing rather than rewrite somebody else's?

Internet vs. Libraries
• Google search goes broadly but not deep
• Libraries and books still best sources for best stuff
• Especially for history that isn't recent—internet not very good at old stuff
• For 16th century, won't find really interesting stuff on internet
• New York Public Library was Rushdie's biggest resource for "Enchantress of Florence"
• Order books from whatever source and read them

Finding How Ordinary People Lived
• Relatively easy to find historical events, who was king, what battles fought
• Much harder to find out how ordinary people lived—requires digging
• That material is in books never digitized, not on internet
• Have to go back to printed word—always most useful

When to Put Research Aside
• Point where you must put research aside and imagine
• No matter how exhaustively you've researched, you have to make it up
• Sometimes can make up things that turn out to be true
• Imagination can stumble on truth without expertise

Ian McEwen Example
• McEwen writing novel where someone chops up body to carry in suitcases
• Friend (medicine lecturer) offered to let him watch dissection
• On the day, little voice said "don't go"—so he didn't
• Made up the chopping up of body
• Surgeon friend read it and asked: "How did you know all that? How did you know where to chop?"
• McEwen: "I just made it up"—he imagined it

Imagination as Ultimate Weapon
• If you're lucky, you can imagine the truth
• Imagination is best, first, and last weapon
• Whole act of writing depends on imagination
• No matter how much research, if can't imagine yourself into that world, into skin of that world, book won't work


🌐 READING GLOBALLY

Breaking Parochial Reading Habits
• Tendency to be parochial in reading—want books about people like us in places like ours
• Natural to want to read books we recognize as variations of our own lives
• But if we only do that, we deprive ourselves of enormous riches
• Books not originally in English have been at least as important to Rushdie as English books

Expanding the Canon
• Question of canon: Are there great books we should all know?
• Answer is yes, but the way canon has been defined has been very narrow
• Yes, read Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Proust, Joyce, Kafka
• But traditional Western canon excludes much writing
• Excludes writing by non-white writers and non-English language writers
• Argument for making another kind of canon—more internationalist and diverse
• A genuinely global literary canon

Rushdie's Reading Journey
• Good fortune never to have studied English literature
• Never had canon imposed by professors
• Didn't have to worship at shrine of D.H. Lawrence
• Read in own idiosyncratic way—suddenly interested in Russian literature, read everything
• Then swing over to Latin Americans
• Adventures in literature as way of self-education

We Live in Age of Great Translators
• Wonderful renderings of books into English from many languages
• The book that unlocks something in your head might be originally in Russian, Japanese, Hungarian
• Worth going on voyages of discovery
• You never know what will show you the way

Examples of Global Influences

Indian Writers:
• Anita Desai: heir of Jane Austen, writing in India
• Like Austen: formidable women limited by cultural constraints
• Strong female characters pushing against boundaries (like "Clear Light of Day")

Japanese Writers:
• Junichiro Tanizaki: writes about transition from traditional to modern Japan
• Tensions and frictions inside families and communities during transition
• Helped Rushdie think about how traditional society changes to modern
• Same subject in different place (India vs. Japan)

Latin American Literature:
• Important to Rushdie though he'd never been to South America when he started reading
• Found things familiar from his South Asian experience
• Both worlds: colonial history (Spanish vs. British), disparity between wealth and poverty
• Conflict between city reality and rural life, armies interfering in politics, history of dictators
• Reading these writers: "This is quite like where I come from, but translated into Spanish"
• Magic realists say they were inspired by "Thousand and One Nights"
• Reading Spanish translation of Arabic stories helped develop their voices
• Gave Rushdie fellow feeling and ways of thinking about his own world

The Circle of Influence
• Interesting how influences circle the world
• Latin Americans inspired by Arabic stories (via Spanish translation)
• Those Latin American writers then inspired writers like Rushdie
• Writing from different cultures can illuminate your own culture in unexpected ways


💡 PRACTICAL TIPS AND EXERCISES

Seven Essential Tips

1. Maximalist vs. Minimalist
• Two ways of writing really well:
  - Minimalist: Very tight focus on one beautiful strand of story—like one hair from goddess of literature, turn it in light
  - Maximalist: Scoop up enormous armfuls of world, try to fit as much as possible—"loose baggy monsters" (Henry James)
• Middle ground less interesting
• Think which is your temperament
• Write a pure, simple "something" book or an "everything" book
• Rushdie usually more maximalist, but admires minimalists like W.G. Sebald

2. Planning vs. Improvising
• Are you better served by planning carefully or improvising?
• No right answer—only what's right for you
• Some writers benefit from carefully worked-out scheme
• Others benefit from winging it, making up as they go
• Think about which is temperamentally closest to your nature
• Stick to that path

3. Write What Feels Deeply Necessary
• Write because it comes from deep place inside you that you really need to express
• Don't write just to make money or be famous (terrible reasons)
• World is full of books—nobody needs your book
• Make sure it feels essential to you to write it
• Make sure you can't not write it
• If you can find reason not to write a book, don't write it
• Write books you can't avoid writing

4. Do Something Dangerous
• Artistically dangerous, not physically dangerous
• Take risks
• Hemingway's bullfighting book: "The great matador works closest to the bull"
• If bull brushes your thighs going past, every move must be perfect or you get a horn in your side
• If bull is far away, not dangerous but also not exciting
• Find way of taking risk that means book has high risk of not coming off
• But if it does come off, it's extraordinary

5. The Commitment to Sit Down
• Hemingway on commitment: "The only commitment a writer needs is the commitment of the seat of his pants to the seat of his chair"
• Just sit down and do your work
• Sit down and don't get up until you've written something
• That commitment to sit there and not get up till you've done work is essential

6. When There's a Problem, Cut It Out
• Told by film director: If you've identified a problem in storyline, solution almost always is to cut it out
• Desire to get in there and fix it
• But often easier to think: "That doesn't work. Take that out. Let's get on."
• Might find book is clarified if you're willing to discard what's not working

7. Writers Finish Books
• Writers are people who finish books—they get to the end
• May seem like truism, but many people have book in head but never get it written
• Don't stop till you get to the end
• When you finish manuscript, you can immediately see what it needs
• To have whole thing in front of you suddenly makes clear: need less here, more there, explain that more, gone on too long there
• Make sure you finish the book—then you will be a writer


Practical Exercises

The Differentiation Exercise:
• Write a scene without saying who's talking
• Show it to someone—see if they can distinguish characters even if just called A, B, C
• If you've really understood how each speaks, reader will know without you telling them
• Only that character would speak like that

The No-Adjectives Exercise:
• Choose a place that means a lot to you
• Write 300 words about it without using any adjectives
• Can't say "beautiful," "green," "ugly"
• Forces you to tell a story about the place
• Cutting adjectives forces you into narrative
• Shows that telling a story is best way to get reader to enter a place

The Opposite Emotional Exercise:
• Try writing a love scene two ways:
  1. Very emotional, lyrical, emotion on surface
  2. Dispassionately, unemotionally—same event, different tone
• See which works better
• Can be effective not to write about something in the most obvious way
• Writing against the grain can be interesting

The Letter to a Friend Exercise:
• Write letters to yourself about the book as if talking to a friend
• Friend asks: "What are you writing about? What do you want to write about?"
• When talking to people, we try to be interesting or they'll say they've had enough
• See if you've got story into shape by imagining telling it to someone orally
• Choose a friend in your mind and talk to them about it
• Do this on paper and see what you think


Developing Observational Skills

Training Your Eye:
• Be a good noticer—set yourself the task of noticing
• Amazing how much you'll begin to notice
• Look at corners of vision, not just straight ahead
• When walking down street, we're often lost in thoughts or distracted
• Set yourself task of noticing—will be surprised by how much you see

Training Your Ear:
• Develop your ear for how people talk
• These days easier—everyone walking down street talking to themselves on phone
• They're talking as if nobody's listening
• Listen to natural world too (like Calvino's character listening to rhythms of bird tweets)
• Hear rhythms in speech—very revealing about people

Always Have a Notebook:
• Always have something to write in
• Rushdie uses phone to make notes, transfers later
• If you don't write things down at the moment, you'll never remember them
• Form the habit of making record of observations

De-familiarizing the Familiar:
• Try to make familiar unfamiliar
• In your own home, you know where doorknob is—don't really look at it
• Try to see it as if never been there before
• What would you see? What would first impression be?
• What would things tell you about person who lives here?
• Pictures? Bookshelves? What does this reveal?

Walking in Cities:
• Walking very good for observation
• Walk in cities as adventure
• See life of city as it moves around you
• Who's there? Where did they come from? What's their story?
• Metropolis is magnet—people from everywhere
• Look at people and think: What's their life? What's their picture of the world?

The Martian Making Toast Exercise:
• Early in career, Rushdie given test question:
• Imagine meeting a Martian who speaks English but doesn't know what bread is
• Explain in 100 words or less how to make toast
• Very hard—so many assumptions about bread, toast, electricity
• Forces you to see familiar things as alien would see them
• Try to make yourself the alien walking through the world


📚 LESSON-BY-LESSON INDEX

Lesson 01 - Meet Your Instructor
• Rushdie's journey: 12-13 years before success, published 20 books
• Warning: Doesn't come easily, need absolute determination
• Joy: When you write something good, incredible exhilaration
• We are "the storytelling animal"
• Drawing on cultures from India, Britain, United States

Lesson 02 - Determine How to Tell Your Story
• The six essential questions (whose, what, why, when, where, how)
• Human nature is the great constant across all literature
• Story is the engine—the "room" in a big car
• Without conflict, no drama—happiness writes in white ink
• Different writers need different levels of planning (symphony vs. jazz)

Lesson 03 - Flesh Out Your Story's Structure
• Plot and structure are skeleton; characters and events are flesh
• Some writers need detailed architecture; others wing it more
• Three-act structure not compulsory—novel can be anything as long as not random
• Novels can start in middle, use flashbacks, have non-linear structure
• Process: Trial and error to find right entry point
• Work notes from "The Satanic Verses" show talking to himself, interrogating ideas

Lesson 04 - Opening Lines With Power
• Most books grow out of a single sentence (Joseph Heller's insight)
• Opening sentence opens gateway through which storyline flows
• Make contract with reader early: "I'm going to tell you this kind of story"
• Start as you mean to go on—establish tone immediately
• Examples: Morrison's "Beloved," Tolstoy's "Anna Karenina," Rushdie's "Midnight's Children"
• The day you find the right opening can be "the day you become a writer"

Lesson 05 - Drawing From Storytelling Traditions
• Myths and ancient stories are storehouse for learning new ways
• They endure because they contain truth—"compressed balls of energy"
• Arabian Nights: Scheherazade uses story power to civilize brute king
• Oral storytelling: Multiple storylines, cliffhangers, story-within-story
• Modern TV series have retaught lesson of complexity being engaging
• First person narration allows playfulness of oral storyteller

Lesson 06 - Conceiving Characters
• Character is traditionally destiny (Heraclitus)
• Modern world: Sometimes our character is not our fate
• Sometimes base central character on variation of yourself for confidence
• Once character comes to life, must respect who they are
• Anybody can write about anything—otherwise nobody can write about anything
• Characters drawn from collection of bits and pieces, glued by imagination
• Even minor characters should feel fully there, not just sketched in
• Writing unsympathetic or bad characters can be more fun—there's freedom in it

Lesson 07 - Bringing Characters to Life
• When characters feel real to you, they feel alive—start listening to them
• You're in conversation with them: What do they need to be fully themselves?
• Can write character biography if it works, but there's a point to put it aside
• Understand the interiority: What has damaged them? What do they yearn for?
• Think of exterior things (clothes, appearance, speech) and work inside
• Flamboyance can hide insecurity or express genuine extroversion
• Before writing, you should be able to hear the people speak

Lesson 08 - Revealing Character
• Begin with idiosyncratic aspect not everyone shares (Bloom eating offal, green hair)
• First vs. third person: seeing from inside vs. outside
• Three ways to reveal character: what they say, interior monologue, action
• Discrepancy between speech and action reveals hypocrisy
• Dialogue: decide what it's doing in the story
• Speech rhythms: Some articulate with long sentences, others short staccato bursts
• Differentiation exercise: Write scene without saying who's talking—can readers tell?

Lesson 09 - Setting as a Character
• Some novels relish setting so much place becomes character
• For Rushdie, place very important—create ground book stands on first
• Can be city, country, street, café, bedroom, or lost place
• Think of place exactly like character: when is it set, what specifics?
• Write about place with strong feeling (love or hate)
• No-adjectives exercise forces storytelling about place
• Visual writing important—create pictures in readers' heads

Lesson 10 - Your Unmistakably Unique Worldview
• All of us create picture of the world—that's a kind of sanity
• Great writers have worldview that's unmistakably their own
• Personal and idiosyncratic writing feels authentically yours
• Need unusual amount of self-knowledge—ruthlessly examine your life
• Write what you know, but expand what you know
• Get out of chair, apartment, go discover
• Dostoevsky read crime pages; travel and research are forms of discovery

Lesson 11 - Observing the World
• Sharp observational powers can be developed
• Set yourself task of noticing—amazing how much you'll notice
• Train your eye (look at corners of vision) and your ear (how people talk)
• Always have notebook—make record of observations
• De-familiarize the familiar—see it as alien would
• Walking in cities good for observation
• Sound is valuable—we move through soundscape every day

Lesson 12 - Developing Your Narrative Style
• Style: manner in which you adhere to or break rules of classical form
• Voice: your touch as author—what makes writing your own
• Focus on most effective way of telling specific story; voice will emerge
• Rhythm absolutely central to meaning—conveys meaning subliminally
• Read work aloud and listen to it—it's a kind of music
• Must know rules (grammar, syntax, sentence structure) even if you break them
• Paragraph is basic unit—needs internal cohesion
• Learn classical style first (Strunk and White), then decide on deviations

Lesson 13 - Building a Surrealist Story
• Now reality is very contested—one person's truth is another's fake news
• Surrealism not escape from real—another way of describing real
• Can be more powerful than naturalism because of intensification
• Works best when has deep roots in reality
• "If the carpet needs to fly, then let the carpet fly"
• Must be part of initial concept—affects how you write from beginning
• Danger of silliness—keep close to emotional truth
• García Márquez: Blood flows to mother's kitchen—surrealistic but emotionally true

Lesson 14 - Researching the Novel
• Writing books is education that never ends
• The past is another country—people don't think like they do now
• Research about circumstances straightforward; imagining thought processes is hard
• Find your entry point into historical figures
• Internet goes broad but not deep—libraries and books best for old stuff
• Research how ordinary people lived requires digging
• Point where must put research aside and imagine it
• Imagination is ultimate weapon—can stumble on truth without expertise

Lesson 15 - All Writing Is Rewriting
• "All writing is rewriting"—very rare not to need work
• Hardest thing: making something out of nothing (blank page terror)
• Important to get something down; then critical imagination kicks in
• Be your own best editor: What's superfluous? What's missing?
• Revise language constantly; sometimes revise action or characters
• Getting to end immediately shows what you needed before
• Always read what you wrote that day again at night
• Attitude of dissatisfaction good—look at every sentence all the time
• Knowing when it's bad should be inbuilt—"good shit detector" (Hemingway)

Lesson 16 - Editing and Feedback: The Confidence to Share
• Embarrassment test: If embarrassed to show it, it's not ready
• When embarrassment reflex disappears, moment has come
• Some writers share constantly; others hug to themselves longer
• Finish solo work when not making things better, just pushing around
• Miraculous effect: Just knowing others' eyes on book makes you see differently
• Need people who tell absolute truth, not pat on back
• Say nothing when presenting work—let book speak for itself
• Take editorial comments seriously, but remember it's your book
• 75% of notes led to changes; 25% he said no
• Good editor points to problems but doesn't tell you how to fix

Lesson 17 - Develop Your Relationship With Writing
• Act of writing opens windows in head—lets in stuff not normally available
• Rushdie's bumpy start: three unpublished novels, 13 years learning
• Need real determination—willing to accept difficulty, rejection, doubt
• If you don't have that real need, do something else
• Writer's block: Call it "very creative phase"—free-form thinking period
• Sit down daily and let something come out without censorship
• Eventually something sticks—that's what you keep thinking about
• Always look for something you haven't done before

Lesson 18 - Salman's Global Canon
• Traditional Western canon has been very narrow
• Need more internationalist, genuinely global literary canon
• Books not in English have been as important as English books
• We live in age of great translators—wonderful renderings from many languages
• Examples: Anita Desai (Indian heir of Jane Austen), Tanizaki (Japanese transition)
• Latin American literature: similar colonial history, wealth disparity
• Magic realists inspired by Thousand and One Nights
• Book that unlocks something in your head might be from any culture/language

Lesson 19 - Seven Useful Tips for Writers
1. Maximalist vs. minimalist—find your temperament
2. Planning vs. improvising—what's right for you
3. Write what feels deeply necessary—can't avoid writing
4. Do something dangerous—work close to the bull
5. Commitment to sit down—don't get up till you've written something
6. When there's problem, cut it out—discard what's not working
7. Writers finish books—don't stop till you get to end

• When you finish manuscript, can immediately see what it needs
• Make sure you finish—then you will be a writer


✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS

Essential Mindsets:
• Writing requires both deep self-knowledge and learnable craft
• Success doesn't come easily—need determination to persist through years
• All writing is rewriting—revision is everything
• Writers are people who finish books
• Write because you can't not write—it comes from deep necessity

The Writing Process:
• Answer six essential questions before starting (whose, what, why, when, where, how)
• Find the opening sentence that unlocks the door to your book
• Create ground the book stands on—establish place and tone immediately
• Hold the book in your head like a spider's web connecting all elements
• When embarrassment reflex disappears, it's ready to share

Character and Story:
• Character is traditionally destiny, but modern world is more complex
• Listen to your characters—when they feel real, you're in conversation with them
• Reveal character through speech, interior monologue, and action
• Make even minor characters fully realized, not just sketched in
• Conflict is necessary for drama—happiness writes in white ink

Style and Voice:
• Style suits the story; voice is unmistakably you
• Rhythm is central to meaning—writing is a kind of music
• Know the rules before breaking them
• Read your work aloud and listen to it
• Differentiation is key—make each character speak uniquely

Research and Worldview:
• Read globally—influences can come from any culture or language
• Expand what you know through travel, research, and discovery
• Imagination is your ultimate weapon—can stumble on truth
• Your worldview should be personal and idiosyncratic
• De-familiarize the familiar—see world through fresh eyes

Practical Wisdom:
• Sit down every day and don't get up till you've written something
• If there's a problem, often best solution is to cut it out
• Take risks—work close to the bull
• Step away from work and come back with fresh eyes
• Know who will tell you the absolute truth—those are your readers

On Rejection and Persistence:
• First reader's report on "Midnight's Children" was devastating
• Took 13 years to learn how to write something people wanted to read
• If you're not willing to hang in there through difficulty, do something else
• The joy of finally writing something good makes it all worthwhile

Final Wisdom:
• The novel is an enormously fluid form—it can be anything
• Anybody can write about anything (or nobody can write about anything)
• Being a writer is a long job—you don't have to retire
• Keep discovering—finding things out gives you a long life as writer
• To thine own self be true—you are the best judge of your work
