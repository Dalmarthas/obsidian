📝 Executive Summary  
The conversation between Jefferson Fisher and Erin McGoff focuses on building real workplace confidence through self-talk, honest communication, and strategic language in high‑stakes interactions like interviews, difficult colleagues, quitting, and asking for a raise. It combines Jefferson’s courtroom/managerial experience and Erin’s research‑driven career content to offer concrete phrasing, mental models, and decision rules for handling common but emotionally charged workplace situations.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)

---

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Confidence starts with how you talk to yourself: treat yourself like a best friend—kind but honest—which creates a stable inner voice and more resilient self‑confidence at work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- For “What’s your greatest weakness?”, use Erin’s **10/90 rule**: 10% on naming the weakness, 90% on how you are actively working to improve it, focusing on a real but non‑fatal skill gap.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- With passive‑aggressive coworkers, don’t take it personally; get curious, ask clarifying questions like “Did you mean that genuinely or were you hinting at something?”, and avoid escalating language.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- When quitting, treat it as a transactional step in a long career, leave with grace (even from toxic environments), and frame the move as part of your broader professional journey to keep doors open.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- When asking for a raise, think like your manager’s manager: bring market data, enroll your boss as an advocate, avoid ultimatums, and use confident but collaborative language (e.g., “I’m confident…” and “and I also know…” instead of “but”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)

💡 Core Concepts

1. **Self‑talk as the foundation of confidence**
    
    - Erin’s “be your own best friend” principle: talk to yourself as you would to a close friend—supportive, empathetic, and honest, not cruel or worshipful.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - She describes actively countering critical thoughts (“those people probably liked you; there’s no indication they didn’t”) until the kind, realistic voice becomes habitual and feels like a single, integrated inner narrative.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Confidence is framed less as inflated self‑esteem and more as a stable, compassionate internal dialogue that allows you to take action even when you’re imperfect.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
2. **The hidden curriculum of interviews (especially “weakness” questions)**
    
    - The question “What’s your greatest weakness?” is not about confession; it’s a test of red flags, self‑awareness, and professional articulation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Erin’s **10/90 rule**:
        
        - 10%: briefly name a real, concrete weakness that does not undermine core job performance (e.g., delegation for an IC, not for a manager; working too fast and missing details), avoiding cliché character labels like “I’m a perfectionist.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - 90%: show awareness and an improvement plan (e.g., delegating one task per week with a sticky note reminder) and early results.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
    - Jefferson emphasizes that honesty about a “working on it” area signals maturity; employers look for self‑awareness more than flawlessness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
3. **Reading the “issue behind the issue”**
    
    - For many employer questions (weaknesses, raises, quitting), the surface question masks underlying concerns: risk, reliability, retention, and ease of working with you.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Erin and Jefferson repeatedly reframe: “What is your greatest weakness?” → “Are you self‑aware and professional?”; “I want a raise” → “Can I justify increased cost for this person?”; “I’m quitting” → “Will this relationship stay constructive?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - This framing pushes you to craft answers that speak to the employer’s real decision criteria instead of just your feelings.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
4. **Handling passive‑aggression as a skill problem, not a moral crisis**
    
    - Erin sees passive‑aggressive behavior as a sign of someone lacking adult, direct communication skills rather than purely malice.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - She suggests two buckets:
        
        - It affects your work (blocks tasks, information, collaboration) → worth addressing.
            
        - It does not affect your work → mostly an annoyance; manage your own response.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
    - Key script: “Did you mean that genuinely, or were you trying to hint at something?” which forces them to either own the hint or clarify intent, while also correcting for your own possible insecurity or misreading.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Jefferson adds variants like “Should I read into that?” or “Can you tell me more about that?” which invite elaboration instead of escalation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
5. **Quitting as a professional, not emotional, event**
    
    - Work is inherently personal because it consumes most waking hours, but Erin insists that quitting is fundamentally a transactional step: they paid you for work, you did the work, and now you are moving to the next chapter.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Even in bad environments, the long‑term optimal strategy is to quit with grace—short, scheduled conversation, appreciation where possible, no table‑flipping or revenge speeches.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Your reputation travels: ex‑bosses can resurface as clients or colleagues; leaving explosively satisfies short‑term emotion but harms long‑term optionality.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Jefferson structures quitting around scheduling the talk, maintaining integrity and dignity, and framing the move as part of a broader career trajectory (“journey,” “path,” “trajectory”) rather than a narrow rejection of one job.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
6. **Raise and promotion conversations as joint problem‑solving**
    
    - Erin advises imagining yourself as the manager and asking: “With everything on my plate and people above me, how would I want a direct report to bring this up?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Key elements:
        
        - Recognize managers are under pressure from above and below; they’re often not the final decision‑maker.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - Bring **market data** and a clear value case (e.g., “Other social media coordinators are paid roughly 10k more for less scope; I’d really like to stay, but this is not sustainable for me.”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - Enroll them as your advocate so they can credibly push your case through finance/HR/upper management.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
    - Jefferson layers in language tweaks: replacing “I think I’d be good” with “I’m confident I’d be good” in interviews and replacing “but” with “and I also know…” when discussing compensation (“I love working here, and I also know my current rate isn’t sustainable”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Both warn against ultimatums (“Give me a raise or I’m gone”), which either trigger refusal or a resentful yes and quickly shift you into the “not really one of us” category.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
7. **Managers and employees as the same humans on different sides of the table**
    
    - They highlight how perspectives flip when an associate becomes a partner or an employee becomes a manager/owner; you suddenly see cost structures, taxes, benefits, and organizational constraints.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - This “both sides” awareness should inform how you communicate: strong requests are fine, but they need to be framed with empathy for the other side’s constraints and incentives.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Erin reinforces that she’s been both a director and a freelancer hired by many companies, so much of her advice reflects repeatedly seeing both perspectives.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. **Upgrade your self‑talk today**
    
    - Notice a self‑critical thought (“I sounded stupid in that meeting”) and immediately respond as you would to a friend (“There’s no evidence they hated it; next time I’ll adjust X.”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Aim for realism plus empathy: neither self‑worship nor self‑abuse; over time this becomes your default internal voice and makes visible confidence easier.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
2. **Use the 10/90 rule for the “weakness” question**
    
    - Prepare 1–2 non‑fatal, job‑relevant weaknesses (e.g., delegating, pacing, over‑indexing on speed vs. detail) that you’re genuinely addressing.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Script your answer so only ~10% is naming the weakness and ~90% is: why it matters, what you’re doing about it, and early positive results.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
3. **Defuse passive‑aggression without escalating**
    
    - If you sense a jab, calmly ask: “Did you mean that genuinely or were you trying to hint at something?” or “Should I read into that?” in a genuinely curious tone.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Use “Can you tell me more about that?” to encourage them to move from hints to explicit statements and avoid “If you’ve got something to say, spit it out,” which tends to deepen defensiveness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
4. **Quit with grace and strategic framing**
    
    - Schedule a short meeting (e.g., “Could we find 10 minutes on Friday to talk?”) rather than blurting it out in the hallway or over chat.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - In the conversation, briefly thank them (if appropriate), state you’re moving to the next step in your career, and avoid unloading grievances; process anger elsewhere (journaling, friends, Glassdoor, therapy, exercise).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Explicitly keep the door open: signal appreciation and that you’d welcome crossing paths again.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
5. **Ask for a raise like a collaborator, not an adversary**
    
    - Do market research first so you can anchor the conversation in external data, not just feelings.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Use language that both asserts and enrolls:
        
        - “I really enjoy working here, and I also know that my current compensation isn’t sustainable given market rates and my responsibilities.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
        - “If you were in my position, how would you approach adjusting compensation?” (making them a teacher and ally).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
            
    - Avoid ultimatums whenever possible; instead, make clear you **want** to stay and are trying to find a sustainable way to do so.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
6. **Sound more confident in any professional answer**
    
    - Swap “I think / I believe” for “I’m confident” in key lines: “I’m confident I’d add value in this role because…”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
        
    - Maintain a calm tone; the word “confident” paired with composure naturally leaves a stronger impression in notes and debriefs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)

🔍 Additional Insights

- Erin’s process for advice is mosaic‑based: she blends research (studies, podcasts), conversations with recruiters, business owners, and job seekers, plus wisdom from family and varied sources, then lightly overlays her own experience. This is presented as subjective but rigorously cross‑checked, not purely autobiographical.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- She emphasizes that many followers don’t need scripts as much as **validation**; they already know what they want to say or do and just need a nudge (“You sound like you know what you’re doing; go do it.”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Both speakers highlight how childhood communication patterns (e.g., passive‑aggressive family norms, being the youngest of many siblings) shape adult workplace communication styles, so “fixing” communication is often unlearning early models.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Ambiguous/unclear area: while they note that sometimes passive behavior may be benign hinting rather than hostility, there’s no detailed guidance on distinguishing chronic toxicity from occasional awkwardness beyond those clarifying questions; judgment and pattern recognition are still required.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H7yNV6Kghb4&list=WL&index=21)
