**Executive Summary**

Jeff Deverett, an experienced producer and distributor, discusses the practical realities and hurdles of getting an independent film onto Netflix. Drawing from his experiences with three Netflix films, he distills the process, the pivotal role of distribution companies, and the need for persistence and proven track records. His candid narrative reveals industry-specific tactics, recurring obstacles, and actionable strategies for aspiring filmmakers seeking streaming deals.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **Short, impactful pitches are critical**: Netflix hears countless pitches and responds best to concise, focused presentations.
- **Distribution is often required**: Even successful direct pitches typically result in Netflix requiring delivery through approved distributors, affecting deal terms and leverage.
- **A strong track record matters**: Prior delivery of quality projects on time and on budget is crucial for repeat opportunities and credibility.
- **Persistence is essential**: Rejection is common; viewing “no” as “not today” is necessary for long-term success.
- **Newcomers need established partners**: Entry often depends on teaming up with an experienced producer or credible intermediary.

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

- **Pitch Optimization**:
    
    Early on, Deverett would deliver long pitches, but quickly learned Netflix (and similar buyers) prefer pitches under three minutes, skipping extended pleasantries and getting straight to the unique selling proposition.
    
- **Distribution Bottleneck**:
    
    Although Deverett could deliver directly, Netflix required that independent films go through vetted distributors to ease internal processing, which forced him to negotiate deals and accept added costs/commissions. This “middleman” requirement can dilute profits and leverage, particularly for less-established filmmakers.
    
- **Delivery Process Complexity**:
    
    Delivering a film to Netflix entails meeting extremely detailed technical requirements, including multiple languages and subtitle tracks—often creating more expense than the initial movie production. Specialized delivery companies (aggregators) frequently handle this aspect.
    
- **Building Credibility**:
    
    Successful delivery, timeliness, and prior commercial success form a feedback loop. Studios and investors prefer working with producers who have a proven record. Metrics (viewership/performance) drive future opportunities.
    
- **Industry Gatekeeping**:
    
    Breaking in without a track record is exceptionally difficult. The most realistic path is aligning with an experienced producer who can vouch for the newcomer’s capability and provide industry access.
    
- **Networking Realities**:
    
    Approaching key players at industry events is comparable to “chemistry at a bar”—success depends on authenticity, timing, and social competence as much as on business acumen. There’s no single formula for forming these connections.
    

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- **Refine your pitch to two minutes or less**; rehearse the delivery for clarity and impact.
- **Prepare all required materials**: Key art, trailer, and a tight log line should accompany your submission.
- **Anticipate working through a distributor**; research and establish relationships with those already approved by Netflix.
- **Persist through rejection**: Treat every “no” as temporary—routine, polite follow-up is necessary.
- **Build alliances with established industry players**: Seek mentorship or co-productions to overcome gatekeeping for first-timers.
- **Professionalism in follow-up**: Always ask about suitable timings for follow-ups and respect boundaries to maintain relationships.

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- **Netflix’s professionalism**: Deverett praises Netflix’s responsiveness and professionalism compared to some in the distribution industry.
- **Metrics-driven decisions**: Netflix uses confidential data to assess repeat deals; success of prior films is the single biggest factor in new opportunities.
- **Ambiguity of event networking**: Social skills and “chemistry” are highlighted, yet acknowledged as unpredictable and hard to teach—there’s no guaranteed approach.
- **Changing odds**: Deverett notes an increase in the effort required; what once took 100 calls may now require 250–300 due to increased competition and market saturation.
- **Consultancy and mentorship**: He often consults or connects filmmakers with vetted industry contacts, acknowledging the proliferation of online “experts”—vigilance is needed when seeking guidance.
