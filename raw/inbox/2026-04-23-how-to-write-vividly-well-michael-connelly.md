## 📝 Executive Summary

Michael Connelly explains how he creates vivid, character‑driven crime fiction by obsessing over “telling details,” rigorous rewriting, and anchoring every element—plot, setting, dialogue—to character and momentum. He shows how city, conflict, and even murder investigations become frameworks for exploring deeper human questions, like how people confront darkness without being consumed by it.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

---

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

- **Use “telling details,” not many details:** One precise, meaningful detail can reveal character and place while keeping narrative momentum, whereas over‑detailing becomes a speed bump.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- **Rewriting is where books are truly written:** Connelly rewrites daily on paper and again at the end of the book, stripping excess, upgrading weak details, and seeding future payoffs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- **Character is above everything else:** Plot, setting, and even research exist in service of character; readers stay for people, not puzzles.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- **Conflict must exist on every page:** Following Kurt Vonnegut’s maxim, every character must want something, even if it is only a glass of water—or a forbidden cigarette.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- **Place and murder are tools to explore society:** Each murder becomes “a tale of a city,” letting the writer pierce all levels of society through an investigator’s license to go anywhere.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 💡 Core Concepts

## 1. Telling Details and Momentum

Connelly looks for “telling details”—small, specific observations that open a window of imagination for the reader. For example, he notices a detective who bites his glasses’ earpiece at crime scenes, leaving a groove; that single detail conveys tension, hard‑bitten experience, and internal clenching without exposition. He warns that too many details kill momentum; the craft is choosing the one that carries the most meaning about character or situation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

Rewriting is his main filter: he removes extraneous details and replaces them with stronger ones once he sees the whole story and knows what will matter 100 pages later. He prints pages, edits them with pencil, uses his own shorthand (e.g., “RW” for rewrite, “NSG” for “not so good”), then updates the digital draft.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 2. Rewriting Routine and Process

His daily process is to begin each morning by rewriting the previous day’s 3–10 pages, always on paper. He has never read an ebook and prefers physical pages because they change how he sees the text and reveal issues he might miss on a screen. At the end of a book, he prints the entire manuscript and marks it up in one continuous pass before entering changes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He organizes his life to “always be writing”: mornings from roughly 6–11 are protected for writing, with meetings, set visits, or interviews in the afternoon. He outsources research where possible (to detectives, lawyers, judges, and an internet researcher), asking for short, precise answers to maintain momentum and avoid disappearing down research rabbit holes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 3. Character Above All

Connelly believes readers fundamentally read for character, and everything else—plot, geography, history, job details—is in service to making a fictional person feel real. His philosophy: he is asking readers to believe in a person who does not exist, so the best way to “sell” that person is to plant the character’s feet in a meticulously real world. That means getting details of jobs, weather, neighborhoods, and contemporary events right and then seeing how characters react to them.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He writes multiple characters as distinct “lenses” on Los Angeles:

- Older, wise Harry Bosch in his 70s.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- A female character navigating a male‑dominated bureaucracy.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Defense attorney Mickey Haller, the ultimate outsider to state power, looking for cracks in the system.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    

Names themselves are tools of characterization and metaphor. Bosch’s original draft name was “Pierce,” referencing Chandler’s idea that detectives pierce all levels of society; Connelly later chose “Bosch,” echoing Hieronymus Bosch’s “Garden of Earthly Delights” and making Los Angeles a modern garden of earthly delights.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 4. Place as Character: Los Angeles and Crime

Los Angeles is a vast, evolving, segregated, second‑chance city whose contrasts (mountains, desert, ocean; freeways as barriers; entertainment wealth vs. have‑nots) create constant friction—perfect material for crime fiction. Connelly sets a specific goal for each book: end up in a neighborhood he has never used before, accepting that he will never fully “cover” LA.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He treats geography as a memory trigger: a neighborhood, diner, or restaurant can bring up a character’s memories of their mother, spouse, or past cases, allowing backstory to emerge naturally through place. Drawing on Richard Price and Chandler, he sees each murder investigation as a framework for studying the city: circle a murder long enough and you get to know the city; each murder becomes a tale of that city.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

Traffic is another example of lived reality he uses to build relatability and tension: Harry Bosch’s view over the 101 freeway becomes a recurring motif for the city’s pulse. Even mundane experiences (traffic, bureaucrats, small frustrations) connect readers—who have never solved murders—to a detective’s extraordinary work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 5. Conflict and Desire

Connelly treats conflict as central to storytelling, echoing Kurt Vonnegut’s advice: “On every page, every character must want something, even if it’s just a glass of water.” He applies this literally—early on, Bosch is a smoker in a society that discourages smoking; his constant, thwarted desire for a cigarette ensures there is always conflict somewhere on the page.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He distinguishes external conflict (e.g., solving a murder, winning a trial, fighting bureaucracy) from internal conflict (e.g., craving a cigarette, fear of aging, doubt over whether one is loved). His favorite conflicts are subtle: conversations that appear to be “about” one thing but actually express deeper issues—such as someone “joking” about how young another person looked years ago, triggering insecurity about age and love.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 6. Dialogue: Compressed, Informative, and Character‑Driven

Connelly argues that dialogue must be cut brutally to feel real; writers often go wrong by making it too long and too on‑the‑nose. His newspaper background trained him to use only quotes that carry new, essential information he does not repeat in exposition. He brings this discipline to fiction: dialogue must convey character and advance story, not exist as filler.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He uses diction, rhythm, and silence to differentiate characters. Bosch, for example, is laconic; he nods instead of saying “yes” or “no,” an early trait so overused that an editor once counted hundreds of nods in a manuscript. Connelly likes dialogue scenes where the surface topic hides deeper conflict; he trusts readers to pick up on subtext and resists editorial pressure to explain everything explicitly.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 7. Darkness, Heroism, and Moral Cost

A core theme across his career is the moral cost of confronting evil. He believes if you spend your life dealing with murder and darkness, some of that darkness inevitably gets inside you; much of his work examines what characters do with that darkness and how they prevent it from metastasizing into a “moral cancer.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He is more interested in heroes than villains: what it means to risk oneself, to bring order from disorder, and to repeatedly walk into moral darkness. Unlike writers who dwell on villain psychology, he focuses on the noble struggle of those who fight evil and how they pay for it psychologically and ethically.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 8. AI, Courtrooms, and Debatable Themes

In a recent legal thriller about AI, Connelly uses a courtroom as a contained arena to “put AI on trial,” staging testimony from multiple sides about whether AI will improve the world or destroy it. He values the courtroom because witnesses can embody different arguments and perspectives while the story still drives toward a binary verdict (guilty/not guilty).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He acknowledges that legal thrillers can feel structurally constrained—every trial leads to thumbs‑up or thumbs‑down outcomes—whereas homicide investigations allow more freedom. His aim is not to be didactic but to compress a broad societal debate into a dramatic, character‑driven frame and “ask questions” more than give answers.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 9. Books vs. TV: Internal vs. External

Books allow depth via interiority—what characters think and feel—while TV and film are limited to what characters do and say on screen. Connelly confesses that this makes him less naturally suited to scriptwriting; his instincts lean toward internal monologue, which scripts cannot show.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He initially hesitated about TV adaptations because for decades he encouraged readers to imagine Bosch’s appearance themselves; TV then “betrayed” that invitation by fixing a canonical face. Some readers refuse to watch the show to preserve their own mental image, a choice he respects. Still, serialized streaming TV (e.g., 98 episodes of Bosch, ~50 of The Lincoln Lawyer) gives him another large canvas to “deliver character” over time, even if it must operate without interior thought.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 10. Craft, Training, and Readers Today

Connelly sees journalism, TV, and law as powerful training grounds because they demand clear, persuasive writing under constraints. He suspects the core craft of novel writing has changed less than television: while TV assumes distracted viewers (phones, second‑screening, subtitles), books still require full attention, though audiobooks introduce multitasking into reading.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

He notes that editors and modern screen expectations often push toward explicitness, but he prefers trusting readers to infer subtext. His long‑term guiding principle remains: write every day (even 15 minutes), stay in the “tunnel” of the story, and keep your eyes on the current page rather than getting lost in notes, continuity Bibles, or excessive planning.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

Ambiguous/unclear content: he mentions not keeping a personal character “Bible” and relying on copy editors for continuity, which implies some small inconsistencies across books; the extent of these inconsistencies and their impact on readers is not fully detailed.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 🔨 Actionable Tips

1. **Train your eye for telling details**
    
    - When observing people or places, do not take notes in front of them; rely on memory and look for one small physical habit, object, or behavior that reveals temperament (e.g., grooves in glasses from clenching teeth).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - In revision, replace generic details with one specific, meaningful detail that reveals character or mood.
        
2. **Build a daily rewriting habit**
    
    - Start each session by rewriting the previous day’s pages, ideally on paper; mark changes by hand, then update your digital file.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - At the end of a project, print the entire manuscript and do a slow, continuous read‑through with a consistent shorthand for fixes (e.g., RW, NSG).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
3. **Organize your life to “always be writing”**
    
    - Block a consistent morning window to write before the world intrudes; treat afternoons as flexible for research, meetings, or other work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Outsource research when possible with very specific questions, asking for concise answers to avoid breaking your writing flow.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
4. **Design characters using environment and names**
    
    - Define each major character as a distinct “lens” on your setting (e.g., an aging insider, a young outsider, a woman in a male bureaucracy).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Choose names with metaphorical resonance; if possible, connect them to themes or cultural references that echo your story’s concerns.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
5. **Put conflict on every page**
    
    - Use Vonnegut’s principle as a checklist: review pages and ask what each character wants right now, even if it is minor.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Layer small, relatable conflicts (traffic, bureaucracy, family friction) under the big central conflict (murder, trial) to keep tension continuous.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
6. **Cut and compress dialogue**
    
    - After drafting a scene, cut at least half the dialogue; remove any line that does not add information or deepen character.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Let subtext carry conflict: write conversations that appear to be about logistics or pleasantries but actually express insecurity, resentment, or love.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
7. **Use place as memory and theme engine**
    
    - When a character enters a location, ask what past event there shaped them; let the place trigger a concise memory that reveals backstory.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Treat your city as a character whose geography, history, and inequalities constantly press on your characters’ choices.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
8. **Make big ideas concrete through narrative frames**
    
    - For complex topics (e.g., AI), choose a contained, high‑pressure frame like a trial or investigation and let different characters embody different sides.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
        
    - Avoid lecturing; instead, stage arguments through testimony, cross‑examination, and consequences so readers infer their own conclusions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)

## 🔍 Additional Insights

- Connelly deliberately avoids keeping extensive character journals or series bibles himself, trusting momentum and letting copy editors manage continuity; this prioritizes forward motion over perfect consistency.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- His love of LA echoes Raymond Chandler’s influence: he rereads a specific Chandler chapter before starting a new book to “raise the flag” and remind himself how sharply a city can be rendered.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- He acknowledges a tension between respecting readers’ imagined versions of his characters and the commercial/creative opportunity of TV adaptations; some readers refuse the shows to preserve their internal Bosch.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- His own path—from newspaper crime reporter to bestselling novelist to producer—suggests that “commercial” writing backgrounds (news, TV, law) can sharpen clarity, economy, and reader awareness in fiction.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
    
- He insists on writing every day, even as little as 15 minutes, because staying constantly in contact with the story keeps it alive in the subconscious, allowing ideas and solutions to surface between sessions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iMeWCnZNJSs&list=WL&index=14)
