Complete Master Summary & Reference Guide

📋 COURSE OVERVIEW

This masterclass provides a comprehensive guide to developing and writing television pilots, taught by Matt and Ross Duffer, the creators of "Stranger Things." The course traces their journey from aspiring filmmakers in North Carolina to successful showrunners, sharing the complete methodology they used to create Stranger Things (originally titled "Montauk"). The brothers emphasize that writing should be an enjoyable, structured process rather than torture, and they present a step-by-step system designed to prevent writers from getting "lost in the woods" - a common pitfall where scripts become broken beyond repair.

The course is designed for writers at all levels: those with 20 unsold scripts, those stuck at page 20, complete beginners staring at a blank page, and anyone who loves storytelling but doesn't know anyone in Hollywood. The Duffers reveal previously unseen documents from Stranger Things' development, including their original 2010 brainstorming notes, Netflix pitch materials, and detailed outlines. Throughout the course, they create a fake "Steve and Dustin spinoff" in real-time to demonstrate their process, showing how ideas evolve from rough concepts to polished scripts.

The fundamental philosophy underlying their approach is "baby steps" - starting extremely zoomed out with broad strokes, then slowly bringing everything into focus through multiple stages of refinement. They compare this to an eye doctor's exam where lenses gradually sharpen a blurry image. Their mantra is "stay wide, stay zoomed out" until each stage is fully developed, because rushing ahead to script too early wastes far more time than thoroughly planning. This methodical approach transformed their writing from years of painful struggle into a smooth, enjoyable process that finally led to success.

🎯 CORE THEMES & PHILOSOPHY

THE BIG IDEA: Foundation of Everything
The most crucial stage of development is finding the right "big idea" - the concept you'll potentially work with for years or even a decade. The Duffers emphasize that conceptual problems at this stage cannot be fixed later, no matter how good your execution becomes. They wasted years trying to write scripts built on faulty foundations, learning that it's better to spend weeks or months finding the right idea than to waste a year writing the wrong one.

A strong big idea must have:
- Personal passion that sustains you through years of development
- A clear DRIVE (what the protagonist wants and has difficulty getting)
- Simplicity and clarity at its core
- Emotional resonance, not just plot mechanics
- Something that excites you so much you want to write it immediately

The drive is the engine of your story - without it, you have a beautiful car that won't run. It doesn't need to be "save the world" level stakes; even a romantic comedy's drive of "win over the person" works. In Stranger Things, the drive was elegantly simple: "Will is missing, we must find Will." Everything else - monsters, psychic powers, government conspiracies - orbited around this central drive.

WRITE WHAT YOU LOVE, NOT WHAT SELLS
One of the Duffers' most transformative realizations was to stop chasing industry trends and write from passion instead. In college, they tried writing found footage scripts because that genre was hot, despite not loving found footage themselves. The lack of genuine passion produced bad scripts. Their breakthrough came when they "cut out all the noise" - stopped reading Variety and Deadline obsessively - and focused purely on what they loved.

This doesn't mean writing autobiography. Instead, combine:
- Things you're obsessed with (for them: conspiracy theories, 80s Spielberg films)
- Personal experiences (their childhood friendships became the kids in Stranger Things)
- Emotional truths from your life (even if the setting is fantastical)

Their first successful script, "Hidden," came from a friend's bomb shelter that terrified them as kids. Stranger Things emerged from their love of the Montauk Project conspiracy theory combined with memories of childhood friendships and summers with friends. The emotional authenticity of those friendships grounded all the supernatural elements.

STAY SIMPLE (Especially for Your First Script)
The Duffers strongly advocate for simplicity, particularly for early scripts. You don't need to write a sprawling epic with extensive world-building. Even telling a simple story well is extraordinarily difficult and involves many complex elements. Benefits of starting simple:

- Write about characters/locations you know intimately
- Avoid requiring a year of research
- Focus on fundamentals of writing and dialogue
- Lean into your strengths (they found kids' voices easier than scientists)
- Easier to avoid getting lost in the woods

On Stranger Things Season 1, Dr. Brenner speaks very little precisely because they found his voice harder to write. That's acceptable - play to your strengths. Simple doesn't mean simplistic; it means not overcomplicating your first effort.

THE PROCESS IS EVERYTHING: Why Structure Matters
The Duffers had a revelation that writing felt like torture only because they were approaching it wrong. For 2-4 years, writing was painful - pulling teeth. The epiphany came when developing "Hidden" under financial pressure: they outlined the story before writing, the outline worked, and the script wrote incredibly fast. It sold in a bidding war to Warner Brothers.

This taught them that process matters more than raw talent. With the right structure, writing becomes enjoyable and efficient. Without it, even talented writers waste years. Their methodology is designed to prevent the most common failure mode: getting lost in the woods because you jumped into script too early without sufficient planning.

THE ZOOM PRINCIPLE: From Blurry to Focused
Think of development like an eye doctor's vision test - starting extremely blurry and slowly bringing the image into focus through progressive stages:

1. Big Idea (extremely zoomed out): Just the core concept
2. Blue Sky Brainstorming (still wide): Free-form exploration
3. Story Outline (starting to zoom in): Major beats
4. Scaffolding Outline (more focused): Scene-by-scene structure
5. Final Outline (quite sharp): Detailed blueprint
6. Script (fully focused): Final product

At each stage, stay completely zoomed out within that stage. Don't jump ahead. The blurriness is normal and good - if you're frustrated that your characters aren't as developed as in a finished show you watched, you're comparing apples to oranges. Even professional writers start with vague, messy ideas.

📊 THE COMPLETE WRITING PROCESS: STEP-BY-STEP

STAGE 1: DEVELOPING THE BIG IDEA

Start by throwing many ideas at the wall to see what sticks. Don't judge too harshly initially. The Duffers' 2010 document included weak ideas like "Make a Monster" (a kid creates a designer pet monster that becomes dangerous), but even bad ideas contained kernels that later proved useful (the monster-raising concept became Dustin's relationship with Dart in Season 2).

Finding Inspiration:
- Watch/rewatch favorite movies and shows (best homework ever)
- Consume diverse media: video games, board games, books, comics
- Study both successes and failures
- Look at completely different genres for techniques
- Take walks (something about walking stimulates creativity)
- Listen to instrumental movie soundtracks (lyrics interfere with thought)
- Don't force it at the computer - ideas come while driving, showering, walking

The "Ordinary Meets Extraordinary" Pattern:
Identify the connective tissue between everything you love. For the Duffers, it was "ordinary people meeting extraordinary circumstances" - E.T., Close Encounters, Jaws, Jurassic Park, Super 8. This revelation helped them understand what to write.

Key Questions:
- What am I personally obsessed with?
- What memories or experiences haunt me?
- What movies/shows do I watch repeatedly?
- What's the connective tissue between my favorites?
- Am I truly passionate about this, or chasing trends?

The Stranger Things Origin:
In 2010, they brainstormed a found footage film about the Montauk Project (government experiments creating psychic children and opening dimensions). They abandoned it but kept the notes. In 2013, watching "Prisoners" sparked the idea of a long-form kidnapping mystery. Combining this with "what if it's a supernatural kidnapping?" led them back to Montauk. Adding the 1980s setting for budget reasons and homage to Spielberg completed the spark. Two words - "supernatural kidnapping" - unified everything.

STAGE 2: WRITING THE LOGLINE/PARAGRAPH

Take your big idea and distill it into a short paragraph (6-8 sentences). This isn't just for pitching - it's a tool to see if the idea flows clearly on paper. If you can't articulate it simply, the idea might be too complicated or muddled.

A good paragraph includes:
- The protagonist (can be vague: "a boy," not necessarily named yet)
- The inciting incident (what starts the story)
- The goal/drive (what they want)
- The obstacles (why it's difficult)
- The stakes (why it matters)
- A hint of tone

Original Montauk Paragraph (2014):
"Montauk. A limited series set in 1980s Montauk, New York [note: Montauk back then was akin to Amity in Jaws, not upscale like today]. The small suburban community is shaken when a 10-year-old boy vanishes into thin air one night while biking home. We follow the boy's friends, family, and teachers as they try to uncover what happened to him. We soon realize that the disappearance is somehow linked to strange happenings at Montauk Air Force Station, home of the Montauk Project, where a series of secret government experiments allegedly took place in the late '70s, early '80s. But the boy's disappearance is just the start, as stranger, more horrifying, seemingly unexplainable happenings take place all over town. An evil has been unleashed here. Think Super 8/80s Spielberg crossed with vintage Stephen King and you'll get the idea."

Notice what's missing: No character names, no mention of Eleven, no sheriff character, very vague on details. That's perfectly fine at this stage.

Assignment: Write paragraphs for 3-5 different ideas. Pick the one that excites you most.

STAGE 3: BLUE SKY BRAINSTORMING

This is free-form, stream-of-consciousness development. No structure yet. Just explore the idea playfully. Document everything in a messy, ongoing file. This stage is about quantity over quality.

What to Explore:
- Character possibilities (ages, personalities, quirks)
- Setting details (locations, time period, world rules)
- Plot possibilities (cool scenes, set pieces, moments)
- Emotional beats (relationships, arcs)
- Tonal references (what movies/shows it feels like)
- Random cool ideas (that might fit somewhere)

The Duffers' Blue Sky Document for Stranger Things:
Their actual notes included terrible spelling, sentence fragments, contradictory ideas, and extremely basic descriptions like "nerd friend" and "Sam Rockwell in a shack" (which became Hopper in a trailer). It was messy and rough, but this messiness is the point - you're exploring without judgment.

Key Principles:
- Don't censor yourself
- Spelling and grammar don't matter
- Contradictory ideas can coexist for now
- You'll use maybe 10-20% of what you generate
- Some bad ideas contain good kernels
- Takes several weeks, not one session
- Keep returning to the document to add ideas

Combining Ideas:
If two mediocre ideas aren't working separately, try combining them. The Montauk Project + supernatural kidnapping + 1980s setting together became greater than their parts.

Resistance: Keep digging until you break through. When stuck, take breaks, walk, listen to music, but keep returning to the document. The idea needs time to simmer.

STAGE 4: DEVELOPING CHARACTERS

Only after blue sky do you start focusing on characters more deliberately. At this stage, characters should still be relatively simple - just getting more defined than "nerd friend."

Character Development Principles:

A vs. B Arcs:
Every major character should go from point A to point B emotionally/psychologically. They should be different by the end of the pilot than at the beginning. For Stranger Things:
- Eleven: From traumatized, voiceless lab subject → Finding voice and first real friendship
- Mike: From ordinary kid → Willing to risk everything for a friend
- Hopper: From checked-out, depressed drunk → Reinvested in caring and protecting

These arcs don't need to be complete in the pilot, but the direction should be established. You're setting up trajectories that will continue through the season.

Avoid Flat Characters:
The worst character is one who doesn't change. Even supporting characters should have hints of depth and arc. The Duffers mention that in early drafts, some characters were too one-dimensional, serving only mechanical plot functions rather than having their own emotional journeys.

Character Questionnaires (Use Sparingly):
Many screenwriting books suggest detailed character questionnaires - What's their favorite food? What's their relationship with their mother? The Duffers find these somewhat helpful but warn against getting lost in endless backstory. Better to:
- Know their current emotional state
- Understand their wound or want
- Know where they start and end (A to B)
- Keep most information in your head, not in 20-page documents

Finding Character Voices:
During production, actors will change how you write characters. The Duffers didn't fully know Eddie's voice in Season 4 until they worked with Joe Quinn for a few weeks. During the COVID shutdown, they went back and refined Eddie's dialogue based on hearing the actor. This is normal - characters evolve when you cast them.

Key Questions:
- What does this character want (surface level)?
- What do they need (deeper level)?
- What's their wound or backstory pain?
- How do they speak differently from other characters?
- What's their function in the story AND their emotional journey?

The Balance:
Don't spend months on character development before outlining. Get them sketched out, then let them develop further as you outline. They'll become more three-dimensional as you write actual scenes. The Duffers emphasize: stay zoomed out, don't get lost in details yet.

STAGE 5: SETTING AND WORLDBUILDING

Simultaneously with character development, start thinking about the world these characters inhabit. This doesn't require Lord of the Rings-level maps or extensive wikis.

Two Types of Setting Work:

Real-World Settings:
For Stranger Things, they didn't do extensive research on Montauk initially. It was just "small town by the beach." But they did start listing specific locations within that world:
- Mike's basement (major recurring location)
- The quarry
- The school gym
- The junkyard
- Tunnels (Season 2)
- The mall (Season 3)

This exercise serves two purposes:
1. Gives you a palette of locations to draw from when outlining scenes
2. Sometimes a location becomes more important than initially thought

The Season 3 writers' room listed potential locations, including "mall," which seemed fun. The mall ended up becoming central to the season. Season 2's tunnels started as "tunnels are cool" and became a major finale location.

Fantastical Settings:
The Upside Down wasn't fully developed in the pilot. Initial ideas:
- Originally called "The Nether"
- First concept: never show it (Poltergeist-inspired, all over radios/walkies)
- Realized for TV they needed to show it eventually
- Simple aesthetic born from budget constraints
- Inspired by Poltergeist, Silent Hill (video game), Under the Skin (film)
- Practical effects (mist, spores, vines) rather than extensive CGI

The name "Upside Down" came in Episode 5 when Mike flips a D&D board: "If this is Hawkins, and this is where Will is... the Upside Down." It stuck.

Eleven's "Black Void" (psychic space):
- Not in original pitch
- Created during writing as a way to visualize her powers
- Inspired by Under the Skin's stark black space with Scarlett Johansson
- Kept practical and simple for budget

Key Lessons:
- It's okay to be vague initially
- Netflix didn't hammer them on mythology details during the pitch
- World-building evolves throughout writing, even after selling the pilot
- Draw inspiration from unexpected sources (experimental films, video games)
- Simple, practical solutions often work better than complex CGI

Assignment: List 5-10 locations where your characters could exist. Keep it simple: "bedroom," "school gym," "abandoned factory." You don't need to know how they'll be used yet.

STAGE 6: DEVELOPING THE SEASON ARC

By now you have: a big idea, characters with A-to-B arcs, and a world. Now consider the overall story arc from A (pilot's start) to B (season finale).

Story Arc Principles:

The Big Drive:
Your story needs an A-to-B arc just like your characters. In Stranger Things, this was crystal clear: Will disappears (A) → Find Will (B). This simple drive held everything together despite the show's complexity (monsters, psychic powers, government conspiracies, multiple storylines).

Multiple Storylines with One Goal:
Stranger Things split characters into three "generational" storylines:
- Kids (Mike, Dustin, Lucas, Eleven)
- Teens (Nancy, Jonathan, Steve)
- Adults (Joyce, Hopper)

All three had the same ultimate goal (find Will) but approached it differently. Each solved one piece of the puzzle:
- Kids: Discovered Eleven and her connection to the Upside Down
- Teens: Uncovered the Demogorgon and its hunting patterns
- Adults: Exposed the government conspiracy and found Will's location

In the penultimate episode, all three groups share information and realize that removing any one storyline would make saving Will impossible. They needed all three puzzle pieces.

This structure repeated across seasons despite different stories:
- Season 2: Will's possession and closing the gate
- Season 3: The Russian base and the Mind Flayer
- Season 4: Vecna and the multiple storylines converging

Don't Plan Too Far Ahead:
Many writers get overwhelmed trying to map out seven seasons. The Duffers focused exclusively on making Season 1 excellent. When asked about Season 2, they had one simple pitch: "Will's been in the Upside Down all season - we don't know what happened to him. Season 2 would explore the effects on Will."

That was enough. Focus on telling one great story first. If it works, you'll find the next story.

Stay Zoomed Out:
At this stage, you should know:
- Where the story starts (inciting incident)
- Where it ends (resolution of the drive)
- Broadly how each major character/storyline contributes
- A few key beats along the way

You don't need:
- Every episode mapped out
- Every scene figured out
- All mythology explained
- The entire series arc

Assignment: Write a clean, organized one-page description of your concept including characters, setting, and story arc from A to B. This should be more polished than your blue sky doc but still relatively high-level.

STAGE 7: THE STORY OUTLINE (Wide Structure)

Now you start building the actual structure. The story outline is still quite zoomed out - think major story beats, not individual scenes. This is where you begin to impose structure on all your blue sky ideas.

What Goes in a Story Outline:
- The pilot's opening (hook)
- Inciting incident (what kicks off the story)
- Major plot beats (the big story movements)
- Character introductions (when we meet major players)
- Act breaks (where tension escalates)
- The climax (confrontation/resolution of the pilot's drive)
- The ending (where the pilot leaves the audience)

It's okay if this takes several weeks. You're building the skeleton that everything else hangs on. The Duffers emphasize: if this doesn't work, nothing built on top of it will work. Better to spend extra time here than to waste months on a broken script.

Conflict in Every Scene:
Even at the outline stage, start thinking about conflict. Conflict doesn't mean yelling or fighting - it means opposing goals. In the Eleven/Benny scene:
- Benny wants: Information about who she is
- Eleven wants: Food and to hide her identity
- Conflict: He withholds food to get information; she remains silent

Even quiet scenes need this tension of opposing goals.

Plant and Payoff:
Start thinking about setup and payoff even in the outline. In Season 4, COVID delays gave them time to add plants:
- Eddie's guitar: Initially not mentioned until the finale. They went back and added setup (Eddie mentions playing guitar to Chrissy in Episode 1, kisses his guitar later). The payoff: Eddie's finale moment rocking out.
- Angela's assault location: Originally a putt-putt course (all near freeways, terrible for sound). Rewrote for a roller rink - can't imagine it any other way now.

Common Pitfalls:
- Rushing through this stage to get to script
- Not testing whether the structure actually works
- Getting too detailed too soon (save that for next stage)
- Making it about pure plot without character moments

Assignment: Write a story outline with 10-15 major beats from beginning to end. Each beat is 1-2 sentences. Focus on what happens, not how it's written.

STAGE 8: THE SCAFFOLDING OUTLINE (Scene-by-Scene)

Now you break down the story outline into individual scenes. This is the "scaffolding" - the framework the script will be built on. Each scene gets a brief description.

Scaffolding Format:
- Scene heading (location/time if relevant)
- 2-4 sentences describing what happens
- Key character beats or moments
- Possible dialogue snippets (if they come to you)

Example from Stranger Things:
"INT. MIKE'S BASEMENT - NIGHT
The boys are in the middle of a D&D campaign. Mike is DM. The campaign is reaching its climax - they're facing the Demogorgon. Will tries to use a fireball. Mike says it's a seven. Will rolls, gets a seven, tries to use a protection spell but time runs out. MIKE'S MOM calls down - Will needs to go home. They beg for more time but she insists. They argue about whether Will should have gotten the protection spell as Will packs up."

Notice: Still relatively brief. Not writing full dialogue yet. Capturing the essence and key beats.

Testing the Structure:
Read through the entire scaffolding outline. Does it flow? Are there saggy sections where nothing's happening? Do characters have moments to breathe between plot beats? Are you setting up payoffs? Does the pacing work?

This is the last chance to make major structural changes easily. Once you move to the final outline or script, restructuring becomes far more time-consuming.

Backing Out When Needed:
If something isn't working in the scaffolding, don't push forward. Back out to the story outline and fix it there first. The Duffers regularly back out of stages when they realize something's broken. It feels like losing progress, but it saves time compared to fixing it later.

The Duffers stress: This might take several weeks or even a couple months. That's normal for a good pilot. Don't rush it.

STAGE 9: THE FINAL OUTLINE (Detailed Blueprint)

The final outline is extremely detailed - almost like a script without proper formatting. Here you're adding:
- More complete description of action/visuals
- Character blocking and movement
- Emotional beats and reactions
- Some actual dialogue (though still brief)
- Transitions between scenes
- More specific detail on everything

Final Outline Format Example:
"INT. BENNY'S BURGER - NIGHT
Back to Benny's Burger. His salmon hisses on a plate (NOTE: This became a burger in the final script). Benny is cooking for Eleven, some actually good cooked food, fish or salmon or something. They sit down together in a small kitchen. Delicious [sic].

Scene where he tries to get info out of her. Asks about it. What's your name? Where are your parents? What are your parents' names? Etc. She finally speaks, but only cryptically [sic]. Says that something bad is coming. Paranoid. Sees headlights out window. Nervous. Someone's looking for her.

Benny picks up on this. Who is looking for you? She won't say, but her little eyes are filled with fear. Benny is concerned. You stay here for now. You're safe here. She takes his hand, says something about his past. I'm sorry about your friend. I'm sorry he drowned.

Benny gets the chills. How do you know about him? Elle removes her hand, goes back to eating, finishes her food. Benny is unnerved. You want more? Elle nods.

As Elle continues to eat, getting a second helping, Benny gets on the phone, keeps his voice down so she can't hear. Hey Jean, hey it's Benny Hammond. Got a little girl here. I'm a little worried she's going to run. Seems like she hasn't had a bite to eat in a year. Hasn't spoken a word. Abused I think. Must be a missing kid. [This is him hearing about Will.] Well I've got a found kid right here. I've got a bad feeling about this. Jots down a number for social services."

Notice: This is much more detailed than the scaffolding, but still has typos, notes to self, and isn't formatted properly. It's a working document, not a finished product.

The Test:
If your final outline works - meaning you can read through it, it flows, character arcs are clear, story makes sense, and it's engaging - then moving to script should be relatively quick. For the Duffers, it usually takes about two weeks to turn a final outline into a script.

What If It Doesn't Work?
Sometimes you get to this stage and realize something's broken. This happened to the Duffers "quite a bit." The solution: back out to whichever stage has the problem. If it's a scene that's not working, fix it in the outline. If it's a structural issue, go back to scaffolding. If it's a character problem, revisit your character development.

Never try to fix major issues in script form - too time-consuming and you'll get lost.

STAGE 10: WRITING THE SCRIPT

Finally, you're ready for the script. This should be the easiest stage if your final outline works. You're essentially taking the outline and:
- Formatting it properly (scene headings, character names, etc.)
- Writing all the dialogue
- Polishing descriptions
- Adding specific details
- Creating smooth transitions

Formatting:
Use screenwriting software (Final Draft, Writer Duet, Celtx, etc.) that auto-formats. Read any professional screenplay to see format conventions:
- Scene headings: INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME
- Capitalize character names when first introduced
- Capitalize character names before dialogue
- Capitalize important sounds/objects sometimes
- Action in present tense

The Duffers don't cover formatting in detail because it's mechanical and well-documented online.

Writing Philosophy: KEEP IT SIMPLE

The biggest mistake new writers make is overwriting. You're not writing a novel. You're writing a blueprint for what will be filmed. Guidelines:

Description:
- Brief, clear, visual
- Present tense
- No flowery prose or elaborate descriptions
- Avoid camera directions unless essential
- Focus on what can be seen/heard
- Keep paragraphs short (3-4 lines max usually)

Example of what NOT to do:
"The sun slowly rises over the majestic mountains, its golden rays painting the sky in hues of orange and pink, as if nature itself is composing a symphony of light that speaks to the eternal human condition of hope emerging from darkness."

Example of what TO do:
"Dawn. Mountains glow orange in the rising sun."

Dialogue:
- How people actually talk (contractions, interruptions, fragments)
- Character-specific voices (each person sounds different)
- Subtext (people don't say exactly what they mean)
- Brevity (cut any unnecessary words)
- Read aloud to test if it sounds natural

The Duffers emphasize: Resist the urge to prove you're a great writer with fancy prose. Prove you're a great writer by telling a compelling story clearly and efficiently.

From Outline to Script - Specific Example:

FINAL OUTLINE VERSION (Benny/Eleven scene):
"Scene where he tries to get info out of her. Asks about it. What's your name? Where are your parents? What are your parents' names? Etc."

SCRIPT VERSION:
"BENNY
Your parents forget to feed you?

The girl doesn't respond, just keeps eating.

BENNY (CONT'D)
That why you ran away?

Still nothing.

BENNY (CONT'D)
They hurt you? You went to the hospital, that it? But you got scared, ran off, found your way here?

The girl finally looks up at Benny. Has he hit close to the mark?"

Changes from Outline to Script:
- Added specific dialogue (actual questions, not paraphrased)
- Included character action/blocking (she keeps eating, looks up)
- Shows rather than tells (we see his questions escalate)
- Added the tension beat (she looks up - has he hit the mark?)
- Improved the conflict (he gets more specific, she stays silent)

Key Improvements Made During Scripting:
1. Benny becomes more active - withholds food to get information
2. Moved the "eleven tattoo reveal" to create connection between them
3. Changed psychic reveal from telling about his friend (too talky) to stopping the swinging door (visual, exciting)
4. Ending the scene on her using powers creates a cliffhanger and dramatic irony (audience knows, Benny doesn't)

When Script Doesn't Work:
If you're in the script stage and something feels wrong, DON'T try to fix it by rewriting the script. Back out to the outline, fix it there, then return to script. Making structural changes while in script is too time-consuming and confusing.

Timeline:
For the Duffers, with a solid final outline, script takes about 2 weeks. This was true for the Montauk/Stranger Things pilot. If it's taking much longer, something might be wrong with your outline.

✍️ CRAFT ELEMENTS: DESCRIPTION, ACTION & DIALOGUE

DESCRIPTION & ACTION

The Visual Medium:
You're writing for a visual medium. Everything should be written with the question: "How will this look/sound on screen?" This is not a novel where you can describe internal thoughts.

Good description is:
- Specific but brief
- Visual and concrete
- Active and dynamic
- Reveals character through action

Example from Stranger Things pilot:
"Eleven stares at the food with wide eyes."
Simple. Visual. Reveals character (she's hungry, amazed).

Bad example (what NOT to do):
"Eleven, feeling a complex mixture of hunger, fear, and longing that stems from her traumatic upbringing in the lab, gazes upon the food."
Too explanatory. Can't film internal feelings. Too wordy.

Show, Don't Tell:
Instead of: "John is angry."
Write: "John's jaw clenches. He grips the steering wheel until his knuckles turn white."

Instead of: "The room is creepy."
Write: "Cobwebs hang from the ceiling. A single bulb flickers. Somewhere, water drips."

Describing Characters:
When introducing characters, keep it simple. You don't need a paragraph.

Good example:
"MIKE WHEELER (12), short brown hair, slight build, determined eyes."

Bad example:
"MIKE WHEELER (12) has the kind of face that speaks to an inner strength born from years of being underestimated, his brown hair perpetually tousled as if reflecting his internal chaos, his slight frame belying a warrior's spirit."

Give actors room to interpret. You're providing a sketch, not a complete portrait.

Pacing in Description:
- Short paragraphs for fast pace (action, tension)
- Slightly longer for slower moments (character beats, atmosphere)
- Never more than 4-5 lines without a break
- White space on the page = easier to read = feels faster

Sound:
Capitalize important sounds or use them to punctuate:
"BANG! The door slams shut."
"The screen door SQUEAKS - e-e-e-e - incessant, annoying."

DIALOGUE ESSENTIALS

Dialogue serves multiple purposes:
1. Reveals character
2. Advances plot
3. Creates conflict
4. Provides information
5. Establishes relationships
6. Sets tone

Character-Specific Voices:
Each character should sound distinct. If you removed character names, could you tell who's speaking?

Dustin speaks differently than Mike:
- Dustin: More enthusiastic, scientific terms, rambles
- Mike: More direct, natural leader voice, protective

Steve's voice evolved:
- Season 1: Typical jock bro
- Seasons 2+: Dry humor, self-deprecating, big-brother energy

Finding voices takes time. The Duffers didn't fully know Eddie until working with Joe Quinn.

Subtext and Conflict:
Good dialogue has subtext - people don't say exactly what they mean.

On-the-nose (bad):
"I'm angry at you because you forgot my birthday and it made me feel unimportant."

Subtext (better):
"Forget something?"
"What?"
"Nothing. Never mind."

The conflict exists between what's said and what's meant.

Natural Speech Patterns:
- Use contractions ("don't" not "do not")
- Include interruptions, overlaps
- People talk in fragments, not complete sentences
- Repetition, verbal tics
- "Um," "uh," "you know" (sparingly)

Example from Stranger Things:
"I don't know, you're a freak."
Natural, teenager-speak, contractions.

Not:
"I am uncertain, but you are rather strange."
Too formal, unnatural.

Dialogue as Character Action:
Characters pursue their goals through dialogue. In the Benny/Eleven scene:
- Benny's goal: Get information
- His dialogue: Series of increasingly specific questions
- Eleven's goal: Avoid revealing herself
- Her dialogue: Silence (which is also a choice)

The conflict drives the scene, and dialogue is the battleground.

Reading Aloud:
Always read your dialogue aloud. If it sounds stilted or awkward, it needs work. Real speech flows naturally. Your ear will catch problems your eyes miss.

Common Dialogue Mistakes:
1. Too much exposition (characters explaining plot)
2. Everyone sounds the same
3. Too formal/literary
4. Says exactly what characters are feeling
5. Too clever (trying too hard to be witty)

Fix by: Cutting unnecessary words, adding subtext, making it messier/more natural, differentiating voices.

WRITING THE HOOK

The hook is your script's opening - the first 5-10 pages (or minutes of screen time). Its job: grab the reader/viewer and make them want to continue. If the hook doesn't work, nothing else matters because they won't keep reading.

What Makes a Good Hook:
- Intriguing question or mystery
- Strong sense of tone/genre
- Introduction of protagonist or key character
- Something unexpected or compelling
- Visual/cinematic (not just talking)
- Represents what the show is about

Stranger Things Hook (Cold Open):
The show opens with scientists running through Hawkins Lab, something chasing them, an elevator scene, then one scientist gets taken. This establishes:
- Something dangerous exists
- Government facility involved
- Horror/sci-fi tone
- Immediate mystery: What took him?
- Visual and suspenseful

It doesn't start with Will disappearing or the boys in the basement - it starts with pure intrigue.

The D&D Scene:
After the cold open, the actual pilot begins with the boys playing D&D. This was crucial because:
- Establishes main characters (the kids)
- Shows their friendship dynamics
- Sets up their world (suburban 80s)
- Contains conflict (D&D battle, then Will wanting protection spell)
- Ends with Will biking home alone (setup for disappearance)

Even this "normal" scene has conflict (Will vs. the Demogorgon in-game, boys vs. Mike's mom about staying up).

Timed Demo - Writing a Hook:
The Duffers wrote the opening hook for their fake "Steve and Dustin" spinoff in real-time under pressure. Their process:
1. Start with a strong visual (Dustin with Cerebro radio)
2. Establish tone (adventure, mystery)
3. Inciting incident (cryptic message)
4. Character moment (Dustin's relationship with his mother)
5. Setup for the journey (recruiting Steve)

They emphasize: even though rushed and imperfect, it captures the essence. First drafts are messy - that's okay.

Hook Mistakes to Avoid:
- Starting too slow (long exposition)
- Too much setup before anything interesting
- Confusing (trying to be clever rather than clear)
- Not representative of show's actual tone
- Introducing too many characters at once

Assignment: Write your pilot's opening 5 pages. Make it intriguing, visual, and representative of your show.

🎬 FROM SCRIPT TO SCREEN

GETTING TO THE PITCH

Once you have a polished pilot script, you're ready to shop it around. The Duffers' path:

Their First Sale (Hidden):
- Wrote under financial pressure (almost out of money)
- Outlined thoroughly first (their new method)
- Script sold in bidding war to Warner Brothers
- Able to order soda with meals (previously could only afford water)
- This proved their process worked

The moment they sold Hidden was transformative - proof that words on a page could earn money, that their careers were viable.

Montauk/Stranger Things Path:
1. Wrote the pilot script on spec (no guarantee of sale)
2. Sent one-paragraph pitch to their agent
3. Agent shopped it to networks/studios
4. Netflix showed interest
5. Took meetings to pitch the fuller vision
6. Netflix bought and greenlit to series
7. Duffers were allowed to direct (unusual for first-time showrunners)

The pitch wasn't just the script - it required a verbal presentation explaining the vision.

BUILDING THE PITCH

The pitch document/presentation for Stranger Things included:

1. The Core Concept
- Logline version (super brief)
- Extended paragraph version
- Tone references (Super 8, 80s Spielberg, Stephen King)

2. The World
- Setting: 1980s Montauk (later Hawkins)
- The Montauk Project mythology (loosely)
- The Upside Down concept (still vague)
- Government conspiracy angle

3. The Characters
Brief descriptions of main players:
- The boys (Mike, Lucas, Dustin, Will)
- Eleven (more developed)
- Joyce (Will's desperate mother)
- Hopper (checked-out sheriff drawn back in)
- Teens (Nancy, Jonathan, Steve)
- Scientists/antagonists (Brenner)

4. The Season Arc
- Pilot: Will disappears
- Season journey: Three parallel storylines (kids, teens, adults)
- How storylines converge
- Resolution: Finding Will
- Tease of Season 2: What happened to Will in the Upside Down?

5. The Generational Structure
Explained their unique approach: three age groups, each solving one piece of the puzzle, coming together in the penultimate episode.

6. Visual References
- Showed images/clips from: E.T., Poltergeist, Jaws, Close Encounters, The Goonies, Stand By Me
- Stephen King book covers
- 80s aesthetic references

7. Why It Works as a Series
- Mystery unfolds over 8 hours
- Character relationships deepen over time
- World expands gradually
- Unlike a movie, can really develop this ensemble

What They DIDN'T Include in the Pitch:
- Complete mythology of the Upside Down (not figured out)
- Detailed multi-season plan (only vague Season 2 idea)
- Every character's complete arc
- Explanation of every mystery
- Extensive world-building documents

The pitch was professional but still left room for development. Netflix didn't demand every answer.

THE NETFLIX PITCH MEETING

Key elements of a successful pitch meeting:

Energy and Passion:
The Duffers were genuinely excited about the concept. That passion is contagious. If you don't love it, why should they?

Clarity:
They could articulate the concept simply: "Supernatural kidnapping in 1980s Montauk inspired by Spielberg films and Stephen King."

Character:
They made the executives care about these characters quickly through their descriptions and the pilot script.

Vision:
They showed they understood the tone, aesthetic, and emotional core - not just plot mechanics.

Flexibility:
When Netflix suggested changing the location from Montauk (Jaws association might be limiting), they were open. It became Hawkins, Indiana - actually improved the show.

Confidence Without Arrogance:
They believed in the project but were open to collaboration and notes.

Common Pitch Mistakes:
- Over-explaining (trying to cram in every detail)
- Under-explaining (assuming they'll read your mind)
- No clear vision of tone/aesthetic
- Can't articulate why it would work as a series
- Don't know your characters well enough
- Defensive about notes or questions

The Call:
Receiving the call that Netflix was buying and greenlighting was "probably the best moment of my life" (Matt Duffer). Not just bought - greenlit to series. And they could direct.

AFTER THE SALE

Once you sell a script, development continues:

Writers' Room (for Series):
If it goes to series, you'll likely have a writers' room with other writers helping break stories for additional episodes. The Duffers hired writers who:
- Understood the tone
- Brought diverse perspectives
- Could write the specific voices
- Shared their sensibilities

Collaboration becomes key. You're no longer writing alone.

Director Involvement:
If you're directing (as the Duffers did), you're intimately involved in every aspect. If you're not directing, you still have input but must trust the director's vision.

Pre-Production:
- Location scouting
- Casting (crucial - actors change how you write characters)
- Production design
- VFX planning
- Budgeting (affects what you can actually do)

Rewrites During Production:
Scripts continue to evolve during filming. What works on page might not work on set. The Duffers regularly tweak dialogue, adjust scenes, even restructure slightly during production.

Post-Production:
- Editing (finding the show in editing)
- Music (The Duffers involve composers early)
- Sound design
- VFX completion
- Color grading

The script is the blueprint, but the show continues to evolve through every stage.

SEASON 4 BEHIND THE SCENES: LESSONS LEARNED

The COVID Shutdown - Blessing in Disguise:

Usually on Stranger Things, scripts are written quickly and shot immediately - no time for revision. COVID shut down production for six months, giving the Duffers unprecedented time to revisit completed scripts.

Benefits:
1. Planted setups they knew would pay off later (Eddie's guitar)
2. Improved character arcs (extra time to ensure each character's A-to-B journey was clear)
3. Refined dialogue (especially for new actors like Joe Quinn as Eddie)
4. Changed scenes that weren't working (Eleven hitting Angela moved from putt-putt to roller rink)
5. Strengthened the overall structure

The Duffers believe Season 4 is better because of this forced pause.

Remote Filming (Dacre Montgomery):
Dacre (Billy) was stuck in Australia, couldn't travel to the US. They needed a scene with him and Sadie (Max) at a cemetery. Solution:
- Sean Levy (director) shot Sadie's side with a stand-in
- Recorded Dacre's audio for Sadie to react to
- Months later, shot Dacre against blue screen in Australia
- Composited him into the scene
- Seamless result - you'd never know

Lesson: Creative problem-solving is essential. Obstacles can lead to interesting solutions.

Continuing Refinement:
Even after years of experience, the Duffers constantly refine:
- Outline longer than usual
- Go back to earlier episodes to plant setups
- Adjust character voices as actors inhabit roles
- Remove or add scenes based on production realities

Writing is rewriting. The script is never truly "done" until it's locked for editing.

Being Your Harshest Critic:
"Everything can always get better." The Duffers push themselves to improve every draft, every scene, every line. This perfectionism (balanced with practical deadlines) elevates the work.

💼 PRACTICAL ADVICE & WRITING HABITS

DEVELOPING A WRITING ROUTINE

The Duffers' Schedule:
- Start early: 9 AM (crucial for them)
- Work until: 6-7 PM (long day provides breathing room)
- Why it works: If mind wanders, not catastrophic; plenty of time to refocus

Their philosophy: If your brain isn't in writing mode early, it won't engage later. Starting at 2 PM never works for them.

Other Approaches That Work:

Stephen King Method:
- 2,000 words per day goal
- Done whenever that's achieved (sometimes 11:30 AM, sometimes 2 PM)
- Very productive because it's specific and achievable

Evening Writers:
- Some people need mornings free
- Write 2 hours after work every night
- Consistency matters more than timing

The Key Principles:
1. Establish a routine (same time/place)
2. Set specific goals (time-based or output-based)
3. Minimize distractions (stay off internet)
4. Find what works for YOU (everyone's different)

Location Matters:
- Duffers prefer working from home (fewer distractions)
- Some need public spaces (coffee shops) for focus
- Some need silence, others need ambient noise
- Experiment to find your ideal environment

Writing Partnership:
Having a co-writer (for the Duffers, each other) provides:
- Accountability ("What are you doing? Get back to the document!")
- Bouncing ideas off each other
- Someone to catch when you're stuck
- Balance (one pushes forward, one ensures quality)

If you don't have a writing partner:
- Find a trusted friend as a sounding board
- Create accountability (promise to send draft by X date)
- Join a writers' group
- Use deadlines to stay focused

THE PROCRASTINATION TRAP

"It's so easy to just not write anything."

The Duffers know many aspiring writers whose downfall is simple: they don't write. Procrastination, distraction, staring at blank pages, surfing the internet. Two years pass with only 10 pages written.

Why It Happens:
- Fear of failure
- Perfectionism (it's not as good as what you imagined)
- Overwhelm (too many choices, too much to figure out)
- Lack of structure (don't know where to start)
- No deadline pressure

Solutions:
1. Follow the process (breaks overwhelming task into manageable steps)
2. Set deadlines (even artificial ones)
3. Start with bad writing (perfection comes in revision)
4. Small goals (write for 1 hour, or 500 words)
5. Accountability partner

Remember: You can't edit a blank page. Bad writing can be fixed. No writing can't.

GETTING LOST IN THE WOODS

"It's so easy to get lost in the woods."

This is the most common failure mode. You're 60 pages in, something feels wrong, you can't figure out what, you abandon the script. The Duffers did this repeatedly before developing their process.

Signs You're Lost:
- Scenes feel aimless
- Characters aren't driving toward anything
- You're writing but have no idea where it's going
- Structure feels broken but you can't pinpoint why
- Rewriting the same section over and over

Prevention:
- Thorough outlining (the Duffers' main solution)
- Stay zoomed out longer before diving into detail
- Test structure at each stage before moving forward
- Back out when something's wrong rather than pushing ahead

Recovery:
If you're already lost, zoom back out:
1. Stop writing the script
2. Return to outline level
3. Identify the problem (usually structural)
4. Fix it at the outline level
5. Then return to script

Don't try to fix structural problems while in script. You'll waste weeks or months.

THE DISCIPLINE PROBLEM

"You could have the best big idea in the world, but if you don't have the discipline to sit down and write it, it's just going to sit there."

Talent isn't enough. The difference between aspiring and professional writers is often just discipline - putting in the hours consistently.

Building Discipline:
- Start small (30 minutes daily is better than 5 hours once)
- Track your progress (satisfying to see accumulation)
- Remove barriers (prep workspace night before)
- Reward milestones (finished outline? Take the evening off)
- Remember why you started (passion for the story)

The Duffers' observation: Before finding their process, writing felt like torture. After? Still difficult sometimes, but mostly enjoyable. Process removes much of the painful uncertainty.

STAYING FOCUSED

The Internet Problem:
"Try to stay off the internet." Easier said than done, but crucial.

Strategies:
- Internet blocker apps during writing time
- Phone in another room
- Website blockers (Block Site, Freedom, Cold Turkey)
- Writing offline
- Accountability partner monitoring you (like the Duffers do)

The Deadline Balance:
- Too much pressure: Anxiety, poor decisions
- Too little pressure: Endless procrastination
- Sweet spot: Enough urgency to maintain momentum

When they wrote Hidden under financial pressure, the deadline helped. But arbitrary deadlines should be flexible - don't move to the next stage before you're ready, even if you miss your target date.

SELF-CRITICISM VS. SELF-SABOTAGE

Be Your Harshest Critic:
"Everything can always get better." The Duffers constantly push themselves to improve each draft.

But avoid:
- Comparison to finished professional work (unfair comparison)
- Premature judgment (your rough draft will be rough)
- Perfectionism that prevents progress
- Giving up because it's not immediately great

Remember: Even Stranger Things started as "Sam Rockwell in a shack" and "nerd friend" with terrible spelling. Your messy first pass is normal and necessary.

🎓 KEY PRINCIPLES & PHILOSOPHY

THE PROCESS IS EVERYTHING

Process Over Talent:
The Duffers emphasize repeatedly that they didn't consider themselves "writers" initially. They struggled for years. What changed wasn't sudden talent acquisition - it was discovering a process that worked.

"If it feels like torture, you're doing something wrong."

A good process makes writing:
- Less stressful
- More enjoyable
- More efficient
- More likely to succeed

The process they developed (and teach in this course) is specifically designed to prevent the most common failures:
- Getting lost in the woods
- Starting with a flawed premise
- Structural problems discovered too late
- Character arcs that go nowhere
- Overcomplication and overwhelm

BABY STEPS

"Take it one step at a time."

This is perhaps their most repeated piece of advice. The overwhelming nature of screenwriting (creating an entire world, multiple characters, 60+ pages of content) freezes many writers.

The solution: Break it into tiny, manageable steps. Each stage should feel doable:
- Don't think about the script while brainstorming the idea
- Don't think about dialogue while outlining structure
- Don't think about seven seasons while writing the pilot

Each stage has its own focus. Master that stage before moving to the next.

Analogy: Building a House
- Big idea = Deciding to build a house
- Blue sky = Gathering ideas for the house
- Characters/setting = Knowing who lives there and where
- Story outline = Basic floor plan
- Scaffolding outline = Detailed architectural plans
- Final outline = Construction begins, frame is up
- Script = Finishing work, making it beautiful

You don't worry about paint colors when you're pouring the foundation.

STAY ZOOMED OUT

"Stay wide. Stay zoomed out."

At each stage, resist the urge to zoom in prematurely. Keep things as broad as possible for as long as possible.

Why this matters:
- Prevents getting lost in details before big picture is right
- Easier to make changes when things are less defined
- Reduces overwhelm
- Allows ideas to breathe and develop naturally
- Prevents wasting time on specifics that might change

The Eye Doctor Analogy:
Your idea starts blurry - that's normal. Different lenses (stages) gradually bring it into focus. Don't be frustrated when it's blurry at first. It should be blurry.

Red flags you've zoomed in too early:
- Writing extensive backstories before outlining
- Detailed world-building documents before knowing your story
- Perfect dialogue before structure is set
- Extensive mythology before the pilot works

WRITE WHAT YOU LOVE

Passion Is Non-Negotiable:
"You have to have a ton of passion for it."

Why passion matters:
- You'll potentially work on this for years
- Passion sustains through difficult revisions
- Passion translates to the page
- Passion is contagious in pitch meetings
- Writing without passion feels like torture

But passion doesn't mean:
- Writing only autobiography
- Ignoring craft and structure
- Being precious about every word
- Unable to take notes

Passion should fuel the work, not blind you to its flaws.

SIMPLICITY IS STRENGTH

"It's okay to be simple."

Many writers, especially beginners, try to prove themselves with complexity:
- Sprawling epics
- Intricate mythologies
- Elaborate plots
- Dozens of characters

The Duffers advocate the opposite: Tell a simple story well.

Stranger Things' core is simple:
- Boy goes missing
- Friends try to find him
- Something strange is happening in town

All the complexity (Upside Down, Eleven's powers, government conspiracy) orbits around that simple drive.

Benefits of simplicity:
- Easier to communicate
- Easier to execute well
- Less chance of getting lost
- More accessible to audiences
- Fundamentals matter more than complexity

"Even telling a simple story is much more difficult than it seems."

OUTLINE, OUTLINE, OUTLINE

"Outline, outline, outline. Plan, plan, plan."

This is the Duffers' primary technical advice. Outlining is not optional in their process - it's the foundation that makes everything else work.

Why outlining works:
- Tests structure before writing pages of script
- Easier to identify problems
- Saves enormous time in the long run
- Provides roadmap so you don't get lost
- Allows focus on craft in script stage (not figuring out what happens)

Their breakthrough: Outlining Hidden thoroughly before writing led to the script writing itself in record time and selling immediately.

Many writers skip or rush outlining because:
- It seems boring compared to "real writing"
- They're impatient to get to script
- They think they'll figure it out as they write

This is a trap. The time saved is illusory - you'll spend 10x as long trying to fix a broken script.

KNOW YOUR ENDING

"You really do need to know where their destination is."

You don't need every detail figured out, but you need to know where the story is going. At minimum:
- Where the season ends
- Where each major character's arc ends
- What question/mystery gets resolved
- What new question/mystery opens for next season

Without knowing the destination:
- Can't set up proper plants
- Character arcs meander
- Story feels aimless
- Ending feels unearned or arbitrary

Stranger Things Season 1:
- They knew: Will would be found and rescued
- They knew: But something happened to him in the Upside Down
- That was enough to structure everything

They didn't know:
- Exact mythology of the Upside Down
- Detailed Season 2-4 plans
- Every story beat along the way

Broad destination is sufficient. The journey can be discovered.

CONFLICT IN EVERY SCENE

"There is conflict... even in the quiet scenes."

Conflict doesn't require yelling, fighting, or action. Conflict means opposing goals - characters wanting different things.

The Benny/Eleven scene:
- Quiet, mostly silent
- But full of conflict:
  - Benny wants information / Eleven wants to hide
  - Benny offers food / Eleven wants more
  - Benny tries withholding / Eleven stays silent

This tension makes "quiet" scenes engaging.

Test your scenes:
- What does each character want?
- Are those wants in opposition?
- If everyone wants the same thing and easily gets it, there's no scene

BACKUP AND ZOOM OUT

"When something's not working, back out."

A crucial part of their process is willingness to retreat to an earlier stage:

In script, something's broken?
→ Back to final outline, fix it there, return to script

In final outline, structure's wrong?
→ Back to scaffolding outline, fix structure, rebuild final outline

In scaffolding, character arc isn't working?
→ Back to character development, fix arc, rebuild outline

This feels like losing progress, but actually saves time. Trying to fix a fundamental problem at the wrong stage wastes weeks or months.

Signs you need to back out:
- Fighting the material constantly
- Rewriting the same section repeatedly
- Something feels "off" but you can't identify it
- Pushing through out of stubbornness rather than confidence

ITERATION AND REFINEMENT

"Everything can always get better."

Writing is rewriting. The first draft of anything is rough. That's not just okay - it's expected.

Stranger Things' evolution:
- 2010: Rough Montauk Project idea (abandoned)
- 2013: Supernatural kidnapping concept
- 2014: Full outline and script
- During Netflix meetings: Further refinement
- In production: Continued adjustments
- Even Season 4: Improved during COVID break

Each iteration improves the work. Don't expect perfection on the first pass.

But there's a balance:
- Iterate and refine: Good
- Endlessly fiddle without finishing: Bad

Know when to move forward. Deadlines help.

BE YOUR OWN WORST CRITIC

The Duffers constantly question their work:
- Does this scene work?
- Is this character moment earned?
- Is this the best version of this beat?
- What would make this better?

But this is different from self-sabotage:
- Be critical of the work, not yourself
- Ask "How can this improve?" not "Why can't I do this?"
- Compare to your previous drafts, not to finished professional products
- Criticism should be constructive, actionable

The goal: Make each draft better than the last.

✅ COMPLETE ACTION ITEMS & ASSIGNMENTS

Lesson 1-2: Finding Your Big Idea
□ Watch/rewatch your favorite films and TV shows
□ Identify the "connective tissue" between what you love (common themes/elements)
□ Brainstorm 10-20 potential story ideas (quantity over quality)
□ Don't censor or judge ideas yet - just generate
□ Look for ideas that combine: personal experience + things you love + original twist

Lesson 3-4: Developing and Writing Your Concept
□ Pick your top 3-5 ideas from brainstorming
□ Write a short paragraph (6-8 sentences) for each idea including:
  - Protagonist (can be vague: "a teenager" not necessarily a name)
  - Inciting incident
  - The drive/goal
  - Obstacles
  - Hint of tone
□ Send these paragraphs to trusted friends for feedback
□ Select the ONE idea that most excites you
□ If torn between two ideas, try combining them

Lesson 5: Establishing Your Process
□ Identify someone to be your writing accountability partner/sounding board
□ Establish your writing routine:
  - What time of day will you write?
  - Where will you write?
  - How long will you write?
  - What's your environment (quiet, coffee shop, music)?
□ Create your first deadline (even if artificial)
□ Set up your writing software (Final Draft, WriterDuet, Celtx, etc.)

Lesson 6: Blue Sky Brainstorming
□ Create a new document for free-form brainstorming
□ Spend 2-3 weeks (not one session) adding to this document whenever ideas strike
□ Explore without judgment:
  - Character possibilities
  - Cool scene ideas
  - Plot possibilities
  - Setting details
  - Emotional moments
  - Visual ideas
□ Don't worry about spelling, grammar, or organization
□ Return to the document repeatedly - ideas build on ideas
□ Listen to instrumental music while brainstorming (if that helps you)

Lesson 7: Character Development
□ Identify your 3-5 main characters
□ For each character, determine:
  - Point A (where they start emotionally)
  - Point B (where they end up emotionally)
  - What they want (surface goal)
  - What they need (deeper truth)
  - Their wound or backstory pain (briefly)
□ Write a 2-3 sentence description of each character
□ Ensure each voice is distinct - how do they speak differently?
□ Don't write extensive backstories yet - stay zoomed out

Lesson 8: Setting and World-building
□ List 8-12 locations where scenes could take place
□ Keep it simple: "basement," "school gym," "abandoned warehouse"
□ Don't worry about how they'll be used yet
□ If your world has fantastical elements:
  - Sketch basic rules (can still be vague)
  - Identify visual inspirations (films, games, art)
  - Keep it simple for budget reasons
□ Note: You DON'T need detailed maps or extensive wikis

Lesson 9: Season Arc Development
□ Identify your story's Point A (opening situation)
□ Identify your story's Point B (resolution/destination)
□ If you have multiple storylines, ensure they all connect to one central drive
□ Write a clean, organized 1-page description including:
  - Refined concept
  - Character summaries
  - Setting overview
  - Story arc from A to B
□ This should be more polished than blue sky doc but still high-level

Lesson 10: Story Outline
□ Write a story outline with 10-20 major beats
□ Each beat is 1-2 sentences
□ Include:
  - Opening/hook
  - Inciting incident
  - Major plot movements
  - Character introduction moments
  - Act breaks
  - Climax
  - Ending
□ Focus on WHAT happens, not HOW it's written
□ Read through - does it flow? Any saggy sections?
□ Spend several weeks on this - it's the foundation

Lesson 11: Scaffolding Outline
□ Break down your story outline into individual scenes
□ For each scene write:
  - Scene location/time
  - 3-5 sentences about what happens
  - Key character beats
  - Possible dialogue snippets (if they come to you)
□ Each scene should have clear conflict (opposing goals)
□ Read through entire scaffolding - test the structure
□ If something doesn't work, back out to story outline and fix there first
□ This stage might take 4-8 weeks

Lesson 12-13: Final Outline
□ Expand scaffolding outline with much more detail:
  - Fuller description of actions/visuals
  - Character blocking and reactions
  - More emotional beats
  - Some actual dialogue (still relatively brief)
  - Transitions between scenes
□ This should read almost like a script but without formatting
□ Spelling and grammar still don't need to be perfect
□ Read through entire final outline - if it works, you're ready for script
□ If something's broken, back out and fix at appropriate stage
□ This stage might take 4-8 weeks

Lesson 14: Writing the Hook
□ Write the opening 5-10 pages of your pilot script
□ Make it:
  - Visually interesting
  - Intriguing (raises questions)
  - Representative of your show's tone
  - Introduces key character(s) or world
□ Test it on trusted friends: Do they want to keep reading?
□ Revise based on feedback

Lesson 15-17: Script to Pitch
□ Complete the full pilot script (typically 50-65 pages for 1-hour drama)
□ Timeline: Should take ~2 weeks if final outline is solid
□ Keep descriptions brief and visual
□ Make dialogue sound natural (read aloud)
□ Proofread for formatting, typos
□ Get feedback from trusted readers
□ Revise based on feedback
□ Prepare pitch materials:
  - Polished one-page logline/overview
  - Character breakdowns
  - Season arc summary
  - Visual references (tone/aesthetic)
  - Brief Season 2 tease
□ Practice pitching verbally (with partner or to mirror)

"How in the world did they do all this? How did they start with just a blank page and it became this? Well it got there very slowly. It's a messy process."

Stranger Things started with "Sam Rockwell in a shack" and "nerd friend" with terrible spelling and bad grammar. It slowly became the show through a long, messy process. The blank page is scary, but if you take it one little step at a time, suddenly it's less scary. You don't have to solve it all at once.

"I promise you it is a lot more doable than it seems."

YOU CAN DO THIS

✓ Find the right big idea (be patient with this)

✓ Take baby steps - zoom out and slowly focus
✓ Be disciplined about your routine
✓ Don't give up when it gets hard
