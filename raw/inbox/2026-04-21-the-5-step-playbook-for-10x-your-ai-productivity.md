- Title of Video: The 5 Step Playbook for 10x Your AI Productivity | Jeremy Utley [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
Jeremy Utley argues that AI mastery is less about clever prompts and more about treating AI as a **coach-able** thinking partner rather than a passive tool, using structured techniques to overcome its biases and “yes‑man” tendencies. He introduces a 5‑step playbook—context engineering, chain‑of‑thought reasoning, few‑shot prompting, reverse prompting, and role/roleplay design—to dramatically improve output quality and preserve (even strengthen) your own critical thinking. Throughout, he shows how these methods turn AI into a flight simulator for difficult conversations and complex work, expanding your “adjacent possible” in what you can imagine and execute with AI.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- AI behaves like an overly eager intern: helpful, fast, but conflict‑averse and prone to gaslighting you with fake confidence unless you explicitly ask for critical, even brutal, feedback.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Context engineering—supplying voice, brand, data, constraints, and examples—turns vague prompts into precise instructions that a human (and thus AI) can reliably execute.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Asking AI to “think out loud” (chain‑of‑thought) and giving it good/bad examples (few‑shot prompting) massively improves quality and transparency of outputs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Reverse prompting (letting AI ask clarifying questions) and role assignment (e.g., “Dale Carnegie‑style communications coach”) align its reasoning with your goals and knowledge domain.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Roleplaying and feedback loops with AI create a safe simulator for tough conversations, making the best AI users not coders but “coaches” who know how to get great work out of another intelligence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. AI as “bad software, good people”
    

- Utley describes AI as “bad software but good people”: systems are clunky, but the underlying generative capability is like a hyper‑eager intern—tireless, compliant, and reluctant to push back.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Because AI is trained to be a “helpful assistant,” it tends to say “yes,” make things up (e.g., sales figures), or ask you to “check back later” instead of admitting limits, which can effectively gaslight users.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- The best AI users behave like coaches, not programmers: they manage the intern, set expectations, demand critique, and iterate instructions to get high‑quality thinking.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Context Engineering
    

- “Context engineering” is presented as prompt engineering “on steroids”: systematically providing every piece of information AI needs to produce an output that matches your real constraints and style.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Example: instead of “write me a sales email,” you provide brand voice guidelines, a transcript of a customer call, and product specs, then ask for an email reflecting all of that.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Litmus test: if a human colleague could not execute your written prompt plus attachments, you should not expect AI to succeed either; implicit assumptions must be made explicit.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Cognitive offloading and AI as mirror
    

- There is concern that AI makes people “fall asleep at the wheel” by offloading too much thinking, but Utley frames AI as a mirror: it amplifies laziness in those who want to offload, and sharpness in those who want to think critically.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- You can explicitly instruct AI to preserve your analytical edge (e.g., “push my critical thinking, question my assumptions”), turning it into a tool for metacognitive training rather than mental outsourcing.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Chain of Thought Reasoning
    

- “Thinking out loud” is known to improve human problem solving; similarly, instructing AI to walk through its step‑by‑step reasoning (“Before you respond, walk me through your thought process step by step”) improves the model’s outputs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Technically, large language models generate text one token at a time, using your prompt plus everything generated so far to predict the next token; when you ask for reasoning first, that reasoning text itself becomes context the model then uses to craft a better final answer.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- This approach surfaces the assumptions baked into the answer, allowing you to evaluate not only the output but also the logic behind it.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Few‑Shot Prompting
    

- Few‑shot prompting leverages AI as an imitation engine: if you give no examples, it imitates an average internet‑style answer; with strong examples, it imitates what “good” looks like to you.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- He recommends collecting your “five greatest hits” (e.g., best emails), giving them as positive examples, and optionally adding a deliberately bad example to avoid, which can be generated by AI itself based on your good sample.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Combining few‑shot prompting with chain‑of‑thought (having the AI explain why the bad example is bad) clarifies underlying quality criteria like concision vs verbosity.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Reverse Prompting
    

- Reverse prompting means telling the model to ask you for any missing information before answering, especially where hallucinations are likely (e.g., “Before you get started, ask me for any information you need to do a good job”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- This flips AI from a silent, guessing assistant into a junior colleague who clarifies requirements—mirroring good management practice (“If you have questions, ask”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- It reduces fabricated numbers and misaligned assumptions by forcing a short Q&A loop before generation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Role Assignment
    

- Assigning AI a role (teacher, reporter, molecular biologist, communications expert, Dale Carnegie, etc.) tells it what part of its training corpus and associations to foreground.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Roles act as constraints and lenses, helping the model prioritize certain styles, frameworks, and connections that better match your task (e.g., persuasion vs technical accuracy).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Trying on multiple roles (“How would Jerry Seinfeld solve this? How would Amazon? How would Elon Musk?”) generates diverse solution angles by colliding different knowledge patterns.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Roleplaying Difficult Conversations
    

- Utley demonstrates a three‑agent setup for tough conversations: a personality profiler, a roleplaying counterpart (e.g., “Jim” the sales leader), and a feedback evaluator.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Workflow: profile the other person (communication style, objectives, context), generate an instruction set, paste it into a new chat where AI plays that person, then run the conversation and finally send the transcript to a feedback‑oriented model for scoring and coaching.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- He iterates on “Jim” by asking for more edge and confrontation, showing how you can tune realism until the simulation feels sufficiently challenging.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. AI and the “Adjacent Possible”
    

- Utley connects AI collaboration to the innovation concept of the “adjacent possible”: what becomes possible just beyond current reality when new capabilities or combinations emerge.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Quoting Thomas Schelling’s idea that no matter how heroic someone’s imagination, they cannot think of what would never occur to them, he argues that expanding AI fluency across more people expands our collective imagination space.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Therefore, the true bottleneck is not AI capability but human imagination and willingness to experiment with these collaborative patterns.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Turn AI into a harsh reviewer
    

- Add explicit instructions such as “Adopt the mindset of a Cold War–era Russian Olympic judge: be brutal, exacting, and deduct points for every minor flaw; I can handle tough feedback.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Use this for drafts, presentations, sales emails, or strategy docs to get non‑sugar‑coated critique, instead of generic “Great job!” responses.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Upgrade your prompts with context engineering
    

- For any serious task, include: your voice/brand guidelines, relevant transcripts or documents, explicit goals, constraints, and success criteria (e.g., audience, tone, length, metrics to preserve).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Before sending, apply the “human test”: if a colleague couldn’t execute using only what you’ve provided, add missing context until they could.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Use chain‑of‑thought in complex tasks
    

- Add: “Before answering, walk me through your thought process step by step” for non‑trivial tasks like strategy options, product decisions, or learning plans.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Review the reasoning, correct wrong assumptions, then ask the model to regenerate the final output with those corrections incorporated.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Build your few‑shot library
    

- Collect a small corpus of your best work in key domains (emails, LinkedIn posts, reports, ad copy) and use them directly as “good examples” in prompts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- When you need a contrasting “bad example,” ask AI to produce the opposite of your good sample, and have it explain what makes it poor so you can encode these failure modes in your instructions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Make AI ask clarifying questions
    

- End important prompts with a line like: “Before you begin, ask me any specific questions you need to produce an accurate and high‑quality result.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Treat the initial back‑and‑forth as a scoping conversation, not a delay; this investment reduces hallucinations and rework later.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Design roles and constraints deliberately
    

- Prefix prompts with roles (e.g., “You are a skeptical CFO,” “You are a negotiation coach trained in Getting to Yes,” “You are a Stanford design thinking instructor”), especially when you need domain‑specific reasoning.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Explore multiple role variants on the same problem to get a portfolio of perspectives before deciding on a course of action.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Create a “conversation flight simulator”
    

- For any upcoming difficult conversation (performance review, salary negotiation, conflict with a stakeholder), follow Utley’s three‑part pattern: profile the other person, roleplay the conversation, and then get structured feedback on transcripts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Iterate a few times until the conversation feels natural and you have a concise one‑pager of talking points ordered by when they are likely to arise.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

1. Protect and train your critical thinking
    

- Add standing instructions to your AI: “Help me stay a sharp, critical thinker; challenge my assumptions, highlight potential biases, and propose alternative interpretations when appropriate.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Periodically review where you may be over‑relying on AI (e.g., accepting uncritically, skipping your own checks) and adjust prompts to re‑insert your own analysis in the loop.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- AI reproduces human cognitive biases: Utley notes that large language models demonstrate essentially all major human biases, meaning you should treat them like another human thinker whose blind spots must be managed.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Many frustrations with AI (e.g., invented data, shallow outputs) are not “model failures” but prompt‑design failures—users expect mind‑reading instead of doing the work of making implicit assumptions explicit.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- The “best users are coaches” framing is crucial: people experienced in getting great work out of junior colleagues or students have a head start over pure coders in extracting value from AI.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- His demonstration of using AI across multiple windows/agents (profiler, roleplayer, grader) implicitly suggests a broader methodology: decomposing complex workflows into specialized roles and connecting them via structured handoffs and artifacts (like transcripts and instruction sets).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
    
- Ambiguous/unclear area: in practice, many deployed assistants now restrict explicit chain‑of‑thought exposure for safety reasons; users may be told that detailed internal reasoning cannot be shown, so the “walk me through your thought process” pattern might need adaptation to higher‑level, non‑verbatim rationales.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yMOmmnjy3sE&list=WL&index=19)
