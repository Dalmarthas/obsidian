Rory Sutherland explains the **Bottleneck Theory (Theory of Constraints)** as a powerful but often underused framework in business and marketing. The central idea is that any system’s performance is limited by its narrowest point or the true constraint—just like water flow is limited by the bottleneck in a pipe. Most brands make the mistake of optimizing areas that aren’t actually limiting their growth. Instead, you should **identify and address the main constraint** in your business—whether it’s logistics, brand awareness, distribution, or another factor—because improving other parts of the system has little impact while the bottleneck remains unrepaired.

He draws examples from:

- **Mental vs. Physical Availability**: Coke is so mentally available that if a restaurant doesn’t have it, it’s odd, whereas something like Dr. Pepper isn’t expected. The same applies to condiments—restaurants must have ketchup or Tabasco, depending on local norms.
- **Changing Bottlenecks**: Fixing one bottleneck often shifts the limitation elsewhere; for example, fixing a narrow road only moves the traffic jam to the next set of badly timed traffic lights. You need to continually look for the _current_ constraint rather than rely on one-size-fits-all solutions or models.
- **Diversity in Approaches**: Over-reliance on single business models has stifled creativity and intuition. Instead, just as a good detective uses every tool (not just DNA), business leaders should deploy various methods and mental models to find and solve constraints.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Find and fix the real bottleneck; improvements elsewhere won’t matter until the constraint is addressed.
- Bottlenecks move—solving one will usually reveal the next.
- Success often comes from improving the vital few (the 20% that yields 80% of your outcomes).

This mindset is valuable for growth in both startups and established companies and challenges the tendency to optimize things that aren’t actually holding back progress.
