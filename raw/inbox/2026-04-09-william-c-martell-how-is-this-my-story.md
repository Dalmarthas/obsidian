**Executive Summary**

This interview with screenwriter William C. Martell explores the pivotal question every screenwriter must answer: “How is this my story?” Martell contends that deep personal connection and authenticity are essential for powerful screenwriting, regardless of the assignment’s topic. He illustrates how writers must find an honest emotional entry point into every project to create work that is both compelling and professionally sustainable.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **Personal Connection:** A screenwriter must find a personal anchor or emotional link within the story, even in assignments or commercially driven projects.
- **Authenticity > Hack Work:** Writing solely for profit, without genuine connection, leads to uninspired work, multiple rewrites, and dissatisfaction.
- **Doorway Principle:** Every narrative requires identifying “your doorway”—the specific way you emotionally or intellectually access and relate to the characters and themes.
- **Assignment Adaptation:** Accept only those projects where you can authentically adapt the premise with your voice; decline stories fundamentally alien or distasteful.
- **Transform Genre Expectations:** Even in restrictive or formulaic genres, writers can (and should) inject personal meaning or subtext that goes beyond the obvious plot.

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

- **“How Is This My Story?”:**
    
    The central inquiry for writers; Martell emphasizes evaluating how a story relates to one’s personal experiences, values, or fears. If such a connection is absent, the work risks becoming sterile and mechanical.
    
- **The Writer’s Isolation and Emotional Mining:**
    
    Writers tend to shun conflict in life but must mine those very emotions—conflict, passion, fear—to craft engaging narratives.
    
- **Assignment Example (Jack Ryan):**
    
    Even when unfamiliar with specific plot circumstances (e.g., not being in the Navy for Jack Ryan stories), Martell identifies with the “fish-out-of-water” archetype—thus finding an authentic entry point.
    
- **Assignment Example (LEGO Movie, Finland ‘TAKEN’):**
    
    Martell demonstrates reshaping projects by inserting personal motifs. For the LEGO movie (rebellion in an “everything is awesome” world) and a “TAKEN” ripoff (novelist thrust into action), this personal storytelling angle adds originality and emotional depth.
    
- **Rejecting Distasteful or Alien Projects:**
    
    Certain subject matter is a “hard no” for Martell—stories that run counter to his core values or require defending indefensible characters/actions cannot yield honest writing and should be declined.
    
- **Genre Transformation:**
    
    In the example of writing a “torture horror” story, Martell explains pitching a subversive angle (forgiveness/understanding over violence), even when the genre typically prioritizes brutality.
    

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- **Probe for Connection:** Always ask, “What part of this story is genuinely mine?” before accepting any assignment.
- **Reframe Assignments:** If a project feels shallow at first, seek ways to reinterpret or adapt it through your emotional lens.
- **Stand by Integrity:** Refuse assignments fundamentally incompatible with your values or creative instincts; long-term, these seldom produce worthwhile work.
- **Subvert from Within:** In restrictive genres, look for opportunities to add substance, deeper meaning, or a quietly personal twist.
- **Voice Your Pitch:** When collaborating with producers, articulate clearly how your unique angle fits within (or transforms) the expected parameters.

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- **Emotional Honesty = Stronger Storytelling:**
    
    Martell’s approach is rooted in the belief that authenticity cannot be faked—audiences sense when a narrative lacks honest emotional investment.
    
- **Assignment Survival:**
    
    For professional screenwriters, longevity is tied to project selection and the ability to bring one’s distinct voice to “work-for-hire” jobs. Mechanical output (“just typing for money”) drains creativity and motivation.
    
- **Personal Red Line:**
    
    Stories or genres that strongly conflict with the writer’s principles are best avoided, even at the risk of lost income or industry connections.
    
- **Framework for Decision-Making:**
    
    Use the “doorway” metaphor as a screening tool: If no entry point exists, decline the project. If one can be constructed, move forward.
    
- **Ambiguity Flag:**
    
    The notion of “reframing” projects is subjective—what works for Martell may not universally apply. Determining one’s own “doorways” requires honest self-reflection and ongoing ethical calibration.
