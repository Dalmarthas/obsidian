**Executive Summary**

In this talk, Jeremy Utley, Stanford professor and AI creativity expert, presents a practical framework for maximizing productivity with AI tools. He emphasizes that effective AI use resembles coaching eager interns rather than programming code—context, role assignment, and critical feedback drive superior results. Utley provides actionable methodologies, including Chain-of-Thought Reasoning and Roleplaying, to help users elicit deeper, more valuable outputs from AI and avoid its misleading tendencies.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **Imagination, not technology, is the main limiter in AI-driven work.** Users should become “coaches,” not coders.
- **AI is designed to be “helpful,” often to a fault—prompting users to specify context, roles, and desired feedback for best results.**
- **Five core productivity techniques unlock AI’s full potential:** Chain-of-Thought Reasoning, Few-Shot Prompting, Reverse Prompting, Role Assignment, and Roleplaying.
- **Collaboration with AI should mimic collaboration with people:** let it ask for missing info, provide examples, and demand honest critique.
- **Customizing AI workflows increases depth, originality, and critical insight—turning shallow replies into high-value partnerships.**

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

- **Context Engineering:**
    
    Evolved from prompt engineering, this practice involves explicitly supplying AI with all necessary guidelines, tone, examples, and supporting documentation for a given task. Without context, AI delivers generic outputs; rich context drives personal, authentic results.
    
- **AI as Eager Intern/Coach Paradigm:**
    
    AI’s base tendency is to say “yes” and provide answers, even when uncertain or lacking information—akin to an intern eager to please. Users must take on a coaching role, strategically guiding the AI.
    
- **Chain-of-Thought Reasoning:**
    
    Direct AI to verbalize thought processes step-by-step before giving an answer. This provides transparency, reveals assumptions, and enables more reliable, logical outputs.
    
- **Few-Shot Prompting:**
    
    Provide 2–5 good (and, if possible, bad) examples of the desired response. This clarity allows AI to imitate the correct pattern, improving quality and relevance.
    
- **Reverse Prompting:**
    
    Instruct AI to ask questions before answering when lacking critical information. This makes interaction more collaborative and less error-prone.
    
- **Role Assignment & Roleplaying:**
    
    Explicitly set the AI’s “identity” (e.g., a tough reviewer, a specific expert, a coach) or simulate conversations with multiple roles to gain deeper insight, tougher critique, or training in difficult interactions.
    

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- Always specify not only “what” but “how” you want the AI to respond: define tone, format, example, and intent.
- Before requesting output, tell AI: “Explain step-by-step your thinking process.” Use this for emails, analysis, and creative tasks.
- If AI invents data (e.g., sales figures), reverse prompt: “Ask me for information you need before answering.”
- Use role assignment when seeking critique: “Act as a harsh Olympic judge, be brutally honest.”
- For preparation (e.g., feedback, negotiation), simulate multi-character conversations—have AI roleplay various participants and a feedback provider.
- Encourage the AI to surface its own questions and assumptions as part of its output.

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- AI displays many human cognitive biases. Treating it as a peer and inviting critical feedback makes it a better partner.
- Practice “context engineering” with real-life documentation: if another human couldn’t perform the task with what you provide, neither can the AI.
- The real bottleneck for AI-augmented productivity is human creativity, not technical limitations. Enhancing “imagination flow” via tools like AI expands what’s possible.
- AI tools can grade, critique, and suggest improvements on practice conversations—enabling “flight simulator” style rehearsal for difficult real-world tasks.
- Role assignment unlocks otherwise inaccessible knowledge; naming experts (e.g., “Think like Dale Carnegie”) or archetypes increases the richness of AI’s output.
- Encourage ongoing iteration—adjust prompts, roles, and context based on feedback and results.
