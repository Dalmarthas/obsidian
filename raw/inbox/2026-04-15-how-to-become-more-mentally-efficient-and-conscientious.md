- Title of Video: How to Become More Mentally Efficient & Conscientious[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

---

📝 Executive Summary  
The speaker argues that conscientiousness is not a fixed trait and can be systematically trained by leveraging altruistic behavior—doing deliberate, effortful things for others at a cost to oneself. By repeatedly setting aside one’s own impulses in favor of helping others, you strengthen the “impulse‑control muscle” that underlies conscientiousness, which then generalizes to studying, working, and long‑term goals. He links this mechanism to both modern personality psychology (Big Five) and ancient karmic ideas about “doing good for others improves your life.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)

---

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Conscientiousness (discipline, follow‑through, impulse control) is strongly associated with success in modern society but is often low in many gamers and procrastinators.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The core of conscientiousness is the cognitive act of setting aside what you want right now, not some abstract “being good.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Altruistic actions that cost you time, comfort, or resources are a practical training ground for this impulse‑control faculty.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- This model provides a psychological explanation for karma: helping others improves your own life by rewiring your brain toward greater self‑control and work capacity.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The approach is supported by Big Five personality research, examples from medical trainees, “altruistic sociopaths,” and the speaker’s own low‑conscientiousness background.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)

💡 Core Concepts

1. Big Five Personality and Conscientiousness
    

- The talk situates conscientiousness within the Big Five: extraversion, openness, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and neuroticism.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Conscientiousness is defined as the ability to sustain focus on tasks, delay gratification, control impulses, and follow through on responsibilities.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- High‑conscientiousness individuals more easily choose tasks they “do not want to do” (e.g., studying) over short‑term pleasures (e.g., gaming), while low‑conscientiousness individuals habitually give in to immediate impulses.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Society disproportionately rewards high conscientiousness (e.g., medical students scoring high on both conscientiousness and neuroticism, worrying about grades and thus working hard).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

1. The Real Cognitive Act: Setting Aside What You Want
    

- The speaker reframes the typical dichotomy “do what is good for me vs what I want” into the simpler mental operation: can I set aside what I want at this moment.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Whether the choice is “study vs Apex Legends,” the key variable is not morality or abstract value but the capacity to suppress the immediate desire.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Conscientiousness is therefore functionally equivalent to reliable impulse control, applied across domains like work, study, and habits.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

1. Altruism as a Training Mechanism for Conscientiousness
    

- Helping others almost always carries a personal cost: time, energy, resources, or lost leisure.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Example: choosing to share a pizza, or cooking soup for a sick friend, forces you to override the impulse to keep all the food or use the time for games.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Each altruistic act involves the same key cognitive step: restraining self‑serving impulses in favor of another person’s benefit.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Thus, intentional altruism becomes a repeatable “gym” for practicing impulse control, which in turn builds the underlying mental structure of conscientiousness.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

1. Distinguishing Empathy from Chosen Altruism
    

- Empathy (feeling what others feel) is different from the mechanism described; many altruistic people are not strongly empathic, they simply choose to act well.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The speaker introduces the idea of an “altruistic sociopath”: individuals who score high on sociopathy scales (low baseline empathy) but perform many prosocial actions for cognitive, chosen reasons.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Professions like psychiatry and surgery may select for people with higher sociopathy scores because some emotional insulation is protective and allows effective work.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- This shows that the training of conscientiousness via altruism does not depend on feeling others’ emotions; it depends on the deliberate act of putting others first.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

1. Karma Reinterpreted Through Personality Psychology
    

- Traditional karmic teachings state that doing good for others improves your own life, which seems metaphysically vague at a “cosmic energy” level.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The speaker proposes a psychological mechanism: when you consistently act for others at a cost to yourself, you literally train your brain’s capacity for impulse control and self‑restraint.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Over time, this improved conscientiousness leads to better studying, more disciplined work, improved relationships, and increased respect—from which life outcomes improve.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- In this reading, you yourself are the “medium” through which karma operates: helping an old lady with groceries and later getting a better life are connected by changes in your own brain and behavior.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    

1. Anecdotal Evidence and Self‑Application
    

- The speaker reports piloting this framework for about six to seven months with several coaching clients who had low conscientiousness and interpersonal problems.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- One example: a person with a degree but no job for eight years first focused on repairing relationships and acting more for others; subsequently, they were able to secure a job in their trained field, experiencing a “night and day” difference.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The speaker also describes himself as “vata‑minded” and low in conscientiousness, saying that thinking about helping his audience motivates him to write tens of thousands of words instead of playing video games.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)

🔨 Actionable Tips

- Intentionally choose small, concrete altruistic acts that cost you something.  
    Examples: sharing food, running an errand for a friend, cooking for someone sick, or helping with chores when you’d rather game.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Treat each altruistic action as deliberate training of your impulse‑control “muscle.”  
    When you notice the impulse (“I want to keep my time/food/money”), consciously choose the other person’s benefit and pay attention to the internal resistance.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Start with low‑stakes situations and gradually increase difficulty.  
    Begin with brief favors (5–15 minutes) and move toward larger commitments (helping someone move, volunteering, mentoring).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Reflect on the mental shift after each act.  
    Ask: “Where did I put myself second? How did I override my impulse?” This metacognitive awareness reinforces the connection between altruism and impulse control.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Transfer the trained impulse control to personal goals.  
    Once you notice it is easier to set yourself aside for others, apply the same mental move to studying, cleaning your room, updating your resume, or learning a new skill instead of defaulting to entertainment.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Use “doing it for someone else” as a motivational hack when self‑interest is not enough.  
    For example, study because your future colleagues, family, or users will rely on your competence; work out because others depend on your health and presence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)

🔍 Additional Insights

- Low conscientiousness is reframed from a fixed personality deficit into a trainable pattern, using a domain (helping others) where many low‑conscientiousness people are surprisingly willing to exert effort.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- The gaming community is highlighted as particularly capable of mobilizing effort for others (e.g., helping friends), suggesting a high potential reservoir for this training approach.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Neuroticism, often seen as purely negative, can be adaptive when paired with conscientiousness, as worry about consequences can fuel hard work and preparation (e.g., pre‑med students).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- There is an implicit critique of purely self‑optimization‑focused productivity advice; here, prosocial behavior is not an ethical afterthought but the central method for building discipline.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
    
- Ambiguities and limitations: evidence is primarily anecdotal (clients, personal experience) and theoretically linked to Big Five research and karmic concepts rather than supported by formal longitudinal trials. The exact dosage, time course, and boundary conditions (e.g., risk of burnout or self‑neglect) are not rigorously specified.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aKHov_Nuhso&list=WL&index=9)
