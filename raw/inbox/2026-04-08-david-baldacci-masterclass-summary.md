📋 OVERVIEW

David Baldacci, bestselling author of political thrillers including "Absolute Power," shares his comprehensive approach to writing compelling thriller fiction. This masterclass covers the entire journey from finding initial story ideas to navigating the publishing business. Baldacci draws from his experience as a former trial lawyer who wrote his first novel while working full-time, eventually achieving the highest advance ever paid for a first novel in U.S. publishing history.

The course emphasizes that writing is not just a job or hobby, but a lifestyle that requires viewing the world through a "writer's prism." Baldacci stresses the importance of immersive research, disciplined writing processes, and building authentic characters and plots grounded in plausibility. He advocates for a collaborative relationship with editors, consistent publication schedules, and treating writing as a craft that improves through dedication and practice.

Throughout the course, Baldacci provides edited manuscript pages, editorial letters, and real examples from his published works, demonstrating how he translates outlines into finished prose and transforms first drafts into polished novels through rigorous editing.

🎯 CORE THEMES

WRITING AS A LIFESTYLE
• Writing isn't an occupation—it's a way of living and seeing the world
• Writers must view everything through a "writer's prism," seeing potential in ordinary moments
• The compulsion to write is what separates true writers from those who merely want to have written
• If you can't stop writing, even without publication, you're meant to be a writer
• This inner drive will sustain you through rejection and periods of doubt

THE POWER OF RESEARCH
• Research adds depth and authenticity that lifts work out of the "slush pile"
• Immersive, firsthand research creates emotional connections to material
• The goal is to experience things, not just learn facts from Wikipedia
• Writers should become journalists, interviewing experts and visiting locations
• Most research should be left out—distill massive amounts of information into small, essential details

PLAUSIBILITY OVER PERFECTION
• Baldacci's rule: "I'm only bound by plausibility—if it could happen, I can write about it"
• Organizations are made up of people who make mistakes, so any scenario is believable
• As one intelligence contact told him: "If you can imagine it, we've already done it"
• Build the real world first, then add a layer of creative liberty on top
• Ground action and plot in realistic consequences and human limitations

COMMITMENT AND PERSISTENCE
• Baldacci wrote for nearly two decades before his breakthrough success
• He worked full-time as a lawyer while writing from 10 PM to 2 AM every night
• Rejection and doubt are universal experiences for writers
• Follow the words and follow your heart—good things will happen
• If you truly need to write, you'll find the time regardless of circumstances

📚 KEY CONCEPTS & FRAMEWORKS

THE WRITER'S PRISM
Definition: A slightly "cocked" way of viewing the world that reveals story potential in everyday scenes
• See the world as everyone else does, but recognize what could be with "fictional pixie dust"
• Connect seemingly unrelated dots that 99.9% of people miss
• Transform ordinary moments (a plane overhead, a truck passing) into compelling stories
• Maintain this perspective 24/7—ideas emerge constantly when living as a writer

Example: At church before his son's confirmation, Baldacci conceived the plot for "One Summer" by reflecting on mortality and family while waiting for mass to begin

THE BIG POP
Purpose: The compelling opening that hooks readers and drives the entire story
• If you don't get the opening right, nothing else matters
• The "big pop" is a self-contained short story that opens plot possibilities
• Everything in the first chapter threads through the rest of the novel
• Return to the first chapter constantly as your "touchstone" and "mothership"

Examples:
- Someone dies or is killed
- A plane crashes
- A government facility is robbed
- A character escapes from an "escape-proof" prison

GEOGRAPHIC LOCATIONS AS CHARACTERS
Principle: Settings should rise beyond mere backdrops to become active story elements
• Locations can imprint themselves on characters' souls and psyches
• Settings can drive motivations and make characters do things they wouldn't otherwise do
• Pick locations strategically for their potential impact on plot and character
• Extraordinary places can be centers of despair rather than exotic destinations

Example: "The Fallen" - Set in a Rust Belt town where opioid addiction and hopelessness permeate both plot and character motivations, making terrible actions understandable

THE RESEARCH BATTLE PLAN
Process: Structured approach to gathering information for authentic storytelling

1. Make calls, write letters, send emails to arrange meetings
2. Prepare thoroughly before interviews—show respect through homework
3. Learn about subjects before meeting experts (never ask stupid questions)
4. Allow flexibility to adapt organically to discoveries on the ground
5. Measure and pace off locations, choreograph action scenes
6. Take detailed notes, photos, recordings (always ask permission)
7. Visit places firsthand rather than relying on third-hand accounts

Research Ethics:
• Always ask permission before recording
• Never record someone without consent
• For government employees, use private email/phones (never official channels)
• Treat sources with courtesy, disclosure, and transparency
• Ask if you can follow up with additional questions

DISTILLATION PRINCIPLE
Rule: Leave almost all research out of the final manuscript
• "If you do nothing else with what I'm telling you on research, do this"
• Example: Entire binder of nuclear weapons research → 2.5 pages in "Zero Day"
• Use an expert character to walk laymen (readers) through technical material
• Simplify without dumbing down—use analogies and accessible language
• The temptation is to include everything you learned—resist it

RESEARCH AS PROCRASTINATION WARNING
• Research is fun but not as hard as actually writing the story
• Writers can convince themselves they're working by over-researching
• Balance is key: research a little, write a little, research a little more
• You'll reach an instinctual point where research is done
• At some point, you must "grow up and stop the research and write the book"

THE THREE QUESTIONS FOR EVERY CHAPTER
Ask yourself for each chapter:
1. Am I conveying information?
2. Am I developing character?
3. Am I moving the plot forward?

If you can't answer at least one positively, don't write the chapter. Each scene should serve multiple purposes—like in screenwriting, justify why the scene exists and what it costs (in reader attention).

PACING THROUGH CHAPTER LENGTH
Evolution: Baldacci shifted from long chapters (15-20 pages) to shorter chapters (2-4 pages)
• Short chapters are "rapid punches in the face"
• Creates rhythm and momentum appropriate for thrillers
• Forces discipline—no room to fudge or hide weaknesses
• Every word must hit its mark in short chapters
• Rapid chapter turnover keeps readers engaged and on edge

CHAPTER DIVERSITY AND BALANCE
Elements to blend within chapters:
• Narrative/expository material (moving characters A to B)
• Interior monologue (character thinking through problems)
• Dialogue (conversations that reveal character and information)
• Action (movement and conflict)

Balance considerations:
• Not every chapter needs all elements
• Three or four chapters of only narrative may feel unbalanced
• Look at chapters visually—break up monotonous blocks
• Consider what comes before and after each chapter
• Add variety for the reader's sake as well as the story's

✍️ THE WRITING PROCESS

FINDING IDEAS

The Writer's Mindset:
• Ideas come from "waking up in the morning and walking out the door"
• Be receptive to what the world gives you, then run it through your creativity filter
• If you look at the world differently, you'll never run out of ideas
• Train yourself to see the "dark side" and potential problems in every situation

Practical Exercises:
• Take two unrelated newspaper stories and combine them into one plausible narrative
• Connect disparate elements (a person walking, a car driving) in believable ways
• These exercises take you out of your comfort zone and fire up creativity

Using Current Events:
• Incorporate real-world issues to connect with readers
• Examples: CTE in football (Amos Decker series), opioid crisis ("The Fallen")
• Present facts without preaching opinions
• Allow readers to form their own conclusions about issues
• Choose topics you genuinely care about for sustained motivation

Letting Ideas Percolate:
• Don't immediately start writing when an idea strikes
• Let ideas germinate in your head for weeks
• Your brain works like a computer, building threads naturally
• Consider: characters, plot twists, setting, dialogue, research needs
• This prevents running out of steam 100 pages into a manuscript

OUTLINING APPROACH

From Outline to Prose:
• Start with bullet points for key scenes
• Example from "The Innocent": "Julie Getty, pepper spray, hit man, bus explodes"
• Six bullet points became two published pages
• Organic growth happens during writing—inspiration adds details not in outline
• Not every bullet point makes it in; new elements always emerge

DAILY WRITING PRACTICE

Finding Time:
• Baldacci wrote 10 PM to 2 AM every night while working as a lawyer
• Used lunch breaks to jot story ideas and notes
• "If you really wanted to be a writer, you would find the time"
• Writing must be incorporated into daily routine, not treated as separate from life
• True writers can't NOT write—there's a void if they haven't written that day

Work Approach:
• Baldacci has no set word count or page count daily goals
• Writes until his "tank is empty" that day (could be 0 words or 4,000)
• Cares about quality of words, not quantity
• Process varies—atypical is typical for him
• Some days are for thinking, researching, interviewing, or editing instead of writing

Writing Environment:
• "The perfect place to write is in your head"
• If you're "in the zone" mentally, you can write anywhere
• Baldacci writes on trains, planes, cars, restaurants with 150 people around
• Create a mental cocoon regardless of physical surroundings
• Don't depend on pristine physical conditions—ground yourself in the material

Immersion Technique:
• Never sit down cold to write a new chapter
• Read opening chapters to get re-energized
• Read two preceding chapters to get back into flow
• This is like warming up before exercise—prevents "pulling a hamstring"
• Sparks ideas and maintains language consistency for the novel

Dealing with Writer's Block:
• All writers stumble over plot problems—anyone who denies it is lying
• Three solutions:
  1. Work on another project (newspaper piece, short story, screenplay)
  2. Take a shower—water triggers plot solutions (Baldacci is "astonishingly clean")
  3. Go for a walk—wandering minds solve complicated plot problems
• Long walks free your mind; tension blocks clear thinking
• Allow your subconscious to work on problems while doing something else

MANAGING DEADLINES

Publishing Schedule:
• Baldacci publishes April and November every year (two books annually)
• Works backward from publication dates to set delivery deadlines
• Knows exactly when first drafts must be delivered
• Plans editing process completion dates in advance

Self-Imposed Deadlines:
• Internal deadlines matter more than publisher deadlines
• Pressure exists but immersion in material helps meet deadlines
• Must live the material and become one with it
• Revelations and creativity come from deep immersion
• Balance pressure with not walking away from material for too long

Editing Speed:
• Baldacci is "the fastest editor in the West"
• Publishers expect manuscripts back in a month; he returns them in four days
• Totally immerses himself in editing process
• Can make up for late first draft delivery through fast editing
• The book always improves significantly in editing

🎭 CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

CREATING COMPELLING CHARACTERS

Deep Character Work:
• Characters must feel real, complex, and flawed
• Show characters in their personal spaces to reveal who they are
• Will Roby's apartment has no personal effects—he's an "invisible force"
• This lifestyle explains his reactions and choices throughout the story

Character Through Action:
• Action scenes reveal character as much as dialogue
• Will Roby's professionalism shows through how he plans and executes missions
• The setup, execution, and egress all demonstrate character traits
• In fight scenes, show consequences—both sides get hurt

Character Consistency in Series:
• Go back and reread previous books before writing new series installments
• Check facts about characters to ensure consistency
• Inconsistency destroys reader trust immediately
• "If readers don't think you can master your own material, they don't trust you to do anything else right"

Character Arcs in Series:
• Characters must evolve plausibly across books
• If a character changes dramatically, provide adequate explanation
• Series allow you to deepen characters book by book
• Like aging—life etches experiences, scars, and growth onto people

⚡ WRITING ACTION AND SUSPENSE

PRINCIPLES OF ACTION WRITING

Action Can Serve Multiple Purposes:
• Provides adrenaline jolts when story energy flags
• Conveys information about locations and motivations
• Reveals character special skills and professionalism
• Shows character through preparation and execution

The Will Roby Example:
• Action scenes show his sense of professionalism
• Demonstrates his tools, planning, and execution under pressure
• Success isn't just completing the mission—must also escape safely
• Every detail matters; small details are where you get tripped up
• Stakes are ultimate—mistakes mean death, not just job loss

Slowing Down Action:
• Don't rush through fight scenes like Bruce Lee movies
• Allow readers to feel emotional impact at every moment
• These are humans, not robots—show consequences
• Let "the moment have its moment"

The Unforgiven Example:
• In most Clint Eastwood westerns, he shoots and rides off—forgettable
• In "Unforgiven," gut-shot man takes half a day to die on screen
• Shows suffering, agony, and impact on the shooter
• Baldacci remembers this scene 20+ years later because it was slowed down

Consequences and Plausibility:
• Both sides in a fight get hurt—distribute consequences
• Even skilled fighters like Will Roby can almost die and be horribly wounded
• This brings action down from "Superman status" to human level
• Readers accept implausible survival if character pays a price
• Show injuries, wounds, and lasting effects

The Realism Diameter:
• Build a perimeter: "here is plausible, out there is sci-fi"
• Stay within plausibility unless writing sci-fi
• Rely on established character skills and setup
• Special skills are believable when accompanied by consequences
• Nobody walks through 14 opponents unscathed—that's unbelievable

CHOREOGRAPHING ACTION

Physical Preparation:
• Visit locations and pace things off
• Measure distances for sniper shots
• Account for earth's rotation in long-distance shooting
• Lafayette Park bomb scene: measured statue locations, pathways, everything

Visual Clarity:
• Readers count on you to tell them exactly what they're seeing
• Be specific about sight lines, distances, timing
• For complex scenes, physically act out movements with props
• Example: Used a long pillow to figure out how to describe forcing someone through a window

Choose Words Carefully:
• Action sequences demand precise language
• Use more words if necessary to help readers visualize
• Ground action in visceral reality
• Clear choreography makes scenes memorable

EXAMPLE: OPENING ACTION (The Innocent, Chapter 2)

Setup: Will Roby in underground Edinburgh, mission to eliminate five targets

Technique Breakdown:
• Slow narration of fast action
• Shows Roby's professional observation skills
• Notes all sensory details (smells, sounds, proximity)
• Uses numbered targets—"faceless, easier to kill that way"
• Demonstrates his hardened mental state
• Reveals his immunity to pleas and threats
• Multiple paragraphs for action that takes seconds

Purpose:
• First impression of who Will Roby is
• Establishes his capabilities and emotional armor
• Shows "he's a rock" mentally and emotionally
• Action defines and deepens character simultaneously
• Sets tone for the entire novel

✏️ EDITING AND REVISION

TWO-STAGE EDITING PROCESS

Stage 1: Continuous Editing During Writing
• Edit throughout manuscript while writing
• Continually go back to previous chapters, especially first chapters
• Revise, revise, revise as you progress
• By the time first draft is finished, may have edited it a dozen times

Stage 2: The "Big Boy" Edit
• Print everything out (500 pages)
• Use red and blue pens
• "Bloody the page"—this is where manuscript becomes a novel
• Know that much from first draft won't stay in
• Expect to move scenes, cut material, develop characters more

Types of Cuts:
• "Preachy moments" or "soapbox time"—get rid of them
• Intermittent passages that aren't necessary to reach chapter goals
• Material that seemed important during writing but isn't essential

Walking Away from Material:
• When manuscript goes to editors, don't look at it
• Work on something else—cleanse your mind and palate
• Return with fresh eyes when it comes back
• This distance reveals issues you'd miss if constantly tinkering
• Same process for page proofs—stay away until they return

WHAT TO EDIT

Everything Is Fair Game:
• Plot points that are out of place
• Missing bridges between action points (A, B, C, D, F—forgot E)
• Character development and descriptions
• Scene placement and order
• Language efficiency—say in five words what took ten

Building Blocks Editing:
• Sometimes focus mainly on plot structure
• Other times characters are set and it's about placement
• Look for missing steps in logic or action
• Ensure proper transitions between plot movements

The Power of Fewer Words:
• If you can say something more powerfully in fewer words, do it
• Fewer words means you've chosen them more carefully
• Careful word choice makes statements more powerful
• Editing often means cutting and replacing with tighter prose

CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER ANALYSIS

Post-First Draft Review Process:
1. Write down what you accomplished in each chapter
2. Note how each chapter dovetails into the next
3. Check chapter lengths for consistency
4. Question chapters that are drastically longer or shorter
5. Consider splitting long chapters or combining short ones

The One-Liner Technique:
• Distill each chapter to a single sentence description
• Lay out all one-liners sequentially
• Much easier to spot problems than reading 3,000-word chapters
• Can quickly see: what's doing in chapter six? Should it be later?
• Reveals flow issues and misplaced material

Interconnected Purposes:
• Some chapters exist solely to set up the next two or three chapters
• Can't think of chapters in isolation
• Consider transitions and how chapters build on each other
• Follow the thread from one chapter through subsequent chapters
• Like a schematic running through the entire novel

THE DANGER OF OVER-EDITING

Wearing Two Caps Simultaneously:
• Must be both writer and psychologist at once
• Writer perspective: worried about telegraphing, being too simple
• Reader perspective: concerned about being too complicated
• Find the balance between making things perfect and making them worse

When to Let Go:
• Writers can work on books for years, seeking perfection
• Seeking perfection guarantees making it imperfect
• "The world is waiting for the next book you want to write"
• When words and story are where you want them, step back
• Listen to feedback, do final read-through, then move on
• Building a career requires finishing books and moving to the next

Signs of Over-Editing:
• Working on same book for many years
• Constantly feeling it's "not perfect"
• Making changes that don't improve anything
• Losing perspective on what works
• Tinkering becomes procrastination

EDITORIAL LETTERS AND COLLABORATION

Example: The Last Mile (Amos Decker #2)

Issue Identified: Alex Jamison character was overshadowed by Amos Decker
• Editor noted Jamison wasn't getting full credit in the novel
• Decker dominated everything—finding clues, driving action
• Jamison needed more opportunities to shine

Solution Implemented:
• Found three or four places where reveals/discoveries occurred
• Shifted some action from Decker to Jamison
• Gave her extra scenes, beats, and lift points
• Allowed her to rise closer to Decker's equal
• Required trust in editor's judgment

Result: "Dramatic change" that improved the story and character balance

🤝 WORKING WITH EDITORS

SELECTING AN EDITOR

Key Criteria:
• Review their previous work—who else have they edited?
• Check if they've edited books in your genre
• Request sample editorial letters to see their style
• Talk to writers they've worked with about the relationship
• Meet the editor to assess personal chemistry

Genre Expertise:
• Thriller writing is highly specialized
• Editor without thriller experience may lack necessary insights
• However, other positive characteristics might outweigh genre inexperience
• Consider the complete package of skills and compatibility

Editorial Styles Vary:
• Baldacci's editor provides long letters with big-picture points
• Includes page-by-page comment list as attachment
• Makes it convenient to go through manuscript systematically
• Comments appear both in writing and electronically on the manuscript
• Find a style that works for your process

THE EDITORIAL RELATIONSHIP

Foundation of Trust and Confidence:
• Must have chemistry and personal connection
• Even great editors won't work if you don't connect
• Trust is essential for accepting feedback
• Respect must flow both ways

Aligned Goals:
• Both editor and writer want to make the manuscript as good as possible
• Editor's job: point out weaknesses, not praise strengths
• Writer instinctively knows where they're strong
• Need editor to identify: weak spots, missed marks, flat dialogue, pacing issues, implausibility

Handling Disagreements:
• Open communication and discussion are healthy
• Writer is "master and commander" and ultimately decides
• Try to articulate reasons for your position
• Allow editor to articulate their argument
• Either party may persuade the other

The Spark Effect:
• Editor comments can trigger unexpected insights
• Comment about character on page 50 might spark improvement on page 285
• Writer knows the manuscript better than anyone
• Editor's spark can illuminate connections only the writer can make
• This happens in every manuscript—be open to it

Respect and Collaboration:
• Give editor opportunity to persuade you
• You should have opportunity to make your case
• Collaborative partnership, not conflict relationship
• "Not any word you write as a writer is set in stone"
• Take comments respectfully, knowing they're well-intentioned

MANUSCRIPT REVISION STAGES

Stage 1: Initial Editorial Letter
• Read through editor's comments
• Decide which changes you agree with
• Make revisions and send manuscript back
• Editor may have more changes or may be satisfied

Stage 2: Copy Editing
• Copy editors check grammar and technical details
• Don't just look for grammar—look for substantive issues
• "The book stops with me"—final responsibility is yours
• Opportunities dwindling after this point

Stage 3: Page Proofs
• Finished, typeset pages
• Can make changes but not extensive ones
• Balance additions and subtractions to avoid pagination problems
• If adding 20 words, subtract 20 words from the page
• Keep it clean—don't create burden for production team

Multiple Passes:
• Adult fiction: typically three opportunities after first draft
• Young adult/middle grade: four or five passes due to longer lead time
• Many eyes review, but buck stops with the writer
• Go word by word, line by line

Reading Backwards Technique:
• Read lines backwards to catch errors
• Errors jump out more clearly in reverse
• Human brain glosses over mistakes when reading forward
• Brain can fool you into thinking everything is fine

📖 WRITING A SERIES

DECIDING TO WRITE A SERIES

Don't Limit Yourself:
• Can start with a series as first novel (Sue Grafton example: A is for Alibi)
• Don't tell yourself you can't do a series because it's your first time
• Decision is about material and story, not experience
• If character can live beyond one novel, "swing for the fences"

Discovering Series Potential:
• Baldacci's first 12-13 books were all stand-alones
• "Split Second" wasn't intended as series start
• After finishing, realized there were unresolved issues between characters
• Felt there was "more fuel in the tank"
• Brought Sean King and Michelle Maxwell back for five more books

Benefits of Series:
• Another chance to dig deeper into characters
• Opportunity to see characters grow and evolve
• Characters become like friends you've known for years
• Allows more latitude in storytelling
• Can plant seeds that won't pay off until later books

SETTING UP SERIES POTENTIAL

Leaving Wiggle Room:
• End book so it could be standalone
• But include element that allows continuation
• Example: Memory Man—case resolved, family killer found, could end there
• But Decker accepts FBI job to investigate cold cases
• Second book begins with him working that job
• Gives flexibility: can continue or leave it standalone

Background Unresolved Issues:
• Conflict with family member not fully addressed
• Change in career or life arc
• Something left hanging that could be explored later
• Don't force it—must feel organic

Series Planning:
• J.K. Rowling knew from start: seven Harry Potter books
• Most writers (including Baldacci) don't know how many books ahead of time
• Goes book by book, seeing if there's more to explore
• Only brings characters back when there's a story that justifies it

The Camel Club Example:
• Five books written, extremely popular
• Fans constantly request more
• Baldacci hasn't brought them back yet
• Reason: "Haven't really thought of a story that would justify it"
• Won't bring characters back "just to bring them back"

PLANTING SEEDS FOR FUTURE BOOKS

Easter Eggs in Series:
• Can lay elements knowing they won't be resolved immediately
• Readers know it's a series, so they wait patiently
• When resolved, it's a payoff

John Puller Series Example:
• Book 1 (Zero Day): Brother Robert is in prison—not resolved until Book 3
• Book 1: Mother disappeared—not resolved until Book 4 (No Man's Land)
• Knew from the start these issues had to be addressed
• Guaranteed at least three books to handle these storylines

Advantages:
• Broad latitude to build characters leisurely
• Time to finish edges and make them more complicated
• Like aging—characters change with experience
• Life etches scars, wounds, and learning onto people

MAINTAINING SERIES CONSISTENCY

The Critical Importance:
• Inconsistency loses readers immediately
• "If readers don't think you can master your own material, they don't trust you to do anything else right"

What to Track:
• Character skills and knowledge base
• Who characters know
• Character backgrounds and history
• Events from previous books
• Physical descriptions and traits

Maintaining Consistency:
• Reread previous books before writing new installment
• Check definite statements against earlier books
• Nobody remembers every detail from 140,000-word book written 10 years ago
• Going back to check shows mastery of material

Character Arc Consistency:
• If character arcs one way in two books, can't suddenly shift
• Must provide plausible reason for any major change
• Example: upstanding citizen suddenly robbing a bank—explain why
• Inadequate explanation blows the arc
• Readers won't forgive unexplained character shifts

STANDALONE vs. SERIES READING

Publisher's Perspective:
• "To fully enjoy this book, read all published books and buy them for friends and family"

Baldacci's Approach:
• Provide enough background in each book for standalone reading
• Better if read in order—some things will make more sense
• Balance: inform new readers without boring sequential readers

The Refresher Course Strategy:
• Even readers who read in order forget details
• Books come out annually—people have lives between books
• A little refresher isn't bad even for sequential readers
• Keep it to a minimum—maybe one sentence

Example: Amos Decker:
• "Because of traumatic brain injury/hyperthymesia, he has perfect recall. Can't forget anything."
• One sentence tells new readers his amazing gift
• Enough for out-of-order readers to understand
• Brief enough not to bore readers who know the character

Trust Your Readers:
• They're smart—they'll get it
• Don't spend pages rehashing
• Not good for newbies or sequential readers
• Brief information allows standalone reading without bogging down
• The more you do it, the better you get at it

📚 NAVIGATING THE PUBLISHING BUSINESS

BUILDING RELATIONSHIPS WITH BOOKSELLERS

Why Booksellers Matter:
• They hand-sell books directly to readers
• One small bookstore sold 1,500 copies of Absolute Power
• Booksellers are the conduit between readers and publishers

How to Build Relationships:
• Visit stores before or during book signings
• Walk around to see how store is set up
• Talk to staff about: sales trends, events, ordering, returns
• Ask about strategies to bring people into store
• Learn from their daily experience with publishers

Information Exchange:
• Booksellers can alert you to distribution problems
• Example: books not being replenished in certain areas
• Can relay concerns back to publisher for resolution
• They know what's working and what isn't in the marketplace

Book Event Advice:
• Go everywhere—even four-person book clubs in Nebraska
• Early career: travel everywhere to build fan base
• Later career: selective but still values reader interaction
• Getting out there is part of the ecosystem

THE ECOSYSTEM OF PUBLISHING

All Parts Must Support Each Other:
• Publishers
• Writers
• Readers
• Booksellers (independents and chains)

Writer's Role:
• Write consistently great stories
• Take marketing and business side seriously
• Build fan base through events and engagement
• Be a good partner to publisher

Two Hats:
• Writer hat—focus on craft
• Promoter/marketer hat—build audience
• You can choose to wear only the writer hat
• But fan base may not grow as quickly without promotion

BUILDING A FAN BASE

Primary Method: Write Great Stories Consistently
• Quality and consistency are foundational
• Readers need to trust that opening your book means enjoyment to the last page

Baldacci's Unique Approach:
• Writes all books himself (no co-authors)
• Multiple series with different characters (King & Maxwell, Camel Club, Amos Decker, Will Roby, Jessica Reel)
• Two books per year
• Readers never know which series or standalone they'll get
• "Like waiting for the ice cream truck—select your flavor"

Meeting Readers:
• Love getting out there and talking to readers
• Ask what they like/don't like about books
• Learn what they want to see coming
• Helps the whole ecosystem when everyone participates

AVOIDING COMMON PITFALLS

The Gap Problem:
• Hot first book, then nothing for five years
• Next book isn't well-written and doesn't sell
• Writer totally disappears

The Marketplace Reality:
• So many writers producing consistently (1-3 books/year)
• Fans follow multiple authors
• Limited budget for books each year
• Five-year gaps mean readers forget and move on
• Not fair, but that's how marketplace works

Building vs. Maintaining:
• One book every five years doesn't build a fan base
• Nothing to keep readers engaged during gap
• Readers who loved first book have no follow-up
• They forget and commit to other authors

Commercial Fiction Timeline:
• Different from literary fiction (where gaps are more acceptable)
• Toni Morrison or literary authors have different expectations
• Commercial fiction requires more consistent output
• Readers expect regular new books in the genre

SELF-PUBLISHING EVOLUTION

Then vs. Now:
• Used to be "dirty phrase"—selling Xerox copies from car trunk
• John Grisham sold "A Time to Kill" from his trunk before "The Firm" changed everything
• Today: legitimate platforms (Amazon, many others)

Modern Self-Publishing Benefits:
• Promotional help from platforms
• Marketing support
• Editing assistance
• Cover design help
• Can sell significant copies and build thriving career

Success Stories:
• Writers on Amazon who have sold many books
• Some started self-published and built successful careers
• Valid, vetted platforms available
• Easy to find opportunities through internet search

Resources for Writers:
• Organizations like ITW (International Thriller Writers)
• Publications where writers can publish short stories
• Platforms that didn't exist before
• Ways to be heard and build audience

AFTER THE FIRST BOOK SELLS

Baldacci's Story: Absolute Power Sale
• Highest advance ever paid for first novel in U.S. publishing history
• Initially thought agent was "on drugs" or "psychotic"
• CEO of Time Warner Publishing called personally
• Nobody at law firm knew he'd been writing for years
• Couldn't share news with anyone at work

First Reactions:
• Euphoric moment mixed with overwhelm
• Wife asked "How much do we have to pay?"—thought he'd have to pay publisher
• Wanted to "jump on table and do electric slide"
• Life about to totally change

Managing the Changes:
• Bought only a video camera for a year (to record one-year-old daughter)
• Made conscious choice to keep life as unchanged as possible
• Continued practicing law for another year
• Started writing second book immediately

Financial Management:
• Set up professional structures immediately
• Great agent handling negotiations
• Great film agent managing those rights
• Financial advisors managing money
• Legal background helped navigate complexity

Compartmentalization Strategy:
• Don't try to deal with everything at once—overwhelming for new writers
• Create discrete parts and attack each separately
• One day: film rights; next day: foreign contracts; next day: marketing
• Solve one issue, structure it properly, then move to next
• Makes overwhelming situation manageable

The Critical Mistake to Avoid:
• Getting swept up in cool, exotic aspects of being published
• Forgetting about "this" (writing)
• Going on tours, doing interviews, meeting people—all exciting
• But writing is what got you there
• Must continue writing during promotional activities

Baldacci's Discipline:
• Took laptop everywhere during promotional tours
• Didn't care where he was—kept writing
• By end of first book's tour year, "Total Control" was done
• Publisher read it, loved it, bought it
• Continued being a good partner to publisher AND to himself as writer

The Balance:
• Do all the things you should be doing
• Be good partner to publisher
• Be good partner to yourself as writer
• Continue writing—that's what makes everything else possible
• "If you forget about that piece, all this other stuff goes away"

💪 LIFE AS A WRITER

THE INNER DRIVE

The Defining Characteristic:
• Those meant to be writers "will be writers because that inner drive will not allow you to stop"
• Baldacci: "If I had never been published even at this date, I would still be writing"
• Writing is "such a critical, vital part of my life that I could not give up"
• Without writing, even unpublished, would be "a lesser soul and lesser person"
• Would be "a shadow image of what I could have been as a human being"

What Sustains You:
• Inner drive stands you through periods where nothing seems to go right
• The fuel and food you draw upon to keep going
• Not a cliché—Baldacci lived it and knows it works
• Perseverance comes from this deep need

For Those Struggling:
• Writer with day job, family, writing at night, facing rejection
• Career seems stalled, confidence ebbing
• Feel like it's not going to happen
• Baldacci: "I was there with you in the same moment in time"

The Simple Truth:
• "I couldn't stop writing"
• "Follow the words and follow your hearts and good things will happen"
• That compulsion separates true writers from those who merely wish they could write

DEALING WITH DOUBT AND REJECTION

Universal Experience:
• All doubts that plagued struggling writers also plagued Baldacci
• Nearly two decades of writing before breakthrough
• Rejection is part of every writer's journey
• "There's power in numbers"—knowing you're not alone helps doubts fade

The Outside Looking In:
• Baldacci was "on the outside of the glass looking in for well over almost two decades"
• Knows what it's like to think "this is never going to happen for me"
• Course designed to dispel doubts through shared experience

What Kept Him Going:
• Couldn't stop writing—it was compulsion
• If you truly need to write, that need will sustain you
• Follow the words even when nothing seems to work
• Trust the process and your commitment

FINDING YOUR PROCESS

No Single Right Way:
• "One person's opinion on how to approach the craft of writing"
• Billions of other approaches exist
• Every writer in this chair or program has different methods
• All comes down to commitment to being a storyteller

Taking What Works:
• Use the tools, tactics, and techniques that work for you
• Tweak the ones you're interested in
• Throw away the ones you don't care about or disagree with
• Build your own process over time

Evolution of Process:
• May use one method for 2-3 years successfully
• Then try something else that works better
• Process can change over your career
• What worked at 20 may not work at 40
• Stay flexible and adapt

THE IMPORTANCE OF FRESH VOICES

Why New Writers Matter:
• "This world always needs fresh voices"
• "Writers change the world. Books change the world."
• Everyone waits for the next brilliant book that makes them "examine my whole life and what the world should be"
• "All of you are the next component"

The Vital Cog:
• New writers keep the world of publishing, books, and reading going
• Baldacci: "I and the rest of the publishing world and the world in general, we need you a lot more"
• Not just about you learning from established writers
• Established writers and readers need what you'll create

Encouragement to Pursue:
• World needs your fresh perspective and voice
• Don't hold back thinking you're not ready
• Your contribution matters to the ecosystem
• Proud to encourage new writers to pursue the craft

🎯 KEY TAKEAWAYS & ACTION ITEMS

FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

1. View writing as a lifestyle, not just a job or hobby
2. Look at the world through the "writer's prism" constantly
3. If you truly need to write, you'll find the time regardless of circumstances
4. Research thoroughly, but leave most of it out of the manuscript
5. Focus on plausibility—if it could happen, you can write about it
6. Immerse yourself completely in your material
7. Trust your inner drive to sustain you through rejection and doubt

IMMEDIATE ACTION STEPS

For Finding Ideas:
□ Practice seeing story potential in everyday moments
□ Take two unrelated news stories and combine them plausibly
□ Connect disparate elements (person walking + car driving) into coherent stories
□ Reflect on current events you're genuinely passionate about
□ Let ideas percolate for weeks before committing to write

For Research:
□ Identify experts you need to interview
□ Learn about subjects BEFORE meeting with sources
□ Visit physical locations you're writing about
□ Pace off distances and measure spaces for action scenes
□ Take detailed notes, photos, and recordings (with permission)
□ For government contacts, use private email/phones only

For Daily Writing:
□ Find your specific time window (Baldacci: 10 PM - 2 AM)
□ Use lunch breaks and spare moments to jot notes
□ Read opening chapters before each writing session
□ Read two preceding chapters to get into flow
□ Write until your "tank is empty" for the day
□ When blocked: work on another project, take a shower, or go for a walk

For Structuring Your Novel:
□ Create the "big pop"—compelling opening scene
□ Return to first chapter constantly as your touchstone
□ Ask three questions for every chapter: Does it convey information? Develop character? Move plot forward?
□ Create one-liner descriptions for each chapter to see overall flow
□ Vary chapter elements: narrative, dialogue, interior monologue, action
□ Consider chapter length based on genre and pacing needs

For Editing:
□ Edit continuously while writing the first draft
□ Print out completed draft and "bloody the page" with pens
□ Walk away from manuscript when editors have it—work on something else
□ Return with fresh eyes when revisions come back
□ Create chapter-by-chapter analysis after first draft
□ Cut "preachy moments" and "soapbox time"
□ Say in fewer words what you originally said in many

For Series Writing:
□ Don't limit yourself—first novel can be series start
□ Leave wiggle room at the end for potential continuation
□ Reread previous books before writing new installments
□ Maintain consistency in character details, skills, and history
□ Only bring characters back when you have a story that justifies it
□ Plant seeds that won't be resolved until later books
□ Provide enough background for standalone reading without boring sequential readers

For Working with Editors:
□ Research potential editors' previous work and genre experience
□ Meet editors to assess personal chemistry
□ Remember: both you and editor want the same thing—best possible book
□ Be open to being persuaded, but remember you're "master and commander"
□ Watch for "spark effect"—comments that illuminate unexpected connections
□ Review manuscript in multiple stages: editorial letter, copy editing, page proofs

For Building a Career:
□ Visit bookstores and talk to booksellers about the business
□ Go everywhere for book events, even small venues
□ Write consistently—don't let five years pass between books
□ Keep writing during promotional activities
□ Set up professional structures (agent, financial advisors)
□ Compartmentalize tasks when overwhelmed
□ Explore modern self-publishing platforms as viable option

LONG-TERM MINDSET SHIFTS

• Writing is a marathon, not a sprint—Baldacci wrote for nearly two decades before breakthrough
• Rejection and doubt are universal—use them as fuel rather than obstacles
• The inner drive to write is what separates writers from people who want to have written
• "Follow the words and follow your hearts and good things will happen"
• The world needs your fresh voice—you're a vital part of the ecosystem
• Not any word is set in stone—everything can be revised and improved
• At some point, you must let go and move to the next book
