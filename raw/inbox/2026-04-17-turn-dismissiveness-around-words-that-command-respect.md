- Title of Video: Turn Dismissiveness Around: Words that Command Respect[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
The video explains how to respond calmly and assertively to three common dismissive phrases: “It was just a joke,” “Don’t take it personally,” and “Don’t be so sensitive.” The core message is that other people do not get to define your experience; you can protect your self-esteem by staying non-defensive and using short, steady phrases that redirect responsibility back to the speaker.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Dismissive comments are attempts to control how you feel and to erase the consequences of hurtful behavior; you, not the other person, decide whether something was hurtful.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Responding with calm, concise lines (rather than emotional defensiveness) keeps you in the stronger position and highlights the other person’s behavior.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- For “It was just a joke,” pivot to the quality of the joke (“Then be funnier,” “Then find new material,” “Was that supposed to be funny?”) to expose that it was hurtful, not humorous.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- For “Don’t take it personally,” assert your agency (“I get to decide that,” “Then don’t say it personally,” “Then say it differently”) to reassign responsibility for their words.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- For “Don’t be so sensitive,” reframe “sensitive” as “sensing” (“No, I’m sensing X…”) to name what you are noticing about their behavior without becoming defensive.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Nature and function of dismissive comments
    

- Phrases like “It was just a joke,” “Don’t take it personally,” and “Don’t be so sensitive” invalidate your feelings and try to delete any accountability for the original comment.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Fisher likens this to someone punching you in the arm and then declaring “That didn’t hurt,” as if they get to define your pain and the outcome of their action.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- He frames these comments as control attempts: the speaker is trying to control both the narrative (“it was harmless”) and your reaction (“you’re overreacting”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

1. Ownership of emotional experience
    

- A central principle is that only you decide what hurts and how personally you take something, just as only you decide whether to “catch” a metaphorical ball someone throws at you.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The line “I get to decide that” is presented as a boundary statement that re-centers your authority over your internal experience.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- This approach treats emotional responses as valid and self-determined, rather than as something the other person can dictate away.
    

1. Staying non-defensive as strength
    

- Becoming overtly defensive (“I’m not sensitive!”) paradoxically makes you look more like the accusation and positions the other person as “right” and you as “wrong.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Calm, steady tone plus short phrases keeps you in a stronger, more grounded position and prevents escalation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- The “verbal punch” is not volume or aggression but composed clarity that exposes inappropriate behavior without losing control.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

1. Wordplay as a communication tool
    

- Fisher repeatedly uses wordplay to redirect dismissive language back toward the behavior (e.g., joke → “be funnier,” personally → “say it personally/differently,” sensitive → “I’m sensing…”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- This method is both disarming and efficient: it highlights the problem while keeping you out of a long argument about intentions.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Wordplay also lowers the emotional temperature; you stand your ground without direct insult or counterattack.
    

1. Context and subjectivity of sensitivity
    

- Fisher stresses that “everybody is sensitive; it just depends on the topic, context, and environment,” challenging the idea that sensitivity is inherently a flaw.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Labeling someone “too sensitive” often masks the speaker’s unwillingness to do the upfront work of thinking about impact and adjusting how they speak.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- This reframes “sensitivity” as normal and situational, not as a defect that justifies hurtful comments.
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. When you hear “It was just a joke”  
    Use one of these short replies in a calm tone:
    

- “Then be funnier.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “Then work on your material,” or “Then find new material.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “Was that supposed to be funny?” or “Was that the funny kind of joke?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

Application notes:

- Use these especially when the “joke” is about your looks, weight, age, or other personal traits.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Keep your body language and voice relaxed; the power comes from the content and composure, not from aggression.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

1. When you hear “Don’t take it personally”  
    Try one of these boundary-setting lines:
    

- “I get to decide that.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “Then don’t say it personally.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “Then say it differently.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

Application notes:

- Think of it as choosing how to “catch” the paper airplane or ball they threw; you are naming that the choice is yours, not theirs.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- If they truly do not want it taken personally, invite them to rephrase: “Say it again a different way.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

1. When you hear “Don’t be so sensitive”  
    Reframe by switching to “sensing”:
    

- “No, I’m sensing you’re not that funny.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- “No, I’m sensing you and I have a different idea of what’s appropriate to say out loud.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- More generally: “No, I’m sensing X” where X is your observation (e.g., “that you think this is okay to say”).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

Application notes:

- Avoid protesting “I’m not sensitive!”—that sounds defensive and weakens your position.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Use the “I’m sensing…” structure to state observations rather than argue labels.
    

1. General guidelines to stay strong, not reactive
    

- Breathe and keep your tone neutral; your composure signals that you are not rattled.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Use concise phrases instead of long explanations; brevity reads as confidence.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Notice when the other person’s dismissive comment is making you feel like you must defend your character; that’s often a sign their framing is working against you.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

1. Workplace-specific example (Scott from Detroit)
    

- With a coworker who always says “I’m just joking,” you can start with “Then be funnier” or “Then work on your material,” knowing it may reset boundaries even if it feels direct.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Alternatively, use: “I’m surprised,” then continue with “I’m surprised you went with that,” “I’m surprised you think that’s okay,” or “I’m surprised you said that out loud.”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- This “I’m surprised…” structure prompts them to self-correct while clearly signaling that the remark is not acceptable, especially when it is affecting your self-esteem or workplace dynamic.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- Psychological mechanism: Dismissive comments do double damage—they hurt in the moment and also tell you your hurt is illegitimate, which can slowly erode self-esteem over time.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Power dynamic: Statements like “You’re too sensitive” and “Don’t take it personally” often function as subtle power plays, asserting the speaker’s right to set both the tone and the emotional rules of the interaction.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Normalization of invalidation: The video implicitly critiques how common these phrases are in families, friendships, and workplaces, where they are often treated as normal banter rather than emotional invalidation.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Self-reflection by the speaker: Fisher notes that he has said “Don’t be so sensitive” himself and calls that unfair, modeling accountability and the idea that respecting others’ reactions is part of mature communication.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
    
- Ambiguity/limitations: The suggested comebacks rely heavily on tone, context, and relative power; in very hierarchical or abusive environments, using them may carry risks that the video does not fully explore, and additional safety or escalation strategies might be needed.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eXE5pCA5viU&list=WL&index=21)
