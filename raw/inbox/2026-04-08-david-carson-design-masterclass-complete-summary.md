DAVID CARSON MASTERCLASS: GRAPHIC DESIGN & TYPOGRAPHY
Complete Course Summary & Reference Guide

================================================================================
📋 OVERVIEW
================================================================================

This masterclass represents David Carson's comprehensive approach to graphic design, taught by one of the most influential and controversial designers who transformed the field in the 1990s. Carson, who came to design late (at age 26) with a sociology degree rather than formal design training, brings an intuitive, risk-taking philosophy that challenges conventional design wisdom. The course covers fundamental design principles while emphasizing the importance of putting yourself into your work, trusting your intuition, and breaking rules purposefully rather than ignorantly.

The masterclass is structured around both theoretical concepts and practical workshops, including three progressive assignments that help students discover their unique design voice. Carson's teaching methodology draws from his extensive experience with magazines (Ray Gun, Beach Culture, Surfer), major corporate clients (Microsoft, Audi, Bose), and cultural projects (Nine Inch Nails album covers, environmental campaigns). His philosophy centers on design as emotional communication that happens before someone starts reading, emphasizing that great design should intrigue, provoke, and reflect the humanity behind the work.

Throughout the course, Carson challenges students to see design not as a safe, corporate exercise but as a powerful tool for communication that should reflect individuality, take risks, and push boundaries. He advocates for working intuitively rather than following grids or systems, cropping images unexpectedly, breaking up logos and type in meaningful ways, and always asking "why not?" rather than accepting conventional limitations.

🎯 CORE PHILOSOPHY & MINDSET
================================================================================

The Rebel Designer Approach
• If everybody loves your work, you're playing it too safe
• You need detractors to know you're onto something meaningful
• Graphic design has become too conservative, clean, and sanitary
• The field needs more rebels who ask "says who?" and "why not?"
• Great design should get people's attention and make them want to engage

Trust Your Intuition
• Everybody has intuition—it's about learning to listen to it
• Your intuition usually has good answers for design problems
• Use intuition as a design tool, not just analytical thinking
• What appears chaotic or random is almost never the case—there are reasons
• Only you have your unique background, upbringing, and life experience

Put Yourself Into Your Work
• The best design comes when you put yourself into the work
• Your uniqueness is your greatest advantage—nobody else is you
• Personal work and side projects increasingly have commercial value
• Clients are drawn to work that feels like there's a human behind it
• Use colors, textures, shapes that have personal meaning to you

Risk-Taking Philosophy
• "If I didn't take risks, if I played it safe, we wouldn't be here today"
• Committee design never gets great work—it gets safe, reasonable work
• Show clients experimental options—they can't pick them if you don't show them
• Small victories matter: breaking up logos, using lowercase, diverse representation
• Spec work can be valuable if it gives you freedom and pushes you as a designer

Life Perspective
• Design what appears to be going on in your life, not just commercial messages
• Consider your mortality and what you want to accomplish (Life Line exercise)
• Most people regret things they didn't do, not things they did
• If you're hoping things work out, good luck—but you should actively make them happen
• The only certainty is that time already passed is gone—use remaining time wisely

💡 FUNDAMENTAL DESIGN PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

Design Communicates Before Reading
• Design has many functions before somebody starts reading
• Does it intrigue them? Does it send a consistent message?
• Will people walk across the street to read the small print?
• Your job is to help people get into content, not just present information
• A gray page of type is deadly—design must invite engagement

The Power of Simplicity
• Simplicity with real impact is hard to do but powerful when achieved
• Work that works best is either simple or complicated—not in the middle
• Sometimes reducing elements makes work stronger
• One element (like a single shark fin) can be more powerful than many
• "Keep paddling" as a tagline—applies to everything, simple but profound

Readability vs. Legibility
• Readability has so much to do with what you're interested in reading
• Legibility alone doesn't guarantee communication
• With the right audience, experimental typography actually helps them identify with content
• Don't automatically put things where they seem to want to go
• Challenge assumptions about what can and cannot be read

The Role of White Space
• White space is absolutely as important as the black ink
• White space can indicate friendliness, openness, breathing room
• Too much organization with rigid borders can feel restricting
• White space around letters and shapes sends powerful messages
• Consider the shape around and inside letters, not just the letters themselves

Avoid Literal Interpretation
• The more literal a logo or design gets, the less interesting it is
• Don't always highlight the obvious word (e.g., making "fear" small in "The Fear Project")
• Hint at meaning rather than spelling everything out
• If you tell people everything simply, they lose interest
• Make people want to know more—that's very powerful

🎨 TYPOGRAPHY & TYPE DESIGN
================================================================================

Typography as Image
• "The word is an image" (William Burroughs)
• Typography sends a message before it spells something
• Type is an image that also happens to spell something
• How type is arranged and worked into layouts is excitingly powerful
• We should emotionally affect people through typography

Font Choice & Personality
• Every font has its own personality: stability, fragility, quaint, loud
• Font choice helps reinforce your message subtly but powerfully
• Look at basic categories: bold, thick, serif, sans serif, italic
• Avoid fonts that are too popular, common, or forgettable
• Combining old and new fonts can create unique looks (Armani example)

Spacing & Arrangement
• Look at space between letters—does it need adjustment?
• Opening up spacing can make type feel more elegant or classy
• Touching letters or words can create impact when done purposefully
• Make decisions about cap height—maybe a tall condensed cap works better
• Don't let the computer automatically space things

Breaking Rules Purposefully
• Turn letters upside down when it has meaning (Tony Hawk's name for skateboarding)
• Break up words across lines if it reinforces the concept
• Reverse type out of body copy to create shapes (Super Chunk example)
• Mix uppercase/lowercase, italic/non-italic, different sizes
• Play with letters: drop one letter to become part of another word (wedge/people)

Hand Lettering
• Hand lettering's main message is: there's a human behind this
• Never use a font made to look like hand lettering—actually hand letter it
• Check that two of the same letters look different (proves it's hand lettered)
• Hand lettering can be happy, sad, angry, urgent, calm—all over the map
• Watercolor pencils, wrong hand, wrong angle can create interesting effects

Type Integration
• Incorporating type into great images while making them stronger is challenging
• Type should feel like it belongs, not like it was tapped on afterward
• Consider how type interacts with shapes, angles, and negative space in your design
• Sometimes the best solution is minimal type or letting type be discovered
• Type can become a graphic element itself (large page numbers as design feature)

Never Use Grids or Systems
• Never used a grid, never knew what they were, still don't use them
• Start with a blank page and arrange things where they feel right
• NEVER snap to guides—the computer shouldn't make your decisions
• Guidelines are like training wheels—eliminate them
• Delete all preset guidelines and bring your own eye to the work
• Too many guidelines leads to boring design, boring designers, boring audiences

📸 PHOTOGRAPHY IN DESIGN
================================================================================

The Art of Cropping
• In photos you choose, or sections of photos, you can give life to work
• Perfectly focused, colored, balanced photos can be totally forgettable
• Look at each photo—maybe find a section that's more interesting
• Sometimes blowing up a small section makes it really strong
• Don't just drop photos into pre-arranged boxes—that kills impact

Extreme Cropping Techniques
• Crop extremely close when appropriate (Kelly Slater wave sequence)
• Sometimes just an eye is more striking than a full portrait (PJ Harvey)
• Crop to just a foot leaving the frame, not the whole person
• Show what the coffee cup sees, not the coffee cup
• Consider: what does the subject see vs. what we expect to see?

Working with Limited Resources
• Limited budgets force you to look harder at what you have
• Photos submitted by the office or participants can work with creative cropping
• When you have only a couple good shots, look for atmospheric sections
• Type and creative cropping can compensate for limited photography
• Restrictions often lead to better, more creative solutions

Found Art & Unexpected Images
• Look for test Polaroids, behind-the-scenes shots, accidents
• The X on the floor where Lyle Lovett stood became the opening image
• Shadows can be more interesting than the subject (Burton snowboard example)
• Contact sheets may contain treasures you initially overlook
• Keep images that intrigue you, even without immediate use

Using Your Own Photography
• Take photos constantly—never be without your phone
• Photograph shapes, textures, colors that register with you
• Your own photos integrate more naturally into design work
• Personal photos can have significance to your life and existence
• Source material you like leads to better results

Happy Accidents
• "Because something is an accident doesn't make it good, but it's a huge part of my process"
• Photos ruined by wrong chemicals became Nine Inch Nails album covers
• Be open to unexpected things: something falls, gets stuck in printer
• Most accidents don't work, but occasionally something is better than planned
• Water references in photography matched lyrics (Nine Inch Nails' water theme)

Integration with Design
• What hints can you take from photography: line, angle, white space?
• Photos should tie together as one piece with your overall design
• Don't demand product always shows—mood and atmosphere matter
• Tight cropping works well with sports and action photography
• Use photography to hint at company values: experimental, current, different

🎨 COLOR, TEXTURE & COLLAGE
================================================================================

The Power of Color
• Color is a huge message sender and emotion sender
• Generally orange and lighter blues are happy, festive colors
• Red is difficult—almost too strong
• Dark reds and dark blues send very different emotions than bright colors
• Yellow is happy, but context changes everything

Color Meaning & Context
• Colors confined and overtaken by grays change their meaning
• Bright colors in organized, bordered layouts can feel trapped or pressured
• Color choices should complement design, not overpower it
• Use colors that have meaning to you personally (house paint example)
• Blues and greens for ocean/water references, but add unexpected elements

Texture & Materials
• Ripped edges send very different messages than clean cuts
• Rough brush strokes vs. delicate application changes meaning
• White on white gets nice texture
• Torn paper vs. cut-out shapes communicates differently
• Back of materials sometimes better than front

Collage Philosophy
• Collage work is invigorating—gets you out from behind the screen
• Work with your hands—people react strongly to human-behind-the-work feel
• Personal work and collage are finding more commercial applications
• Start with color combinations you think could work
• Best work comes when individual pieces have significance to your life

Collage Process & Technique
• Start by gathering materials in groups of color combinations
• Have multiple pieces going at same time—work between them
• What you think you're cutting out for isn't always what's best
• What's left after cutting is often better than what you cut out
• Don't be too precious—you get what you get
• Sit down at the very end—things always move slightly, sometimes get better

Creating Meaning Through Collage
• Don't use things too literal (visible boats, clear objects)
• Hints of letters and words work better than full readable text
• Break up strong colors that are distracting
• Add unexpected elements to sameness
• White space in collage is as important as colored elements
• Abstract collage can represent complex ideas like "predictable chaos"

Light & Shadow
• Become aware of light source when photographing collages
• Real shadows from paper layers add dimension you can't fake on screen
• Depth created by actual paper thickness is authentic
• Physical collage creates genuine texture that digital can't replicate

Client Applications
• Collage suited The Macallan whiskey—hands-on, human, nature-focused
• John Coltrane box set done on hotel floor in Amsterdam
• More clients wanting collage for the human element
• Can be done anywhere—it's your mindset and ways of working, not location
• Recent trend toward feeling there's a human behind the work

🏢 WORKING WITH CLIENTS
================================================================================

Understanding the Brief
• The brief is extremely important—almost all you have to start
• Brief = what agency and client agree are goals for the project
• Contains: who the client is, what they're saying, product benefits, audience
• Read the brief thoroughly—ideas come from this information
• Reading brief gets your mind spinning with possibilities

Research Beyond the Brief
• Find out history of product/company not in the brief
• Study previous work, logos, branding
• See what competitors are doing—know what your audience sees
• Understand the demographic and what they're seeing
• How can you not just add to clutter but elevate it?

Presenting to Clients
• Show a wide range of ideas, from safe to experimental
• Present widely so clients can see where they're leaning
• Follow the brief but also push yourself and show that
• They can't pick the experimental stuff if you don't show it
• Give clients more credit—they're not easily shocked anymore

Small Victories Matter
• Amtrak trains: map with cities not lined up geographically worked
• Microsoft Gothic: one approved font used in unusual ways
• Bose: starting titles with lowercase, having words touch
• Most significant: moving from only white males in ads to diversity
• Sometimes small typographic experiments open doors

Explaining Your Work
• Be able to tell client WHY you did something, not just "it looked cool"
• Have reasons for your design choices
• You won't be able to explain away bad design
• But good design with reasons has better chance of being sold
• For shark fin campaign: fin turned downward, hint of red without being gory

Dealing with Restrictions
• Microsoft wanted only Microsoft Gothic font—saw it as interesting challenge
• Use what you're given in ways somebody else wouldn't
• Restrictions and challenges can lead to better solutions
• See limitations as opportunities for creativity
• "I couldn't get backgrounds clear" became jagged white shapes that worked

Different Client Types Require Different Approaches
• Conservative clients: small steps, small victories
• Established brands: respect history while bringing freshness  
• Experimental clients: push boundaries, show bold options
• Every client is different—it's a skill to work with each type
• Goal: something that works for them and you're happy with as designer

Real-World Examples
• Audi: way ahead/way ahead of competition wordplay
• Shark fin campaign: simplified from many fins to one powerful image
• Microsoft: humanizing through type use and rounded-edge photographs
• Bose: simple remote, simple ad—clean design reflecting product
• The Macallan: collage suited hands-on nature of whiskey making

When Things Don't Work
• Clients may reject work (shopping mall window display example)
• Hillary Clinton logo: cold, sharp, hard-edged—reinforced negative perceptions
• Not every project gets approved, even with good reasoning
• Learn from rejections and keep pushing in future projects

📐 LOGO DEVELOPMENT
================================================================================

Logo Philosophy
• A logo is an image, just like a word is an image
• Should be recognizable from a distance
• People want it on their hats, t-shirts, website, expensive cars
• Separates them from competition without being too literal
• Consider: Are they experimental? Conservative? What's their personality?

The Coffee Shop Test
• Will people want this on their shirt, t-shirt, coffee mug?
• If yes, you've probably stumbled upon a pretty good logo
• Think beyond official uses to personal adoption
• Logos should be inviting and identity-forming

Design Process
• Start by setting main letters/words in different fonts you think could work
• Move things by eye and hand, not using computer buttons
• Don't use grids or any kind of system for positioning
• Try many variations—show clients lots of options
• Work in basic program (Carson uses Quark Express) to generate volume

The Simple/Complex Rule
• Simplicity with real impact is hard but powerful
• Work in black and white first—logos must work in B&W
• Must work tiny and huge
• Color comes after basic arrangement is approved
• Test at multiple sizes before finalizing

Specific Examples & Techniques

Magic Seaweed:
• M and W are same letter flipped—creates continuity
• S turned on side emphasizes motion of surfing/waves
• Name touching letters, part of overall logo
• Simple font, plain and clear, held up well over time

Seascans:
• Crowded wetsuit field—mostly black, hard to distinguish
• Extreme simplicity: just word "sea" with brackets
• Stands out in ocean, foggy conditions
• Sometimes less is genuinely more

Joe Riley Campaign:
• "Joseph P. Riley III" sounded old and formal
• Simplified to "Joe" in green circle
• Humanized the candidate for younger voters
• Political logo that felt friendly and accessible

Lab 78:
• Seven works as an L—trust your instinct on readability
• Mix serif and sans serif if it creates visual interest
• People enjoy having something to figure out (thin line)
• It's okay if it takes a minute to decode

Erase the Hate:
• Avoid clichés (erasing words literally)
• Show experimental options client can't pick if you don't present them
• Even if unlikely to be chosen, put bold ideas in presentation
• Communicate attitude consistent with organization

Dali Museum:
• Avoid expected approach (dripping letters)
• Basic typeface used in unique way
• Font alone rarely solves anything—it's how you use it
• Attitude consistent with Dali himself

Bringing Motion to Logos
• Don't design thinking it will move—solve as stationary first
• Once approved, motion becomes next fun stage
• Consider: where was viewer's eye before type came in?
• Is there natural sequence for reading?
• Movement adds whole new dimension: form, color, shape, AND motion
• Moving logos provoke emotion in powerful ways

📰 MAGAZINE & EDITORIAL DESIGN
================================================================================

Editorial Design Philosophy
• Editorial work has its own advantages
• Through color, texture, shape, choices—reinforce what was just read
• Hard to get people to jump into gray page of type
• Designer's job is to help them get in—or they miss important information
• Every page is a whole new design assignment

Contents Pages
• Traditionally didn't put page numbers on contents
• Surfer Portugal issue: made page numbers huge graphic elements
• Page numbers were actual numbers where articles appeared
• Listed items in order they appear—people browse from back anyway
• Can use great photography (Albert Watson examples)

Article Openings
• Techno dancing article: columns of type with movement and shape
• Don't let computer make three even columns, flush left—that's deadly
• Type columns can touch or almost touch purposefully
• Play with letters: drop E to become part of another word
• Make words feel worth reader's time

Experimental Typography in Context
• Super Chunk: reversed "super" out of body copy, creating chunks
• Each spread reflects the specific content and mood
• Tony Hawk spread: squareness of illustration vs. O in his name
• Reference wheels, movement, skateboarding through design
• Byline X plays big part in arrangement—all elements matter

Covers
• Main goal: get somebody to pick it up
• Don't just politely list everything down the side
• Want reaction: "Whoa, what's going on here?"
• Tony Hawk's name upside down (famous for upside-down tricks)
• Pay attention to details—don't automatically do your work

Cover Strategy
• Every cover a whole new design assignment
• Sometimes simple and clean after crazy previous issue
• Sometimes photo submitted for article becomes perfect cover
• End of summer issue: Anton Corbin's photograph
• Makes magazines more work but much more fun and interesting

Photography Integration
• "The Fear Project": made "fear" really small—paradoxically more fearful
• Don't go to obvious word to highlight
• Portugal 25th anniversary: type down touching black, not centered
• Don't put things where they seem to want to go
• Find more interesting place that only you would find

Legibility Debates
• People said early Ray Gun issues: "you can't read any of that"
• Of course it's readable—readability depends on interest in reading
• With that audience, extreme experimentation helped them identify with magazine
• Not a science/medical journal—it was skateboarding, surfing, rock and roll
• Legibility alone doesn't guarantee communication

Magazine Philosophy
• More interesting place than simply dropping things in
• Allows design to end up somewhere more interesting
• Every page/cover is new design assignment
• "Don't judge a book by its cover" is wrong—if designer did their job, you can
• Designer should have read it, understood it, shown it in cover

🎓 THE THREE-PART WORKSHOP ASSIGNMENT
================================================================================

Assignment 1: Who Are You?
Purpose: Show design communicates before somebody starts reading

Parameters:
• Use only letters of your first name
• Only black and white (some gray acceptable)
• Arrange so we get sense of who you are
• Students don't see each other's work during creation

What the Exercise Reveals:
• Experimental, dreamy, open, mysterious, organized, friendly
• Confidence level (loud vs. quiet)
• Top drawer organization (surprisingly accurate)
• Whether you'd want to meet this person at 3am in an alley
• Edges rough but soft = look difficult but actually kind hearted
• Amount of thought/time invested shows in result

Key Lessons:
• Impossible not to communicate—even "wrong" messages still communicate
• Same assignment, materials = completely different results
• Your personality comes through whether you intend it or not
• Ripped vs. cut edges send different messages
• Bold strokes vs. delicate placement affect meaning
• White space as important as black ink
• Class could accurately describe person from their name design

Design Principles Demonstrated:
• Letters bleeding off edge allows viewer in vs. feeling trapped
• Structured can still be dynamic
• Mystery makes people want to know more (very powerful)
• Literal connections vs. felt connections
• How you cut/tear/place matters enormously

Assignment 2: How's Your Life Going?
Purpose: Show power of color, shape, texture to send messages

Parameters:
• Represent how your life is going RIGHT NOW (today/this week)
• Use color, shape, texture—no restrictions on materials
• No letters, no words—pure visual communication
• Get sense without being told and without reading anything

What the Exercise Reveals:
• Chaotic vs. organized
• Happy/sad emotional states
• Stressed, juggling, overwhelmed
• Positivity, energy, movement
• Pressure, confinement
• Lively and vibrant vs. happy (important distinction)
• Pregnancy, anxiety, isolation, challenging weeks

Color Analysis:
• Orange and light blue = generally happy, festive
• Dark grays overtaking bright colors = darker message
• Colors confined in tight borders = trapped, pressured
• Bright colors with rough energy = not smoothly going
• Use of house paint colors = personal significance

Shape & Texture Messages:
• Ripped vs. cleanly cut edges
• Rough brush strokes vs. delicate application
• Organized rectangles within rectangles
• Things falling off edge
• Central placement with something interfering
• White space indicating openness or loneliness

Key Lessons:
• Color is huge message sender and emotion sender
• Time invested = time audience spends with work
• If done quickly, audience reaction is quick too
• Thought-through work makes people want to know more
• Using personally meaningful materials strengthens work
• Can be too literal or not expressive enough
• Balance needed between organization and chaos

Accuracy of Communication:
• Students reported comments as "spot on," "uncomfortably accurate"
• Even details not consciously intended came through
• Class could distinguish between challenging week vs. horrible week
• Subtle meanings (lively vs. happy) were communicated
• Personal choices (pregnancy, anxiety) could be felt

Assignment 3: Where Do You Want to Be in 10 Years?
Purpose: See the future, use all tools (type, color, texture, shape, size)

Parameters:
• No restrictions—can add type to previous color work
• Wide open creative freedom
• Should be best work since no restrictions
• More personal information = more interesting result
• Show where you see yourself in 10 years

What Makes These Work:
• Personal information with meaning creates unique results
• Total freedom could have led to generic work
• Instead, personal meaning elevated all pieces
• Impossible to do any other way—only these students could create these
• Encourages putting yourself into all future work

Example Interpretations:

"Keep On" Piece:
• Repeated type, optimistic color use
• Wrinkled paper texture = room for mistakes
• Not happy all the time but willing to keep fighting
• Balance rather than pure happiness
• Black contributing to balanced message

"Order" Hidden by Chaos:
• Green O representing order
• Green as "wise color"
• "Predictable chaos" concept
• Still want chaos in 10 years but wiser
• Balance of structure and spontaneity

Window/Peace Piece:
• Mental state more than physical location
• More calm in 10 years
• Literal use of window (maybe too literal)
• Motion in waves—debate about direction
• No type used (breaking assignment slightly)

"Grow/Row" Piece:
• Word reads both ways: moving forward AND growing
• Blue = professional life going upward
• Orange/yellow = happiness and personal life flowing
• Dots = continued, unknown future
• Very strong concept with multiple meanings

Dot-Dot-Dot Future:
• Bright colors, exciting shapes
• Movement and animation felt
• Ellipsis creates uncertainty: "I don't know what's coming"
• Could work as real poster but needs more information
• Mirrored but not equal images show craftsmanship

Key Workshop Lessons:
• Design intrigues before reading starts
• Send message consistent with what they'll read/experience
• Make people want to walk across street to read small print
• Uniqueness only you can bring—no one has your background
• Experiment more, push yourself, put yourself in work
• Free to disagree with instructor—your work, your decisions

⏰ THE LIFE LINE EXERCISE
================================================================================

Purpose & Execution
• Closing exercise to put career and life in perspective
• Title your page "Life Line"
• Draw line across sheet, leaving room on each end
• One end: X for birth date
• Other end: age when you die and that year
• Third X: today's date, placed proportionally on line
• Stare at this line quietly for several seconds

Student Reactions
• "How short I've lived"
• "How bad I am at math"
• "There's a lot left—don't know what it will be like"
• "Thought more of how much is left than how young I am"
• Students estimated 80-105 years old at death
• Exercise is "jarring" to see life visualized this way

The Research Behind It
• Study interviewed people on deathbeds in nursing homes
• Sought to find patterns in what people regretted
• Unexpected finding: very few regretted things they HAD done
• Almost unanimously regretted things they HADN'T done
• Examples: "Why didn't I take that job? Move? Ask her to dance?"
• "What was I thinking?"

The Core Message
• Only thing we know for sure: the past is gone
• Not getting that time back
• Don't know exactly when end is coming, but it is coming
• If there are things you want accomplished, what are you doing NOW?
• If you're just hoping they happen—good luck
• Don't want to get to the end with haunting regrets of things not done

Positive Interpretation
• Not meant to end on negative note
• Hopefully realize to take action on important things
• Simple exercise is powerful in its simplicity
• Take it as positive: "Wow, look how much time I have left!"
• That's exciting—it's unwritten, you can write it
• You will write it regardless, but write it without regrets
• It IS going to end, but the future is yours to create

Design Career Application
• If you don't take risks, you won't end up doing great work
• Playing it safe in design leads to boring, forgettable work
• The risks you DON'T take may be your biggest regrets
• Use your remaining time to push boundaries and experiment
• Put yourself into work NOW, not hoping for "someday"
• This field requires risk-taking to produce meaningful work

Philosophy Integration
• Complements Carson's "take risks" design philosophy
• Sociology background showing through (studied before design)
• Life perspective directly influences design approach
• No guarantees in life or design—make it count
• Regrets about safety vs. celebrating bold choices

Action Items from Exercise
• Hang onto your Life Line—keep it as reminder
• Do this exercise at home—worthwhile to stare at
• Identify what you want to accomplish by life's end
• Start taking action NOW to ensure those things happen
• Don't let fear or playing it safe prevent you from doing
• Write your future in a way you won't regret

💼 CAREER ADVICE & PRACTICAL GUIDANCE
================================================================================

Making Fundamental Decisions

Work vs. Location Priority:
• Most important: Is work most important or where you live?
• This influences decisions whether you know it or not
• If work is most important: go where that work is
• Hollywood movie posters → Go to Hollywood
• Record work → Maybe New York
• Country music posters → Nashville
• If location matters more: choose place first, find work there

Workspace Considerations:
• Make what works for you a priority
• Can work anywhere with laptop: planes, beach, apartment
• Must isolate yourself at some point to really concentrate
• Carson doesn't have internet in Amsterdam apartment (avoids distraction)
• Can use internet during day at work-share places
• For concentration: no Wi-Fi, no disruptions
• Work environment has huge impact on work quality

Committee Design Problem:
• Your work environment affects your work
• Comments from night janitor or office mates register subconsciously
• Early collaboration has place, but trust yourself eventually
• That's why you were hired—for YOUR vision
• Committee design never gets great work
• Gets solid, okay work that's rarely horrible but never great
• "I don't want to live there"—in mediocre-but-safe territory

The Creative Process:
• After meetings and reading brief, isolate yourself
• Work in place you enjoy with favorite music
• Get in "the zone"—3-4 hours disappear
• Best work comes from this focused state
• Need ability to concentrate without interruption
• Protect this sacred creative time

Promoting Your Work

Essential Marketing:
• Could be best designer in world, but if nobody sees work—difficult
• Good chance they won't find you—actively promote yourself
• Today largely social media, but don't rely only on obvious platforms
• Get in front of other successful designers in your specialty
• Send work to people you read about
• Enter competitions for acknowledgement
• Getting seen leads to more work and clients

Creative Promotion Approaches:
• Look for inroads beyond obvious social media
• Direct connection to clients or decision makers
• Can get lost in Instagram maze even while it helps
• Be creative in how you get work in front of people
• That's how you continue doing what you enjoy
• Must have ability—won't matter if you don't
• But if you do have it, help people see it

The Spec Work Debate

Carson's Position:
• "I totally believe in spec"
• If you get freedom to experiment with little/no money—worth it
• Still worth it throughout career
• Still does it: considers project value beyond money
• Factors: Good for environment/people? Fun? Seen worldwide?

When Spec Makes Sense:
• Opportunity for lot of freedom and experimentation
• Project pushes you as designer
• People will see it—may bring more work
• Worth a shot for exposure and growth
• Only you can decide if you're being taken advantage of

When to Say No:

Typography Techniques

Font Combinations:
• Mix old traditional typeface with newer font (Armani example)
• Combine serif with sans serif in same logo (Lab 78)
• Use italic and non-italic together
• Mix uppercase and lowercase purposefully
• Microsoft Gothic: thick alphabetic with rounded edges—make it human

Playing with Individual Letters:
• Turn letters upside down when meaningful (Tony Hawk)
• Turn S on side to suggest waves/surfing motion (Magic Seaweed)
• Make one letter different color or size for emphasis
• Drop letters down to become part of other words
• Break apart words and reassemble unusually

Spacing & Positioning:
• Touch words and letters purposefully
• Vary space between lines dramatically
• Don't let columns be automatically even
• Type can overlap or almost overlap
• Leave breathing room where it feels right

Type as Graphic Element:
• Reverse type out of body copy to create shapes
• Large page numbers become graphic elements (Portugal magazine)
• Let type bleed off page edges
• Stack type in unexpected ways
• Type can disappear and reappear (Super Chunk example)

Photography Techniques

Cropping Strategies:
• Crop extremely tight—just an eye (PJ Harvey)
• Just a foot leaving frame (Mark paddleboard)
• Blow up small sections to get dramatic effect
• Remove expected context to create intrigue
• Crop to hint rather than show everything

Finding the Unexpected:
• Look for test Polaroids in photo packages (Lyle Lovett X)
• Contact sheets may hide treasures
• Behind-the-scenes shots can be perfect
• "What you weren't expecting" can become the cover
• Keep interesting images even without immediate use

Using Photography Non-Literally:
• Shadow instead of person (Burton snowboard)
• Wrong chemicals created perfect mood (Nine Inch Nails)
• Upside down photo reflected negative interview
• Photo sequence frame #5 to drive web traffic
• Close crop on product detail vs. full product shot

Personal Photography:
• Take constant photos with phone—never without it
• Photograph shapes, textures, colors that register
• Caribbean swing girl in Audi ad—personal photo collection
• Elevator number 3 incorporated in design
• Worn laptop keys became graphic element

Integration Approaches:
• Take hints from photo: line, angle, white space
• Match design energy to photo energy
• Square illustration against circular O (Tony Hawk)
• Movement in photo reflected in type treatment
• Don't just drop photos into pre-arranged boxes

Color Application

Color Psychology:
• Orange/light blue = happy, festive
• Yellow = happy but context changes everything
• Red = difficult, almost too strong
• Dark reds/dark blues = very different emotions
• Green = calm, wise (per student interpretation)
• Grays overtaking bright colors = darker message

Contextual Color Use:
• Bright colors confined = trapped feeling
• Pink to offset blues and greens (Channel Islands poster)
• Black contributing to balance, not pure happiness
• White space allowing colors to breathe
• Unexpected color pops creating interest

Color in Client Work:
• Microsoft campaign: color helping humanize
• The Macallan: nature colors, hands-on feeling
• Shark fin: red hint without being gory
• Portugal: anniversary colors with meaning
• Blue magazine: never making blue word blue (too obvious)

Collage Methods

Starting Process:
• Begin with color combinations that could work together
• Gather materials in color groups
• Start multiple pieces simultaneously
• Work between pieces when one isn't flowing
• Don't commit too early to one direction

Cutting & Building:
• What you think you're cutting for isn't always best part
• What's left after cutting often better than what you cut
• Use back of materials sometimes—nice texture
• White on white adds subtle texture
• Don't want readable anything or straight edges

Refinement:
• Add unexpected elements to break sameness
• Need something bolder to offset similar elements
• Turn pieces over to reduce distracting color
• Minor tweaks only your eye notices
• Simplicity often better than busy-ness

Finishing:
• Photograph collages—aware of light source and shadows
• Real shadows add dimension you can't fake digitally
• Sit down at very end—things move slightly
• Sometimes they get better with that movement
• When it feels right to you, it is—trust yourself

Logo Development Process

Initial Exploration:
• Set main words in different fonts you think could work
• Generate many variations quickly
• Move by eye and hand, not computer increments
• Show client wide range from safe to experimental
• They can't pick bold options if you don't show them

Black & White First:
• All logos must work in black and white first
• Test tiny and huge before adding color
• Simple/complicated works better than middle ground
• Sometimes extreme simplicity has most impact
• Color comes only after B&W is approved

Decision Making:
• When does it feel right? Trust your instinct
• Don't mind if takes a minute to figure out
• Thin line between figure out vs. missing people off
• People enjoy something to decode
• Readability can be challenged if meaningful

Testing:
• Coffee mug test: would people want this on merchandise?
• Works from distance and up close?
• Separates from competition?
• Reflects company personality?
• Can be used across all applications?

Magazine Design Approaches

Contents Pages:
• Traditionally didn't use page numbers
• When using numbers, make them graphic elements
• List in order of appearance—readers browse backwards anyway
• Great photography can carry contents (Albert Watson)
• Don't just politely list everything

Article Openers:
• Every opener is new design assignment
• Reinforce content through design choices
• Columns don't have to be even or flush left
• Type can touch, overlap, disappear
• Movement and energy in layout

Covers:
• Goal: get someone to pick it up, not just list contents
• Create reaction: "Whoa, what's going on?"
• Sometimes simple after crazy issue
• Sometimes submitted photo becomes perfect cover
• Details matter: Tony Hawk upside down for skateboard tricks

Spreads:
• Don't leave box for when photo shows up
• Look at photo and decide: enlarge? bleed? crop?
• Type and photo are one piece—design together
• Every element matters including bylines
• If you remove one element, does spread stop working?

FINAL THOUGHTS

David Carson's masterclass is fundamentally about trusting yourself, taking risks, and putting your humanity into design work. His approach challenges the increasingly conservative, corporate nature of modern graphic design by advocating for intuition over systems, experimentation over safety, and personal expression over committee approval. The three workshop assignments brilliantly demonstrate that design communicates powerfully before anyone reads a word, and that your unique background and personality are assets to be leveraged, not hidden.

The course is as much about life philosophy as design technique—the Life Line exercise makes clear that Carson sees design careers as part of larger questions about regret, risk, and living authentically. His father's test-pilot background and his own late entry into design inform a worldview where "taking chances" in design is celebrated precisely because the stakes aren't life-or-death, making hesitation seem foolish.

For students and professionals alike, the key is implementation: actually doing the assignments, actually photographing your environment, actually showing clients the experimental option, actually trusting your gut when something feels right. Carson's revolution wasn't theoretical—it was putting intuition and humanity into practice, one magazine page and album cover at a time, often against resistance and criticism. The masterclass offers both permission and methodology for doing the same.
