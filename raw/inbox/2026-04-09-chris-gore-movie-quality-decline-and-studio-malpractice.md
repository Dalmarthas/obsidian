xecutive Summary

Film critic Chris Gore argues that the decline in movie quality—especially in Hollywood blockbusters—stems from risk-averse, committee-driven studio systems and a loss of original, visionary leadership. He contrasts the rigid, formulaic approach of modern mainstream films with the creativity and risk-taking of earlier periods and highlights how indie films still manage to deliver authentic, engaging stories despite limited resources.

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Hollywood blockbusters increasingly prioritize formula, box-ticking, and global market appeal over originality and storytelling.
- Studio interference and excessive marketing lead to movies that rarely deliver on their hype or satisfy their core fanbase.
- The lack of creative visionaries and dominance of executives with little material passion have sidelined true innovation in mainstream cinema.
- Indie films, though often flawed, exhibit more heart and creativity due to fewer constraints and less oversight.
- The trend towards adaptations and franchises over original content signals a broader cultural and industry-wide risk aversion.

💡 Core Concepts

- **Studio Malpractice**: Gore criticizes studios for mishandling once-great franchises (e.g., Star Wars, Justice League) and failing to leverage their vast resources. He labels their missteps—like not uniting iconic characters or replacing directors with less ambitious alternatives—as "malpractice," arguing there are no excuses for failures on such scales.
- **Committee-Driven Content**: Major studios rely on committees, market research, and an overabundance of executives, leading to movies that are designed to check multiple demographic and political boxes rather than entertain.
- **The Marketing vs. Product Gap**: Gore notes that sophisticated movie marketing often sets unrealistic expectations that the actual films rarely fulfill, leading to disappointment and disenchantment in audiences.
- **Lost Visionary Leadership**: Citing figures like George Lucas, Alan Ladd, and Francis Ford Coppola, Gore laments the industry’s shift away from creative risk-takers and toward “factory filmmaking,” which prioritizes brand universality (especially for international, notably Chinese, markets) over coherent storytelling.
- **Rise of Adaptations**: Nearly all major films are now tied to established IP—books, comics, or franchises—rather than wholly new ideas. Studios aim for concepts that have already “proven” themselves, which stifles innovation.
- **Indie Film Exception**: Despite the industry trend, independent filmmakers continue to produce meaningful work. While indie films often lack polish, their emotional impact and creativity show what’s possible when creators aren’t hampered by excessive external control.

🔨 Actionable Tips

- **Defy Clichés**: Both filmmakers and audiences should recognize and challenge predictable tropes (e.g., final-act explosions, “blue light” battles) and encourage narratives that subvert expectations.
- **Prioritize Entertainment and Story**: Studios should refocus on making movies fun and engaging rather than overtly preachy or didactic.
- **Support Indie Projects**: Viewers who want innovation should seek out and support independent films, which are more likely to provide novel and meaningful cinematic experiences.
- **Advocate for Visionary Voices**: Encourage and recognize creative risk-takers within the industry who push for original storytelling, even within established franchises.

🔍 Additional Insights

- Gore identifies a generational and cultural fragmentation reflected in current cinema; movies today rarely unite audiences or reach consensus as iconic films of the past once did.
- Hollywood’s focus on global appeal dilutes cultural specificity, especially American identity, in stories.
- Gore’s call for risk-taking extends beyond directors to studio executives, highlighting the need for leadership willing to “roll the dice” on unproven concepts, as seen with occasional successes like “Joker.”
- While critical, Gore acknowledges the existence of worthwhile mainstream directors (e.g., Christopher Nolan), but notes even they are forced to compromise with “studio notes” and narrative spoon-feeding.
- He distinguishes between the lessons learned from indie failures and the inexcusable nature of big-budget failures, advocating for holding studios to a higher standard given their resources and influence.
