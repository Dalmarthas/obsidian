- Title of Video: How to Remember Everything You Read[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

📝 Executive Summary  
The video presents a structured system for remembering “everything you need” from what you read by balancing two stages of learning: consumption (taking information in) and digestion (processing it to encode into long‑term memory). It introduces the PACER framework, a five‑category model of information (Procedural, Analogous, Conceptual, Evidence, Reference), each with a specific processing method, to dramatically reduce forgetting and improve real‑world application of knowledge.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)

🎯 Key Takeaways

- Learning depends far more on how you digest information than on how much you consume; over‑consumption without processing leads to “mental vomiting” (forgetting up to ~90% of what you read).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- You should not aim to remember everything you read, but everything you _need_ to remember in a form that supports reasoning and problem‑solving.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Classify information into PACER types during reading and immediately apply the corresponding process (practice, critique, map, store & rehearse, or flashcard‑based recall).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Balance consumption and digestion in real time: if you cannot process with the correct method for a given category, you should stop or slow down rather than keep reading.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Evidence and reference details must not consume the bulk of your study time; priority goes to procedural, analogous, and conceptual knowledge that forms your core understanding.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

💡 Core Concepts

1. Two stages of learning: consumption vs. digestion
    

- Stage 1 (consumption) is reading, watching lectures, listening to audiobooks, etc.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Stage 2 (digestion) is the active processing that encodes information into long‑term memory (e.g., practice, mapping, rehearsal), and is the true driver of retention and usable knowledge.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Most people over‑optimize consumption (reading faster, 3x speed videos) and almost ignore digestion, leading to high forgetting rates and low transfer to problem‑solving.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. “Remember everything” is not the goal
    

- The example of Kim Peek shows that perfect recall does not guarantee good reasoning or performance on higher‑order tasks.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- For academic and professional contexts (university, postgraduate, complex work), higher‑order thinking and problem‑solving matter more than raw memory, so selective, structured retention is the real objective.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. PACER: five categories of information  
    PACER is a classification system for information you read, with each category requiring a different digestion process.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

- P – Procedural: “how to” execute something (skills, techniques, methods, code, language usage, clinical exam steps).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- A – Analogous: information that relates to or reminds you of something you already know, whether within the same domain or across domains (e.g., muscle contraction compared to swimming technique).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- C – Conceptual: “what it is” – facts, principles, theories, explanations, relationships and applications that define understanding in most science and knowledge‑heavy fields.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- E – Evidence: concrete facts, statistics, dates, cases that make conceptual information more specific and support arguments (e.g., historical dates, precise data).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- R – Reference: highly specific details that rarely change your conceptual understanding but sometimes must be recalled (constants, gene names, variable attribute lists, obscure molecule names).[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

Analogous information can appear inside both procedural and conceptual information as a subset; it is not a separate content stream but a relational lens you create between new and prior knowledge.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)

1. Biological limits and the need for forced structure
    

- Cognitive and biological constraints limit how much information can be consumed and stored at once; modern information abundance makes it easy to exceed these limits and become overwhelmed.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- The PACER system is **deliberately** non‑natural; it forces structured processing because “natural” reading under time pressure tends to neglect digestion and plateau learning ability.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Priority of the first three categories
    

- Procedural, Analogous, and Conceptual information form the “foundation of knowledge” that enables real understanding and problem‑solving.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Evidence and Reference are built on top of that foundation and should not dominate your time while reading; over‑investing in memorizing details during reading destroys learning efficiency.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

🔨 Actionable Tips

1. Balance consumption and digestion
    

- After each reading segment, ask: “Have I digested this with the appropriate process yet?” If not, stop adding new content.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Treat over‑reading without digestion as “overeating”: you will “vomit” the information later through forgetting, wasting the original study time and forcing re‑reading.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Procedural information → immediate practice
    

- As soon as you read procedural content (e.g., how to code a function, how to perform a physical exam, how to conjugate a tense), apply it in real life or in a realistic exercise.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- If you do not have time or opportunity to practice, either switch to a different type of material or pause consumption; do not spend that time trying to memorize procedures passively.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Analogous information → active critique
    

- When something reminds you of prior knowledge, explicitly ask:
    
    - In what precise ways are these two things similar?
        
    - Where do they differ?
        
    - Under what conditions does the analogy break?
        
    - Could you adjust or extend the analogy to be more accurate?  
        [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
        
- Use this critique to integrate new concepts into your existing knowledge network instead of letting them sit as isolated facts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Conceptual information → mapping (mind maps / nonlinear notes)
    

- While reading conceptual material, build a non‑linear map that shows concepts and their interconnections rather than linear notes.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Continuously evolve the map as you read more: add nodes, redraw connections, reorganize structure to reflect your growing understanding, and optionally note analogies on or around the map.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Evidence information → store then rehearse
    

- During reading, immediately store evidence items (dates, statistics, case details) in a trusted external system: conceptual maps, second‑brain tools (Notion, Roam, Obsidian), flashcards, or a simple document.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Later (end of day or week), rehearse by using these pieces as examples in problem solving, explanations, or essays, explicitly linking each evidence item to the conceptual claim it supports.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Reference information → store then spaced recall
    

- When you encounter reference details you may need to reproduce from memory, add them straight into flashcards or another spaced repetition system.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Use daily or regular review sessions (e.g., Anki with spaced repetition and active recall) to drill these facts; do not attempt to “memorize them while reading” by rereading.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

1. Protect time for the “big three”
    

- While reading, notice when you are stuck rereading to force details into memory; treat this as a red flag that you are neglecting procedural, analogous, and conceptual processing.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Allocate fixed, separate blocks for rehearsal of Evidence/Reference (e.g., 30 minutes of flashcards) so that reading time remains focused on practice, critique, and mapping.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

🔍 Additional Insights

- Forgetting as “mental vomiting”: The video uses the metaphor of overeating and vomiting to illustrate how excessive consumption without digestion leads to an estimated loss of up to 90% of read content in some studies, despite hours of effort.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Expert vs. novice knowledge structure: Experts hold knowledge as a richly connected network, enabling flexible navigation and problem solving, whereas novices see mostly disconnected concepts; mapping is essentially an attempt to reconstruct this expert‑like network.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Analogy as a high‑leverage tool: Research repeatedly shows analogies are powerful for retention and understanding because they anchor new information onto existing neural networks, making encoding and retrieval more efficient.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Time‑pressure reality: The system assumes that most learners operate under time constraints; “natural” reading is acceptable only when time and outcomes do not matter, but under pressure a forced, structured approach is necessary to avoid cognitive overload.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- Scope of the method: PACER is framed as only a small subset of a broader “map of learning” that includes many other processes needed to become a consistently high‑performing learner; the newsletter is positioned as a resource to explore those additional processes without years of trial and error.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    

Ambiguities / potential debate

- The “up to 90% forgetting” figure is referenced in a general way (“some studies”) without citation, so the exact empirical basis and generalizability are unclear.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- The boundary between Evidence and Reference can be fuzzy in practice; the video notes they are very similar and often conflated, and misclassification could affect how efficiently a learner allocates rehearsal time.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
    
- The insistence on not practicing memorization of procedures or details while reading is strong; in some domains, brief on‑the‑spot rehearsal might still provide benefit, though the video argues it is a net efficiency loss compared with structured digestion later.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=okHkUIW46ks&list=WL&index=14)
