📋 OVERVIEW

Dan Brown's masterclass is a comprehensive guide to writing commercial thrillers, drawing from his experience creating bestselling novels including The Da Vinci Code, Angels and Demons, Inferno, and Origin. The course demystifies the thriller writing process by breaking it down into practical, actionable steps rather than abstract concepts. Brown emphasizes that successful thriller writing is less about innate talent and more about understanding the fundamental mechanics of storytelling—the same elements present in all compelling narratives, from classic literature to blockbuster films.

The masterclass progresses logically from conception to completion: finding ideas and choosing worlds, developing characters, conducting research, building suspense, structuring chapters, editing ruthlessly, and ultimately building a writing career. Brown's approach is grounded in his personal experience, sharing both his successes and failures, including his struggle with self-doubt while writing after The Da Vinci Code's massive success. He advocates for a disciplined process over waiting for inspiration, emphasizing that "protecting the process" is more important than any single day's output.

At its core, the masterclass teaches that thrillers work through the artful balance of questions and answers—making promises to readers and keeping them in unexpected ways. Brown reveals that his novels, despite their complexity, are built on simple foundations: a single dramatic question, a hero with clear motivations, obstacles that seem insurmountable, and payoffs that satisfy while surprising. The course provides writers with a practical toolkit for creating page-turning narratives while maintaining their own creative voice and passion for their subject matter.

🎯 CORE THEMES & PHILOSOPHY

THE ANATOMY OF STORYTELLING
• All successful stories contain the same fundamental elements—like different cars with the same engine components
• The difference between thrillers and other genres is primarily pace, not structure
• Every story needs: a world, a hero, a goal, obstacles, and resolution
• The magic formula: give readers what they want in ways they don't see coming

THE THREE C'S FRAMEWORK
1. CONTRACT: The promise made to readers (Will the hero escape? Solve the mystery? Save the world?)
   - Every promise, no matter how small, must be kept
   - Readers trust writers who honor their commitments
   - At the end of Da Vinci Code, Brown had a list of 17 unanswered promises to address

2. CLOCK: The element of time pressure that creates urgency
   - Compress timelines to increase tension (Brown prefers 24-hour narratives)
   - Try cutting your story's timeframe in half to see if it becomes more compelling
   - The ticking clock doesn't have to be literal but must be present

3. CRUCIBLE: Forces that keep characters from running away
   - Remove escape routes to force confrontation
   - Example: In Jaws, the sinking boat creates a crucible
   - Give your hero only one path forward—and make it treacherous

WRITING PHILOSOPHY
• Write the book YOU would want to read, not what you think others want
• "Write what you want to know" rather than "write what you know"
• Enthusiasm for your subject will shine through to readers
• The process itself must be the reward—external validation is unreliable
• Dare to suck first, knowing that good writers recognize when they're bad and delete accordingly

📚 THE COMPLETE WRITING PROCESS

PHASE 1: FINDING YOUR IDEA

Choosing Your World
• Start by selecting a world/domain that excites you, not a specific plot
• Best worlds contain both "the very old and the very new" for inherent conflict
• Examples: Vatican vs. antimatter (Angels & Demons), Dante vs. genetic engineering (Inferno)
• Research the world to let it inspire story possibilities

The Soul Dramatic Question
• Distill your novel to ONE simple question (the foundation brick)
• Examples: "Will Ahab catch the whale?" "Will the young lawyer escape the corrupt firm?"
• This becomes your North Star when plot gets complicated
• Make it morally ambiguous—questions where you can argue both sides

Finding Moral Gray Areas
• Think like a philosopher first, thriller writer second
• Scan headlines for natural points of conflict
• Example: Digital Fortress emerged from the question "How much civilian privacy will you give up for national security?"
• The best ideas allow legitimate arguments on both sides

Mining for Conflict
• Use Google News to search "[your topic] + lawsuit" or controversy
• Talk to specialists and ask "What's the worst thing that's happened in your field?"
• Look for the intersection where two worlds collide
• The tension in your chosen world will do heavy lifting for your plot

PHASE 2: RESEARCH AS INSPIRATION

Three Types of Research
1. Exploratory: Casting wide to see what inspires you
2. Focused: Deep dives once you've chosen your subject
3. Specific: Fact-checking and detail gathering during writing

Research Strategy
• Read broadly first (books, articles, documentaries)
• Talk to real specialists—they're almost always eager to share knowledge
• Visit locations in person when possible
• First visit: no notebook, just absorb the space
• Second visit: collect everything (notebook, dictaphone, video)
• Video is invaluable—captures sound, movement, exact distances

The 100:10 Rule
• For every 100 pieces of information gathered, you'll use about 10
• The other 90 go into your "Ideas for the Future" file
• Research from one book often becomes perfect for the next
• Research helps you make connections readers don't see coming

Research Dangers to Avoid
• Research can become procrastination—know when to stop and write
• Don't get precious about research that doesn't serve your story
• Use X's as placeholders for information you'll research later
• If it doesn't serve plot or character, it goes in the "future ideas" file

PHASE 3: BUILDING CHARACTERS

Starting with the Villain
• Consider creating your villain first—they define the hero
• Heroes only become heroic through the challenges they face
• Best villains operate in moral gray areas (wrong actions for right reasons)
• Example: Zobrist in Inferno wants to stop overpopulation through a virus
• Avoid money as primary motivation—it's boring

Hero Construction
• Choose a hero suited to your world (archaeologist for archaeology thriller)
• Make them relatable—give them skills AND weaknesses
• Langdon has eidetic memory but is claustrophobic and insecure
• Use humor to humanize and diffuse tension (Langdon's Mickey Mouse watch)
• Ordinary people in extraordinary circumstances often work best

Secondary Characters
• Create traveling companions with complementary skillsets
• Langdon pairs with cryptologists, scientists, people with access he lacks
• Romantic tension between hero and companion creates automatic suspense
• Let plot dictate which characters you need—don't pre-create a cast of thousands

Character Revelation Techniques
• Internal monologue is the most efficient way to reveal character
• Show character through how they interact with their environment
• Use former relationships for instant depth (ex-lovers, mentor-student)
• One or two striking details make characters memorable ("the bull")
• If a character won't return, don't give them a name—signal readers not to remember them

Creating Conflict Through Characters
• Pair characters with opposite viewpoints
• Example: Militant atheist Edmund Kirsch vs. devout Bishop Valdespino
• Research both sides of arguments to make each persuasive
• Characters at spectrum extremes create natural tension

Universal Character Tools
• What does each character WANT?
• Why CAN'T they have it?
• These two questions drive all character action
• Readers can sense when character motivations aren't clear

PHASE 4: STRUCTURE & PLOTTING

The Outlining Approach
• Brown uses extensive outlines before writing prose
• Creates order from chaos, allows seeing the "architecture"
• Makes it easier to move plot elements around
• Doesn't kill spontaneity—there's still discovery in the writing

Parallel Plotlines
• Multiple storylines create instant suspense
• Example: Lonely man in Seattle + divorced woman in Paris = promise they'll meet
• Each plotline should contain its own questions and promises
• Cumulatively, these create page-by-page suspense
• Build suspense through many small bricks, not one big one

Location as Structure
• Choose 4-6 key locations that excite you
• These become the pillars/chapters of your structure
• Order them geographically or thematically
• Locations can inspire plot points (the stairwell that inspired a death scene)
• But remember: readers read for characters, not travelogue

The Chapter Framework
• Start each chapter with ONE bullet point: what must this chapter accomplish?
• Chapters serve different purposes: introduce hero, chase scene, reveal secret, set hook
• That single purpose becomes the armature on which everything else hangs
• Open chapters with something fresh: new information, conflict, character, or location

Painting Into Corners
• Deliberately create impossible situations, then solve them
• Example: Surrounded Langdon in the Louvre, then figured out the GPS tracking dot escape
• Sometimes you won't know the solution until after creating the problem
• This technique leads to creative, unexpected solutions
• Go back and plant necessary setup details once you've found the solution

PHASE 5: CREATING SUSPENSE

The Promise System
• Suspense = making promises and withholding fulfillment
• Make as many promises as possible early in the book
• Answer them at a rate that doesn't confuse or frustrate
• Keep making NEW promises as you fulfill old ones
• Example Origin promises: Where do we come from? What will the killer do? Where is Winston?

Withholding Techniques
• Characters react to something horrifying—don't show readers what it is yet
• Lock information where heroes can't access it (safes, cryptexes, passwords)
• Have characters promise to explain "on the way" or "later"
• Make information physically impossible to obtain without completing tasks
• Never withhold because character chooses not to look—must be believable

The Cliffhanger Arsenal
• End chapters before giving resolution
• Character has revelation ("Eureka!")—don't reveal what they discovered until next chapter
• Answer one question with a surprising twist that raises new questions
• Physical peril that seems insurmountable
• Example: Tracking dot stops moving, "Langdon jumped" (but he didn't)

Starting Chapters with Urgency
• Begin with tension, discomfort, or conflict
• "If I don't make this meeting, I'm dead" vs. "What a perfect day"
• Start with questions: "Oh my God, where are they?"
• Consider cutting the first two paragraphs—start mid-action
• Pull description in after action begins

Impending Danger & Secrets
• Someone gardening while killer lurks in bushes
• Secrets in character backstories that haven't been revealed
• Hidden identities (Who is the Teacher? The Provost?)
• Each creates a promise to eventually reveal

Musical Elements in Pacing
• Vary dynamics: fortissimo (loud/intense) needs pianissimo (quiet) to be effective
• Quiet moments allow character development and reader breathing room
• Example: After chaos in Guggenheim, Langdon and Ambra have quiet boat scene
• The negative space makes the action more powerful

Flashback for Suspense
• Show dramatic moment, then flash back to "three weeks earlier"
• Creates promise: "I'll show you how we got here"
• Example: Breaking Bad opening—man in underwear with gun, then flashback to teacher
• Tension comes from knowing destination but not the journey

The Pulse Technique
• Brief sections that remind readers of ongoing threats
• Two-line paragraphs: "Don't forget the killer's outside the door"
• Keeps multiple threats alive in reader's mind
• Prevents forgetting about parallel dangers

PHASE 6: CHAPTERS & SCENES

Scene Construction Example (Da Vinci Code)
• Setup: Langdon sees photograph of murdered curator
• Withholding: Describe Langdon's reaction, not what's in photo
• Hint: "This symbol here, and the way his body is positioned"
• Result: Reader must turn page to learn what the symbol is
• If revealed immediately, "all air goes out of the balloon"

Musical Lessons for Writers
• Stories, like music, are about asking questions and providing answers
• Create tension and release
• Always return to the tonic—give readers what they want
• But give it to them in unexpected ways
• Wrong final chord = unsatisfied audience

Varying Chapter Purposes
• Introduction chapters
• Chase/action scenes
• Information reveals
• Relationship development
• Hook-setting chapters
• Each serves different function in overall narrative

Obstacles as Character Definition
• Choose obstacles that interest YOU personally
• Physical challenges if you're athletic
• Intellectual puzzles if you enjoy problem-solving
• Your enthusiasm and expertise will show
• Example: Marathon runner writing character who must physically reach destination in time

PHASE 7: DIALOGUE & EXPOSITION

Using Dialogue Characters
• Lee Teabing in Da Vinci Code presents wild theories
• Protects hero's credibility (Langdon can be skeptical)
• Langdon plays reader's role: "Wait, that's crazy"
• Readers know what to believe vs. what to question
• Allows exploring fringe ideas without protagonist endorsing them

Exposition Through Conflict
• Never have two characters agreeing for extended periods
• Pair expert with novice, believer with skeptic
• Disagreement makes information delivery dramatic
• Example: Atheist scientist vs. devout bishop discussing religion

PHASE 8: EDITING & REWRITING

The Painful Truth
• Brown threw out first 150 pages of The Lost Symbol after 1.5 years
• Commitment to idea doesn't mean keeping bad execution
• All writers are bad sometimes—good writers know when they're bad
• The real skill is recognizing what doesn't work and cutting it

Two Types of Editing
1. Editing while writing: Fixing sentences and paragraphs in real-time
2. Fresh perspective editing: Print pages, read away from desk after time has passed
   - Lets text "cool" so you can see it with new eyes
   - Read it as a reader would experience it
   - Take it outside, don't read at your desk

Color-Coding System
• Black: Paragraphs in good shape
• Blue, Green, Red: Increasingly problematic sections
• Red: Total disaster, needs major work
• Visual game: turn entire chapter black
• Helps see at a glance what shape chapter is in

Distillation Process
• Writing lean narrative doesn't mean writing less—means editing more
• Create abundant material, then distill to essential elements
• Metaphor: Maple syrup—boil away everything that isn't the essence
• Readers pay for the words you DELETE, not the words you keep

Evaluating Payoffs
• Inferno example: Initially planned for Langdon to stop virus
• Realized it was "too clean," lazy, too James Bond
• Tried 180-degree opposite: virus escapes
• Result: Much better, more surprising ending
• Always question if payoffs are good enough

When External Opinions Help
• Don't seek feedback too early—trust your taste first
• Too-early praise can make you lazy
• Too-early criticism can make you insecure
• Choose readers who share your taste and will be honest
• Look for people who "get what you're trying to do"

Knowing When You're Done
• When you read a chapter and don't trip over anything
• Chapter accomplishes what it set out to do
• Contains moments (humor, tension, interesting facts) appropriate to your style
• Novels are "abandoned, not finished"
• Be careful not to "fix it to death" and lose the magic

The Commitment Paradox
• Commit to your ideas—don't restart halfway through
• When stuck, ask "How do I make THIS work?" not "Should I start over?"
• But temper commitment with willingness to cut what doesn't work
• Difference between commitment and lazy editing

PHASE 10: PROTECTING YOUR PROCESS

The Daily Ritual
• Brown writes 4 AM - 11 AM, 365 days a year
• "Protect the process and the results will take care of themselves"
• Process is more important than any single day's output
• Signals to yourself: "This is the most important thing you'll do today"

The Process Mantra
• Sticky note on computer: "Protect the process and the results will take care of themselves"
• Job isn't necessarily to write a novel
• Job is to show up every day and do your best
• Eventually pages pile up and you have a novel
• Takes pressure off perfectionism

Being Tough vs. Being Gentle
• Be TOUGH on process: If you're supposed to write at 4 AM, don't sleep in
• Be GENTLE on output: If scene doesn't work, that's just part of the process
• Don't beat yourself up for wrong scenes—they help you find right ones
• Wrong scenes aren't failures, they're part of discovery

The Writing Space
• No internet, no email, no phone
• Private and distraction-free is more important than beautiful
• Brown wrote DaVinci Code outline in parents' laundry room on ironing board
• Wrote Deception Point in unheated office with space heater
• Don't get precious about perfect conditions

Different Kinds of Writing
• Brainstorming/walking with dictaphone
• Bullet-point outlining
• Rough drafting (getting it down, punctuation wrong, spelling terrible)
• Refined drafting (making it read well)
• Polishing (final details)
• Proofing (preparing for publication)
• Don't try to do all types in one pass—you'll waste time

The Hourly Movement Break
• Originally used hourglass, now uses "Time Out" app
• Computer shuts down for one minute every hour
• Forces push-ups and sit-ups
• Sedentary writing needs physical movement
• Best ideas often come while moving/walking

Set the Table for Breakfast
• End each day by writing first paragraph of next chapter
• Engine is already idling when you return
• Don't stare at blank page—return to momentum
• Even if you don't keep that paragraph, it gives you direction

The "Dare to Suck" Philosophy
• Learned from Steven Tyler of Aerosmith
• Band has weekly "dare to suck" meetings
• Everyone brings terrible ideas—9/10 are awful
• But 1/10 becomes "Dude Looks Like a Lady"
• Permission to be fearless in first drafts

Writer's Block Solution
• Writer's block is usually just "writing the wrong thing"
• Cure: Write the wrong thing multiple times until you find the right thing
• Put placeholder X's for unknown information and keep going
• Delete key is the most-used key on serious writers' keyboards
• Type like nobody's watching—because nobody is

PHASE 11: LIFE AS A WRITER

The Humbling Beginning
• Brown's first signing: 3 hours at card table, nobody bought books
• Someone asked him where the restroom was
• Felt as low as possible, questioned everything
• Lesson: Process itself must be reward, not external validation

Building Readership Takes Time
• First book won't sell well—this is normal
• Second probably won't either
• Brown's first three novels sold poorly
• After DaVinci Code succeeded, those earlier books became popular
• Nothing in the books changed—they always had potential

Promoting Your Own Work
• Brown and bookseller filled car trunk, spoke at rotary clubs and computer groups
• Bought ad in Radio & TV News Report offering to discuss topics
• Did drive-time radio across country for two months
• Invested own money and energy
• This commitment convinced publisher to buy second book

The Agent Question
• Most successful writers are "not self-made"
• Success requires team: agent, editor, publisher, marketer
• To make living as writer, agent is "absolutely critical"
• Find them by reading acknowledgments in books you love
• Those acknowledgments are "treasure troves" of industry contacts

Writing a Query Letter
• Must be as exciting and suspenseful as your novel
• Include comp titles: "My book is like X and Y"
• Research agents who represent your type of book
• Lists available online and in published books
• Some won't respond, but some will—that's when you shine

Modern Publishing Landscape
• Technology allows worldwide electronic publishing instantly
• But building real career requires surrounding yourself with right people
• Agent believes in you from "once upon a time" to bestseller status
• Publisher believes your work can make money
• This creates luxury of writing another book

The Luck Factor
• Every creative success involves luck element
• Also requires persistence
• Understanding that failing is part of process
• Fail while writing, eventually get it right
• Fail while publishing, eventually get it right

The DaVinci Code Moment
• Brown read galley and thought "This is the novel I'd want to read"
• Realized if this doesn't work, maybe he shouldn't be a writer
• But if nobody shared his taste, at least he'd written his book
• Fortunately, readers shared his taste
• Validation: Write for yourself first

PHASE 12: THE SECRET OF SECRETS

The Six-Year Struggle
• After DaVinci Code success, next novel took six years
• "Much harder to write when you know people will read it"
• Three years in, questioned whether he could do it
• Created physical prop: fake book with cover "The Secret of Secrets"

The Psychological Trick
• Held the fake book each morning
• Told himself: "I can feel the weight. It exists. I just have to write it."
• Photoshopped New York Times bestseller list with book at #1
• Tricked himself into believing book was already written and successful
• Got him through tough period

The Universal Truth
• Writing a novel is enormous undertaking
• Self-doubt WILL be part of process
• What saves you on bad days: your process, your ritual
• If starting novel: create that process/ritual now
• If in middle of novel: recommit to ritual

Final Encouragement
• Bad days will come when you don't know if you can do it
• Return to your ritual on those days
• The consistency and structure will carry you through
• Trust the process even when you don't trust the work

💡 KEY FRAMEWORKS & TECHNIQUES

THE QUESTION-AND-ANSWER STRUCTURE
• All stories are built on questions (hooks) and answers (payoffs)
• Ask many questions early, answer at a pace that doesn't frustrate
• Make new promises as you fulfill old ones
• The "what" is often obvious; readers care about the "how"
• Questions create suspense; satisfying answers create fulfillment

THE 180-DEGREE TECHNIQUE
• When payoff feels wrong, try exact opposite
• Example: Instead of stopping virus, let it escape
• Surprising but satisfying outcomes often live at extremes
• Forces creative problem-solving
• Tests whether "safe" choices are actually lazy choices

THE ARMATURE METHOD
• Start chapter with single hook: "This is what happens in this chapter"
• Build everything else around that central purpose
• Keeps writing focused and prevents meandering
• Like hanging ornaments on a single hook—one purpose, many details

THE RED HERRING
• Misdirect readers toward wrong villain or solution
• Bishop Aringarosa in DaVinci Code (name literally means "red herring")
• Must be believable enough that readers feel fooled but not cheated
• Part of genre's fun: leading readers astray then surprising them

LOCATION AS CHARACTER
• Great locations do "heavy lifting" for narrative interest
• Same conversation at Denny's vs. Guggenheim: one is automatically more interesting
• Choose locations that excite you personally
• Can inspire plot points (mysterious stairwell = death scene)
• But serve character/plot first, travelogue second

RESEARCHING FOR CONNECTIONS
• Pull 100 random facts from your world
• Much easier to find connections among 100 than among 3
• The more research, the "luckier" you get with surprising connections
• Example: William Blake's poem "sweet science reigns" perfect for Origin themes
• Connections don't appear magically—they appear through abundance

THE CRUCIBLE PRINCIPLE
• Remove all escape routes except one difficult path
• Makes heroes confront rather than flee
• Example: Sinking boat in Jaws prevents easy escape
• Creates satisfying finales because characters must face their challenges
• If characters can just drive away, you don't have a thriller

📖 CASE STUDIES & EXAMPLES

THE DA VINCI CODE
• Soul question: "What if Jesus were mortal and documents proved it?"
• Robert Langdon: Professor, everyman hero, audience's surrogate
• Silas: The muscle (physical threat), but operates in moral gray (rescued by church)
• The Teacher: Faceless puppet master, hidden identity
• Lee Teabing: Grail conspiracy theorist, allows wild ideas without compromising Langdon
• Bishop Aringarosa: Red herring with meaningful name
• Sophie: Cryptologist with complementary skills, romantic tension
• Central technique: Withholding photo of Sonnier's body until reader must turn page

ANGELS AND DEMONS
• Vatican vs. antimatter: old vs. new, religion vs. science
• Antimatter bomb: Literal ticking clock
• Path of Illumination: Intellectual treasure hunt with time pressure
• Camerlengo backstory added mid-writing when plot needed helicopter pilot
• Demonstrates: You control your universe, characters can develop needed skills

INFERNO
• Zobrist: Villain in gray area (creates virus to solve overpopulation)
• Dante's Inferno: Not history but prophecy
• Original ending: Langdon stops virus (too clean, lazy, James Bond)
• Better ending: Virus escapes (surprising, thought-provoking)
• Demonstrates: Willingness to try 180-degree opposite

ORIGIN
• Edmund Kirsch: Militant atheist with big discovery
• Bishop Valdespino: Devout counter-argument
• Avila: Killer with traumatic past and mysterious symbol tattoo
• Winston: AI with hidden location
• Multiple promises made quickly: Where do we come from? What will killer do? Where is Winston located?
• Locations as pillars: Guggenheim, Casa Milà, Sagrada Familia, Supercomputing Center
• Quiet boat scene after Guggenheim chaos: Pianissimo after fortissimo
• Rabbi Cervés: Noble character readers think might be safe—then killed (raises stakes)

DIGITAL FORTRESS
• Emerged from real experience: Secret Service reading student's email
• Question: "How much privacy give up for security?"
• David Becker: Linguistics professor (finding Langdon's archetype)
• First novel—sold poorly initially
• Later became popular after DaVinci Code success

DECEPTION POINT
• Written in freezing, unheated office
• Brown convinced setting influenced Arctic Circle setting
• Demonstrates: Don't need perfect writing space
• Called glaciologist who was thrilled to discuss ice

THE LOST SYMBOL
• Threw out first 150 pages after 1.5 years
• Devastating but necessary
• Remained committed to core idea
• Found better execution through persistence

✅ ACTION ITEMS & EXERCISES

GETTING STARTED
□ Scan Google News and stop when something catches your attention—that's your interest
□ Search "[your topic] + lawsuit" to find natural conflict points
□ List 10 worlds that fascinate you—choose the one you'd love to research for a year
□ Write one-sentence soul dramatic question for your novel
□ Test if you can argue both sides of your central question
□ Choose locations that excite you; make a Pinterest board or folder of images

DEVELOPING IDEAS
□ Write the prologue to 3 different story ideas; see which you're dying to finish
□ Write flap copy for each idea—which would YOU most want to read?
□ Create "Ideas for the Future" file for research that doesn't fit current project
□ Visit one location in person: first time without notes, second time document everything

CHARACTER WORK
□ Create your villain first—define what they want and why they can't have it
□ Give your hero one strength and one Achilles heel
□ List secondary characters your plot needs (not characters looking for purposes)
□ Write internal monologue for main character responding to a painting/object
□ Create two characters with diametrically opposed worldviews
□ Research both sides of your novel's central debate thoroughly

STRUCTURE & OUTLINING
□ Identify 4-6 key locations that will serve as your structural pillars
□ Write single bullet point for what each chapter must accomplish
□ Create outline showing questions you'll ask and when you'll answer them
□ Map parallel plotlines and their intersection points
□ List 17+ promises you make in first 50 pages
□ For each promise, note where/how you'll fulfill it

SUSPENSE BUILDING
□ Paint your hero into a corner, then figure out how they escape
□ Take any chapter opening and delete first two paragraphs—start mid-action
□ End a chapter earlier than feels natural, put resolution in next chapter
□ Create one scene where character reacts to something horrifying you don't show reader yet
□ Write a flashback opening: dramatic moment, then "three weeks earlier"
□ Add three "pulse" sections that remind reader of ongoing threats

RESEARCH EXERCISES
□ Record yourself walking and brainstorming with dictaphone
□ Contact one specialist in your field; prepare questions showing you've done homework
□ Take video walkthrough of a location, noting sounds and exact distances
□ Gather 100 facts about your world; identify 10 surprising connections
□ Create the 90/10 split: which research serves story, which goes in future file?

WRITING PROCESS
□ Establish your daily writing time and protect it for one week
□ Eliminate internet from writing space (use apps to block if necessary)
□ Set hourly movement break (app or timer) and do push-ups/sit-ups
□ Write first paragraph of tomorrow's chapter before stopping today
□ Use color-coding (black/blue/green/red) for one chapter
□ Put X's where you need information instead of stopping to research

EDITING & REVISION
□ Print chapter and read away from desk after 2-3 days
□ Identify one payoff that feels "too clean"—try 180-degree opposite
□ List every promise made in manuscript; check each is fulfilled
□ Remove one subplot or character that doesn't serve core question
□ Read chapter asking: "What if I removed this? What changes?" (If nothing changes, cut it)
□ Share first 50 pages with reader who shares your taste

CAREER BUILDING
□ Read acknowledgments from 5 books in your genre; note agents and editors
□ Draft query letter as exciting as your novel; include 2-3 comp titles
□ Research 20 agents who represent your type of work
□ Create list of outlets (podcasts, blogs, local groups) for self-promotion
□ Start building author platform before finishing manuscript

MINDSET & PERSISTENCE
□ Create physical artifact representing your completed book
□ Write your book's title on a "bestseller list" and post it where you write
□ When doubting, recommit to process (not to producing perfect pages)
□ Read one thriller you love, noting every time you feel suspense and why
□ Keep "Dare to Suck" mentality—write terrible ideas knowing most won't work
□ On bad days, return to ritual even if output is poor

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAYS

FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES
1. All successful stories contain the same elements—thrillers differ mainly in pace
2. Write the book YOU would want to read, not what you think others want
3. Choose a world that excites you; enthusiasm will shine through to readers
4. Start with one simple soul dramatic question you can build everything around
5. The best ideas exist in moral gray areas where both sides have valid arguments

THE WRITING PROCESS
6. Protect your process—consistency matters more than daily output perfection
7. Research abundantly but know when research becomes procrastination
8. Create your villain first; they define what makes your hero heroic
9. Build suspense through many small promises, not one big one
10. Make promises early and often; keep them in unexpected ways

CRAFT TECHNIQUES
11. Withhold information to create suspense—show reactions, not what caused them
12. Paint your hero into corners, then force creative solutions
13. End chapters earlier than feels natural to force page-turning
14. Start chapters with tension, conflict, or questions—not peaceful description
15. Use locations as characters that do narrative heavy lifting

STRUCTURE & PLOTTING
16. Outline extensively to see architecture and move pieces around
17. Each chapter needs one clear purpose—the armature everything hangs on
18. Parallel plotlines create automatic suspense through promised intersections
19. Compress timelines—try cutting your timeframe in half for increased tension
20. Remove all escape routes (crucible) to force characters to confront challenges

CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT
21. Give heroes strengths AND weaknesses (Langdon's memory and claustrophobia)
22. Pair protagonists with companions who have complementary skill sets
23. Create characters with diametrically opposed worldviews for conflict
24. Use internal monologue—most efficient way to reveal character
25. What does each character WANT and why CAN'T they have it?

EDITING & REVISION
26. All writers are bad sometimes—the skill is recognizing it and deleting
27. Read your work printed out, away from your desk, after time has passed
28. If removing something changes nothing in your story, it doesn't belong
29. When payoffs feel wrong, try 180-degree opposite (virus escapes vs. stopped)
30. Keep every promise no matter how small—readers trust writers who honor commitments

PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT
31. Building readership takes time—first books rarely succeed immediately
32. Invest in promoting your own work; take matters into your own hands
33. Find agents by reading acknowledgments in books you love
34. Surround yourself with the right team—success is never self-made
35. The process itself must be the reward; external validation is unreliable

MINDSET & PERSISTENCE
36. Self-doubt WILL be part of the process—ritual saves you on difficult days
37. "Dare to suck" first—give yourself permission to write terribly in early drafts
38. Every creative success involves luck element AND persistence element
39. Commit to ideas but recognize when execution isn't working
40. Trust your taste and the process; if you love it, others will share your taste
