ACTING MASTERCLASS: COMPLETE MASTER SUMMARY
===========================================

📋 OVERVIEW

This masterclass provides comprehensive training in professional acting technique, covering everything from monologue selection and preparation to audition strategies and career development. The course emphasizes practical, actionable techniques for both stage and screen work, with a strong focus on authenticity, emotional truth, and serving the text rather than one's ego. The instructor (clearly an experienced professional actor) draws from decades of experience in theater, film, and television to teach students how to break through superficial performance habits and connect with material at a deeper level.

The curriculum systematically builds skills across three main domains: text work (choosing and preparing monologues, understanding language, carving out meaning), physical and vocal technique (mask work, grounding performances, varying energy), and professional development (auditioning, understanding different mediums, career strategy). Throughout, the emphasis is on doing the work consistently, taking risks, being willing to fail, and always serving the writing rather than demonstrating one's own talent. The course is designed for actors at all levels who want to develop a sustainable, professional approach to the craft.

Key philosophical principles thread through every lesson: theater lives on disappointments and reactions rather than predetermined moods; audiences want to do 50% of the work rather than having everything given to them; acting is about reacting, not acting; and the only way to recognize excellence is to be willing to "stink" and fall on your face in rehearsal. The masterclass ultimately teaches actors how to prepare professionally, make bold choices, stay truthful, and build careers based on consistent craft development rather than relying solely on talent or luck.


🎯 CORE THEMES

AUTHENTICITY & TRUTH
• Acting is fundamentally about reacting, not performing
• Audiences want to see a human heart, not an affectation
• The best performances involve contradictions - complexity and duality that mirrors real human behavior
• Keeping emotion inside (sitting on a "keg of dynamite") is often more powerful than releasing it
• Trust the words and the text rather than feeling you need to illustrate everything physically
• Great acting makes audiences lean forward in silence, completely absorbed

SERVING THE WRITING
• The profession is about serving the writing, not serving yourself
• If you serve the writing, you will serve yourself by extension
• Don't bring a playwright's words down to your level - step up into their vision
• When an actor says "I wouldn't say it like that," it reveals they're missing the point
• Writers choose specific words for specific reasons - understand why
• Never say a piece is "bad writing" until you've exhausted every way to make it work

PREPARATION & CRAFT
• The only time many actors work on their craft is when they have an audition - this is insufficient
• Compare to athletes: Andy Murray doesn't only practice tennis when he has a match
• Consistent work prevents "atrophied muscles" when opportunity arrives
• See plays, read plays, watch great films - engage with the craft monthly at minimum
• Monologue work is necessary for auditions but dangerous because it teaches working alone
• Your work must always involve reacting to others, even in solo pieces

STAKES & INTENSITY
• Audiences only care when stakes are life-or-death high
• Playing high stakes doesn't mean being loud - it can be quiet intensity
• The most important thing you've ever said should feel like the most important thing
• Comedy pieces often fail because actors get seduced by laughs rather than truth
• Raising stakes by just 5-10% can transform a flat performance
• Theater lives on disappointments - characters survive on disappointments

VARIATION & DISCOVERY
• Never play just one mood or note throughout a piece
• Life constantly shifts - performances must reflect this movement
• Every new thought deserves a new breath
• Find where shifts happen in scenes - there could be six turning points in one scene
• Try material in completely opposite ways to discover new possibilities
• Keep surprising yourself and the audience with shifts and discoveries


📊 KEY CONCEPTS & TECHNIQUES

THE ARC OF CHARACTER
• Film and TV actors who've never done theater often don't understand character arcs
• They shoot intense scene after intense scene without understanding the journey
• Result: a "flat line" when edited together instead of a proper arc
• Theater trains you to understand telling a story over 2-3 hours
• For screen work, map out what the character knows in each scene vs. what they find out
• Draw literal lines through your script showing emotional/knowledge progression
• Consider you might be responding to something that happened three pages back

CARVING OUT WORDS
• Can't emphasize everything - must make choices about what matters
• In Shakespeare, rhythm and pattern tell you what to emphasize
• In other texts, emphasizing different words creates different meanings
• Specific technique: identify the exact word that communicates the core meaning
• Don't race through important lines hoping audiences won't notice
• Example: "crucial service" must land clearly, not mumbled as "crucialservice"
• Take time to relish language, especially poetic or heightened text

GROUNDING PERFORMANCES
• Give yourself activities that aren't necessarily in the text
• Activities center something and prevent "dancing" or floating
• Example: doing a romantic monologue while packing a suitcase grounds the emotion
• Example: planting seeds while talking about wanting land makes abstract concrete
• Physical tasks often reveal truth that pure emotional performance misses
• Domestic activities are particularly effective for grounding poetic pieces

MASK WORK - CORE PRINCIPLES
• Masks free actors from self-consciousness and judgment
• Looking in a mirror with a mask lets you see someone else, not yourself
• Allows exploration of completely different physical shapes and ages
• Gets you "over the hump" of hesitating on instincts
• Helps actors commit fully rather than going "half-baked"
• Creates catalyst for freedom to be unlike yourself

MASK WORK - SPECIFIC APPLICATIONS
• Try the same piece as an 82-year-old instead of your age
• Watch what happens when you see the older person in the mirror
• Choices become about the character, not getting laughs
• Material that seemed comic becomes serious and grounded
• Add physical deformities or unusual characteristics to push boundaries
• The stranger you become, the freer you are from limiting yourself

THE 50% PRINCIPLE
• Audiences want to do 50% of the emotional work themselves
• Giving everything to the audience makes them passive
• Holding back makes them lean forward, wanting more
• Example: Roman Polanski deliberately framing shots so audience couldn't see everything
• Result: entire audience leaning forward trying to see around the doorframe
• Apply this to acting: keep some emotion in, don't release everything

TRUSTING THE WORDS
• Actors often think words aren't enough and add excessive movement
• Sit on your hands to force yourself to trust the text
• "Stop fucking moving" and let the material speak
• Too much physical demonstration distracts from language
• Once you trust words, you'll discover natural moments for physical emphasis
• Particularly important in auditions where over-performance is common

ADDING STAKES TO SHIFT PERFORMANCE
• When a piece feels flat, artificially raise stakes to discover truth
• Example: "If he goes through with this, you're going to kill him"
• This false stake helps actor find real underlying urgency
• Another technique: make the monologue the most important thing ever said
• Can start with enormous anger even if that's not the "right" choice
• Heightened stakes reveal layers that moderate performance misses


✅ STEP-BY-STEP PROCESSES

CHOOSING A MONOLOGUE
1. Select material that showcases your specific talents and range
2. Ensure it has "meat on it" - substantial content to grab onto
3. Look for high stakes and consequences, not just "nice stories"
4. Avoid pieces that are all one mood from beginning to end
5. Choose writing you admire and want to serve
6. Make sure it takes you and the audience somewhere
7. Consider whether you'd be proud to be judged on this material
8. Have 2-5 monologues prepared at any time, and keep rotating new ones in
9. Don't rely on pieces you've done since college - keep challenging yourself

PREPARING TEXT - INITIAL ANALYSIS
1. Read the entire play, not just your sides or monologue
2. Look at page numbers - where in the story does this scene fall?
3. Read any crossed-out scenes above your scene - full of clues
4. Identify who you're talking to and what you want from them
5. Define the main problem or issue your character faces
6. Determine what happened immediately before this moment
7. Ask "why am I saying this?" for every line or thought
8. Write down questions about the character, even if you can't answer them

PREPARING TEXT - DEEP WORK
1. Identify every place something shifts in the scene (could be 6+ moments)
2. Look for discoveries, new information, lies told, changes in direction
3. Draw literal lines through script showing where these shifts occur
4. For each line, determine when the idea actually occurred to the character
5. Trace back - often you're responding to something from pages earlier
6. Carve out specific words that carry the essential meaning
7. Decide which words to emphasize and which to throw away
8. Practice saying lines with different emphasis to test interpretations

WORKING WITH SHAKESPEARE
1. Understand that rhythm and pattern indicate what to emphasize
2. Break down every single word to understand its meaning
3. Don't fly by words hoping audience won't notice you don't understand
4. Relish the language - don't just race through to get to the end
5. Carve out remarkable phrases (like "double knavery")
6. Make the music of the language come alive
7. Use vocal exercises to develop dexterity with heightened text
8. Remember: writers of this caliber chose every word carefully

REHEARSAL & EXPLORATION PROCESS
1. Start by working on material multiple times in your initial way
2. Once you think you "have it," flip it completely opposite
3. Try big and dramatic material sitting on your hands, trusting words
4. Try small intimate material with bigger gusto and weight
5. Work in front of a mirror to see yourself objectively
6. Share with friends in informal settings (coffee shops, parks)
7. Be open to ideas from anyone - good ideas come from anywhere
8. Let go of ego and ownership - you're a temporary custodian
9. Keep attacking it different ways until you find what sings

GROUNDING WITH ACTIVITY
1. Choose a domestic or physical task related to character's wants
2. Examples: packing, planting seeds, ironing, playing chess, starting a fire
3. Do the activity while speaking the monologue
4. Let the activity center and focus you
5. Notice how being grounded in task makes language more natural
6. The physical reality often reveals emotional truth
7. Prevents "floating" or performing in abstract space

USING MASKS FOR DISCOVERY
1. Choose a mask that's very different from your appearance
2. Work in front of a mirror first
3. Study the person in the mirror - who is this?
4. Let the mask's face suggest age, attitude, physicality
5. Begin speaking the monologue while watching this other person
6. Notice how choices become about character, not comedy
7. Commit fully to physical choices - go 90% not halfway
8. Allow yourself to become unrecognizable
9. Remove mask and note what discoveries carry over

AUDITION PREPARATION
1. Remember: they want you to be good - you solve their problem
2. Study the sides: notice page numbers, crossed-out scenes, all clues
3. Prepare 2-3 different ways to do the same material
4. Be ready to take direction and shift completely
5. Make bold choices but don't be married to them
6. If it's not going well mid-audition, acknowledge it and ask to start again
7. Open yourself to direction: "I may be in the forest - lead me back"
8. Know that sometimes directors give direction just to see if you take it
9. Practice your craft constantly, not just when you have auditions


🎬 ACTING FOR DIFFERENT MEDIUMS

STAGE VS. SCREEN - FUNDAMENTAL DIFFERENCES
• Theater: you give full performance start to finish, 2-3 hours
• Theater: you learn about character arc through performing it repeatedly
• Theater: you can be better tomorrow night than tonight - always learning
• Film/TV: scenes shot out of order, often over many days
• Film/TV: must map the arc intellectually since you don't perform it linearly
• Film/TV: frozen forever - never get better after it's shot

THEATER - SPECIFIC CHALLENGES
• First previews are terrifying - sharing work before you're confident in it
• Two halves of brain during previews: being in the play + remembering notes
• Extraordinary moment when both halves become one - you're fully "in it"
• Nothing compares to performing night after night for learning
• Learn about stamina, audiences, character depth through repetition
• Must maintain the show - directors return weeks later to remove additions
• Silence is the most powerful sound - entire audience pulling toward you
• Standing on stages where great actors performed before creates profound connection

FILM/TELEVISION - SPECIFIC TECHNIQUES
• Study the shooting schedule to know what scenes film when
• Identify what character knows vs. doesn't know in each scene
• Sometimes film a scene where character discovers info BEFORE filming the earlier scene where they don't know yet
• Must map the arc so when edited together, journey appears organic
• Stay open to what other actors do on set - adjust to unexpected choices
• Hope director/editor uses your best takes and cuts away from weaker moments
• Write down questions about character to prompt yourself during prep
• Tone can be challenge when different actors seem to be in different movies
• Maintain integrity of tone across scenes shot days or weeks apart

PREPARATION FOR SCREEN
• House of Cards example: 10 days per episode, 2 episodes at once = 20 shoot days
• Must analyze all scenes across both episodes before starting
• Draw lines showing character's knowledge progression
• Identify when ideas hit character - might be pages before they respond
• Sometimes responding to someone's expression, not their words
• Screen acting allows complexity and ambiguity - audiences embrace duality
• 12 years of theater (Old Vic) prepared for complexity of House of Cards
• Foundation for screen success is laid through stage work


💡 NOTABLE EXAMPLES & STORIES

THE ROSEMARY'S BABY FRAMING LESSON
• Cinematographer Billy Fraker set up shot of Ruth Gordon on phone
• Could see lamp, phone, table, her back through doorway
• Director Roman Polanski kept moving camera
• Eventually only showed piece of Ruth Gordon through doorway
• Couldn't see phone or lamp anymore
• At first preview, entire audience leaned forward trying to see around door
• Lesson: not giving audience everything makes them want it more

THE FOCUS PULLER'S ADVICE
• Film: Glengarry Glen Ross, scene with standing up and sitting down
• Focus puller Vinnie Gerardo approached after third take
• Said: "People aren't paying to see the top of your head"
• Demonstrated: stand up like THIS (facing camera) not like this
• Sit down like THIS (facing camera) not like this
• Actor took advice - watch the movie, he does it that way
• Lesson: good ideas can come from anyone, including crew
• Leave ego behind and listen to helpful direction regardless of source

THE NEBULOUS CASTING DIRECTOR
• Audition for FedEx commercial - guy walking up post office steps
• Very important, well-respected NYC casting director
• After reading, she said: "I don't think you're nebulous enough"
• Actor responded: "Nebulous? That's a cloud-like airy substance. You mean nebbish?"
• She insisted: "No, I mean nebulous"
• Actor: "Well you don't know what that word means then"
• Agent called later: "What the fuck did you do?"
• Didn't get the job (obviously)
• Lesson: sometimes auditions go badly, and that's part of the process

JOHNNY CARSON LIVE VS. TV
• Actor used to call Tonight Show pretending to be Johnny to get tickets
• Watching Johnny live revealed things TV didn't show
• On TV: you see him from chest up doing monologue
• Live: could see his full body - very erect posture
• Standing on one toe, lifting his body up
• One leg was shaking - Johnny was quite nervous
• Gave him a swivel to turn to Ed or Doc
• If joke didn't work, he had somewhere to go physically
• Actor added this full-body element to his impression
• Lesson: seeing performers live reveals physical dimensions of performance

THE MANAGER'S WISDOM
• Early in career, actor was upset about not being up for certain roles
• Went to manager angry: "Why aren't I auditioning for these parts?"
• Didn't even know what the roles were, just wanted to be considered
• Manager asked: "Do you think you're talented?" Yes. "Do you think you're raw, undeveloped, inexperienced, not ready?" ...Yes.
• "Then why do you want to show everyone where you are today?"
• "Pick roles you're right for. People make decisions about talent very quickly."
• "If you're not ready, don't fight for it. Find things you can do."
• Lesson: strategic career building means knowing when you're ready


📝 PRACTICAL EXERCISES & ACTION ITEMS

DAILY/WEEKLY CRAFT WORK
□ See a play at least once per month
□ Read a play you've heard about but never read
□ Visit public library to read classic plays
□ Watch great classic films (Spencer Tracy, Henry Fonda, etc.)
□ Watch contemporary quality shows (House of Cards mentioned as example)
□ Engage with the craft so "muscles don't atrophy"
□ Work on monologues even when you don't have auditions scheduled

MONOLOGUE ROTATION SYSTEM
□ Maintain 2-5 prepared monologues at all times
□ Rotate in new material regularly (don't keep same pieces from drama school)
□ Choose pieces with high stakes and consequences
□ Ensure variety: comedic, dramatic, classical, contemporary
□ Test material in informal settings with friends
□ Record yourself and listen back to identify patterns
□ Challenge yourself with pieces outside your comfort zone

TEXT ANALYSIS EXERCISES
□ Read the full play, not just your scene
□ Identify every shift point in your scene (mark with lines)
□ Trace back when ideas actually occurred to character
□ Underline specific words you'll emphasize
□ Write questions about character even if you can't answer them
□ For Shakespeare: define every single word you don't fully understand
□ Look up historical context and references in the text

VOCAL & PHYSICAL EXERCISES
□ Practice tongue twisters for dexterity: "Miles and miles of endless beach lined with mimosas"
□ "He thrusts his fists against the posts and still insists he sees the ghosts"
□ Work on breath control - new thought = new breath
□ Experiment with where voice sits (Pacino low and gruff vs. Carson back in throat)
□ Practice articulation - don't let accent be excuse for mumbling
□ Stand facing camera/mirror when speaking (they want to see your face)

MASK WORK EXERCISES
□ Obtain neutral masks and character masks
□ Work in front of full-length mirror with mask on
□ Study the person you see - who are they?
□ Try your monologue as someone 50 years older
□ Add physical deformities or unusual characteristics
□ Commit to choices - go 90% not halfway
□ Notice what discoveries carry over when mask comes off
□ Use masks when piece feels too much like "dancing"

GROUNDING EXERCISES
□ Choose domestic activities: packing, planting, ironing, cooking
□ Perform monologue while doing the activity
□ Let activity inform and center the performance
□ Try pieces that feel "floaty" or poetic with grounding activities
□ Notice how task makes language more natural and specific

VARIATION EXERCISES
□ Take prepared monologue and flip it to opposite interpretation
□ If you do it big, try it sitting on hands trusting only words
□ If you do it small, try it with much more energy and intensity
□ Start with enormous anger even if that's not "right" choice
□ Artificially add high stakes to discover truth ("you'll kill him if...")
□ Try sitting when you usually stand, or vice versa
□ Change location (what if this happens while packing? Playing chess?)

AUDITION PREPARATION DRILLS
□ Practice: stopping mid-performance to say "That was not good, may I start again?"
□ Practice: "I've made choices but may be in the forest - please guide me"
□ Prepare 2-3 completely different ways to do same sides
□ Take any direction and shift fully - practice this skill
□ Record yourself to identify habitual patterns (sawing air, mumbling, racing)
□ Study sides for page numbers and all contextual clues
□ Read crossed-out scenes above your scene

BUILDING YOUR NETWORK
□ Write letters to casting directors introducing yourself
□ Write to directors whose work you admire
□ Write to theater companies, including out-of-town productions
□ Take any job that puts you near industry professionals (even switchboard)
□ Invite your agent/manager to see your work in productions
□ Share work-in-progress with fellow actors for feedback
□ Build relationships with like-minded artists
