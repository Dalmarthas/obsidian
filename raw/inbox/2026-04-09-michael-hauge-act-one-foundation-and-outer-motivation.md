**Executive Summary**

Michael Hauge, a leading script consultant, outlines three critical mistakes screenwriters often make in Act 1 that undermine the entire screenplay. He emphasizes the necessity of a clear, visible goal for the protagonist, establishing the character’s routine life to foster empathy, and building conflict gradually without rushing the story’s progression. Hauge’s insights are aimed at helping writers lay a strong narrative foundation to enhance both clarity and audience engagement.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **A clear protagonist’s goal is fundamental:** All successful stories are guided by an unambiguous, visible objective driving the hero.
- **Establish everyday life and empathy:** The audience must quickly connect with the protagonist by seeing their ordinary world before the plot escalates.
- **Do not rush into the main storyline:** Act 1 should gradually orient the viewer and build up conflict before introducing the hero’s pursuit of their goal.
- **Overcomplicating early stages kills clarity:** Layers of themes and subplots without a central throughline result in confusion.
- **Empathy precedes character flaws and inner conflict:** Emotional connection must be prioritized above psychological depth early on.

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

**1. Hero’s Outer Motivation**

- Every compelling screenplay is anchored by a _visible finish line_—a tangible goal the protagonist strives for. Too often, writers focus on character depth or thematic complexity at the expense of narrative clarity, resulting in scripts bogged down by extraneous events rather than a focused story.
- Example: Even complex films like _Inception_ are built on a clear goal that can be stated in a single sentence.

**2. Show the Everyday Life First**

- Before the protagonist’s journey begins, the script shall briefly but distinctly show “who this character has been for some time,” setting context and creating empathy.
- Real-world illustration: In _First Blood_, John Rambo’s initial scenes are designed to evoke empathy by showcasing his mundane struggles before conflict arises.

**3. Don’t Rush the Story**

- There is a misconception that screenplays must launch straight into the action to keep audiences hooked. Hauge warns this can backfire, leading to burnout by page 70. Act 1’s purpose is to build orientation, establish stakes, and generate conflict at a measured pace before the protagonist actively pursues their main goal—ideally at the 25% mark of the script.

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- **Define your hero’s visible goal in one sentence.** Test if you—and your readers—can immediately envision what achieving the goal looks like.
- **Include the protagonist’s status quo in Act 1.** Let the audience meet the character’s routine and environment.
- **Evoke empathy quickly.** Do this before delving into flaws or psychological complexity.
- **Build, don’t rush, to the pursuit phase.** Use early scenes to layer conflict and opportunities without moving straight to the main goal.
- **Regularly check for narrative simplicity.** Remove unnecessary subplots or ideas that distract from the hero’s central objective.

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- _Story problems trace back to the lack of a clear outer motivation._ If a screenplay’s structure feels disjointed, reassess the protagonist’s visible goal.
- The advice transcends screenwriting; it applies to broader narrative and storytelling contexts where engagement relies on clarity and emotional connection.
- Hauge references both classic and contemporary films to reinforce the need for simplicity, proving that even intricate stories succeed because their foundation is straightforward.
- Establishing empathy does not mean making a hero flawless, but it requires inviting the audience to emotionally invest before tackling deeper psychological issues.
