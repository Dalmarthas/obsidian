Executive Summary

Aaron Sorkin describes his approach to writing the screenplay for "The Social Network," emphasizing the primacy of intention and obstacle in storytelling, the use of conflicting narrators, and the collaborative process with director David Fincher. Sorkin’s methodology focuses on crafting precise dialogue, refusing to dictate audience interpretation, and leveraging thorough research to underpin his narrative. He frames the story not as a technology tale, but as a timeless drama about ambition, friendship, and betrayal.

🎯 Key Takeaways

- **Storytelling Foundation:** Sorkin constructs stories around clear character intentions and formidable obstacles, making them universally relatable.
- **Conflicting Perspectives:** He deliberately presents multiple, often contradictory, narratives—mirroring courtroom drama and the Rashomon effect—to highlight subjectivity in truth.
- **Collaborative Precision:** The script’s density and style necessitated exact rehearsal with Fincher and actors, allowing no improvisation and ensuring every dialogue beat was intentional.
- **Research-Driven Writing:** Sorkin’s script was grounded in multifaceted research: legal documents, blogs, personal interviews, and real-world records.
- **Audience Autonomy:** The film avoids taking sides, inviting viewers to form their own judgments about events and characters.

💡 Core Concepts

- **Zeitgeist vs. Storytelling:** Sorkin contends that capturing "the spirit of the age" isn't directly writable; instead, focusing on compelling intentions and obstacles leads to resonant stories. He believes efforts to artificially write “zeitgeist” are futile.
- **Genesis of the Project:** Sorkin was drawn to the project after reading Ben Mezrich’s book proposal, working in parallel with the author and relying on ongoing mutual research.
- **Multi-Perspective Structure:** Rather than choosing a single factual account, Sorkin presents all three conflicting accounts from the real-life Facebook lawsuits, emphasizing unreliable narration and the subjectivity of memory. This structure is likened to the Rashomon method.
- **Characterization Approach:** Sorkin prefers to show what characters want over explicitly telling the audience who they are, believing true personality emerges through desire and challenge.
- **Writing Methodology:** He advocates for extensive brainstorming ("a rolodex of 50,000 bad ideas"), eschews detailed outlines, and instead writes toward the scene’s emotional core. He uses index cards, treating the writing journey as walking with a flashlight into the unknown.
- **Rehearsal & Language:** Because the script relies heavily on language and rhythm, Sorkin and Fincher conducted lengthy table reads to embed dialogue deep within the actors. No improvisation was permitted due to the script’s density.
- **Antihero and Empathy:** When writing morally ambiguous figures, Sorkin emphasizes the importance of respect and empathy for every character, viewing the writer’s job as advocating for them before an impartial judge.

🔨 Actionable Tips

- **Build characters around clear desires and significant obstacles to drive drama.**
- **Let action and desire reveal character, rather than direct description.**
- **In research-heavy scripts, embrace multiple perspectives without forcing resolution—allow ambiguity.**
- **Rely on structured rehearsal and precise language, especially for dialogue-driven scripts.**
- **Respect the audience’s intelligence by not dictating conclusions; allow multiple interpretations.**
- **Draw inspiration from unlikely sources—dialogue as musicality, or emotional triggers from real events.**

🔍 Additional Insights

- The screenplay was completed in parallel with Mezrich’s book, exemplifying how different media forms can inform each other during development.
- Sorkin does not align himself with technology but instead interprets modern phenomena through the lens of classical storytelling themes: betrayal, jealousy, power, and friendship.
- The finished script ran longer than industry norms, but Fincher and Sorkin proved its conciseness through read-throughs—demonstrating the importance of pacing over mere page count.
- Sorkin’s musical upbringing influenced his dialogic style, prioritizing rhythm and flow as much as meaning.
- The line “creation myths need a devil” originated from a Facebook communications director, revealing how offhand remarks can become central thematic anchors.
- Sorkin flags the challenge of writing young characters for the first time, recognizing the need for authentic, non-cliché representation.
