**Executive Summary**

This brief talk by Max Landis distills his perspective on what sets apart a good screenplay: sparseness, clarity, momentum, and fun-to-read execution. Landis emphasizes that while great ideas matter, it’s the disciplined application of clear, readable, and forward-moving scripting that grabs both readers and industry professionals. Good scripts, he argues, entertain even on the page and avoid needless complexity or verbose direction.

---

🎯 **Key Takeaways**

- **Sparseness and Clarity:** Use minimal, sharp writing—avoid overwriting or excessive direction.
- **Momentum and Structure:** Maintain always-forward narrative energy with a coherent three-act structure.
- **Character Consistency:** Characters do not need traditional “arcs” but must have clear direction and never act against established traits.
- **Fun-to-Read Factor:** Scripts should be enjoyable as written documents, not solely as blueprints for movies.
- **Industry Realities:** Success varies—selling original scripts, getting assignments, and being produced require different, sometimes unrelated, skills.

---

💡 **Core Concepts**

- **Good Execution in Screenwriting:**
    - Sparseness: Write only as much as needed; every word should add value.
    - Clarity: Direct, unambiguous prose makes scripts easier and more enjoyable to read.
    - “No cringe out louds”: Dialogue must sound natural when read aloud; awkward or forced lines should be eliminated.
    - Avoid over-direction: Trust the reader and future collaborators to interpret the script without being micromanaged.
    - Intent and Forward Momentum: Every scene and line should push the story ahead—avoid stagnant moments.
    - Optimal Page Count: Preferably between 95–100 pages, but never exceeding 121.
    - Three-Act Structure: Not dogmatic, but essential as a tool for managing momentum.
    - Character Trajectory: Even absent a “character arc,” every character must have purpose and a clear path.
    - Know Endpoints: The writer should have at least an idea of where characters and the narrative are headed.
- **Approach to Each Script:** There’s no single formula; what’s “good” varies by project. Each script is like building a unique, beautiful house—different methods and visions suit different genres and stories.
- **Fun on the Page:** Even unproducible scripts may sell well if they offer enjoyment during the read, capturing the imagination of executives and producers.

---

🔨 **Actionable Tips**

- Read your script out loud: Cut or revise dialogue that sounds unnatural or forced.
- Keep direction sparse; show trust in your collaborators’ interpretations.
- Outline characters’ destinations before starting, even if loosely.
- Focus on story momentum—every page should build or progress narrative or character.
- Trim to essentials: Strive to fit the script under 110 pages, prioritizing sub-100 if possible.

---

🔍 **Additional Insights**

- **Industry Context:**
    - Many writers earn substantial income from selling “fun-to-read” scripts that may never be produced.
    - Success is not always about production—there are many lucrative, unseen aspects of the screenwriting business.
    - Artistic grace and a dash of “art” in the script can distinguish work—don’t write “like an idiot,” respect audience intelligence.
