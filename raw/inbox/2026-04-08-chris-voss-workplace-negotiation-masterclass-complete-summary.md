🎯 CORE PHILOSOPHY & PRINCIPLES

THE FOUNDATION OF TACTICAL EMPATHY

Tactical empathy is NOT agreement - it's the demonstration of understanding another person's perspective, emotions, and motivations. This distinction is critical: you can understand someone's position without endorsing it. The purpose is to:

• Create neurological changes in the counterpart through oxytocin release (bonding hormone)
• Deactivate the amygdala (emotional threat response) to enable rational thinking
• Build trust and influence that enables difficult conversations
• Generate insights that move both parties toward better outcomes

KEY OPERATING PRINCIPLES

1. EMOTIONS DRIVE DECISIONS
   • People are 31% smarter in positive emotional states
   • Negative emotions activate the amygdala and shut down rational thinking
   • Addressing emotions first enables logical discussion later
   • Never ignore or dismiss emotional reactions

2. VISION DRIVES DECISION
   • People need to see the path (how to get there)
   • They need to understand the outcome (where they're going)
   • They need to grasp duration (how long it will take)
   • Paint verbal pictures to influence decision-making

3. COLLABORATION OVER COERCION
   • Short-term wins through pressure damage long-term relationships
   • Every community is small - reputation matters
   • Implementation matters more than agreement
   • Sustainable deals require genuine buy-in from both sides

4. LOSS AVERSION PRINCIPLE (Nobel Prize-winning insight)
   • Losses sting twice as much as equivalent gains
   • People take more risks to avoid losses than to achieve gains
   • Frame propositions around loss avoidance, not gain achievement
   • Compare different potential losses to shift perspective

5. THE POWER OF "NO"
   • "No" makes people feel safe and in control
   • "Yes" makes people suspicious of traps and hidden agendas
   • Saying "no" protects from perceived harm and reduces emotional arousal
   • Design questions to get productive "no" responses

SKILL #1: THE LATE NIGHT FM DJ VOICE

DESCRIPTION: A downward-inflecting, smooth, calming tone that conveys authority and trustworthiness without aggression.

HOW TO USE:
• Lower your voice slightly (tuck chin down to help)
• Use downward inflection at end of sentences (not upward)
• Speak slowly and deliberately
• Maintain a soothing, confident quality

EFFECTS ON LISTENER:
• Creates immediate sense of trust and credibility
• Calms both speaker and listener simultaneously
• Signals importance without saying "this is important"
• Used by news anchors and trusted communicators

WHEN TO USE:
• When delivering important information or asks
• During tense or emotional situations
• When you need to calm an agitated person
• For paraphrasing and summaries (80% of conversations)

COMPLEMENTARY TONE: THE CURIOUS VOICE
• Genuine curiosity tone disarms opposition
• Impossible to be angry while genuinely curious
• Makes you 31% smarter by creating positive mindset
• Encourages others to open up and explain themselves

SKILL #2: MIRRORING

DEFINITION: Repeating the last 1-3 words someone just said, creating a psychological connection and encouraging elaboration.

THE BASIC TECHNIQUE:
• Listen for the last 1-3 words of their statement
• Repeat those words with a downward inflection (or upward with curiosity)
• Use dynamic silence immediately after
• Wait for them to expand or clarify

EXAMPLE:
THEM: "I'm not looking for new partners right now."
YOU: "Right now?" [silence]
THEM: [expands on timing, reveals true concerns]

ALTERNATIVE MIRRORS:
• "New partners?" - explores their definition and criteria
• "Not looking?" - uncovers what would change their mind

WHY IT WORKS:
• Triggers people to rephrase and expand (not just repeat louder)
• Buys you time when caught off guard
• Helps counterparts hear themselves and reconsider
• Creates stream of consciousness without mental fatigue
• Demonstrates attention without interrogation

ADVANCED APPLICATION:
• After building skill, mirror selective words (not just last 1-3)
• Choose words that carry implications worth exploring
• Use to redirect conversation toward important topics
• Combine with labels for deeper excavation

COMMON MISTAKE TO AVOID:
Asking "What do you mean by that?" instead of mirroring. This often causes people to repeat the exact same words louder rather than expanding with new information or perspective.

SKILL #3: LABELING

DEFINITION: Verbally acknowledging emotions, thoughts, or dynamics you observe or predict in your counterpart.

THE FORMULA:
• "It seems like..."
• "It sounds like..."
• "It looks like..."
• "It feels like..."

Choose the phrase that feels most natural in the moment or situation.

HOW TO CONSTRUCT LABELS:

FOR EMOTIONS:
• "It sounds like you're frustrated by this situation"
• "It seems like this makes you feel unappreciated"
• "It looks like you're concerned about the timeline"

FOR DYNAMICS:
• "It seems like this is really important to you"
• "It sounds like you want to feel heard"
• "It looks like you're trying to protect your team"

THE CRITICAL FOLLOW-UP: DYNAMIC SILENCE
After any label, immediately stop talking. This is "the quick 2+1":
• The 2: Two parts of the statement
• The 1: One beat of silence
• Let the label sink in and work its neurological magic
• Observe their reaction with your eyes
• Wait for them to respond or continue

WHY LABELS WORK:
• Deactivate negative emotions by acknowledging them
• Create oxytocin bonding when people feel understood
• Inoculate against future negative emotions
• Generate trust without agreement
• Provide data when you get corrected

THE DENIAL-TO-LABEL SHIFT:
WRONG: "I don't want you to think I'm picking on you"
RIGHT: "It might seem like I'm picking on you"

The two-millimeter shift from denial to label is psychologically profound. Denials reinforce what you're denying; labels deactivate it.

LABELS FOR POSITIVE EMOTIONS:
While labels work powerfully on negatives, they also reinforce positives:
• "It sounds like this is exactly what you were hoping for"
• "It seems like this solution really resonates with you"

CONFRONTATIONAL LABELS (USE SPARINGLY):
For serious behavioral issues that must be addressed:
• "It seems like you think it's okay to call me that" [pause]
• Use analyst voice (slow, low, enunciated)
• Reserve for moments when boundaries must be set
• Follow with silence to allow recognition

SKILL #4: PARAPHRASING

DEFINITION: Restating the content/meaning of what someone said in your own words to confirm understanding.

KEY DISTINCTION FROM MIRRORING:
• Mirroring = exact words (1-3 words)
• Paraphrasing = same meaning, your words (more words)

HOW IT FUNCTIONS:
• Confirms your understanding of content (not emotions)
• Shows you're tracking the facts and circumstances
• Interrupts to demonstrate understanding, then returns conversation
• Builds toward summary by collecting key points

THE PARAPHRASE PATTERN:
1. Listen to what they're saying
2. Interrupt briefly with paraphrase
3. Immediately give conversation back
4. They initially startle, then appreciate
5. Connection strengthens as they feel heard

EXAMPLE:
THEM: [explaining complex project challenges]
YOU: "So the timeline shifted and now you're short-handed?"
THEM: "Exactly! And also..."

WHY PEOPLE ACCEPT THE INTERRUPTION:
Most interruptions steal the conversation. Paraphrasing interrupts to give it back, which people recognize and appreciate after initial surprise.

WHEN TO USE:
• During ongoing conversations to confirm understanding
• When gathering information for later summary
• To slow down emotional conversations
• To ensure you're both discussing the same facts

SKILL #5: SUMMARY (THE MOST POWERFUL SKILL)

DEFINITION: Combining paraphrase of facts with labels of emotions in a structured format that triggers "That's right!"

THE GOLD STANDARD STRUCTURE:

"So far you've told me [paraphrase facts and circumstances]. As a result, you feel [label emotions]."

BREAKING IT DOWN:

PART 1 - "So far you've told me":
• Use "you've told me" NOT "I've heard" (keeps focus on them)
• Paraphrase THEIR facts, not your facts or what you wish they said
• Include what's important to THEM, not what benefits you
• Be comprehensive but concise

PART 2 - "As a result, you feel":
• Label the emotions arising from those circumstances
• Use multiple emotion words if appropriate
• Distinguish past-focused (anger, disappointment) from future-focused (frustration, anxiety)
• Go for depth - the more accurate, the more powerful

THE MAGIC WORDS: "THAT'S RIGHT"

This is the goal of every summary. When someone says "that's right," it means:
• They feel completely understood
• Oxytocin (bonding hormone) releases
• Epiphanies and insights occur
• They become open to influence
• Trust levels dramatically increase
• They move toward solutions

WHAT "THAT'S RIGHT" IS NOT:
• "You're right" = appeasement, still defensive
• "I understand" = dismissive, shuts down dialogue
• "That's correct" = acknowledgment of facts, not feelings

THE FIVE KEY MOMENTS FOR SUMMARY:

1. AT THE BEGINNING
   • Clear out known negativity
   • Establish understanding early
   • Set collaborative tone

2. BEFORE ANY ASSERTION
   • Demonstrate understanding first
   • Earn the right to be heard
   • Reduce resistance to your points

3. FOR CLARIFICATION
   • When conversations get complex
   • To ensure alignment
   • Before moving to next topic

4. BEFORE COUNTER-OFFERS
   • Show you understand their position
   • Make them receptive to alternatives
   • Build goodwill before asking

5. AT THE END
   • Confirm mutual understanding
   • Cement agreements
   • Create shared vision of implementation

SELF-REGULATION BENEFIT:
The Black Swan client who says "Just before I feel like saying 'f*** you' to someone, I'll summarize to get a 'that's right'" is using summary to regulate her OWN emotions, not just the counterpart's. The process of summarizing forces you to:
• Slow down your thinking
• Process their perspective
• Calm your own reactions
• Respond strategically instead of emotionally

REAL-WORLD POWER: THE TERRORIST NEGOTIATION EXAMPLE

In the Philippines kidnapping case with Jeffrey Schilling:
• Terrorist demanded $10 million for 500 years of oppression
• Negotiator summarized EVERYTHING from terrorist's perspective
• "So far you've told me... 500 years of oppression, the Spanish invaders, the Japanese invaders, the American invaders, the atrocities, the economic harm, war damages, puppet government, separate moral homeland, injustice of it all..."
• Went completely silent after summary
• Terrorist said: "That's right"
• Ransom demand disappeared permanently
• Months later, hostage escaped when terrorists became disorganized
• Terrorist later called to say he had planned to kill the hostage but changed his mind
• "I don't know what you said to me... They should promote you"

Key lesson: Even a sociopathic terrorist responds to being understood. The power of summary transcends all contexts.

SKILL #6: DYNAMIC SILENCE

DEFINITION: Strategic pauses that create space for thinking, observation, and deeper responses.

THE CHALLENGE: 2 OUT OF 3 PEOPLE STRUGGLE WITH SILENCE

Different people interpret silence differently:
• Thinkers: "Finally, I can process!"
• Talkers: "They want me to fill the space"
• Accommodators: "They're angry and giving me the silent treatment"

HOW TO MASTER DYNAMIC SILENCE:

1. UNDERSTAND YOUR OWN RELATIONSHIP WITH SILENCE
   • Are you comfortable or uncomfortable?
   • Do you rush to fill pauses?
   • Can you tolerate 3-5 seconds of quiet?

2. USE SILENCE STRATEGICALLY AFTER:
   • Mirrors (always)
   • Labels (the "+1" in "quick 2+1")
   • Summaries (let "that's right" emerge naturally)
   • Calibrated questions (let them think)
   • No-oriented questions (give them autonomy)
   • Meaningful statements from the counterpart (show appreciation)

3. OBSERVE DURING SILENCE:
   • Watch facial expressions
   • Note body language shifts
   • Gather data about impact
   • Read their emotional state
   • Prepare your next move

4. RECOGNIZE ANGER SILENCE:
   When someone is angry and you go silent:
   • They often exhaust themselves
   • Burn through their emotional energy
   • Create opening for your label
   • Give you time to avoid reactive response

THE "ANGRY SPEECH" PRINCIPLE:
"Give a speech when you're angry and it'll be the greatest speech you'll ever regret."

Silence protects you from this. It:
• Prevents escalation
• Gives you thinking time
• Lets counterpart process
• Shows respect for their emotion
• Creates space for de-escalation

SILENCE AS APPRECIATION:
After meaningful statements, silence shows:
• You're genuinely considering their words
• What they said matters
• You appreciate their sharing
• They deserve thoughtful response

This makes people feel heard and valued, triggering oxytocin bonding.

SKILL #7: CALIBRATED QUESTIONS

DEFINITION: Open-ended "what" and "how" questions designed to make people think, reveal information, and shift perspective.

THE CORE QUESTIONS: WHAT & HOW

These replace: who, when, where, and especially WHY

WHY NOT "WHY":
• "Why" triggers defensiveness universally
• People are conditioned from childhood that "why" means they did something wrong
• "Why did you do that?" = accusation of wrongdoing
• Even when genuinely curious, people react negatively
• Avoid "why" unless using it as a surgical emotional strike (advanced technique)

WHAT QUESTIONS - Uncover Problems:
• "What's the biggest challenge you face?"
• "What are we up against here?"
• "What's keeping you up at night?"
• "What about this overwhelms you?"
• "What are you most afraid of happening?"
• "What's stopping you from making a decision?"

HOW QUESTIONS - Reveal Implementation:
• "How do you want to proceed?"
• "How should we deal with this?"
• "How does this affect where we want to be?"
• "How can I weigh out my options here?"
• "How would you like this to work?"

THE POWER QUESTION: "HOW AM I SUPPOSED TO DO THAT?"

This is the Black Swan version of saying "no" to impossible requests:
• Deferential tone is critical
• Forces counterpart to see your challenges (forced empathy)
• Makes them think about implementation difficulty
• Avoids direct confrontation
• Tests their willingness to collaborate

DELIVERY OPTIONS:

DOWNWARD INFLECTION (Analyst Voice):
"How am I supposed to do that?" [definitive statement]
• Signals serious constraint
• Commands attention
• Shows firm boundary

UPWARD INFLECTION (Curious Voice):
"How am I supposed to do that?" [genuine question]
• Invites problem-solving
• Maintains collaboration
• Seeks creative solutions

Both work - develop instinct for which through practice.

THE BAD VERSION (What NOT to do):
"How am I supposed to do that?!" [angry, accusatory tone]
• Inner voice betrays outer voice
• If you think they're an idiot, it will show
• Creates defensiveness instead of understanding
• Damages relationship

FORCED EMPATHY CONCEPT:

You demonstrate empathy to GET empathy. Calibrated "how" questions make counterparts:
• See your position
• Understand your constraints
• Consider your challenges
• Think about implementation from your perspective

This isn't manipulation - it's necessary for collaboration. Mutual understanding of both sides' needs is required for sustainable agreements.

THE COLLABORATION TEST:

If you ask "How am I supposed to do that?" and they respond "That's your problem," you've learned something valuable:
• They won't collaborate long-term
• They don't care about mutual success
• You should move on from this relationship
• Better to know now than after commitment

CALIBRATED QUESTION SEQUENCING:

Before difficult questions, add empathy:
• "This is going to sound harsh..."
• "This might seem selfish..."
• "This could come across wrong..."

Then ask: "How am I supposed to continue working for you if I'm not getting paid?"

The mini-accusation audit makes the question land better.

PRACTICE SCENARIOS:

OVERWHELMED EMPLOYEE:
• "What about this project overwhelms you?"
• "What's gotten you down?"
• "How is this affecting you?"
• "How does this differ from what you expected?"

BUDGET REQUEST DENIED:
• "What's driving that decision?"
• "How do we stay on track if we're scouting for a new team?"
• "What position does this leave us in?"

DELAYED DECISION:
• "What's stopping you from making a decision?"
• "How can I weigh my options here?"
• "What kind of position does waiting leave me in?"

SKILL #8: NO-ORIENTED QUESTIONS

DEFINITION: Questions designed to elicit "no" responses, making people feel safe and in control.

THE REVOLUTIONARY CONCEPT: GET THEM TO SAY "NO" INSTEAD OF "YES"

Traditional approach seeks "yes" responses. Black Swan Method recognizes:

YES = TRAP SUSPICION:
• "Would you like to make more money?" [Obviously yes, what's the catch?]
• "Would you like to travel the world for free?" [Sounds great, but I don't trust you]
• People have been trapped by "yes" too many times
• Creates wariness and defensiveness

NO = SAFETY AND CONTROL:
• Saying "no" makes people feel protected
• "No" means I'm safe from harm
• Triggers feeling of autonomy
• Reduces emotional arousal
• Opens them to continued dialogue
• Makes them trust you

THE NEUROLOGICAL REALITY:

From childhood, we learned:
• Hearing "no" as a child = I did something wrong (bad feeling)
• SAYING "no" as adult = I'm protecting myself (good feeling)

Change your questions so their "no" works for you.

COMMON QUESTION TRANSFORMATIONS:

INSTEAD OF: "Do you want to go to Geno's?"
ASK: "Are you against going to Geno's?"

INSTEAD OF: "Do you want to order in?"
ASK: "Are you guys against ordering in?"

INSTEAD OF: "Do you have a few minutes to talk?"
ASK: "Is this a bad time to talk?"

THE "BAD TIME TO TALK" POWER MOVE:

Two possible responses:
1. "No, no, it's never a bad time. What do you got?"
   = You have their undivided attention (for limited time)

2. "Yeah, it is a bad time..."
   = They propose a good time, which is what you wanted anyway

NO-ORIENTED QUESTIONS IN PRACTICE:

DELAYING JOB OFFER:
Manager: "I'm not sure I can offer you the job"
You: "Would you suggest I start looking for other opportunities in the meantime?"

Effect: Gentle, assertive, respectful - makes them want to solve YOUR problem

PROJECT BEING CANCELLED:
Boss: "We're letting go of your team"
You: "What's driving that decision?" [calibrated question first]
Then: "What position does that leave us in if we're scouting for a new team?"

OVERWHELMED EMPLOYEE:
Employee: "I'm overwhelmed by expectations"
You: "Would you be opposed to walking me through your biggest concerns?"

THE DOWNWARD INFLECTION DIFFERENCE:

"Would you suggest I start looking for other opportunities?"↘
• Conveys understanding of delicate situation
• Respectful of their position
• Neutral, not challenging

"Would you suggest I start looking for other opportunities?"↗
• Sounds like sarcasm or challenge
• "Get off your rear end and fix this"
• Creates defensiveness

Practice the downward inflection - it changes everything.

AFTER NO-ORIENTED QUESTIONS: SILENCE

Critical: After asking, go completely silent
• Works in person, phone, text, email
• Wait for their response
• Don't fill the space
• Let them process and respond

SKILL #9: ACCUSATION AUDIT

DEFINITION: Proactively labeling all negative thoughts, feelings, and accusations the counterpart might have about you, the situation, or your ask.

THIS IS A STRATEGY, NOT JUST A SKILL:

The accusation audit is a collection of negative labels used together to:
• Get ahead of negativity
• Deactivate negative emotions before they build
• Inoculate against potential reactions
• Accelerate conversation toward productive dialogue
• Clear the air so you can be heard

THE MODIFIED LABEL FORMAT:

Change from: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..."
To: "You probably think..." / "You're probably thinking..."

This stronger phrasing is appropriate for the audit because you're being proactive rather than reactive.

HOW TO CONSTRUCT AN ACCUSATION AUDIT:

1. LIST EVERY NEGATIVE THEY MIGHT THINK:
   • About you personally
   • About your profession/company
   • About the situation
   • About your motives
   • About what you're asking
   • Based on past interactions
   • Based on stereotypes

2. LOOK FOR SYNONYMS:
   One label might be perfect for you, but its synonym might be perfect for them
   Example: frustrated, annoyed, irritated, bothered, troubled

3. DON'T HOLD BACK:
   If you leave negatives out, they'll come back to haunt you
   Better to over-label than under-label
   "If you're not laying it on thick, you're not laying it on thick enough"

4. CLUSTER THEM TOGETHER:
   Deliver multiple negative labels in sequence
   Don't pause for response until you've said them all

EXAMPLE FROM COURSE OPENING:

"You probably think I'm doing this class just to make money. You probably think I'm doing this class just because it's about self-aggrandizement and making me more visible and more famous. Probably thinking since this guy's just a small town boy from Iowa, he didn't get enough attention growing up."

THE THREE KEY TIMES TO USE ACCUSATION AUDITS:

1. AT THE BEGINNING OF INTERACTIONS
   When circumstances suggest heavy negativity exists:
   • First meeting with someone who has reasons to distrust
   • Following a conflict or misunderstanding
   • Approaching someone with bad history
   • When your profession/role carries negative associations

2. BEFORE ANY ASK
   Because asks trigger negative reactions:
   • "You probably think this is expensive..."
   • "This might seem like I'm asking too much..."
   • "You're probably thinking you don't have time for this..."

3. BEFORE BAD NEWS
   To diminish impact of inevitable negative reaction:
   • "You're probably going to think this is unfair..."
   • "This is going to seem like terrible timing..."
   • "You're probably thinking we should have told you sooner..."

THE PREP SHEET EXERCISE:

For important negotiations:
• Hand-write at least 6 negative labels in advance
• Look for synonyms of each
• Plan when to deploy each one
• Read them in the moment if needed
• Use ALL of them during the interaction

WHY HAND-WRITING MATTERS:
Creates deeper neural encoding and helps with recall under pressure

THE INOCULATION EFFECT:

Once you label a negative, it loses 50-75% of its power:
• Like a vaccine - small exposure prevents big reaction
• Cannot hit you at full force
• Gives them permission to acknowledge it
• Often they'll actually deny or minimize what you said
• Creates psychological safety

🎯 STRATEGIC CONCEPTS & FRAMEWORKS

CONCEPT #1: FAIRNESS - THE F-BOMB

FAIRNESS AS MANIPULATION:

Common uses (all damaging long-term):
• "We've given you a fair offer"
• "I just want what's fair"
• "Be fair about this"

WHY IT'S EFFECTIVE SHORT-TERM:
• Triggers self-doubt: "Have I been unfair?"
• Creates immediate pressure to comply
• Makes people question themselves
• Often gets quick concessions

WHY IT DESTROYS RELATIONSHIPS:

The 3-to-12 Rule:
• Do something right: 3 people know
• Do something wrong: 12 people know
• Every community is small
• Reputation precedes you

Long-term consequences of using the F-bomb:
• People bad-mouth you in the community
• Future negotiations take longer
• Agreements fall through mysteriously
• Fewer people want to work with you
• You never know directly because victories mask damage

WHEN SOMEONE USES THE F-BOMB ON YOU:

Recognize what it reveals:
• They feel their position is weak
• They have no external criteria to support position
• They're vulnerable emotionally
• They're open to empathy and understanding

YOUR RESPONSE:
DO NOT go for the throat. Instead:
• Apply MORE empathy
• Use labels, mirrors, dynamic silence
• Ask calibrated questions
• Use no-oriented questions
• Build the relationship

THE PROACTIVE FAIRNESS APPROACH:

Say this at the beginning:
"I want you to feel like you've been treated fairly every step of the way. So if any point comes where you feel you're being treated unfairly, we will stop and address it right there."

When they later claim unfairness:
"You've probably forgotten this, but I said at the beginning it was important that you feel fairly treated. Now that you've let me know, let's go back to where the unfairness started and deal with it."

This diffuses the F-bomb and maintains control.

CONCEPT #2: DEADLINES

UNDERSTANDING DEADLINES:

Most deadlines are vague attempts to:
• Create urgency
• Move the process forward
• Reach the articulator's desired outcome faster
• Pressure the other side

THE SPECIFICITY TEST:

Firm deadlines answer all these:
• WHO will do it?
• WHAT will happen?
• WHEN exactly will it occur?
• HOW will it be done?

If you can't answer all four with specificity, the deadline has flexibility and face-saving outs built in.

EXAMPLE: THE PHILIPPINES KIDNAPPING

Kidnapper: "Tell the father if he doesn't pay, he's going to lose an egg"

Analysis of vagueness:
• WHAT: "Lose an egg" (child) - which one? Multiple children
• WHEN: Not stated, only implied
• WHO: Not specified who would do it
• HOW: Not explained

Conclusion: Intentionally vague threat designed to create pressure without commitment

RESPONDING TO DEADLINES:

Use labels:
• "Sounds like this is really important"
• "Sounds like time is of the essence"
• "Sounds like you want to get this done"
• "Sounds like you want us to pay attention"
• "Sounds like you don't want us to waste your time"

Then test gently with calibrated questions:
• "This might sound ignorant... What happens if we miss the deadline?"
• "How does a deadline affect where we want to be after we reach an agreement?"

SHIFT FOCUS TO IMPLEMENTATION:

"How do we ensure successful implementation given this timeline?"

This moves thinking from pressure-to-agree to quality-of-outcome, which is where real issues live. Deadlines ignore implementation - your job is to refocus attention there.

WHEN YOU HAVE A REAL DEADLINE:

If you're genuinely constrained, share it - but with empathy:

THE "MY PROBLEM" FORMULA:
"Here's my problem. The fact that my hair is on fire doesn't mean your hair has to be on fire too."

Say this multiple times:
• "This is my problem, not your problem"
• "This is not your fault"
• "I don't want you blindsided by this"

EXAMPLE: HOTEL CHECKOUT

"I'm sorry, this is my problem, this is not your problem, this is my problem..."
Result: Staff immediately stepped to his side: "No, no, what is it? How can we help?"

Anticipating their perspective and expressing it disarms their resistance.

CLOSING AN INDECISIVE CLIENT:

WRONG APPROACH: Throw out arbitrary deadline as pressure

RIGHT APPROACH: Use empathy first:
• "Sounds like you're having trouble making up your mind" (label)
• "Sounds like you have other priorities" (label)
• "Sounds like you don't yet feel the value" (label)

Then express YOUR constraints:
"Here's my problem. This is not your fault and this is not your problem. If I don't close this by [deadline], I'm going to have to move on. This is not your problem. I want to maintain a long-term relationship with you and let you know I'll be there to help you solve problems for as long as possible. So if I move on, I don't want you surprised by it, feel blindsided by it, or feel like I haven't warned you. I just want you to know what my problems are and the decisions I'm going to have to make."

This is empathic deadline expression:
• Takes burden on yourself
• Doesn't make it their fault
• Preserves relationship
• Gives them full information
• Lets them make the decision

CONCEPT #3: LOSS AVERSION (Nobel Prize Winning)

THE RESEARCH: Daniel Kahneman's Prospect Theory (2002 Nobel Prize in Behavioral Economics)

CORE PRINCIPLE:
Losses sting twice as much as equivalent gains

BEHAVIORAL IMPLICATIONS:
• Losing $5 feels like losing $10
• People take MORE risks to avoid losses than to achieve gains
• Decisions are primarily driven by perception of loss
• Status quo bias exists because change = potential loss

HOW MOST PEOPLE SELL (INCORRECTLY):
"This will improve your productivity by 20%!" (gain-focused)
"This will make you better off!" (gain-focused)
"This is great for you!" (gain-focused)

THE PROBLEM:
People aren't calculating the 20% gain - they're calculating:
• What will this cost me?
• What do I have to give up?
• What might I lose?

HOW TO LEVERAGE LOSS AVERSION:

Frame the SAME proposition as loss avoidance:
"Look, don't do this, keep the status quo - it'll cost you 20% each and every day that you don't move."

This is identical mathematically but psychologically completely different.

THE BUSINESS SAYING:
"I've never lost money on a deal I didn't make"

REFRAME TO REVEAL THE LOSS:
"How much money are you leaving on the table long-term by NOT making that deal and moving forward?"

COMPARING LOSSES:

People may focus on wrong loss:
• Focusing on loss today (dollars)
• When real loss is tomorrow (thousands of dollars)

YOUR JOB:
• Understand which loss they're seeing
• Use influence tools to get them to see different loss
• Have them compare the two losses
• The comparison favors the better decision (if you're doing this ethically)

CALIBRATED QUESTIONS FOR LOSS AVERSION:
• "What's it going to cost you NOT to do that?"
• "How much money are you leaving on the table?"
• "What happens if we maintain the status quo?"
• "How does waiting affect your position?"

SCENARIO #2: FRUSTRATED EMPLOYEE ABOUT OUTDATED SOFTWARE

SITUATION: Brandon wants software update. You know budget doesn't allow it this year. Goal: Make him feel heard.

THE INTERACTION:

BRANDON: "I think it's time we update our software for this project."

BRANDON: "We are using an interface from eons ago. I just think it's outdated."

YOU: "It seems like the fact that we're still using this software is very frustrating for you." (label)

BRANDON: "Yeah, it is frustrating."

YOU: "It can be very frustrating." (label reinforcement with downward inflection)

BRANDON: (calmer) "It can be very frustrating."

YOU: "What about the current software doesn't appear to be working anymore?" (calibrated question)

BRANDON: "I just think there are more efficient ways for us to achieve our goals."

YOU: "So far what you've told me is that although our current software has worked, it relies on outdated technology. And as a result, you feel frustrated because we could be doing so much better if we had a newer updated system." (summary)

BRANDON: "That's right. Thanks for hearing me out on this."

YOU: "Would it surprise you to know that we don't have the money in the budget for this this year?" (no-oriented question)

BRANDON: "No, not at all surprising. You know, if we are planning to include a software upgrade for next year in the budget, I'd be happy to help with that."

• He felt taken into account
• He became part of the solution
• To deliver good summary, you MUST actually listen
• The clarification creates oxytocin bonding moment
• Group summaries are especially powerful

THE ALTERNATIVE (WRONG) APPROACH:
"We don't have the money for this" - destroys everything built up

THE SETUP MATTERS:
No-oriented question works because of everything that came before it. Without proper setup, budget denial would feel like punch in the mouth.
