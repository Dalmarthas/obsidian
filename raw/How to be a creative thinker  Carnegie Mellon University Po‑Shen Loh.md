## 📝 Executive Summary

Po‑Shen Loh argues that in the age of AI and tools like ChatGPT, creativity means learning to “grade the homework” rather than just doing it: you must evaluate, adapt, and invent solutions instead of repeating patterns. Drawing on his journey as a mathematician, Olympiad coach, and social entrepreneur, he describes an educational ecosystem that trains students to think independently using challenging problems, live human interaction, and improv‑style communication skills. He frames creative thinking as a social, entrepreneurial activity: systematically discovering real pain points by engaging with people and then designing scalable, win‑win structures that create value for everyone involved.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

- Standardized tests and cram‑style competition prep destroy opportunities for students to invent and practice mental flexibility.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 💡 Core Concepts

## 1. From “doing homework” to “grading homework”

Loh opens by contrasting old‑style schooling (learn to do homework and pass exams) with the emerging reality where people routinely ask ChatGPT or similar tools for first‑pass answers. The human’s new role is to grade that output: check correctness, adapt it to context, and improve it. He likens this to how good software engineers use StackOverflow: they search for related examples, then critically integrate and adapt them rather than copy‑pasting. The essential capability is fast, analytical thinking to detect flaws and choose appropriate tools.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 2. Creativity as mental flexibility, not pattern drilling

Loh recalls that in the 1980s, math competitions worked as a training ground for creativity because every new problem was a chance to practice mental flexibility. Today, he sees a huge test-prep industry that tries to remove surprise by exposing students to every possible “strange” question type, leading to long hours of repetitive drilling. This not only exhausts students but, more importantly, removes their chance to invent and to struggle productively with novel situations. For Loh, creative thinking emerges precisely from confronting unfamiliar, non‑rehearsed problems and figuring out your own method.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 3. Win‑win‑win ecosystem design

Over about a decade, Loh iterated from a free explanation website (XP) with no business model to a recorded‑lecture product and finally to a scalable live ecosystem. He identifies three “pain points”:[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

- Middle school students and parents who want high‑quality math learning and thinking skills.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Very strong math high school students who need advanced, meaningful development opportunities.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Drama/acting talents needing flexible, paid, part‑time work that uses their communication skills.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    

By combining these groups, he creates a system where: middle schoolers get live classes taught by smiling “math geniuses” and supported by high‑quality drama‑trained coaches; high schoolers grow into polished communicators; actors have flexible, meaningful jobs. This win‑win‑win structure makes the model scalable while preserving human warmth and quality.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 4. Educational philosophy and curriculum focus

Loh’s live program intentionally teaches only a small set of subjects: algebra, geometry, combinatorics, and number theory. These areas are chosen because they form a curriculum that teaches how to think, not just what to memorize. He uses middle school math competition problems as a source of questions that students have not seen in school, preserving novelty. The key difference from typical training centers is that his program uses each problem to practice thinking processes, rather than to rehearse a catalog of tricks. The end goal is that, having finished this track, students realize they can learn anything and no longer need classes at all.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 5. Radical “win‑for‑the‑student” discipline

Within his company, Loh insists on a strict rule: they will only ask a high school student to do something if they can convincingly explain to the parent that it is the best use of that student’s limited time. If he cannot personally justify this, they don’t do it. This principle ensures alignment of incentives and pushes them to design activities that are genuinely developmental, not exploitative or filler.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 6. Creativity through real-world interaction

Loh emphasizes that you cannot create real value without interacting with people; purely theoretical ideation is not enough. He describes deliberately traveling city to city, giving park talks with full AV setups, which incidentally became large‑scale customer discovery as he spoke with thousands of parents and students. He also continues to go into schools, including those serving disadvantaged students, to personally teach sixth grade and see challenges first‑hand. For him, creative thinking is deeply social: understanding how diverse people “tick,” what they need, and what constraints they face.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 7. Improv and communication as creativity multipliers

Loh took improvisational comedy / acting classes to become better at communicating math and engaging audiences. He discovered that even a self‑described “math nerd” could significantly upgrade interpersonal skills with such training. This insight led him to incorporate drama‑trained actors into his ecosystem as coaches, giving students exposure to Broadway/Hollywood‑level communication skill while they learn math. Creativity here includes the ability to present, connect, and make complex ideas emotionally resonant.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 8. AI era and entrepreneurial mindset

Loh frames the AI era as a “Wild West” full of new opportunities, where those who are best at creating value will succeed. He argues everyone should be “a bit of an entrepreneur” or intrapreneur, constantly looking for ways to build new things either independently or within organizations. Success, for him, is not just making money; it is convincing a huge number of people to enjoy being thoughtful. Social entrepreneurship means designing systems that solve customers’ problems as fast as possible, even if that is bad for traditional lifetime customer value metrics.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 🔨 Actionable Tips

1. **Practice “grading” AI outputs.**
    
    - When you use ChatGPT or similar tools, treat the response as a draft: check logic, test edge cases, and ask “What’s missing or wrong here?” before accepting it.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Build a habit of comparing multiple possible approaches rather than relying on a single generated answer.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
2. **Seek novel problems, not more drills.**
    
    - Intentionally work on questions and tasks you haven’t seen before, especially in domains like math, strategy, or product design.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Limit repetitive “pattern” practice once you understand a technique; move on to variations that force you to think differently.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
3. **Design personal win‑win‑win projects.**
    
    - When you create a new initiative (course, side project, startup), map out three groups it could benefit and how each group clearly wins.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Stress‑test your design by asking: “Would a skeptical parent/manager agree this is the best use of each participant’s time?”[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
4. **Use communication training to enhance expertise.**
    
    - Combine your technical skill (e.g., math, engineering, marketing) with improv, acting, or storytelling practice to become more engaging and persuasive.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Look for roles or volunteer work where you teach or present; treat every session as practice in making complex ideas accessible.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
5. **Do real‑world customer discovery.**
    
    - Instead of guessing what users/learners need, go where they are: schools, online communities, public events, meetups.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Have structured conversations about their challenges, and look for repeated pain points that suggest high‑leverage problems to solve.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
6. **Constrain your curriculum to deepen thinking.**
    
    - In self‑learning or program design, pick a narrow set of areas that best train general thinking skills, and go deep rather than wide.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
        
    - Use each new topic as a context to practice your thinking process (how you approach problems), not just accumulate facts.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)

## 🔍 Additional Insights

- The matchstick puzzle (six matchsticks forming four equal triangles by building a 3D pyramid) illustrates “thinking outside the box”: the shift from 2D assumptions to 3D is a micro‑example of creative reframing.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Loh estimates his model could scale to about 100,000 high school students in the US teaching roughly a million middle school students, showing that thoughtful human‑centric models can still be large‑scale in the AI age.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- He repeatedly stresses that repetitive tasks should be offloaded to calculators, computers, or AI, preserving human effort for genuinely cognitive work; learning should mirror this by avoiding excessive repetition.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- There is a tension between classic business metrics (maximizing lifetime customer value) and his social‑entrepreneurship model, which aims to make students independent as quickly as possible; this is a deliberate, values‑driven trade‑off.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
    
- Ambiguity/unclear point: the talk does not detail the exact instructional format inside each live class (structure of sessions, assessment methods, specific roles of actors vs. math coaches), so implementation details of the ecosystem remain somewhat abstract.[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JpYA7WXkHyI&list=WL&index=25)
