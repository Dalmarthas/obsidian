###### Stress

## Stress may actually be beneficial in some kinds of memory tasks.

For practically as long as university students have been doing homework, they’ve been coming up with justifications for putting that homework off until the last possible minute. As a university professor, I never cease to be amazed at the creative angles that students come up with for making their [procrastination](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/procrastination "Psychology Today looks at procrastination") sound acceptable and in some cases even noble. The most popular by far is the one in which a student claims to intentionally put things off because he or she “works better under pressure.” Under the general heading of “Do as I say, not as I do” (considering my own bad habit of putting things off), I typically respond to this excuse with an amused shake of the head and an explanation of how misguided their reasoning is. The so-called “pressure” under which they believe they thrive, I explain, is really just stress, and far from helping them study to more efficiently, stress actually interferes with the cognitive processing of information, making them less likely to remember the material they are studying than if they reduced their stress level by studying at a more leisurely pace. Or so I tell them.

According to a recent [study](http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1074742716301009) at Witten/Herdecke University in Germany, however, I may have to rethink my sage advice. It turns out that studying under stress may actually help people remember certain kinds of information better than they would if they tried to learn the same material in a less [stressful](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/stress "Psychology Today looks at stressful") state of mind.

In an experiment designed to evaluate the effects of stress on prospective [memory](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/memory "Psychology Today looks at memory"), researchers had two groups of participants complete a prospective memory task called “Plan a Holiday.” In order to evaluate each of the phases involved in prospective memory—planning, retention, retrieval, performance, and evaluation—participants were asked to “plan, memorize, and retrieve/execute four events on each of the four days of the virtual holiday week.” Immediately prior to the planning phase of the task, one group of participants was subjected to “acute stress,” (placing their hand under ice cold water and leaving it there for three minutes). During the same period, the control group held a hand under warm water for three minutes. In order to determine the degree of stress induced by the ice water procedure, experimenters took saliva samples from both groups and measured levels of the stress [hormone](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/hormones "Psychology Today looks at hormone") cortisol, determining that the ice bath did, indeed, increase the stress level of the participants who had been subjected to it.

Following the stress induction, both groups of participants proceeded to the “planning” phase of the prospective memory task, memorizing four appointments that had to be recalled in a certain order on each of the virtual “days” of the vacation. Then, after a significant period of time (40 minutes), they implemented their fictional holiday week, virtually going “to each of the planned locations at the planned time…or after a planned event…for each respective day of the week.” In the first two “days” of the retrieval phase, both groups performed equally well, but on the third day, the performance of the non-stressed control group decreased, while the performance of the stressed group remained stable throughout the entire task.

The experimenters speculate that the decrease in performance among the control group can be explained by the “primacy effect,” which involves “better free recall performance for earlier than later learned items, based on a better rehearsal of early learned information and an overwriting effect of later information.” Whatever the precise cause of the control group’s decrease in performance, the significant finding of the experiment was that the group that was stressed before the planning phase experienced no such decrease in performance.

While it is true that not all college work requires the same type of prospective memory as that involved in the “holiday planning” task of the Witten/Herdecke study, some memory tasks that students face do bear some similarities to the planning task. They might thus exhibit a similar performance improvement in the presence of stress. The virtual task of memorizing a sequence of four appointments a day for four successive days is quite similar, for example, to the memorization and later retrieval of sequential chains of information in such subject areas as history and chemistry.

Now, I am not in any way advocating a study regimen based on systematic procrastination as a means of creating stress. The detrimental effects of stress (not to mention the sheer unpleasantness of it) are far too great a price to pay for the small prospective memory benefit that might result. If, however, you happen to be a college student and find yourself up late on a Sunday night trying to memorize the stages of glycolysis for an Organic Chemistry quiz the next morning, you might as well embrace the acute stress that is settling in your stomach like an undigested burrito. It might give you the edge you need to retrieve that information when you need it. And even if it doesn’t, at least you’ll have one more new argument for justifying your procrastination.
