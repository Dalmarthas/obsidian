# Summary

This source adds a film-culture and studio-system critique layer to the filmmaking branch. Chris Gore argues that contemporary Hollywood too often behaves like a corporate factory optimized for franchise safety, global blandness, and committee logic rather than for original vision, meaningful character arcs, and audience respect. Its strongest vault contribution is the distinction between factory filmmaking and creator-led filmmaking, with indie film positioned as the current refuge for more specific and risk-bearing work.

# Key Claims

- Corporate studio decision-making often produces safer but weaker films.
- Reboots and sequels fail when they neglect character, catharsis, and audience trust.
- Original creators and indie voices have been deprioritized in favor of globally marketable properties.
- Audiences are more sophisticated than studios assume and can detect shallow pandering.
- Test screenings and audience feedback can help, but should not replace creative judgment.
- Viewers and creators both benefit from a balanced media diet that includes indie, documentary, foreign, and mainstream work.

# Important Details

- The source contrasts auteur-era protection of strong filmmakers with current committee-heavy franchise management.
- It uses legacy-character handling as a diagnostic test: spectacle and nostalgia do not compensate for weak emotional payoff.
- It argues that some modern franchise choices feel like creative malpractice because the icon or story world is not being stewarded with care.
- Globalization is treated as a double-edged force: broader reach can come at the cost of local identity and sharper cultural specificity.
- Gore positions independent film as a more fertile place for originality because it can take creative risks without the same corporate drag.
- The source also introduces `balanced media diet` as a viewer and creator habit that expands taste and resists blockbuster monoculture.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a full primary transcript.
- Some claims about representation, globalization, and audience response are interpretive and should be treated as contested rather than universally settled.
- The source is strongest as a provocative industry diagnosis, but weaker on detailed studio economics or production constraints.
