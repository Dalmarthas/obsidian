# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Daniel Kahneman's two-systems model of judgment. Its strongest additions to the vault are a clean distinction between fast associative thinking and slow deliberate thinking, a sharper rule for when intuition deserves trust, and a durable warning that confidence usually tracks narrative coherence more than evidence quality. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-daniel-kahneman-two-systems-thinking|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Human judgment is shaped by two interacting modes: fast automatic processing and slow effortful reasoning.
- Most cognitive bias comes from fast pattern-completion and question substitution that happen before conscious scrutiny catches up.
- Reliable intuition exists, but only in environments regular enough to teach real patterns through repeated feedback.
- Subjective confidence is not a dependable indicator of accuracy.
- Better decisions often require formal structures that compensate for the limits of unaided intuition.

# Important Details

- The source treats `System 1` and `System 2` as useful explanatory models:
  - `System 1` is fast, associative, emotional, low-effort, and constantly active
  - `System 2` is slower, effortful, more reflective, and capable of monitoring or overriding first impressions
- Its view of intuition is conditional rather than romantic:
  - intuition can be excellent in domains with stable patterns and prompt feedback
  - examples named here include chess and anesthesiology
  - intuition is much less trustworthy in noisy, irregular, or feedback-poor domains such as stock picking or long-range punditry
- The source also explains why bias feels invisible:
  - the mind often answers an easier question instead of the hard one actually being asked
  - associative coherence turns partial evidence into a convincing story
  - priming and recent stimuli quietly shape later judgment
- Confidence is framed as especially dangerous:
  - people feel certain when a story feels fluent and internally coherent
  - that feeling can remain strong even when the evidence underneath is thin
- The practical response is procedural, not merely philosophical:
  - slow down in novel, complex, or high-stakes situations
  - use checklists, formulas, and explicit evidence review when intuition may be operating outside its valid range

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct transcript or close review of the underlying studies.
- The `two systems` language is presented by Kahneman as a useful fiction, not as two literal brain modules.
- Awareness of bias helps, but the source also implies a limit: people cannot simply reason themselves out of automatic cognition on demand.
- The summary is strongest on the judgment model and examples, not on detailed practical debiasing protocols beyond procedural caution.
