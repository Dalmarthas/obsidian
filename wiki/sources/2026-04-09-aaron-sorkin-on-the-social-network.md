# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Aaron Sorkin's account of writing *The Social Network*. Its strongest additions to the vault are: conflicting-perspectives structure as a way to preserve ambiguity, research-heavy dramatization built from legal and public records, audience autonomy in morally ambiguous stories, rhythm-dependent dialogue that limits improvisation, and a concrete case study showing how intention and obstacle can carry material that might otherwise be mislabeled as merely topical. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-aaron-sorkin-on-the-social-network|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Intention and obstacle remain the bedrock of both story and scene design.
- Conflicting narrators can create dramatic force without forcing one official truth.
- Rigorous research can support dramatization while still leaving room for interpretation.
- The writer should not dictate moral judgment when the material is stronger as contested human drama.
- Dense, rhythmic dialogue may require extended rehearsal and little or no improvisation.
- Topical material works best when reframed through timeless human conflict such as ambition, friendship, jealousy, and betrayal.

# Important Details

- The project is framed as a drama about power, betrayal, and status rather than as a `tech movie`.
- Sorkin uses the conflicting accounts from the Facebook lawsuits as a structural asset rather than a factual inconvenience.
- The source explicitly compares the method to a Rashomon-like multi-perspective design.
- Research inputs included legal documents, blogs, interviews, and other public records.
- Sorkin prefers revealing character through what someone wants and how they pursue it rather than through descriptive labeling.
- His process includes heavy brainstorming, many bad ideas, index cards, and partial structural guidance without exhaustive outlining.
- Fincher's collaboration sharpened a rehearsal-heavy, no-improv approach because the script's rhythm and density were load-bearing.
- The source also sharpens Sorkin's antihero logic: the writer should approach morally ambiguous characters with respect and advocacy rather than contempt.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary interview transcript or script document.
- The raw text contains encoding noise, so a few phrases are clearer in intent than in exact capture.
- The no-improv lesson is partly style-specific: it applies most strongly when the writing depends heavily on exact rhythm and rhetorical timing.
- Conflicting-perspectives structure can preserve ambiguity, but without a strong dramatic through-line it can also become muddy or merely withholding.
