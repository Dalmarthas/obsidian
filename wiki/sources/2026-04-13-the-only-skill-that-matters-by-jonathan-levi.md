# Summary

This source is a compact secondary summary of Jonathan Levi's `superlearning` framing. Its strongest reusable value in the current vault is a three-part anti-plateau model for learning: pair adjacent skills or subskills so they cross-pollinate, attack one topic through multiple teachers and formats, and force learning upward through a six-level pyramid from understanding to creation. The branch matters because it treats confusion and exposure at higher levels as evidence of real learning rather than as proof of incapacity.

# Key Claims

- Learning can accelerate when two related skills or subskills are studied in parallel because comparison and transfer reveal nuances that isolated study misses.
- A single teacher or medium is often a bottleneck, so the learner should switch explanations and formats until the concept actually clicks.
- Most people stop at recognition-level understanding, which creates an illusion of mastery.
- Real learning becomes more durable when it progresses through recall, explanation, application, analysis, and finally creation.
- Plateaus are often a signal to redesign the learning method rather than abandon the goal.

# Important Details

- `Multi-learning` is framed as intentional cross-pollination rather than generic multitasking.
- If no second domain is available, the same method can be simulated by pairing complementary subskills within one domain.
- `Brute force learning` means attacking one concept from several sources and media rather than trusting the first explanation that fails to land.
- The six levels are: understand, recall, explain, apply, analyze, and create.
- The source's examples matter because they translate each level into concrete behavior rather than leaving mastery abstract.
- The `create` level is not only original research; it includes writing a guide, making a model, or codifying understanding in a form useful to others.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary of a practical-learning explainer rather than a primary-source ingest of Levi's full argument.
- The exact six-level pyramid should be treated as heuristic scaffolding rather than as a strictly validated scientific model.
- The source is strong on method design and weaker on how to decide when one skill should stay primary rather than being diluted by too much parallel learning.
- Multi-learning can easily collapse into fragmentation if the paired skills are not close enough to cross-pollinate or if total load is not controlled.
