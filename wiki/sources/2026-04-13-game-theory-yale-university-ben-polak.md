# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of an introductory Yale lecture on game theory centered on timing games, dominance reasoning, and backward induction. Its strongest additions to the vault are a clearer distinction between robust and fragile game-theoretic conclusions, a timing-focused duel model where the key choice is when to act rather than what to do, and a behavioral warning that overconfidence and proactive bias can make people move too early. It deepens the game-theory branch by adding sequential-play logic and a more disciplined way to reason backward from clear endpoints. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-game-theory-yale-university-ben-polak|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Some strategic problems are mainly timing problems rather than choice-among-options problems.
- Dominance reasoning is especially robust because it can hold regardless of what you believe the opponent will do.
- Backward induction can solve finite sequential games from the endpoint backward, but it depends more heavily on shared rationality assumptions.
- People often act too early because of overconfidence or cultural pressure to be proactive.
- Formal models are useful not because they predict everything, but because they show exactly which conclusions depend on which assumptions.

# Important Details

- The source treats game theory as a general analysis of strategic situations where outcomes depend on multiple decision-makers.
- Its main teaching device is the sponge duel:
  - two players move closer or throw
  - throwing earlier gives a lower hit probability but waiting can let the opponent act first
  - the core problem is choosing the right moment to switch from waiting to action
- The lecture contributes two main tools:
  - dominance reasoning: choose the action that is best regardless of the opponent's move
  - backward induction: solve from the last possible move backward to the present
- The source's strongest methodological addition is a robustness distinction:
  - some conclusions, like `don't throw early` in the duel, survive even against irrational opponents
  - others depend on the opponent also reasoning properly from the end of the game
  - this separates sturdier insights from more assumption-sensitive ones
- The timing examples broaden the branch beyond textbook dilemmas:
  - duels
  - breakaways in cycling
  - product launches and network-effect markets
  - in each, acting too early can destroy value even when everyone knows the eventual action they want
- The behavioral section adds a useful correction:
  - real people often move too soon
  - overconfidence and proactive bias distort timing choices
  - cultural admiration for bold action can override better odds from waiting

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is still a secondary summary of one lecture, not a full course treatment of sequential games, extensive form, or equilibrium refinements.
- The sponge duel is clarifying but stylized; real launches, negotiations, or races usually involve more actors, noisier information, and more action options.
- Backward induction is powerful in clean finite settings, but the lecture itself shows it is more fragile than dominance reasoning when rationality or common knowledge weakens.
- The behavioral evidence is suggestive rather than rigorous in this summary, especially around classroom observations and proactive-bias generalization.
