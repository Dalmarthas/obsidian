# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a video connecting game theory to real-world negotiation. Its strongest contribution is not basic equilibrium vocabulary by itself, but the bridge from abstract game structure to practical bargaining moves: BATNA as outside option, commitments as game-changing constraints, signaling and screening under incomplete information, and repeated interaction as a reason to prefer credibility over one-shot opportunism. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-game-theory-and-negotiation|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Negotiations can often be modeled as games with players, strategies, and payoffs, which clarifies what each side is actually optimizing.
- BATNA functions like an outside option that sets a reservation floor and prevents acceptance of dominated deals.
- Dominant strategies and Nash equilibrium help predict likely bargaining behavior, but good negotiators can sometimes reshape incentives rather than merely accept the default equilibrium.
- Commitments, threats, and promises matter only when they are credible under the actual payoff structure.
- Incomplete information makes signaling and screening central because each side is partly negotiating against uncertainty about the other's valuation, limits, and willingness to walk away.
- Repeated interaction and reputation often make cooperative, trustworthy behavior more rational than single-round extraction.
- Purely rational models are useful but incomplete because fairness concerns, emotions, and bounded rationality can change the effective payoffs.

# Important Details

- The source's main structural move is to treat negotiation as game diagnosis before tactic selection:
  - identify the players
  - list the strategic options
  - estimate payoffs and outside options
  - ask whether the setting is zero-sum or variable-sum, one-shot or repeated
- BATNA is presented as the negotiation translation of an outside option:
  - it sets the minimum acceptable payoff
  - it helps define reservation value
  - it also sharpens inference about the counterpart, because their alternatives constrain what agreements are plausible
- The source deepens equilibrium logic operationally:
  - dominant strategies matter because they reveal moves that stay attractive across counterpart responses
  - Nash equilibrium matters because it predicts stable bargaining outcomes under current incentives
  - negotiation skill sometimes means changing those incentives through framing, package design, deadlines, or credible commitment
- Commitment is treated as strategic self-binding:
  - restricting your future options can make a threat or promise believable
  - non-credible threats usually weaken trust more than they improve leverage
  - the point is not theatrical toughness but altering what the other side expects you can rationally do
- Incomplete information introduces two practical tools:
  - signaling: acting in ways that communicate seriousness, constraints, or type
  - screening: structuring offers or menus so the counterpart's response reveals priorities or private information
- The source's repeated-games layer overlaps with the current tit-for-tat branch but applies it directly to negotiation:
  - reputation compounds
  - consistency affects future bargaining power
  - small-market or long-term relationships reward reliability over marginal short-term extraction
- The source also adds a behavioral caution:
  - fairness norms and reference points can change how an offer is perceived
  - formally attractive deals may still be rejected if they threaten face, legitimacy, or relational trust
  - game-theory analysis therefore has to be filtered through live human psychology

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary, so the formal concepts are explained cleanly but without mathematical detail or transcript-level nuance.
- The source is strongest on two-party bargaining and may understate how messy multi-stakeholder negotiations become once coalitions, politics, or hidden audiences enter the game.
- Commitment and signaling can be analytically useful while still becoming manipulative theater if detached from real constraints or honest intent.
- The source warns against naive rationalism, but it does not fully specify when psychology merely perturbs the game versus when it fundamentally redefines the payoff structure.
