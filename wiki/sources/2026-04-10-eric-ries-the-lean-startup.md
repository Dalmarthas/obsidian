# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Eric Ries's Lean Startup method, focused on validating business ideas through cheap, fast experiments instead of full product builds. Its strongest additions to the vault are a cleaner account of `validated learning`, a sharper distinction between customer behavior and customer opinion, and a compact decision loop built around MVPs, real-world measurement, and `pivot or persevere` judgments. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-eric-ries-the-lean-startup|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- The first job of an early product is to test the riskiest assumption, not to express the full vision in finished form.
- An MVP is the minimum version of a product or offer needed to learn whether a critical assumption is true.
- Customer behavior is more trustworthy than interviews, encouragement, or stated intent.
- Concierge MVPs and smokescreen MVPs are two common ways to test value before building heavy infrastructure.
- The core operating loop is iterative: build, measure, learn, then either pivot or persevere.
- Waste usually comes from building too much before reality has been tested.

# Important Details

- The source treats vision and experimentation as compatible rather than opposed:
  - keep a clear picture of the intended user experience
  - isolate the assumption that most threatens the whole plan
  - test that assumption first
- The MVP idea is framed narrowly and usefully:
  - not a stripped-down product for launch theater
  - a specific instrument for testing one important unknown
- Two reusable MVP patterns are highlighted:
  - `concierge MVP`
    manually deliver the value before automating the workflow
  - `smokescreen MVP`
    market or pre-sell the concept before building the full thing
- The examples sharpen the logic:
  - IMVU is used as a warning about overbuilding before validation
  - Zappos is used to show manual fulfillment as a valid early test
  - Exploding Kittens is used to show pre-order demand testing before production
- The source insists on behavior-grounded metrics such as purchases, pre-orders, clicks, or other meaningful commitments.
- The decision rule after testing is not "did people like it?" but "did real behavior support the underlying hypothesis strongly enough to continue?"

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct reading of Ries's book or talk.
- Smokescreen-style testing can drift into misleading promises if the offer implies certainty the team cannot honor.
- Behavior is stronger than opinion, but weak tests can still measure curiosity, novelty, or poor traffic quality rather than true demand.
- The method is strongest for uncertainty-heavy early products; some capital-intensive or regulated businesses cannot test as lightly as software-adjacent ventures can.
