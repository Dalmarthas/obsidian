# Summary

This source turns the existing Johnny Harris branch from a mostly structural explainer model into a fuller production model. Harris describes premium YouTube documentary making as a months-long pipeline that joins early packaging, a research dossier, visual-first scripting, concrete active prose, bespoke art direction, fact-checking, iterative editing, and viewer empathy. The key addition is that the finished video is not treated as a script plus visuals; the writing itself is a coded audiovisual plan where each line is tied to image, motion, sound, evidence, and audience expectation. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-25-johnny-harris-reveals-how-he-writes-youtube-videos|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Harris's premium videos run through a repeatable but flexible pipeline: pitch, reporting brief, research dossier, visual-first script, filming, shame cut, rough cut, fine cut, audio lock, and picture lock.
- The title and thumbnail are not late marketing decorations. They define an early audience promise that the story must reinforce quickly and eventually fulfill.
- Good video writing is audiovisual direction: narration, visuals, animation, sound, archival evidence, and source notes are designed together.
- Harris favors active, concrete prose built around agents doing things rather than abstractions naming things.
- Viewer empathy is part of factual craft: the maker should respect smart viewers with little context while anticipating how sensitive subgroups may hear each phrase.
- Bespoke art direction lets each story find its own visual grammar instead of forcing every topic into a rigid channel brand.
- Harris treats human enthusiasm, curiosity, and obsession as a strategic advantage, especially as AI makes generic explanation cheaper.

# Important Details

- Research pipeline:
  - Harris begins with a reporting brief full of questions.
  - A researcher spends several weeks building a large information document with facts, visuals, context, and possible story directions.
  - Harris then wrestles with that dossier until he can find the story's promise, sequence, and visual path.
- Protected scripting:
  - Harris protects 9am to 2pm writing blocks from meetings and messages.
  - Scripting week is treated as a high-focus production sprint, not casual writing time.
  - The process requires enough flexibility to extend a story when it has not been cracked yet.
- Visual-first script:
  - Harris writes in a two-column format, with prose on one side and explicit visual, motion, and sound instructions on the other.
  - He uses script coding and color systems to guide asset collection, animation nesting, future editorial work, and fact-checking.
  - Every line of voiceover is expected to have a visual job.
- Promise and packaging:
  - Titles and thumbnails often take the form of `why` or `how` questions.
  - The simple promise becomes the viewer's entry point into a wider topic.
  - A video can delay the full answer if it quickly reinforces the promise and keeps signaling that a deeper explanation is coming.
- Writing style:
  - Harris draws on a classic-style preference for clear agents and actions.
  - He replaces abstract nouns with active scenes: people flee, governments act, companies send goods, places change.
  - Maps, icons, and visible actors are loaded with motives and fears so viewers can track spatial and causal relationships.
- Fact-checking:
  - Assertions are highlighted, checked, annotated, and connected to source documents.
  - The production ideal is accessible storytelling with visible evidence, not just persuasive polish.
- Voice and tone:
  - Most of the video uses a crisp explainer voice that advances the story.
  - Harris often shifts near the end into a slower reflective voice that makes room for ambiguity, grief, beauty, critique, or moral complexity.
  - He tries to praise and criticize in the same piece when the subject deserves mixed treatment.
- Art direction:
  - Each video gets its own mood board and `art ingredients` document.
  - Maps, archival treatment, typography, set design, animation style, and symbolic systems are chosen for the specific story.
  - The team values scrappy, self-taught visual problem-solving over rigid television convention.
- Scale and bottlenecks:
  - Harris's team previously pushed higher output, but the machine threatened to drain the curiosity that made the work distinctive.
  - The current strategy accepts creator obsession as a bottleneck and aims to launch more creator-led channels rather than a faceless content factory.

# Tensions / Caveats

- The four-month pipeline and annual output numbers are informal estimates, not universal production laws.
- Early packaging can clarify audience promise, but it can also pressure editorial judgment if the marketing frame becomes too controlling.
- Harris's protected writing blocks are transferable as a principle, but not every creator or team can copy his exact schedule or staffing model.
- Visual-first simplification can open access, but it still risks overcompression if the memorable visual is not representative.
- Creator-led bottlenecks protect voice but can limit scale and create sustainability pressure.
