# Summary

This source is an article by Noa Kageyama built around a 2016 retrieval-practice study and a musician-facing application. Its strongest addition to the vault is not merely that active recall improves learning, but that retrieval-based learning may hold up better under acute stress than repeated restudy. It turns `practice from memory early` into a more specific claim: recall attempts can build memory that is less likely to collapse when pressure rises. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2016-12-18-the-practice-strategy-that-could-bulletproof-your-memory-under-pressure|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Stress reliably impairs recall, but the impairment is not equally severe across memorization strategies.
- Retrieval practice appears to protect memory against acute stress better than repeated study or review.
- Memory stability is not just about total exposure time; the mode of rehearsal matters.
- Attempting recall from the beginning of learning may produce stronger performance memory than waiting to memorize only at the polishing stage.
- The practical value for performers is to integrate imperfect recall attempts early, not to treat memorization as a final-phase add-on.

# Important Details

- The article centers on [[wiki/sources/2016-12-18-the-practice-strategy-that-could-bulletproof-your-memory-under-pressure|Smith et al. 2016 as reported here]]:
  - 120 participants were assigned either to repeated study or retrieval practice
  - both groups learned 30 nouns and 30 photos
  - the study group repeatedly reviewed the material
  - the retrieval group received one initial presentation, then repeatedly tried to recall the items without re-study
- The stress test mattered as much as the learning condition:
  - half the participants later faced a speech-and-mental-math stress task in front of observers
  - recall was tested during the stress window, including near expected cortisol peak
  - the non-stress group completed the same recall tasks without the induced pressure
- The result pattern is the article's main durable contribution:
  - the study group recalled fewer items under stress than without stress, with the article reporting averages of `7` versus `8.7`
  - the retrieval-practice group showed little stress-related drop, with averages of `11.1` under stress versus `10.3` without stress
  - retrieval practice under stress therefore outperformed repeated study even when study participants were not stressed
- The article's practical translation for musicians is specific:
  - start recall attempts from day 1 instead of postponing memorization until late polishing
  - use imperfect, halting, partially remembered run-throughs as part of normal learning
  - even recalling a phrase or two each day counts as retrieval-based encoding
- The article reinforces the practical transfer with a second reference:
  - [[wiki/sources/2016-12-18-the-practice-strategy-that-could-bulletproof-your-memory-under-pressure|Chaffin 2007 as cited here]] is used to show an expert pianist emphasizing memory from the beginning of learning rather than after the piece was already fully stabilized

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is an interpretive article, not the primary paper itself, so the musician-facing takeaways are one step removed from the experiment.
- The lab task used nouns and photos rather than complex motor sequences, performance repertoire, or long-form conceptual material.
- The practical leap from list recall to music performance is plausible, but it remains an extrapolation rather than a directly tested one in this article.
- Retrieval practice can expose weak memory, but without feedback or correction it could also reinforce mistakes in some domains.
