# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Steve Jobs on organizational focus as refusal, pruning, and integration rather than expansion-by-enthusiasm. Its strongest additions to the vault are a cleaner definition of focus as repeatedly saying `no`, a sharper distinction between local project logic and company-level strategic coherence, and a stronger leadership model for absorbing the social and reputational cost of cancellations without losing direction. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-12-focusing-is-about-saying-no-steve-jobs|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Real focus in a company means saying `no` to many interesting initiatives so talent and attention can converge on a few coherent directions.
- Strong engineers cannot rescue weak strategic integration if management allows effort to scatter across too many parallel projects.
- Some projects make sense at the team level but fail at the company or ecosystem level, so leadership has to judge macro coherence rather than local enthusiasm.
- Cancelling projects creates anger, press backlash, and internal pain, and leaders must absorb those costs without public scapegoating.
- The reward for disciplined pruning is an integrated product system where the whole becomes greater than the sum of the parts.

# Important Details

- The source defines focus negatively rather than positively:
  - not adding more promising work
  - not preserving every locally defensible effort
  - but repeatedly refusing misaligned directions
- It adds a strong systems-level diagnosis:
  - many projects can each be `arguably interesting`
  - but the portfolio can still become sub-additive
  - talented teams do not automatically produce strategic coherence
- The micro-versus-macro distinction is especially reusable:
  - a project can make sense to the people inside it
  - it can make technical sense in isolation
  - and still fail when judged against ecosystem direction, platform shifts, and company-wide integration
- The OpenDoc example adds a concrete kill-criteria model:
  - weak strategic fit
  - doubtful underlying technology quality
  - and visible drift toward a different architecture all count against continued investment
- The leadership layer is unusually blunt:
  - leaders have to anger people
  - accept bad press and one-sided narratives
  - and still protect confidentiality and institutional maturity instead of retaliating publicly

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary of a short clip, not a full strategic postmortem of Apple's 1997 product decisions.
- The OpenDoc evaluation is asserted more than demonstrated technically in the summary.
- Jobs's language is clarifying about refusal, but it can become over-romantic if copied without attention to process quality, evidence thresholds, or the value of optionality in some contexts.
- The source strongly favors concentration, but the vault should preserve that some businesses genuinely require portfolio logic, experimentation breadth, or staged de-risking.
