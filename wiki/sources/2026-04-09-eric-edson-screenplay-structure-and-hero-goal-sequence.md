# Summary

This source adds a highly operational structure layer to the filmmaking branch. Eric Edson argues that story structure is not merely an industry convention but a psychologically grounded pattern through which audiences process change, emotion, and meaning. Its strongest vault contribution is the `Hero Goal Sequence` model: a repeatable narrative unit in which an active protagonist pursues a short-term visible goal until fresh news forces a new direction.

# Key Claims

- Structure is a psychological necessity rather than a bureaucratic formula.
- A screen story needs three core forces: a sympathetic active hero, a visible high-stakes goal, and a powerful adversary.
- The most useful micro-unit of screenplay movement is the `Hero Goal Sequence`.
- Act transitions work best when they land as major reversals or `Stunning Surprises`.
- Writers gain freedom from structure when they understand it well enough to diagnose drift, repetition, and passivity.

# Important Details

- Edson frames the three-act model as `three worlds`: ordinary world, special world, and maturity.
- The source stresses externalized action in visual storytelling: plot reveals character through decisions, pursuit, and visible conflict rather than through interior exposition.
- A `Hero Goal Sequence` usually runs 3 to 7 pages and follows a recurring pattern: short-term goal, pursuit, fresh news, transition to a new goal.
- The source presents an empirical planning heuristic of roughly 21 to 23 sequences across a feature, with especially strong emphasis on the first and second act turning points.
- `Stunning Surprise 1` and `Stunning Surprise 2` are framed as emotionally forceful reversals rather than merely technical beat markers.
- The ending should be known before scene drafting so the writer can build toward a coherent payoff instead of wandering into repair-heavy drafting.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a full primary transcript or book chapter.
- The sequence counts and act-partition heuristics are useful scaffolds, but they should not be mistaken for inviolable laws.
- Experimental, nonlinear, or strongly anti-classical narratives may not fit the model cleanly even when they still rely on change, surprise, and emotional orientation.
- The source is strongest on functional structure, but less detailed on subtext, tone, and stylistic distinctiveness than some neighboring vault sources.
