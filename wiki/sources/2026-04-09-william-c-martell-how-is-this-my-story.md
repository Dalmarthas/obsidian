# Summary

This source centers on William C. Martell's core screenwriting question: `How is this my story?` Its strongest contribution to the vault is a practical rule for assignments and commercial genres: a writer still needs a real emotional or intellectual doorway into the material, otherwise the work becomes mechanical, rewrite-heavy, and creatively deadening. The source treats authenticity not as self-expression in the abstract, but as a diagnostic test for whether a writer can bring honest energy, subtext, and sustainable effort to a project.

# Key Claims

- Strong screenwriting requires a personal anchor, even in assigned or commercially constrained projects.
- Hack work done only for money produces weaker writing and drains long-term sustainability.
- Every project needs a `doorway`: a specific way the writer relates emotionally, psychologically, or thematically to the material.
- Writers should adapt assignments through their own angle rather than merely execute the obvious version of the premise.
- Some projects should be declined when the writer cannot honestly connect to them or they violate core values.
- Genre constraints can still hold personal meaning if the writer finds a deeper or more subversive angle inside them.

# Important Details

- Martell frames the writer's job as mining emotions they often avoid in daily life, including conflict, fear, and passion.
- The source's assignment examples show that literal experience is not required; emotional equivalence can be enough.
- The `doorway` idea doubles as a selection filter: if a writer cannot find an entry point, the project may be wrong for them.
- The source also ties professional longevity to project selection and to the ability to keep bringing a personal voice into work-for-hire jobs.
- A project can satisfy genre expectations while still being reframed through forgiveness, outsiderhood, rebellion, or another personal motif.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript or direct essay from Martell.
- `How is this my story?` is a strong heuristic, but different writers may find or define their doorway differently.
- The advice is especially strong for sustaining a writer's voice, but it may be harder to apply in highly constrained studio or franchise work.
