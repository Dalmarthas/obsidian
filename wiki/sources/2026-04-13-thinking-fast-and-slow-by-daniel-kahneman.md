# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Daniel Kahneman video built around the `Thinking, Fast and Slow` frame. Its strongest additions to the vault are not the basic `System 1` versus `System 2` distinction, which is already present, but three more operational bias patterns: mere exposure, status quo bias through loss aversion and the endowment effect, and WYSIATI-style tunnel vision. It also adds a compact decision-checklist layer for using slow thinking against fast but costly first judgments. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-thinking-fast-and-slow-by-daniel-kahneman|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Most judgment errors start with uninspected trust in fast intuitive thinking.
- Familiarity often gets mistaken for truth, quality, or safety.
- Loss aversion and the endowment effect make people cling to the current state even when change is better.
- WYSIATI leads the mind to form confident stories from incomplete evidence while ignoring base rates and missing data.
- Better decisions often require brief deliberate questions that force attention onto alternatives, tradeoffs, and disconfirming evidence.

# Important Details

- The source restates the two-systems model in a more behavioral way:
  - `System 1` rapidly generates answers, stories, and preferences
  - `System 2` can inspect them but often does not engage unless deliberately summoned
  - the practical problem is therefore not absence of intelligence but lazy review
- It adds a concrete `mere exposure` layer:
  - repeated contact with a name, slogan, product, or person can increase liking and trust without improving evidence
  - this helps explain distorted judgment in voting, hiring, and large purchases
- It sharpens status quo bias through two submechanisms:
  - loss aversion makes potential losses feel heavier than comparable gains
  - the endowment effect inflates the value of what is already owned
  - together they make failing investments, bad jobs, and stale defaults feel safer than they are
- The WYSIATI section is one of the source's strongest additions:
  - the mind builds a coherent story from what is immediately visible
  - absent information is not naturally felt as absent
  - base rates and conflicting evidence are easily ignored when a stereotype or vivid story is available
- The source also contributes a portable review checklist:
  - am I trusting familiarity rather than evidence?
  - am I preserving the current state because losses loom larger than gains?
  - am I missing counterevidence, alternatives, or base rates because the visible story feels complete?

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary of a short explanatory video, not a direct treatment of the full book or the underlying studies.
- The source is useful as a bias-recognition layer, but its corrective methods are brief and lightweight rather than deeply procedural.
- `Fast thinking` is treated mostly as a liability here, which underplays Kahneman's own point that fast cognition is often necessary and sometimes highly skilled.
- The examples are memorable, but they simplify real-world decisions that may involve more context, incentives, and reversibility than the clip can show.
