# Summary

This source captures a Simon Sinek and [[wiki/entities/don-yaeger|Don Yaeger]] conversation about leadership that actually changes teams over time. Its center of gravity is not tactical management advice, but the long-game discipline of building people, standards, appreciation, and mentorship structures strong enough that winning becomes a byproduct rather than the obsession.

The strongest example is [[wiki/entities/john-wooden|John Wooden]]. Wooden's coaching philosophy treated championships as lagging evidence of deeper work: character, teamwork, humility, discipline, and consistent standards of being. His practices, including the Pyramid of Success, the Bill Walton hair-length story, and requiring scorers to thank the passer, make culture concrete through small repeated behaviors rather than slogans.

The source also extends Sinek's people-first leadership branch through Ed Bastian's Delta example, mentorship as time-giving rather than status association, and family presence as a leadership-integrity problem. The recurring warning is that many leaders know people-first leadership works, but short-termism, fear, rationalization, and scoreboards pull them back toward visible output at the expense of durable human infrastructure.

# Key Claims

- Winning is better treated as a byproduct of building great people and great teams than as the direct object of obsession.
- High standards should include [[wiki/concepts/leadership/standards-of-being|standards of being]]: how people show up, treat teammates, recognize others, and embody the team's values.
- Standards become credible only when they apply to stars and ordinary contributors alike.
- Appreciation is a systematic practice. Looking for what is good in another person trains perception, increases visible recognition, and makes leaders more likely to catch people doing things right.
- Mentorship is not mainly status access. A real mentor makes time, develops a relationship, and often learns from the mentee as well.
- Employee-first leadership can form a virtuous cycle: invest in employees, employees create better customer experiences, customers reward the company, and upside is shared back with employees.
- Long-horizon leadership requires presence at home as well as at work; "I do this for you" can become a rationalization for absence.
- The biggest gap is not awareness. Leaders often know this advice, but fail to follow it because quarterly pressure, fear, and immediate reward make people-first leadership costly.

# Important Details

Wooden's model is presented as a corrective to trophy-centered leadership. His Pyramid of Success places traits such as industriousness, teamwork, and greatness above title-winning. The source emphasizes that Wooden rarely spoke directly about winning; he concentrated on the behaviors and character that make winning more likely.

The Walton hair-length story gives the standards branch its cleanest enforcement pattern. Wooden had a rule with a practical reason: hair should not interfere with team availability and readiness. When Bill Walton challenged the rule, Wooden did not escalate emotionally or personalize the conflict. He effectively made the choice clear: the player could keep the hair or remain aligned with the team standard. This illustrates standard enforcement without favoritism or theatrics.

The "thank the passer" habit converts gratitude into team mechanics. The scorer's visible recognition of the assist teaches that the celebrated moment is not the whole contribution chain. This directly overlaps with the vault's existing high-performance culture branch: unseen cultural labor has to be noticed or the system will over-reward visible finishers.

Yaeger's practice of writing weekly letters to his wife generalizes Wooden's love-letter story into a perception discipline. A repeated appreciation practice creates a search habit: the writer has to keep finding specific things to value. In leadership, the same habit shifts attention from policing failure to reinforcing desired behavior.

Ed Bastian's Delta example gives the employee-first branch an operating loop. The company invests in employees through assets, training, communication, appreciation, and profit sharing; employees then create better experiences; customers tolerate premium pricing or remain loyal; profits are shared back with employees. The Valentine's Day profit-sharing detail is treated as a symbolic "love letter" to employees, not only a compensation mechanism.

The mentorship section distinguishes mentors from champions, gurus, and distant role models. Sinek's threshold is practical: a mentor makes time for you. The relationship usually evolves through repeated contact, thoughtful questions, application of advice, and mutual learning rather than through a formal first-day request.

The family-presence section uses Teddy Roosevelt and Teddy Jr. as a caution about being historically significant while missing the inner life of one's own child. Yaeger's three-nights-away rule turns values into a scheduling constraint, which makes presence operational rather than aspirational.

# Tensions / Caveats

- Wooden-style standards are powerful, but the vault should not generalize every historical rule as automatically wise. The reusable lesson is reasoned, consistent standard enforcement, not any one cultural norm.
- Appreciation can become sentimental if it is not paired with accountability and clear standards.
- Employee-first leadership needs real economics behind it. Profit sharing and investment in employees are more credible when they survive pressure, not only when conditions are easy.
- Mentorship language is ambiguous. Deep study of historical figures can shape behavior, but it is different from a living mentor who makes time and gives responsive feedback.
- The source relies heavily on narrative case examples rather than controlled evidence, so it should be treated as a leadership heuristic branch.
