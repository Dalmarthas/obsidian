# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Rory Sutherland's TEDxOxford talk arguing that many real-world behavior problems are misdiagnosed because institutions rely on over-rationalized economic models that ignore luck, social contagion, path dependence, and the power of simple shared rules. Its strongest addition to the vault is a canonical `social heuristics` concept, plus a sharper argument that named, binary, socially visible rules often outperform precise numeric guidance when the actual problem is collective behavior rather than isolated individual optimization. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-the-lost-genius-of-irrationality-rory-sutherland|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Human behavior is often better modeled as a social, path-dependent system than as a set of isolated rational calculations.
- Institutions over-attribute success and failure to individual agency while underweighting luck, norms, and network effects.
- Binary, shared, socially visible rules are often easier to follow than continuous numeric recommendations.
- Well-named heuristics and scripts can change behavior more effectively than abstract incentives or top-down explanation.
- Many adoption, policy, and market problems are really norm-design problems rather than purely technical or price problems.

# Important Details

- The source repeatedly contrasts engineering-style logic with weather-like human complexity.
- Football commentary is used to show how strongly people impose neat narratives onto noisy, luck-heavy systems.
- Language learning is used to show that apparent personal weakness is often a network-structure problem: incentives, peer behavior, and expected payoff differ sharply by position in the system.
- Wine, smartphones, e-cigarettes, and electric cars are used to show that status, respectability, and visible social proof often matter more than objective product logic.
- The source's strongest practical idea is that `designated driver`, `zipper merge`, no-drinking days, and similar labels work because they turn diffuse advice into recognizable social roles and scripts.
- Religious and ritual rules are treated as psychologically sophisticated not because they are always rational, but because they are sticky, legible, and collectively enforceable.

# Tensions / Caveats

- Some examples are suggestive rather than rigorously evidenced here, especially where the source generalizes from anecdote.
- The praise for arbitrary or ritualized rules should not be flattened into a blanket endorsement of all tradition or authority.
- The claim that post-religious or post-monarchical societies struggle to design effective social rules is provocative here and should be treated as tentative.
- Binary rules can be psychologically powerful while still being overly rigid or morally questionable in particular contexts.
