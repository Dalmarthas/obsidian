# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Jesse Cole conversation on how the Savannah Bananas built extreme demand by treating baseball as a fans-first live entertainment system rather than as a conventional sports product. Its strongest contribution is to turn remarkability into an operating model: design the entire journey, obsess over the least-privileged seat, reverse normal extraction logic, run constant small experiments, and measure whether people feel seen enough to return, travel, and tell others. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-16-why-this-baseball-team-has-a-4-2-million-person-waitlist-with-jesse-cole|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- A strong brand can be built by making the experience itself the marketing rather than by buying attention.
- The least prioritized customer segment is often the clearest truth test for whether a `customer-first` standard is real.
- Live entertainment gets stronger when rituals, surprise, and emotional arc are engineered end to end rather than left to chance.
- Long-term trust can require refusing short-term monetization moves such as fees, gouging, sponsorship clutter, or investor pressure.
- High creative throughput becomes sustainable only when paired with structured debriefs that convert experiments into reusable operating knowledge.
- Rejected or overlooked talent can become unusually committed when the organization gives them a larger identity and mission than the old system did.

# Important Details

- The core standard is `fans first`, used as a decision filter for pricing, programming, staffing, and game presentation.
- Cole frames baseball as the canvas, not the finished product; the real product is the blended show made of competition, music, choreography, ritual, and crowd interaction.
- The source borrows heavily from Walt Disney's guest-journey logic, PT Barnum's talk-worthiness logic, and Saturday Night Live's cadence of frequent new bits.
- The organization runs a structured `LCP` review after games:
  - `Learn`
  - `Change`
  - `Plus`
- `Win the upper deck` functions as a practical metric:
  - leaders spend time in the cheapest seats
  - the team collects `upper deck stories`
  - special moments should not be reserved only for premium customers
- The business model deliberately resists normal sports extraction:
  - low ticket prices relative to demand
  - no junk fees
  - included food and drinks at home games
  - face-value resale controls
  - free YouTube access
  - reduced dependence on conventional sponsorship clutter
- Merchandise, loyalty, and generational fandom are treated as healthier economic outputs than squeezing each event for maximum cash.
- The organization reframes players who were cut or overlooked by traditional baseball as entertainers, community figures, and part of a mission-centered family.
- Stories of delight, grief, belonging, and childhood memory are used as cultural anchors, not as ornamental anecdotes.

# Tensions / Caveats

- The model depends on intense creative energy, high operational attention, and strong founder taste, which may be difficult to scale without dilution or burnout.
- Scarcity and multi-year waitlists can conflict with the organization's stated inclusivity if access remains too constrained.
- Showmanship can drift into gimmickry if the entertainment layer outruns genuine competitive stakes or emotional sincerity.
- The source is enthusiastic and strategy-rich, but it is still a secondary summary rather than a primary operating document from the company.
