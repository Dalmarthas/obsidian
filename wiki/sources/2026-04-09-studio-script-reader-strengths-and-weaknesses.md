# Summary

This source is an image-derived note built from three screenshots of a studio script reader's list of common strengths and weaknesses in spec scripts. Its strongest additions to the vault are: a reader-side scorecard for premise, hook, protagonist, antagonist, dialogue, action, world-building, and third-act payoff; a mirrored weakness list showing how scripts commonly fail through late starts, passivity, flat openings, stitched-together story logic, repetition, coincidence, and anticlimax; and a useful reminder that readers are often evaluating not just isolated craft categories but whether the whole script feels alive, specific, and dramatically under control. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-studio-script-reader-strengths-and-weaknesses|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- A screenplay needs a premise, not just a circumstance, scenario, or world tour.
- The opening must hook fast; banter, routine, and exposition-heavy drift often damage the read early.
- Protagonists and antagonists both need distinctiveness, pressure, and dramatic usability.
- Dialogue, action, research, and world-building matter most when they serve story rather than decorate it.
- Weak scripts often fail through passive heroes, generic characters, flat conflict, confusion, repetition, coincidence, tonal whiplash, and weak endings.

# Important Details

- `Common strengths`:
  - inventive and dramatic premise with real ground to cover
  - attention-grabbing intro
  - clever twists that still fit prior material
  - clean, direct action that preserves character specificity
  - research that enriches realism without drowning the drama
  - natural or sharp dialogue rooted in character motivation
  - protagonist strong enough to carry the story
  - antagonist formidable and original
  - vivid or fitting story world
  - climactic third act with converging storylines and satisfying resolution
- `Common weaknesses`:
  - story starts too late and drifts through an oversized act one
  - standard-issue hero or cliched villain
  - supernatural or genre rules left undefined
  - flat, talky opening
  - multiple story ideas stitched together without cohesion
  - passive protagonist
  - indistinguishable characters
  - style over substance
  - world tour without enough actual story
  - weak escalation, random/confusing plotting, melodrama, coincidence-driven solutions, repetition, needless complexity, fact-over-drama adaptation, exposition told not shown, tonally jarring scenes, repetitive horror beats, and anti-climactic endings
- `Most reusable compression`:
  - readers respond to scripts that feel specific, active, readable, escalating, and controlled
  - readers lose trust when a script feels generic, inert, overwritten, or like it is improvising its own rules

# Tensions / Caveats
