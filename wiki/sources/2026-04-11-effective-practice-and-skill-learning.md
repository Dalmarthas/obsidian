# Summary

This source is a secondary summary about the science of effective practice for physical skill acquisition. Its strongest addition to the vault is not a brand-new learning philosophy, but a sharper account of what makes repetitions effective: focused attention, slow accuracy before speed, short sessions with recovery, targeted work near the edge of competence, and mentally rehearsed movement. It also contributes a mechanistic explanation through myelination rather than leaving practice quality as a vague slogan. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-11-effective-practice-and-skill-learning|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Practice changes performance by changing neural pathways, especially through increased myelination.
- `Muscle memory` is better understood as faster, cleaner neural signaling rather than literal memory stored in muscle.
- Effective practice depends on quality and focus, not just total hours.
- Slow, correct repetitions build stronger performance foundations than rushed, sloppy repetitions.
- Mental rehearsal can reinforce established skill patterns and sometimes produces gains close to physical practice.

# Important Details

- The source distinguishes practice quantity from practice quality:
  - many hours alone do not guarantee improvement
  - repetitions matter most when they are attentive, accurate, and targeted
- Its main mechanism claim is `myelination`:
  - repeated correct signaling reinforces white-matter pathways
  - this increases speed, ease, and reliability of performance
- The practical model of `effective practice` includes:
  - distraction reduction
  - slow execution first
  - deliberate work on weaknesses just beyond comfort
  - short, frequent sessions
  - rest breaks
- The source also adds a useful distinction around `mental rehearsal`:
  - vivid visualization can reinforce already-understood movement patterns
  - it appears especially useful when some real competence is already present
- It pushes against crude mastery myths:
  - `10,000 hours` is not presented as a magic threshold
  - the governing variable is how the practice is structured, not just how much time is logged

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct paper review.
- The source is strongest on physical or coordination-heavy skills and is less explicit about transfer to abstract cognitive work.
- The myelination explanation is useful, but the summary does not provide enough detail to evaluate how much of skill growth it explains relative to feedback quality, motivation, or task design.
- Mental rehearsal is promising here, but the source itself implies it may work better for experienced performers than total beginners.
