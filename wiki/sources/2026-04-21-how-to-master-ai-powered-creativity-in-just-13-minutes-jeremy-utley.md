# Summary

This source extends Jeremy Utley's AI-collaboration branch by arguing that most professionals underuse AI because they treat it as a passive tool rather than as a teammate that can question, coach, and co-create. The practical shift is to let AI interview the user about workflows, responsibilities, KPIs, and objectives, then propose both obvious and non-obvious ways to augment the work.

The source also sharpens Utley's creativity model. AI does not change the definition of creativity; it changes how easy it is to stop at "good enough." Creative use of AI requires explicitly asking for volume, variation, unusual angles, and critique, then using human judgment to sort the resulting options.

# Key Claims

- AI should be treated as a coached teammate, not a one-shot tool.
- The most underused move is to ask AI how to use AI: have it interview the user, understand the work, and recommend workflow-specific augmentation opportunities.
- Non-technical professionals can build valuable AI workflows with natural language when they focus on dreaded, repetitive, or bureaucratic work.
- Creativity means going beyond the first thing one thinks of; AI makes first-pass adequacy easier, so users must deliberately push for more options.
- Inspiration is a discipline: the user's experiences, inputs, examples, and taste are what make AI outputs distinct from everyone else's.
- Human objectives determine whether AI amplifies creativity or suppresses it into average answers.

# Important Details

Utley's "AI expert consultation" prompt asks the model to interview the user one question at a time until it understands workflows, responsibilities, KPIs, and objectives well enough to suggest two obvious and two non-obvious AI use cases. This turns prompting from request-writing into workflow discovery.

The source's tool-versus-teammate distinction is behavioral. A tool mindset abandons AI after mediocre output. A teammate mindset gives feedback, asks what the model needs to know, requests alternatives, and iterates until the work improves.

The Glen Canyon ranger example illustrates non-technical AI creativity: a backcountry ranger reportedly built a natural-language workflow that reduced statement-of-work paperwork and saved large amounts of labor across the National Park Service. The exact labor-savings estimate is not documented in the source, so it should be treated as illustrative rather than independently verified.

Utley also gives a creativity anti-satisficing rule. Because humans tend to settle for the first adequate idea, and AI can produce adequate ideas quickly, users should ask for 5 to 10 more variations, wild alternatives, counterintuitive options, edge cases, metaphors, or analogies before deciding.

# Tensions / Caveats

- The source cites productivity and quality gains, but the summary does not identify the specific studies or methods behind those numbers.
- The National Park Service labor-savings claim is attributed to internal estimates without enough detail to audit.
- The teammate metaphor is useful, but it can overstate AI agency if users forget that responsibility, taste, verification, and final judgment remain human.
- Asking for more ideas improves exploration only if the user has a filtering standard; otherwise volume can become option clutter.
