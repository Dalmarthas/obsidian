# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Jefferson Fisher video on handling dismissive phrases such as `it was just a joke`, `don't take it personally`, and `don't be so sensitive`. Its strongest additions to the vault are a cleaner model of dismissiveness as invalidation plus control, a short self-authority phrase in `I get to decide that`, and a set of composed wordplay responses that redirect the burden back onto the speaker without long defensiveness. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-17-turn-dismissiveness-around-words-that-command-respect|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Dismissive comments often try to control both the meaning of what was said and the legitimacy of your reaction to it.
- Other people do not get to define whether something hurt, whether it was personal, or whether you are allowed to notice disrespect.
- Calm non-defensive replies are often stronger than emotional denial because they expose the original behavior rather than proving the accusation right.
- Short redirecting phrases can return accountability to the speaker without escalating into a long argument about intent.
- `Sensitivity` is context-dependent and normal; the phrase `don't be so sensitive` often functions as a way to avoid thinking about impact.
- Composed wordplay can work as a practical boundary tool when it highlights the mismatch between the speaker's excuse and the actual effect.

# Important Details

- The source sharpens the communication branch's invalidation model:
  - dismissiveness does not only insult or hurt
  - it also tries to erase your right to interpret the event as hurtful
  - this makes it a control move over both narrative and reaction
- Its strongest ownership phrase is:
  - `I get to decide that`
  - this is used as a boundary against `don't take it personally`
  - the phrase re-centers authority over your internal experience without requiring a long defense
- It offers three dismissiveness-specific response families:
  - `just a joke` -> challenge the quality of the joke or ask whether it was actually funny
  - `don't take it personally` -> return ownership and invite them to say it differently
  - `don't be so sensitive` -> reframe into `I'm sensing...` and name what you are observing
- The `I'm sensing...` move matters because it:
  - avoids the defensive trap of arguing over whether you are sensitive
  - keeps the reply observation-based rather than identity-based
  - redirects the talk toward appropriateness, tone, and impact
- The source also adds another useful daylighting phrase:
  - `I'm surprised you said that out loud`
  - this works as a low-volume exposure move that signals a norm violation without a lecture
- A repeated theme is that brevity plus calm tone is the real force multiplier:
  - not louder
  - not longer
  - not more wounded
  - just more grounded and harder to manipulate

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a transcript or primary capture.
- Some of the suggested lines rely heavily on tone and relative power; in highly hierarchical or retaliation-prone settings, they may need to be softened, delayed, or replaced by documentation and escalation.
- Wordplay can be effective, but it can also sound theatrical or sharp if delivered with obvious contempt.
- The source assumes many dismissive comments can be corrected through composure and exposure, but some people will double down rather than self-correct.
