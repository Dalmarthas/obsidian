# Summary

This source is a large secondary summary of Aaron Sorkin's screenwriting masterclass. It presents a coherent craft system built around intention and obstacle, audience trust, scene propulsion, dialogue musicality, character through tactics, extensive research, and ruthless rewriting. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Drama begins with a character who urgently wants something and faces a formidable obstacle.
- The audience should be treated as an intelligent collaborator, not as a passive recipient of explanation.
- Character is revealed by tactics under pressure, not by background biography alone.
- Every scene must move the story from one state to another; otherwise the script is standing still.
- Dialogue should be written for rhythm, sound, performance, and intention rather than for literal conversational realism.
- Rewriting works best after a full draft exists, because revision is a diagnostic process rather than a blank-page process.

# Important Details

- The source frames intention and obstacle as the driveshaft of dramatic writing.
- It argues that the strongest conflicts are often conflicts of ideas rather than purely physical impediments.
- The story guidance is classical:
  - exposition before inciting action
  - clear stakes
  - Act 1 setup, Act 2 conflict, Act 3 resolution
  - Act 3 payoffs must be prepared earlier
- The character guidance is anti-biographical:
  - avoid elaborate backstory that does not serve the story
  - define people by what they want and the tactics they use
  - never judge even antagonists
- The dialogue guidance is distinctive:
  - rhythm matters as much as denotation
  - speech is tested aloud
  - avoid repeating information the audience already has
- The rewriting guidance is procedural:
  - finish the draft first
  - cut anything the script can live without
  - respond to notes by diagnosing the actual problem
  - trusted editors should understand the writer's voice rather than overwrite it

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a synthesized summary, not a primary transcript of the class.
- The guide compresses a long course into digest form, so some nuance may be lost.
- The raw file includes formatting and encoding noise, which makes it useful as a reference source but not ideal as a clean canonical quote source.
- Some examples and craft claims are strongly tied to Sorkin's own style and may not generalize cleanly to every form of screenwriting.
