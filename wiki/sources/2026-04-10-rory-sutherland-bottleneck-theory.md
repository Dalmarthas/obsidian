# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Rory Sutherland applying bottleneck logic, or theory of constraints, to business and marketing. Its strongest additions to the vault are a canonical `bottleneck theory` concept, a sharper warning against optimizing non-constraints, and a more explicit link between growth strategy and the diagnosis of whatever factor is currently limiting progress. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-rory-sutherland-bottleneck-theory|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- System performance is limited primarily by its current bottleneck, so improvements elsewhere often have little effect.
- Many brands and companies waste effort optimizing visible but non-limiting areas.
- The real constraint can be physical, logistical, distributive, cognitive, or brand-related rather than purely operational.
- Fixing one bottleneck usually reveals the next, which makes diagnosis an ongoing discipline rather than a one-time exercise.
- Mental availability can function as a bottleneck just as much as physical availability.
- Good strategy requires using multiple mental models and methods rather than forcing every problem through a single favorite tool.

# Important Details

- The source's core system rule is simple:
  - find the narrowest point
  - improve that point first
  - then reassess because the constraint will likely move
- The strongest marketing-specific addition is the `mental vs physical availability` layer:
  - some brands become expected defaults in a context
  - others are not mentally present enough to be missed when absent
  - distribution and awareness therefore interact rather than operating as separate problems
- The road-traffic analogy matters because it shows:
  - local improvement can fail to increase total throughput
  - the visible jam may simply migrate
  - solving a constraint is meaningful only relative to the whole system
- The source also adds a method critique:
  - business leaders often overtrust one model
  - genuine diagnosis requires multiple tools
  - creativity and intuition remain useful because the bottleneck is not always where dashboards first point
- Its most reusable action rule for the vault is:
  - do not celebrate broad optimization
  - isolate the limiting factor
  - prioritize the vital few changes that release system flow
  - expect the map to change after each success

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a fuller primary-source treatment from Rory Sutherland or Goldratt-style operations work.
- Bottleneck logic is clarifying, but identifying the real constraint is often harder than the model makes it sound.
- The source leans anti-one-size-fits-all for corrective effect; used carelessly, that could become an excuse for intuition without measurement.
- Mental-availability examples are useful, but the summary remains lighter on how to measure whether awareness, distribution, price, product quality, or operations is the real limiting factor in a given case.
