# Summary

This source deepens the Chris Gore branch by making his Hollywood critique more concrete. Instead of talking only about `factory filmmaking` in the abstract, it names specific failure patterns: studio malpractice with major franchises, marketing that oversells films the work cannot satisfy, executive risk aversion around original ideas, and the way adaptation dependence hollows out mainstream filmmaking. Its strongest new vault contribution is a more explicit case for `franchise stewardship` as a real craft and governance responsibility.

# Key Claims

- Modern blockbuster decline is tied to committee logic, executive timidity, and loss of visionary leadership.
- Studio failures are more blameworthy than indie failures because large franchises have abundant resources and still squander them.
- Marketing often promises a stronger film than the final work delivers, widening audience disappointment.
- Overdependence on adaptations, sequels, and known intellectual property reflects systemic risk aversion.
- Independent films still preserve more heart and originality because fewer controls distort the work.

# Important Details

- Gore frames some big-budget franchise mishandling as `studio malpractice`, especially when iconic characters and once-strong properties are handled without coherent dramatic vision.
- The source adds a sharper distinction between marketing excellence and product excellence: studios can still be brilliant at selling movies they have not made strong enough to justify the campaign.
- `Lost visionary leadership` becomes a structural explanation for decline: executives and committees replace passionate stewards who would otherwise protect the material's identity.
- Global-market smoothing remains part of the diagnosis, but here it is joined by adaptation dependence and corporate fear of unproven concepts.
- Indie cinema is presented not as always polished, but as a vital contrast class showing what can happen when stories are not overmanaged.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- Some examples are rhetorical and polemical rather than carefully evidenced production case studies.
- The source is strongest as a critique of governance and stewardship, but weaker on the financing logic studios would use to defend these choices.
- Claims about cultural fragmentation and global dilution should be treated as contested diagnoses, not settled facts.
