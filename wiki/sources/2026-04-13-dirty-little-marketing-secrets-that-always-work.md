# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Rory Sutherland conversation arguing that many of the strongest marketing and systems moves are missed because institutions optimize for fast feedback, visible metrics, and reductionist logic while ignoring serendipity, status, signaling, and lived experience. Its strongest additions to the vault are a canonical `hidden functions` concept, a sharper distinction between fast-feedback and slow-feedback domains, and a clearer account of why trial and first adoption matter more than argument for products that create a `no going back` effect once experienced. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-dirty-little-marketing-secrets-that-always-work|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Organizations overvalue what produces fast, measurable feedback and undervalue slower but more durable effects such as brand, loyalty, trust, and service.
- Many products and practices contain `hidden functions` such as status, bonding, reassurance, or serendipity that get destroyed when leaders reduce them to their obvious mechanical role.
- Product adoption often depends less on persuasion than on getting people to try an experience that permanently changes their reference point.
- Pricing, fairness, and policy are felt behaviorally rather than processed only as numbers, which means frequency and context matter more than standard models admit.
- Cost-cutting and policy design frequently fail because they optimize explicit functions while ignoring tacit ones and because they add rules more easily than they remove them.

# Important Details

- The source gives the Rory branch a stronger `fast versus slow feedback` distinction:
  - performance advertising, stand-up comedy, and some retail operations produce quick signals
  - banking, insurance, service quality, and brand building often produce slower, blurrier feedback
  - this makes organizations systematically underinvest in loyalty, service, and emotional salience
- It adds a cleaner `no going back` adoption model:
  - some products look marginally better before trial but overwhelmingly better after lived use
  - downloads, electric vehicles, boiling-water taps, and similar examples flip behavior by changing the user's reference point
  - for such offers, subsidized trials and onboarding often matter more than rational persuasion
- The source's strongest new concept is `hidden functions`:
  - hotel doormen do more than open doors
  - small gifts, human greetings, and informal perks often generate status, bonding, trust, and memory
  - once leaders reduce a role or touchpoint to its explicit mechanical task, they may destroy value they never learned to count
- It also extends `psychological leverage` into work and workspace design:
  - remote work changes not only cost but autonomy, energy use, and lifestyle feasibility
  - offices should be designed around quiet focus, collaboration, and non-recorded conversation rather than one generic open-plan logic
  - serendipity and occasional in-person contact can have compounding information and morale effects
- The source strengthens the vault's treatment of price and fairness:
  - equal nominal fees can feel trivial for occasional users and punitive for frequent users
  - people react to prices as feelings shaped by repetition, status, and perceived justice
  - caps, subscriptions, and better segmentation may solve more than raw price changes alone
- It adds a more explicit rule about `addition versus subtraction`:
  - institutions often solve problems by adding steps, compliance, or features
  - behavioral side effects and perverse incentives then accumulate
  - the source implicitly argues for testing simpler defaults and subtraction-first redesign

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct transcript.
- The source is rich in examples but many are illustrative heuristics rather than rigorously quantified case studies.
- Several political and cultural examples mix descriptive observation with Rory Sutherland's normative judgments and should not be treated as neutral evidence.
- `Hidden functions` is a useful corrective concept, but it can also become an excuse to preserve costly legacy practices that no longer create enough real value.
