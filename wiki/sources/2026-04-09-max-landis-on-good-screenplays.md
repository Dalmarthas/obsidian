# Summary

This source is a brief secondary summary of Max Landis distilling what makes a screenplay good. Its strongest additions to the vault are: screenplays should stay sparse, clear, and forward-moving; they should entertain on the page as documents, not only as future movies; dialogue should survive read-aloud; structure should be used as a tool rather than a religion; and industry outcomes like selling, being assigned, and actually being produced should not be confused with one another. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-max-landis-on-good-screenplays|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Sparseness and clarity make scripts easier to trust and harder to abandon.
- A screenplay should be fun to read on the page, not only useful as a production blueprint.
- Momentum matters at scene, line, and page scale.
- Characters need direction and consistency even without a traditional arc.
- Three-act structure is useful as a momentum tool, not as dogma.
- Selling a script, being assigned work, and getting produced are related but different games.

# Important Details

- Line-level discipline:
  - avoid prose bulk and over-direction
  - cut dialogue that sounds false or embarrassing out loud
  - trust collaborators enough not to micromanage every effect in text
- Movement:
  - every scene and line should push forward
  - the writer should know where the story and characters are headed, even if loosely
- Character handling:
  - characters may not need a conventional arc
  - they still need a clear vector and should not violate their own established nature for convenience
- Readability as value:
  - some scripts sell because the read itself is entertaining
  - the document has to reward reps, executives, and producers before production exists
- Structural pragmatism:
  - three-act structure is treated as a practical momentum scaffold
  - page count should stay disciplined, ideally near 95 to 100 pages and not above 121
- Industry realism:
  - the market rewards different skills at different stages
  - writers should distinguish between being saleable, employable, and producible

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The raw text has encoding noise, so a few captured phrases are clearer in intent than in literal wording.
- Landis is intentionally blunt, so some of the advice can sound more rigid than the deeper principle probably is.
- Page-count rules and commercial advice may vary by era, genre, and format.
