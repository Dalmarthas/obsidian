# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Jefferson Fisher video on sounding more confident in professional communication through delivery mechanics rather than personality transformation. Its strongest additions to the vault are a `breath first` anti-filler rule, a cleaner warning about uptalk and reassurance-seeking phrasing, and a compact method for answering unknowns with pause, experience, and confident next steps. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-17-respond-with-confidence-tips-to-improve-your-communication-skills|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Confidence is communicated heavily through delivery variables such as silence, tone, and word choice rather than through content alone.
- Filler words and verbal `fluff` often signal discomfort with silence more than lack of knowledge.
- Replacing filler with a short pause makes speech sound more thoughtful and controlled.
- Rising sentence endings and reassurance-seeking phrases can unintentionally weaken perceived authority.
- Small wording shifts such as removing `just` or replacing `Does that make sense?` with input-seeking questions make communication sound firmer and more respectful.
- Even when you do not know an answer, you can sound confident by pausing, anchoring in experience, and naming clear next steps.

# Important Details

- The source's strongest reusable distinction is between:
  - changing how you speak
  - versus trying to become a different person
- It treats `fluff` broadly:
  - filler sounds like `um` and `uh`
  - habitual softeners like `you know` and `I mean`
  - comfort words like `basically`, `essentially`, and `literally`
  - apologetic minimizers like `just`
- Its main behavioral rule is simple:
  - let your breath be the first `word`
  - use silence instead of noise while thinking
  - train yourself not to panic at the gap between question and answer
- The source adds a more professional-delivery version of confidence:
  - lower or flatter endings on statements
  - less validation-seeking phrasing
  - direct invitations for discussion rather than self-undermining comprehension checks
- It gives useful replacement phrases:
  - replace `Does that make sense?` with `What are your thoughts?`
  - or `Do you have any questions?`
  - or `Do you have any feedback?`
- It also adds a clean `I don't know` response structure:
  - pause
  - say what you have or have not seen in experience
  - state what you are confident can be done next
  - turn uncertainty into process and follow-through rather than visible panic

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a transcript or primary capture.
- The source is strongest for professional meetings, presentations, and client-facing conversations, not for every casual or intimate context.
- Advice about removing `just`, adverbs, or reassurance phrases can be useful, but over-applied it may produce stiffness or false bluntness.
- Delivery cues can change perceived confidence without guaranteeing actual clarity, judgment, or substance.
