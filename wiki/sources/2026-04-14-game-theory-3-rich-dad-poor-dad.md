# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a lecture that uses `game theory` language to argue that success is shaped less by isolated personal traits than by class-conditioned environments, parenting strategies, and the openness or closure of the larger social game. Its strongest contribution is a structural corrective to individual-success psychology: growth mindset, self-control, and deliberate practice may still matter, but they are partly downstream of trust, stability, and social mobility rather than clean independent causes. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-14-game-theory-3-rich-dad-poor-dad|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Many popular success theories confuse correlation with causation by treating traits common among the successful as if they were the main cause of success.
- Rich and poor families often train children for different environments:
  - negotiation, explanation, and institutional confidence for the rich
  - obedience, caution, and survival compliance for the poor
- The marshmallow test is interpreted less as innate self-control than as a trust test shaped by environmental reliability.
- Social mobility is the main stabilizing feature of a society because it keeps talent, ambition, and reward more loosely tied to birth.
- Elites tend to re-rig institutions over time so their own children retain advantage, reducing mobility and increasing social instability.
- Revolutions and populist upheavals are interpreted as `game resets` when blocked mobility, debt, and elite overproduction make the old system intolerable.

# Important Details

- The source's central move is methodological:
  - individual psychologies are not dismissed entirely
  - they are reinterpreted as products of environment, class position, and credible future expectations
  - this makes `self-control`, `resilience`, and `self-reflection` partly structural phenomena rather than purely personal virtues
- Rich-world parenting is described as training for institutional navigation:
  - larger vocabulary
  - more explanation and reason-giving
  - more stable promises
  - more comfort interacting with teachers, bosses, and authority figures
- Poor-world parenting is described as training for danger management:
  - shorter and more command-oriented communication
  - more threat-based discipline
  - less reliable promises because conditions are volatile
  - stronger emphasis on staying quiet around authority because confrontation can be costly
- The source is especially useful where it reframes familiar findings:
  - delayed gratification becomes partly a forecast about whether promises will be kept
  - resilience becomes partly confidence that support systems exist
  - self-reflection becomes easier when life is stable enough to examine rather than merely endure
- The `game theory` label is used more metaphorically than formally:
  - different classes are depicted as inhabiting different games with different payoffs
  - mobility matters because it determines whether people can switch games or are trapped in the one they were born into

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a compact secondary summary of a strong structural argument and not a balanced survey of mobility research.
- The source generalizes heavily across `rich` and `poor` families and across political systems.
- The lecture uses game-theory language, but its durable contribution is closer to class reproduction and institutional design than to formal equilibrium analysis.
- The anti-psychology framing is clarifying as a corrective, but it can overstate the case if it treats individual skill, judgment, or effort as nearly irrelevant.
