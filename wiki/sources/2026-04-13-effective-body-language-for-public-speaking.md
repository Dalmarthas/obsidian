# Summary

This source adds a concrete nonverbal layer to the vault's live-speaking branch. Its strongest contributions are a balanced anti-sway stance, gesture-range guidance that avoids visually constricted nervousness, nonpatterned audience eye contact, movement rules tied to speaking moments, and explicit seated and online adaptations. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-effective-body-language-for-public-speaking|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Audience judgments of confidence and credibility are strongly shaped by nonverbal delivery, not just verbal content.
- Stable posture and purposeful movement reduce visible nervousness better than generic `be confident` advice.
- Open hand gestures and well-distributed eye contact make speaking feel more connected and believable.
- Movement should support transitions and engagement, not become constant pacing.
- The same nonverbal principles need context-specific adaptation for seated meetings and online presentations.

# Important Details

- The source gives the branch a concrete stance model:
  - head level
  - shoulders and hips square
  - feet parallel under the shoulders like train tracks
  - balanced vertical posture to reduce rocking and swaying
- It sharpens gesture guidance:
  - gestures should extend outward toward the audience rather than stay pinned close to the torso
  - gesture range should stay above the waist and break the shoulder line when emphasis is needed
  - relaxed reset positions matter between gestures, such as arms loose at the sides or lightly clasped in front
- The source adds a simple eye-contact rule:
  - hold brief contact with one person long enough to finish a phrase or thought
  - then move on in a nonrepetitive pattern instead of darting or scanning mechanically
- Movement is framed as event-linked rather than continuous:
  - step toward the audience at the start
  - step toward question askers during Q and A
  - move during transitions, then re-establish the balanced stance
- The source broadens the branch's portability:
  - seated settings call for grounded feet, a slight forward lean, visible hands, and fewer objects available for fidgeting
  - online settings call for head stillness, square shoulders, and looking at the camera rather than the screen image

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is practical delivery guidance, not a rigorous evidence review of nonverbal-cue research.
- Claims about body language mattering more than content are directionally useful but easy to overstate.
- Eye-contact advice is explicitly North America-coded and may need cultural adaptation.
- Some speakers can imitate the outer forms of confidence without real grounding, which can turn body-language advice into stagey performance.
