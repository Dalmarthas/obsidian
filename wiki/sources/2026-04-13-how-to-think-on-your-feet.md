# Summary

This source is a practical presentation-skills article on responding clearly in real time while presenting, elaborating, and answering questions. Its strongest addition to the vault is a simple model of `thinking on your feet` that turns the skill away from speed theater and toward presence, topic mastery, point-first brevity, clarification, and preparation for likely questions. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-how-to-think-on-your-feet|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Thinking on your feet is less about raw speed than about giving people the most relevant information clearly and accurately.
- Real-time speaking improves when attention stays in the room instead of being consumed by self-talk.
- Point-first delivery and anti-rambling discipline matter more than impressive spontaneity.
- Better answers often begin with clarifying the question rather than replying to the first vague version of it.
- `Unexpected` questions are often only unexpected to the unprepared speaker.

# Important Details

- The article divides the skill into three situations:
  - presenting information clearly in the moment
  - elaborating when more context is needed
  - answering questions under immediate social pressure
- Its presence model is basic but reusable:
  - breathe and reduce self-talk
  - clear the mind of irrelevant internal noise
  - keep attention on the information that needs to be delivered
- The article's speaking standard is executive-facing:
  - know the topic deeply enough to speak from actual knowledge
  - avoid rambling and excess background
  - get to the point quickly, then support the point
- The elaboration model is narrow and practical:
  - elaborate only to improve understanding
  - use a short example, anecdote, or application case
  - return to the main thread before the explanation becomes drift
- The question-answering model contributes the most durable structure:
  - listen for key words and phrases in the question
  - clarify broad prompts before answering them
  - search memory through categories, subtopics, or sensory cues
  - jot a few notes if needed before responding
  - answer only from real knowledge rather than inventing confidence
- The article's strongest hidden claim is about preparation:
  - people who appear naturally quick often anticipated the question class in advance
  - conference recaps, client meetings, and public presentations all have predictable follow-up questions
  - visible spontaneity is often pre-built readiness rather than magic
- A final optional layer is improvisation training:
  - improv is presented as a way to stretch on-the-spot thinking
  - but it is framed as a supplement, not a substitute for content mastery and preparation

# Tensions / Caveats

- The article is practical but thinly evidenced and reads more like training advice than research-grounded theory.
- Its focus is business presentation and meeting performance, not adversarial interviews, negotiation, or deep improvisational speaking.
- `Know your topic` is correct but incomplete; some speaking failures come from anxiety, status pressure, or threat appraisal even when knowledge is strong.
- The improv suggestion is plausible, but the article does not distinguish when improvisation helps versus when it encourages verbal overproduction.
