# Summary

This source turns chess calculation into an explicit decision process rather than a vague talent. Its strongest contribution is a two-layer model: a simple anti-blunder checklist for all players and a deeper candidate-move process for stronger players, where ideas are generated, tested, and discarded if the opponent has a refuting resource.

# Key Claims

- Calculation means evaluating both your own ideas and the opponent's best responses rather than only imagining your plan in isolation.
- A checklist of checks, captures, attacks, and opponent threats is a universal blunder-reduction tool.
- Stronger calculation requires candidate moves, falsification, and willingness to abandon attractive ideas.
- Board vision, pattern recognition, and visualization are foundations for deeper tactical and strategic search.
- Time and attention should be spent where positions are complex or unclear rather than wasted on obviously flawed ideas.

# Important Details

- The source explicitly separates beginner one-move blunder checks from intermediate and advanced calculation processes.
- "Prove yourself wrong" is one of the most reusable principles here: assume your idea fails, then search for the defense that kills it.
- Example-driven learning through games and puzzles is treated as a practical way to build calculation depth.
- The source argues against "hope chess," where players move first and trust the opponent to miss the punishment.
- The raw summary includes a side note about Armenia and chess education, but the durable value of the source is the calculation model, not that footnote.
