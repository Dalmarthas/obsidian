# Summary

This source is a second secondary summary of Patrick Lencioni's `ideal team player` model. Its strongest additions to the vault are not the core `humble / hungry / smart` framework itself, which is already present, but the model's origin story inside one firm's values, a clearer coaching posture of naming the missing virtue directly without turning archetypes into identities, and a more explicit claim that consistent expectations often produce either development or self-selection out of the culture. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-20-the-ideal-team-player-by-patrick-lencioni|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- The `humble / hungry / smart` model emerged from a practical values system that proved broadly portable across organizations.
- Most team-player failures are best coached by identifying the missing virtue directly rather than by treating the person as globally broken.
- Archetype labels are useful for internal diagnosis but should not be turned into public identity tags.
- Consistent expectations and repeated coaching often improve people over time; those unwilling to grow frequently self-select out.
- The three virtues should be embedded into hiring, reviews, and everyday norms rather than taught as a one-off team exercise.

# Important Details

- The source adds an origin story that matters for interpretation:
  - the model began as one firm's internal values
  - client demand revealed that the pattern generalized beyond the original company
  - this supports treating the framework as practical pattern recognition rather than merely personal preference
- It reinforces the three-virtue model with slightly sharper nuance:
  - humility remains central and is linked explicitly to thinking of yourself less, not denying real strengths
  - hunger is framed as steady contribution energy rather than minimum-viable effort
  - smart remains interpersonal and situational rather than academic
- Its most useful addition is the coaching stance:
  - do not call someone a `lovable slacker` or `skillful politician` to their face
  - identify the missing virtue instead
  - keep the feedback behavior-specific and persistent rather than dramatic
- The source also adds a culture-enforcement rule:
  - if the organization calmly but repeatedly reinforces the virtues
  - most people adapt
  - those who do not want that standard tend to opt out, which reduces the need for constant forced removals
- It strengthens the operating-system interpretation:
  - the framework belongs in hiring criteria
  - in development conversations
  - in performance reviews
  - and in daily language about teamwork
- A final addition is its dignity claim:
  - teams built around these virtues are not only more productive
  - they are also more fulfilling and dignifying places to work
  - this frames the model as both performance logic and human-environment design

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is still a secondary summary relying on qualitative consulting judgment more than rigorous assessment science.
- The self-selection claim may be true in some cultures, but could be overstated in labor markets where people stay despite misfit.
- Embedding simple virtue language into reviews can help clarity, but also risks flattening complex performance problems into moral shorthand.
- The model remains useful as a simplifier, but not as a full substitute for structured hiring and management systems.
