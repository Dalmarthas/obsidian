# Summary

This source is a large secondary summary of Daniel Pink's persuasion masterclass. It reframes persuasion as a human-centered skill grounded in attunement, resilience, clarity, ethical service, autonomy, and context design rather than in old images of pushy selling. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-08-daniel-pink-persuasion-masterclass-complete-summary|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Modern persuasion changed because information asymmetry collapsed into information parity.
- The three governing capacities are attunement, buoyancy, and clarity.
- Context matters more than personality in many persuasive outcomes.
- People act more readily when they can generate their own reasons and see the path as their choice.
- Ethical, service-oriented persuasion is not only morally cleaner but usually more effective.
- Timing and sequencing shape outcomes as much as message content.

# Important Details

- The attunement branch is unusually operational:
  - perspective-taking over pure self-expression
  - reduce felt power to improve perspective-taking
  - distinguish thoughts from feelings under cognitive load
  - use the empty chair and direct questions to test assumptions
- The buoyancy branch gives a full rejection-management model:
  - interrogative self-talk before action
  - stay present during action
  - reframe failure afterward with Seligman's Three P's
  - use self-compassion instead of catastrophe
- The clarity branch is also multi-part:
  - move from information access to information curation
  - move from problem-solving to problem-finding
  - use the Five Whys to surface underlying issues
  - keep to the one percent that explains the other ninety-nine
  - prefer three arguments maximum over overload
- The action and motivation branch is highly reusable:
  - agitate rather than irritate
  - use readiness scales and "why not lower?" questions
  - apply social proof
  - build off-ramps by reducing friction
  - make work personal and purposeful
- The source also contains a durable timing layer:
  - use fresh starts and premortems at beginnings
  - treat midpoints as alarms
  - use endings to energize and elevate
  - give bad news before good news
  - use deadlines carefully depending on task type

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The file contains encoding noise.
- Lesson 13 appears mislabeled or duplicated in the source summary.
- The source strongly prefers positive-sum and service-oriented persuasion, which is clarifying but may understate contexts that are more adversarial or structurally zero-sum.
- Some advice is tuned for commercial or cognitive-load contexts and may not transfer cleanly to intimate, therapeutic, or explicitly moral conversations.
