# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Rory Sutherland talk arguing that many institutions optimize for defensible logic rather than real-world outcomes, which makes them systematically undervalue persuasion, framing, psychological design, and messy experimentation. Its strongest additions to the vault are a sharper contrast between `defensible reasoning` and `market feedback`, a more explicit B2B version of Rory's framing logic, and a useful warning that hiring and policy systems become brittle when they sort through narrow proxy metrics rather than plural evidence of real capability. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-rory-sutherland-why-logic-is-killing-your-business|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Large organizations often optimize for consistency, legal defensibility, and blame avoidance rather than for what actually works.
- Framing, labeling, and psychological salience can create large behavioral changes at far lower cost than infrastructure-heavy interventions.
- Narrow educational and hiring proxies repeatedly reward the same type of person while missing curiosity, weirdness, and real-world capability.
- B2B buying is also heavily psychological: price framing, brand personality, and reassurance often shape decisions as much as product capability.
- Marketing matters not only because it sells inventions, but because it converts technical possibility into adopted innovation.

# Important Details

- The source's core institutional distinction is:
  - bureaucracies and policy systems optimize for reasoning that can be defended in advance
  - markets optimize for response after the fact
  - this creates systematic blind spots around low-cost interventions that look irrational before they work
- It adds a stronger `persuasion before compulsion` rule:
  - policymakers often default from law to economics to persuasion
  - the source argues that persuasion, framing, defaults, and labeling are often cheaper and more behaviorally effective than coercion-first designs
  - the same logic extends beyond government into product, pricing, and service design
- Several examples deepen the vault's `psychological leverage` branch:
  - London Overground adoption changed when the system became cognitively legible on the map
  - energy and calorie labels altered both customer behavior and supplier behavior by making hidden attributes visible
  - annual price pain can be dissolved by per-day or per-unit framing without changing the nominal economics
- The source also adds a clearer B2B marketing layer:
  - pricing structure matters as much as price level
  - service and retention are often underfunded because acquisition is easier to measure
  - branding can matter in low-engagement categories when personality makes trust easier
- Its talent and hiring layer is one of the more reusable additions:
  - standardized credentials create narrow opportunity bottlenecks
  - curiosity, side obsessions, and weird hobbies can signal broader cognitive range
  - task-based trials often reveal more than prestige-based filters
- The source also extends Rory's process critique:
  - procurement-heavy briefs and hourly billing reward linear compliance
  - creative value often requires derailing the brief early and questioning the problem itself
  - psychological rules expand the solution space beyond what physical constraints alone seem to allow

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct transcript.
- The source is strongest as an anti-bureaucratic corrective, but read too broadly it could romanticize eccentricity, underweight evidence quality, or excuse avoidable rigor failures.
- Several remarks about neurodiversity, conspiracy openness, and tribal identity are better treated here as provocative heuristics than as settled claims.
- Some case examples are illustrative rather than well-sourced in this summary, so exact effect sizes should remain tentative.
