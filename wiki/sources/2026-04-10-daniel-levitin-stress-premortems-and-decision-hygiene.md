# Summary
This source is a secondary summary of Daniel Levitin's stress and decision-making material. Its strongest additions to the vault are a sharper account of how stress degrades executive judgment, a more explicit premortem method for anticipating failure before crisis, and a practical model of decision hygiene built from advance planning, fixed-location systems, and clearer medical risk questions. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-daniel-levitin-stress-premortems-and-decision-hygiene|Raw source]].
# Key Claims
- Stress impairs rational and executive function, making preventable mistakes more likely in high-stakes moments.
- Good judgment under pressure is often created in advance rather than improvised during crisis.
- Premortems improve outcomes by identifying likely failures before action begins.
- Fixed environmental systems reduce stress-amplified forgetting and chaos.
- Medical and life decisions should be discussed before crisis, when cognition is less compromised.
- Risk understanding requires concrete numbers, not just vague reassurance.
# Important Details
- The source's governing stress claim is narrower than generic self-help:
  - cortisol and stress physiology are framed as adaptive for rapid survival response
  - the same response degrades careful reasoning and executive control
- The main reusable method is prospective hindsight, or the premortem:
  - assume something has gone wrong
  - ask what likely caused the failure
  - install preventive or damage-limiting systems before the event
- The source also adds a practical organization layer:
  - use stable homes for keys, passports, and other essential items
  - rely less on stressed recall and more on environmental consistency
- Its medical-decision layer is unusually concrete:
  - ask about 
umber needed to treat
  - ask about side-effect rates and tradeoffs
  - understand that many interventions look different when framed statistically
- The source extends beyond medicine into family and life planning:
  - discuss difficult decisions in advance
  - do not wait until fear, ambiguity, and urgency narrow the available thinking
# Tensions / Caveats
- This is a secondary summary rather than a transcript or direct review of Levitin's evidence.
- Some numerical medical examples in the summary should be treated as prompts for verification, not as durable figures.
- The source is strongest on decision hygiene and preparation, not on deeper treatment of chronic stress, trauma, or systemic medical complexity.
- Premortems are useful, but they can become anxiety rituals if they are not paired with action, proportionality, and stopping rules.
