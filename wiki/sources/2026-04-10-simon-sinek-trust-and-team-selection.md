# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Simon Sinek using military examples to argue that strong teams should evaluate character and trustworthiness alongside raw performance. Its strongest additions to the vault are an explicit `trust-performance matrix`, a sharper warning about `toxic high performers`, and a recognition principle that organizations decay when they reward output while ignoring the people who make others feel safe, supported, and willing to cooperate. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-simon-sinek-trust-and-team-selection|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- High-trust team members can be more valuable than higher-output but low-trust individuals.
- Toxic high performers damage culture and should not be advanced simply because they produce results.
- Teams often already know who the strongest performer is and who reliably has others' backs, but organizations reward only the first category.
- Trust, character, and reliability should be measured and recognized explicitly rather than left invisible.
- Coaching is preferable to immediate dismissal for talented but behaviorally weak people, but uncoachable toxicity should not be protected.

# Important Details

- The source builds around a simple matrix:
  - performance as job capability
  - trust as character, reliability, and social safety
- Its selection rule is clear:
  - high performance plus high trust is ideal
  - medium performance plus high trust can still be highly valuable
  - high performance plus low trust is culturally dangerous
- The military example matters here not because business equals combat, but because high-stakes environments make the cost of low trust easier to see.
- The source also adds a recognition-layer critique:
  - many teams honor visible output
  - fewer teams reward the people who create cohesion, protection, and emotional safety
- It offers a practical diagnostic:
  - ask who the top performer is
  - ask who always has the team's back
  - compare how the system rewards each answer

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a direct transcript or a broader Simon Sinek corpus.
- Military analogies can overstate clarity; corporate contexts often have murkier incentives, weaker shared mission, and less immediate feedback about trust failures.
- `Character metrics` are easier to call for than to implement well; badly designed trust measurement can become popularity theater or leader favoritism.
- Coaching toxic high performers is useful only if the organization has both the courage and the consistency to act when coaching fails.
