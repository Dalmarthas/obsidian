# Summary

This source is a compact secondary summary of David Allen's GTD model. Its central claim is that productivity becomes less stressful when commitments stop living in the head and are instead captured, clarified, organized, and reviewed inside a trusted external system. In the current vault, the main value is not generic `get organized` advice, but a specific anti-overwhelm operating model: uncaptured commitments keep consuming background attention, while defined next actions, project lists, and regular review restore presence and reliable follow-through.

# Key Claims

- The mind performs better as an idea generator than as a storage device for commitments, reminders, and unresolved decisions.
- Open loops remain mentally sticky until they are captured and clarified into a concrete next action and intended outcome.
- Stress drops when commitments live in a trusted external system that the user consistently reviews and believes.
- GTD depends on a disciplined three-habit loop: capture, process, and review.
- Weekly top-down review is what keeps a task system aligned to longer horizons instead of degenerating into stale list-hoarding.

# Important Details

- The source uses an `open programs` metaphor: uncaptured commitments behave like background applications that steadily consume processing power.
- It distinguishes capture from processing. Writing things down is only the first step; each item must later be clarified into `do`, `defer`, `delegate`, `store`, or `drop`.
- A `next action` must be concrete and physical enough that the brain no longer has to renegotiate what to do.
- Multi-step outcomes belong on a project list rather than being disguised as single tasks.
- The system uses distinct containers such as calendar, next-action lists, follow-up lists, someday-maybe, project lists, and reference material.
- Context lists are introduced as a scale solution: when task lists grow large, work should be organized by context so the right set is visible when conditions match.
- The weekly review moves from longer-horizon life vision to projects and then to immediate actions, refreshing trust that the system still reflects reality.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary of a GTD explainer rather than a primary-source ingest of Allen's book.
- The source is strongest on individual task management and weaker on shared-team systems, collaborative workflows, or highly interrupt-driven roles.
- `Capture everything` is useful as anti-overwhelm advice, but it can become bureaucratic if people over-collect without clarifying or pruning.
- The fixed examples for processing and review times are illustrative rather than universal.
