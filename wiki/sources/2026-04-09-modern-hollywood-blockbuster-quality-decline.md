# Summary

This source is a generalized critique of perceived blockbuster decline in modern Hollywood. Its strongest contribution to the vault is not a blanket `movies were better before` claim. It gives a reusable industrial and storytelling diagnosis: studio profit logic, demographic broadening, global market smoothing, shortened runtime expectations, and fear of boredom can push films toward generic emotional triggers and constant stimulation instead of earned tension, cultural specificity, and deeper character-story integration.

# Key Claims

- Profit maximization often overrides narrative depth and artistic specificity in blockbuster decision-making.
- Broad demographic targeting can flatten tone, complexity, and mature emotional texture.
- Global-market smoothing reduces local specificity, idiom, and cultural sharpness.
- Formula and runtime compression can reduce room for setup, subplotting, character development, and contextual tension.
- Many blockbusters substitute stimulation and instant emotional cues for structurally earned suspense and meaning.

# Important Details

- The source explicitly warns against treating nostalgia bias as proof:
  audiences remember the best films from the past and forget much of the weaker material.
- It frames audience boredom-anxiety as a major creative distortion:
  films become packed with action and emotional triggers even when those moments are weakly integrated into the underlying story.
- Demographic expansion is treated not only as a business strategy but as a creative constraint that can push films toward generic universality.
- The source treats marketing and box office math as central causes of creative simplification rather than as downstream side issues.
- It also adds a practical viewing diagnostic:
  ask whether an emotional or action beat advances the story's tension, context, or character logic, or merely stimulates for a moment.

# Tensions / Caveats

- The source explicitly acknowledges nostalgia bias, which is useful:
  not every quality complaint about modern blockbusters should be treated as objective fact.
- This is a secondary summary and the speaker is not identified in the raw title, so attribution should remain conservative.
- Some claims are industrially plausible but still interpretive rather than empirically settled.
