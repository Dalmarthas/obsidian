# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Steve Jobs's 2005 Stanford commencement address. Its strongest additions to the vault are a retrospective `connecting the dots` model for career uncertainty, a reinvention logic that treats major setbacks as creative resets rather than final verdicts, and a mortality-based filter for deciding whether present work is worth continuing. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-steve-jobs-2005-stanford-commencement|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Important career moves often make sense only in hindsight, so uncertainty has to be navigated before coherence is visible.
- Genuine love of the work matters because it helps people survive setbacks and keep searching rather than settling.
- Getting fired, failing, or losing status can become a liberating reset rather than pure ruin.
- Mortality clarifies priorities by stripping away ego, embarrassment, and borrowed expectations.
- Authentic work requires resisting dogma and external scripts about what a life is supposed to look like.

# Important Details

- The `connecting the dots` story gives the source its career logic:
  - curiosity-led detours can become future advantages
  - obvious utility is not always visible up front
  - trust is required before retrospective coherence appears
- The `love and loss` story gives it a reinvention model:
  - Jobs treats being fired from Apple as devastating but strangely freeing
  - losing success status restored beginner energy
  - the next phase produced NeXT, Pixar, and eventually a return to Apple
- The mortality layer is unusually direct:
  - daily awareness of death is presented as a decision filter
  - fear of embarrassment, failure, and others' judgment weakens when time is felt as limited
  - mortality is framed as a practical clarifier, not only a philosophical theme
- The speech also adds a voice-and-identity layer:
  - do not live by dogma
  - do not let other people's thinking drown out your own inner signal
  - stay willing to remain curious and somewhat unpolished even after success

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than the full speech transcript.
- The address is rhetorically powerful, but it risks survivorship bias if read as proof that all uncertain detours will become valuable.
- `Follow your heart` can become vague or reckless if separated from skill-building, livelihood constraints, and feedback from reality.
- Jobs's account is strongest as a meaning-and-courage filter, not as a complete operating system for ordinary financial or institutional constraints.
