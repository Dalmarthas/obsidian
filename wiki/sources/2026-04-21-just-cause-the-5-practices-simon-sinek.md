# Summary

This source presents `Just Cause` as the first of Simon Sinek's five practices for playing the infinite game. A Just Cause is an idealized future state that does not yet exist, is morally meaningful enough to invite sacrifice, and stands above any specific product or service. It gives people a reason to endure hardship because the work feels connected to a larger future.

The source strengthens the vault's Sinek branch by making the "cause before scoreboard" idea more concrete. The cause is not a quarterly objective, a competitive win, or a product-quality claim. It is a future world the organization is trying to help bring into being.

# Key Claims

- A Just Cause is a future-oriented ideal that people are willing to sacrifice for.
- Finite victories are milestones inside the larger infinite game, not the final meaning of the work.
- A Just Cause should be independent of specific products and services.
- People show deeper loyalty when they internalize the cause as their own.
- Leaders must intentionally offer a cause if they want long-term resilience and commitment.
- Product excellence alone is usually too narrow to sustain meaningful sacrifice.
- The cause attracts certain kinds of employees and customers because it gives them an identity to join.

# Important Details

Sinek uses the U.S. Declaration of Independence as the canonical example. The statement of equality and rights did not describe the present reality when written; it described a future state worth pursuing. The Revolutionary War is treated as a finite victory inside a much longer infinite game of trying to live up to the ideal.

Apple under Steve Jobs is the organizational example. The personal computer was not the cause itself. It was a product that advanced a deeper cause: empowering individuals to stand up to large institutions. That cause, more than product superiority alone, helped attract creative people and devoted customers.

The source also broadens sacrifice beyond heroic or life-and-death stakes. In organizational life, sacrifice can include travel, late nights, forgone pay, and discomfort. The issue is not that people enjoy sacrifice, but that the sacrifice feels worth it when tied to a meaningful cause.

The practical tests implied by the source are:

1. Can the cause be stated as an ideal future human outcome?
2. Does it remain meaningful if product or service names are removed?
3. Would it make real sacrifice feel worth it to aligned people?
4. Does it guide what the organization says yes or no to after finite wins?
5. Can people see themselves as participants in it, not only employees or buyers?

# Tensions / Caveats

- The source relies on examples more than on a formal checklist, so the boundary between a real Just Cause and inspiring rhetoric remains partly interpretive.
- "Sacrifice for the cause" can become exploitative if leaders ask for employee hardship while preserving their own comfort or rewards.
- Product excellence may be more meaningful to some craftspeople than the source allows, so the claim that nobody cares about "best products at best value" should be treated as rhetorical overstatement.
- A cause can attract loyalty and identity, but it can also drift into zealotry if not constrained by ethics, feedback, and accountability.
- The Declaration of Independence example is powerful but historically charged because the ideal coexisted with exclusions and contradictions.
