# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a short explanatory video on game theory. Its strongest additions to the vault are a clean distinction between individually rational versus collectively optimal outcomes, a compact explanation of Nash equilibrium through the Prisoner's Dilemma, and a fairness-allocation layer through the Shapley value. It expands the current decision branch by shifting attention from bias inside one mind to strategic choice between interdependent actors. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-how-decision-making-is-actually-science-game-theory-explained|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Game theory studies situations where each person's payoff depends on what other people choose.
- In competitive settings, Nash equilibrium identifies strategy profiles that no player can improve by changing alone.
- Individually rational behavior can still produce worse collective outcomes, as shown by the Prisoner's Dilemma.
- In cooperative settings, the Shapley value divides gains or costs according to average marginal contribution across possible coalition orderings.
- Formal fairness rules can be analytically consistent while still clashing with moral or social intuitions in real institutions.

# Important Details

- The source broadens `decision making` beyond solo judgment:
  - some choices cannot be evaluated only by preferences, evidence, or bias
  - they also depend on expectations about what other people will do
  - this makes strategic interdependence the core unit of analysis
- The Prisoner's Dilemma is used as the main noncooperative example:
  - each player prefers confessing regardless of what the other does
  - mutual confession is therefore stable under unilateral deviation
  - that stable outcome is still worse for the pair than mutual silence
- The source's strongest competitive concept is Nash equilibrium:
  - it is not the same thing as the best total outcome
  - it names a locally stable outcome under given incentives
  - the gap between stability and desirability explains why bad systems can persist without any actor behaving irrationally
- The cooperative branch adds a different question:
  - once a coalition forms, how should gains, costs, or rewards be divided?
  - the source answers with the Shapley value, which averages each player's marginal contribution across possible coalition orderings
- The fairness section rests on four axioms:
  - equal contributors should be treated equally
  - noncontributors get zero under the model
  - contribution is measured marginally rather than sentimentally
  - multi-part games should be decomposed consistently rather than flattened into one crude split
- The cookie example contributes a practical allocation intuition:
  - value should not always be split equally
  - nor should it be assigned only by visible output in one fixed order
  - average marginal contribution is a more defensible middle path when collaboration creates extra surplus

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a short secondary summary, so it introduces the ideas clearly but does not supply much mathematical depth or real-world edge-case analysis.
- Nash equilibrium is descriptive of stable incentives, not automatically prescriptive of what people ought to choose or build.
- The Shapley-value treatment is elegant, but the source itself acknowledges that the `dummy player` rule can feel ethically wrong in contexts involving care, leave, disability, or social insurance.
- The source is strong on clarity, but weak on repeated games, trust-building, enforcement, and how real institutions escape prisoner's-dilemma traps over time.
