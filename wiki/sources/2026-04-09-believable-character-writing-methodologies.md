# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of multiple screenwriting approaches to building believable characters in film and television. It emphasizes empathy for every character, desire-and-obstacle logic, human hypocrisy, multiple points of view, character interviews, and real-world research as the main tools for creating the illusion of depth on the page. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-believable-character-writing-methodologies|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Characters begin as flat constructions on the page and acquire apparent depth through emotionally truthful writing.
- Writers need a point of personal empathy with every character, including villains and anti-heroes.
- Desire, intention, and obstacle are the practical backbone of believable characterization.
- Real people are contradictory and hypocritical, so characters should not be forced into clean internal consistency.
- Character interviews and real-world analogue research can expose hidden motivations, fears, and voice.

# Important Details

- The source frames `depth` as an effect created in the audience rather than as a literal property of the page.
- It contributes a useful empathy rule:
  - do not morally flatten antagonists
  - find the human logic of every major character
  - imagine each character as if they were the lead in their own story
- It sharpens wants-over-traits logic:
  - who the character is should be inferred from what they want
  - what blocks them
  - what tactics they choose
- The hypocrisy layer is explicit:
  - people judge others differently than themselves
  - they hold conflicting desires
  - consistency is less realistic than selective self-justification
- The source adds one concrete discovery tool:
  - `character interviews`
  - writers ask the character direct questions to surface voice, wounds, fear, history, and contradiction
- Research is framed as specificity support:
  - talk to real-world counterparts
  - use lived or observed detail to avoid generic scenes and `writerly` behavior

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The raw text has encoding noise, so some phrasing is likely clearer in intent than in capture.
- The source is stronger on character truth than on larger structural architecture.
- `Depth` can become a vague compliment unless it is translated into actionable writing choices at the level of desire, contradiction, and scene behavior.
