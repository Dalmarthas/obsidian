# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Rory Sutherland conversation arguing that great campaigns emerge from behavioral realism rather than spreadsheet realism. Its strongest additions to the vault are a canonical `transaction utility` concept, a more explicit `Doorman Fallacy` version of hidden-function loss, and a sharper claim that call centers, frontline staff, and crisis-resolution moments can multiply the whole brand far more than classic campaign assets alone. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-13-the-playbook-behind-every-great-campaign|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- People decide comparatively, emotionally, and through trust proxies rather than through isolated rational optimization.
- Organizations routinely destroy value when they optimize what is easy to count instead of what customers actually feel.
- Human service moments can act as force multipliers on the whole brand, especially when they create memorable recovery or reassurance.
- Status, fairness, and identity strongly shape willingness to pay even when functional utility is unchanged.
- Marketing is fat-tailed, so incentive systems built for linear, measurable output under-reward the ideas and touchpoints that matter most.

# Important Details

- The source argues that people need contrast and reference points, which is why decoys and comparative framing are often more psychologically accurate than single best-answer interfaces.
- The `Doorman Fallacy` is the clearest diagnostic example:
  - a doorman is easy to model as a door-opener
  - but the real value may include safety, status, welcome, taxi-hailing, reassurance, and hotel feel
  - automation preserves the visible task while deleting the hidden value
- `Brand quakes` are described as unusually positive or negative service moments that permanently shift brand perception out of proportion to their frequency.
- Royal Mail and Dyson are used to show that trust in a human representative can outweigh harder service metrics and create disproportionate loyalty.
- The source ties pricing psychology to `transaction utility`: the same object feels differently priced depending on context, seller identity, occasion, and what the purchase seems to say about the buyer.
- It also argues that shareholder-value logic, quarterly scoreboards, and consulting-style quantification bias often strip away exactly the human judgment and relationship value that create premium economics later.

# Tensions / Caveats

- Several claims are directional rather than rigorously evidenced here, especially around public-versus-private firm behavior and legal-system critique.
- The praise for human judgment should not be flattened into a rejection of metrics, automation, or process discipline.
- Strong service moments are clearly valuable here, but the source is lighter on operational rules for scaling them without waste.
- `Transaction utility` can explain real behavior while still being used manipulatively if firms optimize feel without sufficient delivered value.
