# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Seth Godin conversation on ethical marketing, audience choice, positioning, and pricing. Its strongest new contribution is to define marketing as helping a specific group create the change they seek, then making positioning itself a service that reduces buyer confusion rather than a loud attempt to dominate attention. The source also sharpens the Seth branch through the `first ten` test, professionalism over authenticity theater, price as story, and free content as a trust engine. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-12-seth-godin-marketing-strategies-that-work|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Marketing is ethically making change for people who want it, not manipulating the masses.
- Effective work starts with a smallest viable audience and a clear promise about the change being offered.
- Positioning is a service to buyers because it helps them navigate choices and find what fits them.
- Price is part of the story the offer tells; competing on cheapness usually signals fear and drives a race to the bottom.
- Creators and professionals should seek advice from the right people, ignore generalized criticism, and keep promises consistently.

# Important Details

- The source adds a clear marketing definition:
  - marketing is the act of making change happen
  - impact is not real until someone is actually changed
  - ethical marketing works with people rather than being done to them
- Smallest viable audience remains central, but the source adds a practical threshold:
  - if the first ten right people would not miss the work or tell others, either the offer or the audience is wrong
  - early spread is a stronger test than generic mass approval
  - serving everyone is treated as disrespect for the actual distinctness of the people being served
- The positioning layer is the strongest reusable addition:
  - positioning helps people choose rather than merely helping the seller shout louder
  - a useful offer should occupy a clear corner on meaningful axes in the category
  - strong positioning includes referring misaligned prospects elsewhere instead of pretending the offer is for everyone
- The empathy template is concrete:
  - `my product is for people who believe...`
  - `I will focus on people who want...`
  - `I promise that engaging with what I make will help you get...`
  - this turns positioning into a customer-belief and desired-change exercise rather than a self-description
- Professionalism becomes an explicit rule:
  - authenticity is weak when used to excuse inconsistency or self-indulgence
  - market trust depends on keeping promises repeatedly
  - serving the audience may require choices that are more disciplined than merely expressing the maker's mood
- Price and offer design are framed narratively:
  - price is part of the signal about value, seriousness, and identity
  - cheapness can become fear wearing a rational mask
  - higher pricing only works when the story and delivery justify it
- The source also sharpens feedback handling:
  - ask for advice from intended customers and trusted peers
  - ignore critics who universalize their own taste into `no one wants this`
  - public reviews are often more emotionally sticky than strategically useful
- Free content is given a stronger business role:
  - free can build trust and attention without being a surrender of value
  - many beneficiaries will never pay, but the contribution still matters
  - premium and free layers can coexist inside one trust-based model

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The source is strong on positioning and posture, but lighter on measurement, market research method, and how to validate willingness to pay before investing heavily.
- The anti-cheapness argument is useful as a corrective, but some categories do require low-cost access or high-volume economics to be viable.
- `Professionalism over authenticity` is clarifying, though it can become too rigid if it underestimates the trust value of selective warmth, candor, or human texture.
