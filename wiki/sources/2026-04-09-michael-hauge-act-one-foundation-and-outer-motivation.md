# Summary

This source adds a focused Act 1 diagnostic layer to the filmmaking branch. Michael Hauge argues that many screenplays fail early because they do not provide the protagonist with a clear visible goal, do not establish enough ordinary-life context to create empathy, or rush too quickly into the central plot before orientation and investment have formed. Its strongest durable contribution to the vault is the emphasis on `outer motivation` as the visible finish line that keeps the story coherent.

# Key Claims

- A screenplay needs a clearly visible protagonist goal.
- The audience should first understand the protagonist's ordinary-life context before the story escalates.
- Empathy should arrive before heavy psychological layering or flaw exposition.
- Act 1 should build toward the central pursuit rather than leap there too abruptly.
- Early overcomplication weakens narrative clarity.

# Important Details

- Hauge frames the protagonist's goal as an external, visible finish line that can be stated simply.
- He argues that many story problems trace back to the absence of a clear outer motivation.
- The source treats everyday-life setup as an empathy engine, not as dead exposition.
- `Don't rush the story` is presented as an anti-burnout principle: over-accelerated openings can exhaust the script's energy long before the ending.
- The advice favors narrative simplicity over early thematic clutter or subplot sprawl.
- Hauge's examples imply that even complex films remain legible because their foundation is straightforward.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The source is strongest on Act 1 clarity and setup, but less expansive on later-act complexity, subtext, or stylistic distinctiveness.
- Some fast-starting forms may use abbreviated ordinary-world setup without becoming weak, so the rule should be treated as a function test rather than a stopwatch law.
- The source privileges visible external goals, which may fit some genres more naturally than others.
