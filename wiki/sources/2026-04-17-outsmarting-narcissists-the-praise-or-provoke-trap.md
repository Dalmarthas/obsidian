# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a Jefferson Fisher video on dealing with people who show strong narcissistic behaviors. Its strongest additions to the vault are a `praise or provoke` model for manipulative interaction, a clearer rule that the goal is to stop playing rather than to win or repair the person, and a practical toolkit of neutral micro-responses, tight contact limits, and audience-based reframing for situations where full avoidance is not realistic. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-17-outsmarting-narcissists-the-praise-or-provoke-trap|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Strong narcissistic behavior can turn interaction into a rigged `praise or provoke` game organized around control and emotional supply.
- In these dynamics, explaining feelings or trying to force empathy usually deepens the trap rather than producing accountability.
- The realistic goal is often to disengage from the game while still functioning in unavoidable contact.
- Short flat phrases such as `Noted`, `Okay`, and `Good to know` can preserve control because they give little emotional fuel and little material to twist.
- Keeping interactions short in time and depth is often more effective than trying to produce a breakthrough conversation.
- People driven by image may respond more to audience pressure or social optics than to direct personal appeals.

# Important Details

- The source's core model is simple but strong:
  - if you praise them, they feed on admiration
  - if you do not, they may provoke you to get emotional intensity another way
  - either lane keeps you investing attention and energy into a rigged interaction
- It adds an important realism constraint:
  - `just never talk to them again` is often not usable advice
  - the source is built for bosses, family, partners, and other unavoidable contact
  - the practical win is reduced emotional capture, not moral transformation
- Its strongest live tactic is the flat neutral micro-response:
  - `Noted`
  - `Okay`
  - `Interesting`
  - `Good to know`
  - the value is not cleverness but low-reactivity and low-twistability
- The source also adds a stronger contact-management rule:
  - brief contact
  - neutral response
  - exit
  - this matters because depth itself can become fuel in manipulative dynamics
- A distinctive addition is the `audience` lever:
  - some narcissistic people care more about how they appear to others than about how they affect you
  - references to colleagues, friends, juries, or `what other people would think` can shift tone faster than direct emotional appeals
  - the source warns this is often image management, not empathy
- It also makes a diagnostic distinction:
  - narcissistic behaviors are not identical to a clinical diagnosis
  - the practical separator is capacity for empathy, accountability, and sustained change

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a transcript or primary capture.
- The source offers communication strategy, not clinical diagnosis or therapy guidance.
- Audience pressure and low-reactivity tactics may be useful, but they do not make high-risk or abusive situations safe.
- The source is strongest for recurring manipulative contact, not for situations requiring legal, therapeutic, HR, or physical-safety intervention.
