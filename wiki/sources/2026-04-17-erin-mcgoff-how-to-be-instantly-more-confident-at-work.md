# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of a conversation between Erin McGoff and Jefferson Fisher on workplace confidence under emotionally loaded but common professional situations. Its strongest additions to the vault are confidence as self-talk discipline, Erin McGoff's `10/90 rule` for weakness questions, collaborative raise framing, graceful quitting as long-horizon reputation management, and passive-aggression handling through curiosity rather than escalation. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-17-erin-mcgoff-how-to-be-instantly-more-confident-at-work|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Workplace confidence starts upstream in self-talk rather than only in external delivery tricks.
- Many work conversations hide a deeper decision criterion behind the stated question, so better answers speak to the issue behind the issue.
- The best answer to `What's your greatest weakness?` is not confession theater but self-awareness plus an improvement process.
- Passive-aggressive coworkers are often best handled through curious clarification rather than moral outrage or retaliatory tone.
- Quitting should usually be treated as a professional transition, not an emotional purge, because reputation and relationships compound across a career.
- Raise conversations work better when framed as collaborative business reasoning backed by market data rather than as ultimatum or grievance.

# Important Details

- Erin McGoff's confidence model is built on `be your own best friend`:
  - speak to yourself with realism plus compassion
  - interrupt self-critical stories that have weak evidence
  - make the kinder but still honest voice habitual enough that it becomes your default internal narrator
- The source's strongest interview contribution is the `10/90 rule`:
  - 10 percent: name a real but non-fatal weakness
  - 90 percent: explain what you are doing about it and what early change looks like
  - the goal is to demonstrate self-awareness, judgment, and coachability rather than flawlessness
- The source repeatedly emphasizes the `issue behind the issue`:
  - `greatest weakness` often really asks whether you are self-aware and workable
  - `raise` often asks whether increased cost is justified and sustainable
  - `quitting` often asks whether this relationship will stay constructive after you leave
- Its passive-aggression model is narrow but useful:
  - first separate annoyance from real work interference
  - then use clarifying questions such as `Did you mean that genuinely, or were you hinting at something?`
  - keep the tone genuinely curious so the other person must either own the hint or retreat from it
- The quitting section is more strategic than emotional:
  - schedule a short direct conversation
  - leave with dignity and appreciation where possible
  - frame the move as part of a longer career path rather than as a dramatic rejection of one employer
- The compensation section is collaborative and managerial:
  - gather market data
  - think from your manager's constraints and incentives
  - enroll them as your advocate rather than cornering them as your obstacle
  - use `and` rather than `but` to hold both loyalty and unsustainability in the same sentence
- The source also contributes a simple confidence-language upgrade:
  - swap `I think` or `I believe` for stronger phrases like `I'm confident`
  - pair that with calm delivery so confidence sounds settled rather than inflated

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a transcript or primary capture.
- The source is broad and practical, but some of its advice is heuristic rather than evidence-heavy, especially around passive-aggression interpretation and quitting tone.
- `Be your own best friend` is useful, but without stronger structure it can stay inspirational rather than diagnostic for deeper confidence problems.
- The source is strongest for common white-collar work interactions, not for severe power abuse, legal risk, or highly adversarial organizations.
- Collaborative raise and quitting language is often wise, but the source leaves open how much tone should shift when the employer is clearly exploitative or acting in bad faith.
