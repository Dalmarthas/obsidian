# Summary

This source argues that interview performance depends less on improvisational brilliance than on deliberate preparation for predictable moments, especially the opening `Tell me about yourself` question. Its strongest addition to the vault is the `origin story` method: a short personal narrative that demonstrates a role-relevant trait, builds rapport quickly, and explicitly bridges that trait to why the candidate fits the target role. The source therefore sits at the overlap of career design and communication, translating preparation, specificity, and storytelling into a practical interview tactic.

# Key Claims

- Interview `luck` is mostly preparation meeting opportunity, not random charisma.
- The most predictable questions in interviews should never be answered cold.
- `Tell me about yourself` is a high-leverage opener that should be answered with a prepared story rather than with generic adjectives.
- Interview stories work best when they reveal character through evidence and then tie that identity directly to the role.
- Early rapport changes the emotional tone of the interview and can shift it from interrogation toward conversation.

# Important Details

- The source frames interviews as predictable enough to script around:
  - `Tell me about yourself`
  - strengths and weaknesses
  - why this company
  - where you see yourself in five years
  - why they should hire you
  - conflict handling
  - greatest achievement
  - why you are leaving
  - stress handling
  - what questions you have
- The main failure mode is adjective-based self-description:
  - `hardworking`
  - `honest`
  - `punctual`
  - `perfectionist`
  These are treated as weak because they are abstract, interchangeable, and unsupported by evidence.
- The alternative is an `origin story`:
  - a two to three minute personal episode
  - selective rather than comprehensive
  - emotionally engaging enough to be memorable
  - strategically tied to traits the role actually requires
- The source's Apple `head of innovation` example demonstrates the underlying pattern:
  - tell a personal story that shows a repeated trait
  - name the trait in practical terms
  - connect it directly to what the company needs
- The source treats rapport as functional rather than ornamental:
  - light humor can relax the room
  - personal detail can humanize the candidate
  - a more conversational room usually favors the prepared candidate who can keep linking story to fit
- The deeper communication principle is `replace self-description with evidence-backed narrative`.

# Tensions / Caveats

- The source is pragmatic and useful, but its `almost guarantees` framing is obviously overstated.
- Storytelling can improve recall and rapport, but it can also look manipulative, immature, or culturally miscalibrated if the humor or vulnerability level is wrong for the context.
- The source is strongest on the opening narrative and less developed on follow-up probing, technical screens, or interviews where concise analytical answers matter more than personal storytelling.
- Career changers may need more help than the source gives in identifying which personal episodes credibly bridge into a new role.
