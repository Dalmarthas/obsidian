# Summary

This source deepens the vault's chess branch by treating trades as a strategic and tactical decision rather than a simplifying reflex. John Bartholomew's main contribution is disciplined trade logic: exchange only when the result is clearly neutral or favorable, and judge the trade by its effect on structure, activity, tactical resources, and who controls the resulting flow of the position.

# Key Claims

- Every trade should answer the question: what does this achieve?
- Trades are justified by tactical necessity, structural gain, activity improvement, or long-term positional benefit, not by vague desire for simplification.
- The value of a trade depends on position-specific features such as open lines, bishop-versus-knight balance, undefended pieces, and pawn structure.
- In-between moves and tactical details can completely change the evaluation of an apparently straightforward exchange.
- Many club-level errors come from trading automatically for clarity or equality rather than on principled terms.

# Important Details

- The source emphasizes that players should try to make trades happen on terms that benefit their own structure or activity.
- Bishop-versus-knight evaluation appears here as a practical rule-of-thumb rather than a rigid law: bishops often improve in open positions, but the real decision remains concrete.
- Pawn structure is treated as one of the most important outputs of trading decisions, not a side issue.
- Trade sequences should be checked for zwischenzug resources, tactical intermezzos, and hidden defensive or offensive possibilities.
- Post-game review should ask not only whether a trade lost material, but whether it worsened long-term piece quality or structural prospects.

# Tensions / Caveats
