# Summary

This source is a dialogue-focused roundtable on how screenwriters make spoken scenes feel sharp, dramatic, and playable instead of expositional or flat. Its strongest contribution to the vault is a practical hierarchy: visual storytelling comes first, dialogue supports scene intention rather than carrying the whole story, and the best lines emerge through brevity, subtext, character-specific voice, and repeated read-aloud revision.

# Key Claims

- Dialogue should support the story rather than perform the full narrative job by itself.
- Exposition is usually stronger when shifted into action, visuals, staging, or conflict instead of being spoken directly.
- Strong dialogue is brief, pressure-driven, and cut harder than most first drafts expect.
- Subtext and misunderstanding make conversations more dramatic than literal self-explanation.
- Character voice should emerge through rhythm, vocabulary, pet phrases, and differing modes of response.
- Dialogue quality improves through rewriting, table reads, and oral testing rather than through first-draft inspiration.

# Important Details

- The source recommends cutting at least 20 percent of dialogue in revision to remove redundancy and over-explanation.
- It treats scene objective as a dialogue filter: lines should come from what the character wants, not what the writer needs to explain.
- `Talking heads` scenes are framed as a warning sign that the visual and dramatic layer has gone thin.
- Public and private objectives, hidden feelings, and deliberate misunderstanding create stronger exchanges.
- Historical dialogue need not be archaeologically exact, but it should feel texturally appropriate to the time.
- The source also distinguishes memorable `bumper sticker` lines from empty cleverness: a line sticks when it is compact and dramatically earned.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary of a multi-voice discussion rather than a fully attributed transcript.
- Some advice is broad craft consensus rather than a clearly isolated individual method.
- The raw file contains encoding noise in some punctuation and symbols, but the core ideas remain recoverable.
