# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Andrew Huberman's morning routine. Its strongest contribution to the vault is not a celebrity schedule but a compact `day-start` framework: use morning light, hydration, delayed caffeine, early cognitively hard work, and a repeatable physical-training rhythm to shape alertness, mood, and focus for the rest of the day. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-andrew-huberman-morning-routine|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Morning outdoor light is a major lever for circadian timing, alertness, and mood regulation.
- Delaying caffeine after waking may reduce later crashes by allowing adenosine clearance first.
- Early distraction-free cognitive work improves focus and carries benefits into the rest of the day.
- Pairing the morning with a physically demanding task supports energy, discipline, and overall health.
- Hydration and electrolytes are treated as low-friction performance supports.
- A good morning routine is simple, repeatable, and adaptable rather than perfect.

# Important Details

- Light exposure is the routine's anchor:
  - get outdoor light within the first three hours after waking
  - avoid relying on indoor light when sunlight is available
  - cloud cover changes the required duration
- The caffeine layer is framed mechanistically:
  - wait roughly 90 to 120 minutes after waking
  - use water and possibly electrolytes first
  - the goal is steadier energy later, not maximal instant stimulation
- The focus layer is explicit:
  - do one mentally demanding task early
  - avoid email and social media before it
  - protect that block from digital scatter
- The movement layer is structured but not exotic:
  - resistance training on some days
  - lighter cardio or rehabilitation movement on others
  - minimal phone use during training
- Meal and recovery timing appear as secondary supports:
  - first meal often follows training
  - midday nap or Yoga Nidra can function as an energy reset
- The source also implies a sequencing logic:
  - biology first
  - then deep work
  - then training and meals
  - with distraction management acting as protection around all of it

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary, not a primary transcript or research review.
- The source presents a coherent routine, but several details such as caffeine delay magnitude, light duration, and nap effects are context-sensitive and not equally settled for every person.
- The routine is easier to endorse than to apply for parents, shift workers, commuters, or people with little schedule control.
- Some advice may function better as heuristic than as universal prescription, especially around meal size and training timing.
