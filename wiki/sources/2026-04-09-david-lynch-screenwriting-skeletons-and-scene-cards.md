# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of David Lynch explaining a more direct, practical screenwriting method than the earlier vault source emphasized. It focuses on translating ideas into words, using early scripts as skeletal memory tools, and organizing a feature through simple 3x5 scene cards before elaborating the draft. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-david-lynch-screenwriting-skeletons-and-scene-cards|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Screenwriting begins by getting the idea out of the head and into words before it fades.
- Early drafts do not need polish; they can function as `skeletons` that preserve the core form of the project.
- A screenplay can be planned effectively through one-card-per-scene indexing rather than through immediate full-scene drafting.
- Around 70 scenes is presented as a practical feature-length benchmark, not as a hard law.
- Common sense and intuitive clarity matter as much as formal methodology.

# Important Details

- The source adds a more procedural Lynch layer than the earlier creativity-focused summary:
  - writing is not only catching ideas but fixing them in a usable form
  - the earliest written form can stay minimal if it preserves recall
- Frank Daniel's method is the operational centerpiece:
  - use 3x5 cards
  - assign one scene or scene heading per card
  - lay out the whole film as a visible sequence of units
  - reorder or refine the sequence before committing to dense scene writing
- The source frames structure as a support for creativity rather than a rival to it:
  - cards make shape visible
  - minimal headings reduce friction
  - iteration stays cheap while the story is still fluid
- The `70 scenes` note works as a scale heuristic:
  - it gives the writer a practical sense of feature size
  - it should not be mistaken for a universal rule

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The raw text has encoding noise, so some phrasing is likely cleaner in intent than in capture.
- The `70 scenes` benchmark is a heuristic, not a universal structural requirement.
- Lynch's claim that screenwriting is guided by common sense may understate how difficult dramatic clarity becomes in practice.
