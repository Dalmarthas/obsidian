# Summary

This article frames a screenplay as a production document rather than prose and argues that early discipline matters more than improvising a draft. Its five practical rules are: read finished scripts, outline before drafting, respect structure, learn screenplay formatting early, and avoid overspending on software. Source: [[raw/inbox/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- A screenplay should be treated as a blueprint for a visual production, not as prose written mainly to be read.
- Reading produced scripts is one of the fastest ways to internalize how a finished screenplay works.
- Outlining before drafting reduces friction and improves payoffs because setup requires knowing where the story is going.
- Classical structure, especially the three-act model, is a useful constraint rather than an enemy of originality.
- Correct formatting should be learned before drafting because retrofitting format later creates avoidable rework.
- Free or cheaper tools can handle early screenwriting needs; expensive software is not required to start.

# Important Details

- The article treats the three-act structure as a practical default:
  - Act One sets up character, world, and story direction.
  - Act Two contains the main conflict.
  - Act Three resolves the dramatic question.
- It also presents a nine-point heuristic for major story beats, including the inciting incident, break into act two, midpoint, all-is-lost moment, second turning point, and resolution.
- The author uses *Star Wars* and *Pitch Black* as examples of how conventional structure can still support memorable stories.
- Tool recommendations in the piece include Celtx and Scripped, while David Trottier's *The Screenwriter's Bible* is recommended for formatting questions.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is one practitioner's advice rather than a comprehensive theory of screenwriting.
- The article was published on `2010-01-08`, so specific software recommendations may now be stale.
- The source strongly favors classical feature-film structure; experimental or non-classical work may diverge.
