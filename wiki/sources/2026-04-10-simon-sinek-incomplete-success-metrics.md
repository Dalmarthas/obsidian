# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Simon Sinek arguing that traditional measures of success are not wrong but incomplete. Its strongest additions to the vault are a clearer `success metrics` concept built around momentum and trust, a sharper warning against rewarding outcomes without regard to process, and a stronger bridge between infinite-game thinking and the practical design of incentives, scoreboards, and recognition systems. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-simon-sinek-incomplete-success-metrics|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Profit, revenue, rankings, and target achievement are incomplete measures of success when used alone.
- Momentum matters because steady progress, morale stability, and growing capability can be healthier than erratic short-term spikes.
- Trust should be treated as a real success metric rather than a soft afterthought.
- Reward systems that ignore `how` results are achieved tend to normalize toxicity, burnout, or manipulation.
- Sustainable success depends on incentives that honor integrity, collaboration, and long-term strength alongside visible output.
- Vanity signals can create false confidence because public labels and rankings do not necessarily reflect durable value.

# Important Details

- The source adds a useful distinction between:
  - necessary metrics
  - sufficient metrics
- Its point is not to abandon numbers, but to widen what gets counted so that the scoreboard does not quietly reward organizational self-harm.
- Momentum is the most distinct new layer in this source:
  - not just present performance
  - but whether the person or organization is building capability, morale, and direction in a sustainable way
- The overlap with the earlier Simon Sinek trust source is strong but newly operational here:
  - trust is not only a personnel preference
  - it should shape what the institution measures, promotes, and rewards
- The source also sharpens the process-versus-outcome distinction:
  - outcomes still matter
  - but the method, consistency, and cultural cost of achieving them matter too
- Vanity-metric critique matters because it shows how systems can mistake manipulated signals for real demand or real contribution.
- The strongest reusable organizational move is:
  - keep result metrics
  - add momentum and trust indicators
  - review the `how` alongside the `what`
  - redesign incentives when they reward visible numbers but hidden damage

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a larger primary-source Sinek branch.
- The source is strongest diagnostically and weaker on precise operational definitions for momentum, trust, or culture health.
- `Measure trust` can become shallow quickly if teams replace behavior with vague popularity scoring.
- Some environments do need hard outcome pressure, so the challenge is not anti-metric thinking but broader metric design.
- Momentum can also be romanticized if it becomes an excuse for permanently underperforming results.
