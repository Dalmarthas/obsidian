# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Clayton Christensen on the drivers of long-term economic and corporate growth. Its strongest additions to the vault are a durable `jobs to be done` lens, a four-part innovation typology that separates sustaining, disruptive, efficiency, and still-potential innovations, and a sharp warning that common finance metrics can systematically starve the kinds of bets that actually create new growth. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-10-clayton-christensen-sustainable-growth-and-jobs-to-be-done|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Growth comes from innovation that solves real jobs in customers' lives rather than from improvement theater or cost cutting alone.
- Managers always act from theories of causality, whether they recognize those theories or not.
- Sustaining innovation is necessary but usually insufficient for generating new long-term growth.
- Disruption works by making something more accessible and affordable to non-consumers or overlooked segments, not just by offering better technology.
- Efficiency innovations generate cash but can weaken long-term growth if they become the only game companies know how to play.
- Metrics such as RONA and IRR can bias organizations toward safer, shorter-horizon decisions and away from genuinely growth-generating innovation.

# Important Details

- The source organizes innovation into four types:
  - `potential innovations`
    ideas whose viability depends on whether the underlying job is real and important
  - `sustaining innovations`
    improvements that help incumbents compete inside existing markets
  - `disruptive innovations`
    new business models that make solutions accessible to people previously excluded or overserved by the current market
  - `efficiency innovations`
    changes that free cash or reduce cost but do not necessarily create fresh demand
- `Jobs to be done` is the central interpretive tool:
  - focus less on customer attributes
  - focus more on the situation, struggle, and progress the customer is trying to make
- The milkshake example matters because it shows that the stable object is not the product category but the job in the user's life.
- The source argues that disruption is not mostly a technology story:
  - business model design matters more than technical superiority alone
  - incumbents often ignore disruptive opportunities because the early economics look unattractive by existing standards
- The metrics critique is especially sharp:
  - ratio-based finance can reward low-risk efficiency moves
  - those same metrics can make disruptive bets appear irrational even when they are necessary for long-term growth
- The source extends this logic beyond firms:
  - economies can stagnate when capital allocation increasingly favors efficiency over market-creating innovation
  - personal lives can drift for similar reasons when people optimize what pays back quickly instead of what matters most over time

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary Christensen text or lecture transcript.
- `Jobs to be done` is powerful, but it can become empty jargon if teams stop doing real observation and reduce it to rebranded segmentation.
- Disruption language is often overused; not every cheaper or newer offering is actually disruptive in Christensen's sense.
- Efficiency innovation is not bad by itself. The problem is portfolio imbalance and the false belief that efficiency alone can sustain broad-based growth.
