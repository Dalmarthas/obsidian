# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Jony Ive and Larry Ellison reflecting on Steve Jobs's working style. Its strongest additions to the vault are a sharper definition of `real focus` as saying no to ideas you genuinely love, a stronger end-to-end user-experience model that includes purchase, packaging, and onboarding rather than product features alone, and a more explicit editorial-execution model where the rare skill is turning good ideas into finished, coherent products through relentless iteration. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-12-all-you-need-to-work-like-steve-jobs|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Real focus means refusing even excellent or exciting ideas when they threaten the higher-priority product direction.
- Product quality includes the entire user journey, not only the object or software in isolation.
- Great products come less from having ideas than from iterating hard enough to turn good ideas into finished systems.
- Jobs's distinctive strength combined aesthetic taste, technical grasp, and editorial judgment across the whole stack.
- Obsessive intensity can contribute to extraordinary execution, but it is psychologically costly and not safely generalizable as a universal model.

# Important Details

- The source sharpens the meaning of sacrifice:
  - saying no matters only when the rejected option is genuinely attractive
  - `fake sacrifices` do not prove focus
  - real focus therefore has an emotional price, not just a scheduling benefit
- It deepens the end-to-end systems layer:
  - store experience, payment flow, packaging, activation, software behavior, and network connection all count as one product
  - the product is the full journey, not the device alone
  - quality therefore depends on coherence across physical, digital, and service touchpoints
- The execution layer is unusually strong:
  - ideas are common
  - productizing them is rare
  - Jobs is framed as an editor who kept reviewing iterations until the whole thing became `insanely great`
- The source also adds a persuadable-editor model:
  - Jobs could change his mind quickly when a better argument won
  - what mattered was not consistency of ego but consistency of quality bar
  - this makes his centralization slightly more nuanced than pure stubbornness
- Its psychological layer needs caution:
  - obsessive-compulsive drive is described as part of the engine
  - but the page itself implies that intensity without Jobs's unusual synthesis of taste, system sense, and judgment would be easy to copy badly

# Tensions / Caveats

- This source is mediated through admirers and colleagues, so it naturally risks heroizing Jobs's intensity and standards.
- The obsessive-work model is vivid, but the page gives less attention to burnout, collateral damage, or selection effects than the vault should preserve.
- The `follow the better idea instantly` claim is valuable, but it sits alongside many stories of Jobs's harshness, so the branch should not flatten this into a sanitized portrait.
- The source is strongest on execution, product coherence, and editorial standards, not on proving that obsessive intensity is broadly sustainable or desirable.
