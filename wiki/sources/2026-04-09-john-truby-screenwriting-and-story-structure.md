# Summary

John Truby presents storytelling for film and television as a deep-structure craft rather than a beat-sheet game. In this source, genre is both the market language and the creative foundation of story design, character weakness is the engine of plot, and real structure comes from an internally coherent story anatomy rather than surface obedience to formulas. Truby's strongest operational claim is process-oriented: premise testing, logline work, scene-sequence planning, and structural critique should happen before expensive drafting and before dialogue polish.

# Key Claims

- Genre is not cosmetic packaging; it is the underlying story grammar that film and television markets understand and buy.
- Writers should not merely satisfy genre beats. They should transcend genre through combination, surprise, and deeper originality.
- Structure matters, but reducing structure to rigid three-act formulas is misleading.
- Character weakness is the core structural engine because plot should force the protagonist to confront what is broken or missing in them.
- Preparation solves structural problems more cheaply than rewriting after pages already exist.
- Serious writers need structural criticism from informed readers rather than praise or surface-level notes.

# Important Details

- The source attributes to Truby both a seven-step core model and a fuller 22-step anatomy for shaping stories beyond shallow beat heuristics.
- Premise testing is framed as a practical gate before drafting: the story should have a strong desire line, a robust opponent, and enough conflict to sustain feature length.
- Loglines and scene sequences are treated as serious pre-draft tools, not optional accessories.
- Early rewrites should focus on structure, not line-level dialogue polish.
- The source also includes industry claims: breaking in is harder because paid development has shrunk, and writing quality has to carry more of the burden.
- Television is presented as a medium currently capable of more ambitious writing than film, though that claim is context-bound.

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript or direct reading of Truby's books.
- The source may overstate the contrast between `deep structure` and practical act heuristics; elsewhere in the vault, act scaffolds are treated as useful but non-exclusive tools.
- The industry and television-quality claims are more time-sensitive and contestable than the craft claims.
