# Summary

This source is a secondary summary of Patrick Lencioni's TEDx talk on the `ideal team player`. Its strongest additions to the vault are a compact three-virtue model for team effectiveness, memorable dysfunction profiles built from missing one virtue at a time, and a simple coaching-and-selection process that makes teamwork character discussable rather than vague. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-20-are-you-an-ideal-team-player|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- In a team-based world, technical skill alone is increasingly insufficient; behavioral virtues now decide much of long-run success.
- The ideal team player combines three traits at once: humility, hunger, and people-smarts.
- Missing one of those traits creates predictable failure modes even when the other two are present.
- Humility is the central virtue because the most dangerous team failures often come from capable but self-oriented people.
- Emotional intelligence in teams is not IQ but awareness of interpersonal impact.
- Teams can improve meaningfully through explicit self-diagnosis, peer feedback, and small behavioral experiments around the weakest trait.
- Hiring and promotion should weigh these virtues alongside credentials and technical ability.

# Important Details

- The source's core model is deliberately simple:
  - humble means genuinely other-centered without false modesty
  - hungry means hard-working and self-driven without collapsing identity into workaholism
  - smart means people-smart rather than academically smart
  - all three are needed simultaneously for reliable team contribution
- It adds three highly reusable dysfunctional profiles:
  - `accidental mess maker`: humble and hungry, but not people-smart
  - `lovable slacker`: humble and smart, but not hungry
  - `skillful politician`: hungry and smart, but not humble
- The `skillful politician` is treated as the most dangerous type:
  - they often interview well
  - they perform alignment convincingly
  - but they use social intelligence and ambition for self-advancement rather than team benefit
  - by the time the organization detects the pattern, trust and collateral damage may already be significant
- The source sharpens humility in an important way:
  - humility is not self-erasure or denial of real ability
  - under-claiming talent can still distort team functioning
  - the healthier target is accurate self-placement without ego-centrality
- The hunger branch also gets a useful boundary:
  - hunger is about contribution standard and initiative
  - not compulsive overwork or deriving identity entirely from output
  - this helps separate healthy drive from workaholic distortion
- The source is unusually practical about development:
  - ask each person to rank their strongest and weakest of the three traits
  - have them explain their weakest area
  - turn teammates and family into coaches who can point out drift
  - choose small, visible behavior changes rather than abstract intention
- It also makes a broader institutional claim:
  - schools, families, and companies all over-reward measurable individual accomplishment
  - and under-train the teamwork behaviors that modern work increasingly depends on

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary based more on consulting logic and vivid examples than on strong psychometric or longitudinal evidence.
- The humility branch is useful, but it can be misused as a culture-coded demand for self-suppression if not behaviorally defined.
- The hunger branch is useful, but organizations may weaponize it if they confuse healthy initiative with permanent overextension.
- The people-smart branch is practical, but it still risks being confused with charm or social fluency unless tied to actual impact on others.
- The model is a strong diagnostic shorthand, but it should likely be paired with richer evaluation methods in real hiring or promotion systems.
