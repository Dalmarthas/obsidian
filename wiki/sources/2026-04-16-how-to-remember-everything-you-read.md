# Summary

This source argues that forgetting after reading is usually a digestion failure, not merely a consumption problem. Its main contribution is the `PACER` system, which sorts information into Procedural, Analogous, Conceptual, Evidence, and Reference, with each category demanding a different post-reading process such as practice, critique, mapping, rehearsal, or spaced recall. Compared with the vault's earlier learning pages, this source adds a sharper intake-to-processing workflow and a more explicit rule for when to stop consuming and start digesting.

# Key Claims

- Reading more does not reliably produce retention unless the material is processed in the right way.
- The goal is not literal memory of everything read, but selective retention of what is needed for reasoning, application, and problem-solving.
- Different kinds of information need different encoding methods; a single generic note-taking style is inefficient.
- Procedural, analogous, and conceptual information deserve more attention during reading than evidence and reference details.
- If the proper digestion step cannot be done yet, further consumption often becomes counterproductive.

# Important Details

- The source distinguishes two stages of learning:
  - consumption: reading, watching, listening
  - digestion: transforming material into durable, usable knowledge
- The `PACER` categories are:
  - Procedural: how-to knowledge that should be practiced immediately
  - Analogous: similarity relations that should be actively critiqued
  - Conceptual: explanatory structure that should be mapped nonlinearly
  - Evidence: supporting specifics that should be stored and later rehearsed in use
  - Reference: details that should be offloaded into spaced-repetition or external storage
- The source treats `analogous` information as a special high-value lens:
  - connect new material to prior knowledge
  - inspect where the analogy holds
  - inspect where it breaks
  - refine the analogy until it becomes useful rather than vague
- The `big three` are Procedural, Analogous, and Conceptual:
  - they create the foundation for real understanding
  - they support later reasoning and application
  - they should dominate actual reading-time digestion
- Evidence and Reference are treated as important but secondary:
  - store them quickly
  - review them later in targeted blocks
  - do not let them consume the main study session
- The method explicitly recommends external systems for detail storage:
  - concept maps
  - second-brain tools
  - flashcards
  - simple documents
- A practical rule runs through the whole source:
  - if you cannot perform the correct digestion step for what you are reading, stop reading or slow down

# Tensions / Caveats

- The source is operationally strong, but some claims such as `up to 90% forgetting` are cited vaguely rather than with strong visible evidence.
- The line between `Evidence` and `Reference` is acknowledged as fuzzy, so the system may require adaptation by domain.
- The source is persuasive about not memorizing details during reading, but some domains may still benefit from light immediate rehearsal inside the reading session.
- The method is strongest for serious study and may feel heavy for low-stakes exploratory reading where retention demands are lower.
