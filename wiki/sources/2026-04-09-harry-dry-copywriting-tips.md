# Summary

This is an English secondary summary of Harry Dry's copywriting advice. In this vault, the branch is strongest as a practical model for writing persuasive business language that is concrete, visualizable, falsifiable, and distinct to the product or brand. It also adds a useful process layer: write for the actual medium, revise aggressively, use facts as structure, and integrate feedback with design rather than treating writing as a detached text-only task. Raw source: [[raw/inbox/2026-04-09-harry-dry-copywriting-tips|Raw source]].

# Key Claims

- Great copy should pass three filters: it should be visualizable, falsifiable, and specific enough that only this brand or product could credibly say it.
- Concrete, image-forming language outperforms abstraction because people remember what they can picture.
- Facts are not dry support material; they are often the most effective raw material for memorable, persuasive writing.
- Good copy begins with audience state change: know what the reader thinks now, what they should think next, and what specific language can bridge that distance.
- Strong writing is iterative and format-aware: the page, layout, and design context should shape the writing process rather than receiving words only at the end.

# Important Details

- The source revolves around five reusable layers:
  - Harry Dry's three-rule filter for copy
  - concreteness over abstraction
  - fact-based communication
  - contrast and structure through before/after or us/them framing
  - revision in the final medium with feedback loops
- The three core tests are:
  - visualizable: the reader can picture it
  - falsifiable: the claim is concrete enough to be checked against reality
  - unique: the line says something competitors cannot easily copy
- The process layer includes:
  - understand current versus desired audience belief
  - write directly into the intended format where possible
  - use design tools or layout context early
  - create multiple versions and seek outside feedback
  - keep paragraphs short and structurally legible
- The source also adds a worldview layer:
  - quality is part of professional identity
  - AI can assist, but taste, conviction, and observed reality still separate strong copy from generic output
  - opinions should be anchored in facts, distinctions, or observable detail

# Tensions / Caveats

- This is a secondary summary, not a primary transcript or a full archive of Harry Dry's examples.
- The branch is strongest on crisp heuristics, but lighter on longer-form direct-response mechanics, pricing psychology, or detailed funnel context.
- The uniqueness rule is strategically strong, but it depends on honest differentiation; weak products cannot fully solve themselves with phrasing.
- The source strongly favors concreteness, but some categories still need abstraction, emotional framing, or symbolic language that the summary does not explore deeply.
