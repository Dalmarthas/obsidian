# What It Is

Elicitation is the practice of drawing out information through statements, assumptions, and narrative prompts instead of relying only on direct questions. In the current vault, the main idea is that sensitive information often appears more easily when the other person is invited to correct, clarify, or complete a plausible statement than when they are interrogated head-on.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the communication branch a durable alternative to the false choice between blunt questions and silence.
- It helps explain how disclosure can happen without turning the interaction into a visible extraction attempt.
- It creates a bridge between empathy, framing, and practical influence.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Chase Hughes source explicitly argues that the more sensitive the information, the fewer direct questions you should ask.
- The same source recommends soft, plausible statements that invite correction or elaboration rather than trigger defense.
- It also frames summary statements and light disbelief as ways to keep a person talking without making them feel cornered.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/calibrated-questions|Calibrated Questions]], elicitation is less direct and more oblique: it seeks disclosure through correction or completion rather than through explicit open-ended questioning.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/tactical-empathy|Tactical Empathy]], it is less about making someone feel understood and more about creating a low-resistance path for information to surface.
- Compared with the broader [[wiki/concepts/communication/effective-communication|Effective Communication]] branch, elicitation is a narrower high-sensitivity tool rather than a general communication style.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Elicitation can become manipulative if it is used to hide extraction intent instead of creating safer conversation.
- It can also become inaccurate if the prompting statement is clumsy, implausible, or overconfident.
- The vault still needs more comparison with investigative interviewing, counseling, and negotiation practice that separates ethical elicitation from covert pressure.
