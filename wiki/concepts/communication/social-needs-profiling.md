# What It Is

Social needs profiling is the attempt to infer which identity need or status hunger most strongly organizes a person's behavior, language, and reactivity, then communicate through that need rather than only through surface facts. In the current vault, Chase Hughes frames these recurring needs as things like significance, acceptance, approval, intelligence, pity, and strength or power.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the communication branch a more precise way to talk about what people are actually trying to protect or feed in conversation.
- It can improve persuasion by shifting the message from `what is true` to `what this person most needs to hear about themselves`.
- It also sharpens the ethical question because targeting identity needs can help, manipulate, or exploit depending on how it is used.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Chase Hughes source says repeated speech patterns often reveal a dominant social need.
- Achievement and legacy language are treated as possible significance cues.
- Group-belonging language is treated as a possible acceptance cue.
- competence-display language is treated as a possible intelligence cue.
- repeated hardship identity can be read as a pity-oriented profile.
- The source treats these needs as doors into deeper fears, which then shape influence strategy.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/attunement|Attunement]], social-needs profiling is less about broad perspective-taking and more about identifying the self-story the other person most wants reinforced.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/persuasion|Persuasion]], it is more diagnostic and identity-centered than most other influence pages in the vault.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/tactical-empathy|Tactical Empathy]], it moves from understanding feelings toward inferring the need structure beneath them.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Need typologies can be insightful, but they also tempt the user into reductionism and amateur personality diagnosis.
- The source provides high practical confidence but relatively weak evidence for universal categories.
- The vault still needs counter-models from evidence-based psychology and counseling traditions that distinguish flexible needs from fixed profiles.
