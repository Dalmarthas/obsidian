# What It Is

Intention and impact is the distinction between what someone meant to do and what their actions actually did to other people. In the current source base, Welteroth treats this as a practical accountability rule: when harm is involved, lead with impact, not with self-exonerating explanation.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents defensive communication from closing off repair before it begins.
- It helps people operating publicly or across difference avoid confusing sincerity with harmlessness.
- It gives the vault a portable accountability concept that can apply well beyond career design.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Welteroth argues that `that wasn't my intention` is usually the wrong first move when people are hurt.
- The first responsibility is to acknowledge the impact and show understanding of why it was harmful.
- Only after people feel heard does explanation of intention become productive.
- The broader lesson is that authentic voice does not remove the need for filters, humility, and responsibility.
- The current Jefferson Fisher source adds a live-dialogue version of the same principle: fake apologies often fail by talking about the listener's feelings instead of the speaker's action.
- That source also adds a direct corrective move: push the conversation back from emotional deflection to behavioral ownership.
- The new dismissiveness source extends the same accountability branch from apology failure into everyday invalidation:
  - `it was just a joke`, `don't take it personally`, and `don't be so sensitive` all function as attempts to overrule the listener's interpretation of impact
  - `I get to decide that` pushes authority over felt impact back to the listener
  - `I'm sensing...` turns the exchange away from label-defense and back toward observed behavior and appropriateness

# Source Comparisons

- Welteroth frames the concept as a moral and professional accountability rule: impact has to be acknowledged before self-exoneration enters.
- Jefferson Fisher frames it as conversational defense against manipulation: if someone apologizes for your feelings or hides behind `if`, the accountability structure itself has broken.
- The newer dismissiveness source extends Fisher's side further: not all dodging happens in apologies; much of it happens through casual minimization, humor cover, and identity labels like `too sensitive`.
- Together they make the concept more usable across contexts: Welteroth explains the principle, Fisher shows what it sounds like when the principle is being dodged in real time.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- An impact-first norm can become shallow if it turns into ritual apology without actual learning or structural change.
- Some situations involve contested or ambiguous harms, so accountability still requires judgment rather than formula.
- The branch still needs stronger distinction between poor apology skills, active deflection, and genuinely coercive psychological manipulation.
- The new dismissiveness source sharpens another edge case: impact ownership matters, but the vault still needs better rules for when asserting your right to interpret harm should lead into repair, when it should become a firmer boundary, and when the interaction is too low-quality to keep negotiating at all.
