# What It Is

Concrete specificity is the communication principle that messages land better when they are vivid, checkable, and anchored in particular detail rather than abstract generalities. In this vault, the current branch treats it as a bridge between memorability and trust: people remember what they can picture, and they trust what sounds tied to reality.

# Why It Matters

- It upgrades clarity into memorability.
- It reduces empty language that sounds polished but says little.
- It gives both business writing and broader communication a reusable test for whether a claim is actually legible.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Harry Dry's branch argues that the reader should be able to visualize the line, check it against reality, and hear why it belongs uniquely to this product or brand.
- The source also says facts are often the strongest raw material for persuasive language because they provide both structure and credibility.
- Short paragraphs, contrast, and parallel structure help make specificity easier to perceive rather than burying it in noise.
- The current Johnny Harris source adds a visual version of the same rule: the anchor has to be concrete enough that the audience can grasp it immediately rather than decode it abstractly.
- It also sharpens the distinction between representative specificity and generic support visuals; the anchor works because it feels like the thing itself, not like illustrative filler.
- The Harris writing-process source adds a prose-level version from classic style: clear agents doing visible actions are stronger than abstract nouns naming large forces.
- It also links specificity to maps and icons: a complex historical or geopolitical explanation becomes trackable when viewers can see actors, places, motives, and movement.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/effective-communication|Effective Communication]], this page is more language-level and writing-specific.
- The current Johnny Harris source widens the concept from sentence-level specificity to image-level specificity:
  the explainer's first proof point should be intuitively legible and memorable on sight.
- The newer Harris source connects both levels: the sentence should be active and concrete, while the paired visual should make the action spatially or materially graspable.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/persuasion|Persuasion]], this page focuses less on context and timing and more on the quality of the claim itself.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]], this page is downstream: strategy identifies the point, while concrete specificity determines whether the point survives contact with an actual reader.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Concreteness is usually stronger than vagueness, but not every valuable message can be reduced to a purely falsifiable statement.
- Some categories rely on mood, aspiration, or symbolic meaning more than direct proof language, and the vault still needs better examples of that tension.
- The current branch is built from one compact source, so the principle is promising but not yet cross-validated by many writing sources.
- Concrete detail can clarify, but a vivid phrase or map icon can also overcondense messy realities if the fact-checking layer is weak.
