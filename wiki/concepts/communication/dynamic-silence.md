# What It Is

Dynamic silence is the deliberate use of a pause after a mirror, label, summary, or question so the other person has space to think, feel, and keep talking. In the current source base, Voss makes it an explicit negotiation instrument while Stephanopoulos shows a related interview version.

# Why It Matters

- It creates room for disclosure that constant talking destroys.
- It helps regulate emotional intensity without direct confrontation.
- It improves listening because the speaker can observe instead of rush.
- It turns absence of speech into an active part of communication design.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The detailed workplace Voss source treats silence as a core follow-up move after mirrors, labels, calibrated questions, and summaries.
- That source also says silence helps with anger because people often exhaust themselves when not met with verbal escalation.
- The earlier Voss source already presented dynamic silence as part of the negotiation stack rather than as conversational awkwardness.
- Stephanopoulos uses a softer public-accountability version: ask clearly, then let silence pressure the answer without theatrics.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Silence can feel punitive or passive-aggressive if tone and context are wrong.
- Some people will fill silence with useful truth; others will shut down.
- The technique depends heavily on emotional steadiness from the person using it.
