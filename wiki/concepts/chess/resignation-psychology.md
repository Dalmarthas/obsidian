# What It Is

Resignation psychology is the practical discipline of staying objectively engaged with the position instead of emotionally deciding the game is over too early.

# Why It Matters

Many players lose twice: first on the board, then in their head. This concept matters because hidden defensive resources, drawing chances, stalemate ideas, and opponent counter-blunders still exist in positions that feel emotionally lost.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-ben-finegold-blunders-in-chess]] is the clearest current vault source on the topic. It argues that players should not resign until they have searched seriously for saving resources and practical resistance.
- The source also links resignation psychology to the opposite failure mode: complacency in winning or equal positions, where relaxation becomes the decisive blunder.
- Its core practical claim is that results reward survival and conversion, not beauty points for recognizing that your position "should" be losing.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with the current John Bartholomew branch, this concept is less about specific tactical causes of blunders and more about the mental decision to keep fighting after errors occur.
- Compared with the current Gotham branch, it is less about move-selection heuristics and more about practical emotional resistance and non-collapse.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The branch still needs stronger guidance on when practical resistance becomes empty hope rather than disciplined resource-search.
- Later sources may justify splitting premature resignation, complacency, and defensive-resource search into more specific concepts.
