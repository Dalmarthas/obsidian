# What It Is

Development tempo is the pace at which a player brings useful forces into the game without wasting moves on premature repetitions, decorative pawn moves, or exchanges that relieve the opponent before coordination is complete.

# Why It Matters

Many opening problems are not about memorization but about time. A player who falls behind in development often becomes tactically vulnerable, struggles to coordinate, and makes bad exchanges because the position feels urgent.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-piece-and-pawn-coordination]] adds the clearest current statement of this concept by warning against moving the same piece twice without reason, making purposeless pawn moves, or trading while still behind in development.
- The source also adds a practical recovery method: when already worse-coordinated, use mini-goals to stabilize and catch up instead of forcing a premature solution.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-recurring-beginner-chess-mistakes]] reinforces the same theme with more concrete failure cases: repeated minor-piece advances, development lost to pawn-chasing, and the positional mistake of closing the position when the more active side should be opening it.
