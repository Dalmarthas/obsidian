# What It Is

Piece coordination is the alignment of development, protection, activity, and mobility so that pieces support one another rather than obstructing or abandoning one another.

# Why It Matters

Many beginner collapses are not deep calculation failures but coordination failures. Pieces become loose, block each other, or sit on squares that look active but make the whole position less functional.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-beginner-chess-mistakes]] highlights loose pieces, poor development sequence, and self-blocking setups as recurring causes of mistakes.
- The source also shows that premature pawn moves can damage coordination by shutting lines or making later plans harder to execute.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-essential-chess-principles]] adds the cleaner classical coordination layer: connect the rooks, prefer efficient development, and place pieces so activity is supported by the rest of the army rather than isolated from it.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-piece-and-pawn-coordination]] makes the relation between pieces and pawns more explicit: pawn moves should earn their place by helping development, structural aims, or activity, and good coordination often shows up first as the absence of loose, lopsided, or overworked pieces.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-recurring-beginner-chess-mistakes]] adds the repeated club-player failure modes: blocking your own bishop with careless pawn structure, jumping minor pieces into squares where they can be chased, and letting one side of the board become overdeveloped while the rest of the army lags.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-undefended-pieces]] tightens the safety side of coordination: a piece may look active, but if it is loose or under-defended, the whole position is often less coordinated than it appears.
