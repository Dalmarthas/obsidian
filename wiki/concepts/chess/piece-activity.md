# What It Is

Piece activity is the practical effectiveness of a piece in a specific position: how many useful squares it controls, how well it cooperates with the rest of the army, and how much pressure it creates relative to the structure and phase of the game.

# Why It Matters

Players often know abstract piece values but still place pieces badly. Activity explains why a bishop can dominate one game and underperform in another, why rooks need open lines, and why a king changes from a liability to a fighting piece in the endgame.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-essential-chess-principles]] adds the clearest current summary of activity-based placement: bishops thrive in open positions, knights in closed ones, rooks on open or half-open files, and kings become active endgame pieces.
- The source also frames activity as position-dependent rather than automatic, which keeps rules like "knights on the rim are dim" useful without pretending they decide every case alone.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The branch still needs more material on prophylaxis and how to restrict the opponent's activity, not just improve your own.
- Future sources may justify separating general activity from more specific themes such as bad bishops, rook lifts, or king centralization.
