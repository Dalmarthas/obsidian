# What It Is

Problem-first chess training is a study model where the core unit of improvement is solving many structured positions and short instructive games rather than consuming explanations, opening lines, or entertainment-first content.

# Why It Matters

This method tries to build real chess logic through repeated contact with positions. It trains calculation, visualization, tactical pattern recognition, and judgment under slow thinking conditions instead of relying on memorized answers or shallow familiarity.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-polgar-long-term-chess-improvement-plan]] presents the clearest current vault statement of the method: thousands of positions, concise solutions, and gradual progression through tactical, middlegame, and endgame material.
- The source argues that physical-board solving and writing answers before checking improve transfer to tournament play better than shallow speed-based puzzle habits.
- It also ties the method to a long-term, repeatable curriculum rather than one-off drills or quick-fix courses.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with the current Gotham branch, this concept is less about practical game heuristics and more about the study architecture that should build underlying skill over years.
- Compared with the current John Bartholomew branch, it is less diagnostic about specific beginner mistakes and more explicit about what the core training vehicle itself should be.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The branch still needs stronger direct evidence on how this method compares with annotated-game study, coaching, engine review, and adult-learning constraints.
- The source's de-emphasis on opening theory is useful as a corrective, but may be too strong if read as an absolute rule rather than a priority claim.
