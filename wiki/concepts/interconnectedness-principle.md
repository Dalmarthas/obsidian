# What It Is

The interconnectedness principle is the claim that ecological, social, and moral systems are linked tightly enough that they should not be treated as isolated problems. In the current source base, this principle begins in forest observation and then expands outward into conservation, poverty, education, health, consumption, and climate. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-conversation-jane-goodall-masterclass-complete-summary|Conversation Jane Goodall Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It prevents single-issue solutions from ignoring the real drivers of damage.
- It links local action to larger systems without making the problem too abstract to act on.
- It provides a durable systems-thinking concept for future environmental and social sources in the vault.
- It explains why conservation efforts often fail when they ignore human livelihood.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Forest ecosystems model the idea that every element affects others.
- Conservation must address health, education, agriculture, and family planning together.
- Consumer decisions connect personal behavior to corporate and ecological outcomes.
- Thinking only globally can create paralysis, so local action is used as an entry point into larger change.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Systems thinking can become so broad that it loses operational clarity.
- Local interventions need enough scaling logic to avoid remaining isolated successes.
