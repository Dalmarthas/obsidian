# What It Is

Growth mindset is the belief that meaningful abilities can be developed through learning, strategy, effort, feedback, and sustained practice. In this vault, it is not generic optimism. It is a way of interpreting difficulty that treats challenge as part of development rather than as proof of fixed limitation.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why some people become more effortful and adaptive under difficulty while others become more defensive and avoidant.
- It gives the vault a clearer concept for linking learning behavior, resilience, and culture design.
- It helps distinguish real development-oriented ambition from identity-protective performance.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Carol Dweck source defines the core contrast:
  - fixed mindset treats ability as static and challenge as threat
  - growth mindset treats ability as developable and challenge as part of learning
- The same source argues that praise language matters:
  - talent praise can make people more fragile and pedestal-dependent
  - process praise can reinforce experimentation, persistence, and strategy revision
- The source also adds the `power of yet` as a reusable framing move that keeps present limitation from collapsing into identity.
- The new locus-of-control source clarifies an adjacent but different mechanism: growth mindset asks whether ability can improve, while locus of control asks whether present actions can influence outcomes enough to justify effort.
- The source extends mindset beyond the individual:
  - growth cultures reward learning and experimentation
  - fixed cultures reward status protection, secrecy, and image management
- [[wiki/concepts/leadership/high-performance-mindset|High-Performance Mindset]] overlaps with the persistence side of this concept, but is broader and more performance-system oriented.
- [[wiki/concepts/leadership/organizational-culture|Organizational Culture]] overlaps with the institutional side, but is broader than mindset alone.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/high-performance-mindset|High-Performance Mindset]], growth mindset is narrower and more learning-theory driven. It is less about elite standards and more about how difficulty is interpreted.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/organizational-culture|Organizational Culture]], growth mindset is more specific: it explains one key axis along which a culture can become developmental or defensive.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/future-self-prioritization|Future-Self Prioritization]], growth mindset is less about long-term choice architecture and more about what people believe ability and failure mean in the moment of challenge.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/locus-of-control|Locus of Control]], growth mindset is more developmental and less causal-attributional: a person may believe skills can grow in principle yet still feel that their own actions have little leverage in a given environment.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Growth mindset can become empty ritual if leaders praise effort while ignoring weak strategy, poor teaching, or structural constraints.
- `False growth mindset` is a real risk when people claim the label without becoming more challenge-tolerant or feedback-capable.
- The strongest open question is how the vault should distinguish developmentally useful pressure from growth-language that merely repackages blame.
