# What It Is

An infinite-game mindset is the discipline of leading as if the real goal is to keep advancing a cause, preserve institutional vitality, and continue improving over time rather than to declare final victory over rivals. In this vault, the idea is not anti-performance or anti-metrics. It is a correction against treating temporary scoreboards as if they were the whole meaning of the work.

# Why It Matters

- It gives leaders a better frame for domains where there is no true finish line, only survival, adaptation, and stewardship.
- It protects organizations from mistaking short-term wins for long-term health.
- It makes purpose and values operational by forcing leaders to ask what the institution is actually trying to sustain and become.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Simon Sinek finite-versus-infinite source argues that business is structurally closer to an infinite game than to a contest with permanent winners.
- That source warns against arbitrary goals such as status-only market leadership when those goals are detached from a deeper cause.
- It also says leaders should filter tactical decisions through purpose and values before optimizing for narrow interests.
- The same source reframes competition:
  - rivals can be useful catalysts
  - but the strongest benchmark is often the organization's own previous standard
- Trust and belonging remain part of the model, not a separate issue:
  - infinite institutions need circles of safety
  - fear-based systems may still hit numbers but erode long-term commitment

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], this page is less about choosing a small set of priorities and more about choosing the time horizon and moral frame inside which those priorities make sense.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/framework-driven-action|Framework-Driven Action]], the infinite-game mindset is a higher-order filter: it asks whether the framework is oriented toward enduring health and purpose or only toward immediate advantage.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/trust-based-collaboration|Trust-Based Collaboration]], this page is more strategic and temporal. Trust-based collaboration explains how people work together; the infinite-game mindset explains what kind of future the collaboration is trying to protect.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/values-driven-companies|Values-Driven Companies]], this page is broader than values language alone. It includes values, but also legacy, endurance, self-competition, and resistance to arbitrary win conditions.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Infinite-minded rhetoric is easy to fake if compensation, promotion, and capital allocation still reward only short-term extraction.
- Some environments genuinely do require aggressive short-horizon decisions, so the challenge is integration rather than total rejection of finite tactics.
- A cause that is too vague can become decorative language instead of an operational standard.
- The main open question is how to tell whether a company's stated purpose is a real decision filter or a branding layer pasted over finite incentives.
