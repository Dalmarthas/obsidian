# What It Is

Organizational culture is the patterned behavior, expectation, language, and felt social reality that tells people what kind of place they are in and what will really be rewarded, tolerated, or punished there. In the current source base, culture is not a poster on the wall. It is what people say at dinner tables, what gets corrected, and what spreads if leadership looks away.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a company-level culture concept distinct from pure team-performance culture.
- It explains why strategy and values often fail in practice: the surrounding behavior system quietly overrides them.
- It makes culture measurable through behavior, not just through declared principles.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Schultz is the strongest current source: culture is imprinted through daily action, should be audited deliberately, and is visible in how employees talk about the company when leaders are not in the room.
- Schultz also links culture to open forums, no-retribution feedback, heritage storytelling, and swift correction of behavior that violates the company's purpose.
- Auriemma adds a competitive-team version where standards, accountability, and shared expectations become the operating norm rather than inspirational language.
- Klopp adds a club-culture version where togetherness extends from stars to staff, and where belonging, intensity, and local identity become part of the operating environment rather than post-match sentiment.
- Rhimes adds a fast creative-production version where culture determines whether a room generates real work or burns energy on ego and posturing.

# Source Comparisons

- Schultz is strongest on company-wide imprinting: culture is scaled behavior plus moral tone across a large organization.
- Auriemma is strongest on pressure-tested standards: culture becomes visible in what people accept during hard work and correction.
- Klopp is strongest on emotionally unified culture: the environment gains force when people feel the institution is theirs, not merely where they currently work or play.
- Rhimes is strongest on contribution norms: culture is the difference between useful collaboration and performative collaboration.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strong culture creates coherence, but it can also become rigidity if leaders confuse inherited norms with permanent truth.
- `Humanity` and `high standards` are both easy to say; the hard part is proving they can coexist when results are weak and patience is thin.
- Klopp's branch adds a continuity tension: leaders help culture most not only by shaping it intensely, but by leaving it transferable enough that successors can build on it.
