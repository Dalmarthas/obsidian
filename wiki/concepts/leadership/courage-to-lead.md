# What It Is

Courage to Lead is Simon Sinek's capstone practice for the infinite game: the willingness to act consistently with a Just Cause, trust, ethics, and long-term stewardship when short-term incentives punish that choice. It is not generic boldness. It is values under pressure.

In this vault, the concept completes the current Sinek five-practices branch. Just Cause names the horizon, Trusting Teams create the human substrate, Worthy Rivals sharpen self-honesty, Existential Flexibility enables radical renewal, and Courage to Lead is the pressure-bearing practice that keeps the other four from collapsing into slogans.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why infinite-minded leadership is emotionally and institutionally hard.
- It turns values from language into tradeoff behavior.
- It distinguishes legal compliance from ethical leadership.
- It links trust damage to short-term financial management practices such as projection-driven layoffs or rank-and-yank systems.
- It extends leadership beyond personal success into stewardship of successors, employees, customers, and the next generation.
- It reframes courage as relational rather than purely individual: people take principled risks when a trusted net exists around them.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-21-courage-to-lead-the-5-practices-simon-sinek]] argues that leaders face constant pressure to abandon long-term values for finite gains.
- The CVS example shows courage as visible revenue sacrifice: removing cigarettes aligned the company with its health mission despite expected financial loss and market criticism.
- Rite Aid and Boots are used as contrast cases where health-oriented language did not become matching tradeoff behavior.
- The source critiques shareholder supremacy for making the legal minimum and investor return the center of business responsibility.
- The source links fear-based practices such as routine layoffs and rank-and-yank to lower trust, concealment, and weaker psychological safety.
- The source extends infinite-mindedness to personal life through legacy: the question becomes who becomes stronger because of one's influence.
- The 21st-century overview adds the `courage net` idea:
  - soldiers take risks partly because they believe comrades would do the same
  - performers attempt difficult moves because there is a net
  - leaders build courage before crisis by creating relationships and systems that catch people when principled risks hurt

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/just-cause|Just Cause]], Courage to Lead is the stress test. A cause is only real when leaders absorb cost to preserve it under pressure.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/worthy-rival|Worthy Rival]], Courage to Lead guards against win-at-any-cost rivalry: tripping the other runner may win a finite race but does not make the organization better.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/existential-flexibility|Existential Flexibility]], Courage to Lead supplies the ethical backbone for radical shifts. Leaders need courage both to disrupt the current model and to avoid reckless pivots dressed up as purpose.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/trust-based-collaboration|Trust-Based Collaboration]], this page explains why trust depends on leader sacrifice: people infer whether the organization is safe by watching what leaders protect under pressure.
- The 21st-century overview narrows that further: courage is easier when trust is already present, so leaders should build the net in ordinary times instead of asking for bravery only during crisis.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/values-driven-companies|Values-Driven Companies]], Courage to Lead is narrower and sharper. Values-driven companies name the operating philosophy; courage names the costly decision moments that prove it.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/success-metrics|Success Metrics]], Courage to Lead asks whether the scoreboard is allowed to overrule ethics, trust, and long-term cause alignment.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Courage can become theatrics if leaders make symbolic costly moves without operational follow-through.
- Courage can also become recklessness if leaders ignore economics, cash constraints, or second-order harm to employees.
- The source's anti-shareholder-supremacy argument is useful but should be balanced later with stronger corporate-governance countermodels.
- The claim that infinite-minded organizations outperform needs more evidence than the source provides.
- The open practical question is how to design boards, incentives, metrics, and leadership succession so courage is structurally supported rather than dependent on rare individual virtue.
- A neighboring question is how to build courage socially without creating loyalty pressure that makes people take reckless risks for the group.
