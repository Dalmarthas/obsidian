# What It Is

A Just Cause is Simon Sinek's term for an idealized future state that does not yet exist but is meaningful enough that people willingly endure sacrifice to help advance it. In this vault, the concept sits between purpose language and strategy: it is not a product claim, a slogan, or a finite goal, but a durable horizon that helps people interpret why the work matters.

# Why It Matters

- It makes the infinite-game branch more operational by naming what leaders are supposed to keep advancing.
- It distinguishes meaningful organizational purpose from product excellence, market share, or temporary wins.
- It explains why some employees and customers become unusually loyal: they see themselves as participants in a future they want to help create.
- It creates a test for whether sacrifice language is grounded in a genuine shared cause or merely in management pressure.
- It gives worthy-rival comparison an ethical anchor: rivals should sharpen how the cause is pursued, not replace the cause with status competition.
- It gives existential flexibility a strategic compass: radical change is justified only when it better advances the cause, not when leaders are chasing novelty.
- It separates future-facing cause from past-facing identity: a cause describes the world being built, while `why` explains where the actor's conviction comes from.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-21-just-cause-the-5-practices-simon-sinek]] defines a Just Cause as a future state so meaningful that people are willing to sacrifice their own interests to advance it.
- The source uses the Declaration of Independence as a cause that named an unrealized ideal rather than the current reality.
- It treats the Revolutionary War as a finite victory inside the longer infinite game of trying to live up to that ideal.
- The source uses Apple under Steve Jobs as a business example: the cause was individual empowerment against large institutions; the personal computer was a product serving that cause.
- It argues that a cause should outlast any specific product or service and should make ordinary organizational sacrifices feel worth it to aligned people.
- The Worthy Rival source adds a calibration role: because rivals and competitive arenas change, a Just Cause must be expressed through evolving products, standards, and stakeholder concerns rather than frozen around an old opponent.
- The Existential Flexibility source adds a sacrifice-of-the-current-model role: a Just Cause should be durable enough that leaders can abandon an existing product, revenue stream, or strategy when a better path to the cause appears.
- The 21st-century overview sharpens the difference between Just Cause and numeric vision:
  - a numeric goal like becoming biggest by a given year is a finite target, not a cause
  - a Just Cause should describe a better world that may never be fully completed
  - people do not need to invent their own cause to lead infinitely; they can join and advance a cause they genuinely believe in

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/infinite-game-mindset|Infinite-Game Mindset]], Just Cause is the content of the horizon. Infinite-game mindset says the game has no final win; Just Cause says what future the organization keeps advancing.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/golden-circle|Golden Circle]], Just Cause is less about communication order and more about the substance of the `why` itself.
- The 21st-century overview makes this distinction sharper: `why` is identity-rooted and backward-looking, while Just Cause is future-facing and mobilizing.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/values-driven-companies|Values-Driven Companies]], Just Cause is future-oriented and mobilizing, while values describe the principles and constraints by which the organization pursues the future.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]], Just Cause can shape brand identity, but it is deeper than brand positioning if it actually guides sacrifice, hiring, incentives, and refusal.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/worthy-rival|Worthy Rival]], Just Cause is the enduring future being served. Worthy rivals are temporary comparison points that help reveal whether the organization is still improving in service of that future.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/existential-flexibility|Existential Flexibility]], Just Cause names the reason for the leap. Existential flexibility names the willingness to leap when the current model no longer serves that reason well enough.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Just Cause language can become decorative if it does not change decisions, incentives, or tradeoffs.
- Sacrifice language is dangerous when leaders ask employees to give more without reciprocal protection, fairness, or agency.
- Product or craft excellence may itself be meaningful for some people, so Sinek's product-versus-cause contrast should not be overgeneralized.
- A cause can inspire loyalty, but it can also produce groupthink or moral overconfidence if criticism is framed as betrayal.
- The vault needs better practical criteria for auditing whether a cause is clear, just, inclusive, durable, and decision-relevant.
- A cause can also become opponent-dependent if leaders define it only against the current rival. The Worthy Rival source helps by making rivals replaceable learning mirrors rather than permanent identity anchors.
- A cause can also become a cover for churn if leaders invoke it to justify every new strategic fascination. Existential flexibility needs a higher threshold than ordinary adaptation.
- A cause can also become a disguised target if it is written as a rank, size, or date-bound achievement rather than a better future state.
