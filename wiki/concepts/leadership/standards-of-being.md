# What It Is

Standards of being are behavioral and character standards for how people show up inside a team: how they treat teammates, recognize others, handle ego, prepare, and embody the group's values. They differ from performance standards because they govern the manner of contribution, not only the measurable output.

# Why It Matters

- They prevent talented people from becoming exceptions to the culture.
- They make values observable through repeated behavior.
- They protect teams from over-rewarding visible finishers while ignoring the contribution chain that made the result possible.
- They help leaders pair care with accountability instead of choosing between warmth and standards.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-21-the-leadership-advice-nobody-follows-but-everyone-should|The Leadership Advice Nobody Follows But Everyone Should]] uses John Wooden as the central example.
- Wooden's Pyramid of Success emphasizes traits and behaviors such as industriousness, teamwork, humility, and greatness rather than treating championships as the direct target.
- The Bill Walton hair-length story illustrates equal application: even a star player has to choose between individual preference and the team standard.
- Wooden's requirement that scorers thank the passer turns recognition into a game habit. It teaches that the visible score depends on unseen setup and that the team should honor assists, not only finishers.
- The source generalizes this into a leadership practice: define a few behavioral standards, explain the reason for each, and review how people uphold them alongside performance results.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/high-performance-team-culture|High-Performance Team Culture]], this page is narrower. High-performance culture includes trust, role clarity, pressure, and motivation; standards of being name the behavioral norms that make that culture concrete.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/character-based-leadership|Character-Based Leadership]], this page is more team-operational. Character-based leadership asks whether a leader is trustworthy; standards of being ask what repeated behaviors the whole group must practice.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/trust-based-collaboration|Trust-Based Collaboration]], standards of being are a boundary condition. Trust is easier when people know which behaviors are expected from everyone, including high performers.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Standards of being can become vague moral branding if they are not tied to observable behavior.
- Equal application matters, but leaders still need judgment when a rule is outdated, exclusionary, or more symbolic than useful.
- The current source is strong on memorable examples but lighter on modern assessment methods for standards of being in workplaces.
