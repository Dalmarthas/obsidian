# What It Is

Validated learning is the discipline of testing a business hypothesis through real-world behavior before committing to full-scale buildout. In this vault, the idea is broader than "ask users what they think" and narrower than "just experiment with everything." It means naming the riskiest assumption, designing the smallest credible test for it, measuring committed behavior, and updating direction from the result.

# Why It Matters

- It reduces waste by stopping teams from confusing effort with evidence.
- It gives strategy an observable test instead of leaving it at the level of opinion or founder conviction.
- It creates a reusable bridge between vision and execution: the team can keep a long-term idea while still forcing near-term contact with reality.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The first Eric Ries source defines an MVP as the smallest testable vehicle for learning whether a critical assumption is true.
- That same source insists that behavior outranks opinion:
  - purchases
  - pre-orders
  - meaningful clicks
  - other forms of commitment
- The first source also gives two practical testing modes:
  - `concierge MVP`, where value is delivered manually before automation
  - `smokescreen MVP`, where demand is tested before the full product exists
- The second Eric Ries source adds the management layer around validated learning:
  - startups should be defined by uncertainty, not by company size
  - build-measure-learn is a speed discipline for discovering truth before resources run out
  - learning needs explicit accountability through innovation-accounting logic rather than vanity metrics

# Tensions / Open Questions

- A weak experiment can create false certainty if it measures curiosity rather than actual willingness to buy or continue.
- Faster tests are often better, but some categories need longer trust cycles and cannot be judged by superficial signals alone.
- Smokescreen-style validation is useful, but it needs ethical limits so the test does not become deception.
- The newer Eric Ries source adds a second measurement risk: teams can praise learning while still rewarding dashboards that mostly document motion, status, or internal effort.
- The main open question is how the vault should distinguish `strong commitment signals` from `interesting but weak activity metrics`.
