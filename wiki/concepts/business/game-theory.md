# What It Is

Game theory is the study of decisions made under strategic interdependence, where the payoff of any move depends partly on what other actors choose. In this vault, it adds a missing layer to the current decision-making branch: some bad outcomes are not mainly caused by bias, weak character, or poor analysis inside one mind, but by incentive structures that make individually rational choices collide with collective interests. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-how-decision-making-is-actually-science-game-theory-explained|How Decision Making is Actually Science: Game Theory Explained]].

# Why It Matters

- It clarifies when a problem is strategic rather than merely psychological.
- It helps explain why stable bad outcomes can persist even when the actors are not confused.
- It gives the vault a portable language for competition, cooperation, coalition design, and incentive-aware fairness.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source defines a `game` broadly as any interaction where each person's result depends on the choices of others.
- The newer introductory source sharpens the formal definition:
  - every game has players, strategies, and payoffs
  - the rules and possible outcomes are usually treated as common knowledge even when actions are uncertain
  - this makes game theory a modeling language, not only a collection of examples
- It uses the Prisoner's Dilemma to show a recurring pattern:
  - each player protects themselves by choosing the dominant move
  - the resulting equilibrium is stable under unilateral deviation
  - the total outcome is still worse than the cooperative alternative
- That same newer source also adds two useful distinctions:
  - zero-sum conflict is only one subclass of strategic interaction
  - dominant strategy is narrower than Nash equilibrium, because Nash also applies where no option is best regardless of others' choices
- The source therefore distinguishes three different questions that are often blurred together:
  - what is individually rational?
  - what is collectively best?
  - what outcome is actually stable under the incentives in front of the players?
- The newest source adds a repeated-games correction:
  - one-shot logic can overreward defection
  - repeated interaction changes incentives because reputation, retaliation, and future cooperation all start to matter
  - this makes strategies like [[wiki/concepts/business/tit-for-tat|Tit for Tat]] durable even when they do not maximize any single round
- The Yale lecture adds a sequential-games layer:
  - some strategic problems are mainly about timing rather than about choosing among qualitatively different actions
  - dominance reasoning can identify robust early-stage moves
  - [[wiki/concepts/business/backward-induction|Backward Induction]] can then resolve finite endpoint logic by reasoning backward from the last move
- The negotiation source adds a bargaining-design layer:
  - outside options matter because [[wiki/concepts/communication/batna|BATNA]] sets reservation value and leverage
  - incomplete information makes signaling and screening central rather than optional side tactics
  - credible commitments can change the game by changing which future actions the other side expects are really available
- The newest repeated-games source sharpens the ecological and noise layers:
  - cooperation can spread when clusters of reciprocal players interact often enough with each other
  - strategy quality depends partly on the surrounding population, not only on isolated pairwise logic
  - generous reciprocity can outperform strict reciprocity where accidental defections would otherwise trap both sides in escalating punishment
- The cooperative branch adds another strategic layer:
  - once people can form a coalition, the problem shifts from rivalry to allocation
  - the key question becomes how surplus, credit, or cost should be split without hiding contribution differences
- The source is especially useful because it keeps `rationality` narrow:
  - rationality here means responding coherently to incentives and expectations
  - it does not guarantee morality, trust, or the socially best result
- The same source also adds a behavioral warning:
  - classical predictions depend on rational-agent assumptions
  - real players often cooperate, punish, or reason with bounded rationality instead
  - formal elegance therefore does not guarantee empirical fit
- The newer Simon Sinek war source adds a framing caution:
  - not every source that uses `game theory` is really contributing equilibrium logic
  - in that case, the durable contribution is a strategic time-horizon lens about finite versus infinite conflict
  - that still belongs near this branch because it studies interdependent actors, but it is less formal and more interpretive than Nash or Shapley-style material

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/two-systems-thinking|Two-Systems Thinking]], game theory is less about how one mind misjudges and more about how multiple actors shape one another's outcomes.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/negotiation-and-mediation|Negotiation and Mediation]], game theory is more structural. Negotiation focuses on interaction tactics and interest discovery; game theory focuses on the incentive geometry underneath the interaction.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], game theory is narrower and more formal. Strategy decides where and how to compete; game theory helps describe the payoff structure of the field and whether the field itself traps players in bad equilibria.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/zero-sum-games|Zero-Sum Games]], this concept is broader. Zero-sum logic names one special class of rivalry; game theory covers zero-sum, non-zero-sum, cooperative, and many other interaction structures.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/tit-for-tat|Tit for Tat]], this concept is broader. Tit for tat is one repeated-games strategy; game theory names the wider field that explains why that strategy works under some conditions and fails under others.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/backward-induction|Backward Induction]], this concept is broader. Backward induction is one tool for finite sequential play; game theory covers simultaneous, repeated, cooperative, and many other structures as well.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/infinite-game-mindset|Infinite-Game Mindset]], this concept is more analytical and multi-actor. The Sinek branch contributes a horizon-and-endurance frame rather than a formal account of equilibrium, coalition value, or incentive design.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Formal strategic clarity can tempt people to ignore trust, norms, ethics, and repeated-relationship effects that often change the real game.
- A short introductory source can make equilibrium logic feel more deterministic than many live human situations actually are.
- The newer Sinek source sharpens a different tension: `game theory` can become a loose rhetorical label for strategic framing rather than a precise formal method.
- The newest introductory source sharpens a neighboring tension: the cleaner the rational model becomes, the easier it is to forget how much prediction quality depends on whether real human behavior matches the model's assumptions.
- The repeated-games source adds another tension: cooperation-friendly strategies can be robust, but only where retaliation is feasible, future interaction is likely, and noise does not constantly create false defections.
- The Yale lecture adds a neighboring tension: some conclusions are robust by dominance, while others are fragile because they require opponents to reason backward in the same disciplined way.
- The negotiation source adds another caution: formally clean commitments, threats, and signals can backfire if the human side reads them as bluffing, humiliation, or manipulation.
- The newest repeated-games source adds a neighboring caution: even a strong reciprocal rule can become too brittle if noise, false signals, or forced defections are common.
- The current branch still needs deeper sources on repeated games, coordination failures, commitment devices, auctions, and mechanism design.
