# What It Is

Experience design is the deliberate shaping of how people move through, feel, interpret, and remember an encounter with a product, service, event, or brand. In the current vault, the concept is broader than interface design or hospitality polish: it includes arrival, expectations, rituals, visibility, pricing feel, recovery moments, and exit memory.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why meaning is often created through the journey around the product, not only through the product's core function.
- It gives strategy a practical embodiment layer: the brand promise becomes believable when the touchpoints enact it.
- It helps the vault connect marketing, service, pricing, operations, and ritual instead of treating them as separate disciplines.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-16-why-this-baseball-team-has-a-4-2-million-person-waitlist-with-jesse-cole|Why This Baseball Team Has a 4.2 Million Person Waitlist With Jesse Cole]] presents experience design as the core product:
  - map the emotional journey from approach to exit
  - create signature rituals and surprise moments
  - design for the `upper deck`, not only for premium buyers
  - treat pricing fairness and anti-gouging as parts of the experience
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-17-savannah-bananas-the-5-es-to-creating-raving-fans|Savannah Bananas The 5 E's To Creating Raving Fans]] sharpens that Jesse Cole branch into a more explicit operating sequence:
  - eliminate friction before trying to delight
  - entertain at every touchpoint rather than only inside the core product moment
  - maintain a constant experiment loop so the experience stays fresh
  - deepen engagement until customers feel like participants
  - empower both staff and customers to spread and extend the experience
- The Jesse Cole source also shows that experience design can function as marketing:
  - a remarkable event becomes self-spreading
  - rituals and visible moments give people stories to tell
  - generosity can produce stronger loyalty than short-run extraction
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-rory-sutherland-perspective-is-everything|Rory Sutherland Perspective is Everything]] adds the psychological layer that experienced time, uncertainty, and perceived control can matter as much as objective performance.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-the-playbook-behind-every-great-campaign|The Playbook Behind Every Great Campaign]] adds a touchpoint-economics layer: recovery moments, frontline discretion, and the feel of the transaction can multiply the whole brand up or down.
- Across the current business branch, the concept implies that a company is not only selling a function. It is also designing what the customer gets to feel, notice, remember, and repeat to others.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]], experience design is more embodied and downstream. Brand strategy defines the promise; experience design is how the promise becomes lived reality.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/experience-goods|Experience Goods]], this page is more managerial. Experience goods explains why value may appear only after use; experience design explains how to shape that use so the value becomes legible sooner and more memorably.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/transaction-utility|Transaction Utility]], this page is broader. Transaction utility focuses on the feel of the purchase moment; experience design covers the whole arc before, during, and after use.
- Jesse Cole is strongest on ritualized joy, category redesign, and `upper deck` empathy.
- The newer Jesse Cole source is strongest on operational sequence. It makes experience design more actionable by naming friction removal, entertainment, experimentation, engagement, and empowerment as distinct but linked levers.
- Rory Sutherland is strongest on the psychology of control, waiting, visibility, and uncertainty inside the experience.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Experience design can become manipulative theater if it is used to decorate a weak product rather than to reveal genuine value more clearly.
- Richly designed journeys can be expensive, founder-dependent, or hard to standardize without deadening them.
- The branch still needs stronger criteria for deciding when experience design is the main growth lever and when fixing the core product or economics matters more.
- The newer Jesse Cole source adds a practical design tension: removing friction often pushes toward simplicity and smoothness, while entertainment and experimentation introduce noise and unpredictability, so the branch still needs better rules for balancing clean operations against theatrical energy.
