# What It Is

Success metrics are the measures a person, team, or organization uses to decide whether progress is real. In this vault, the concept is not just KPI design. It is the deeper question of whether the scoreboard captures durable health or merely rewards visible numbers while hiding strategic drift, cultural damage, or false progress.

# Why It Matters

- It determines what behavior systems quietly encourage.
- It connects leadership philosophy to incentives, promotions, and daily judgment.
- It helps the vault distinguish between honest measurement and metrics that create success theater.
- It creates a reusable home for cross-source thinking on output, learning, trust, and long-horizon health.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Simon Sinek source argues that profit, achievement, and rankings are incomplete when they exclude momentum, trust, and the quality of the process that produced them.
- That same source says success should include whether capability, morale, and sustainable direction are improving rather than only whether the latest result looks good.
- The Eric Ries branch contributes the startup version of the same problem through `innovation accounting`: teams can confuse vanity metrics with validated learning unless the measurement system is built for uncertainty.
- The Clayton Christensen branch adds a portfolio version: financially elegant metrics can make firms look disciplined while starving the bets that create future demand.
- The strongest shared lesson is that metrics are never neutral. They shape behavior by defining what the institution treats as legible, rewardable, and real.
- The Seth Godin strategy source deepens this by separating decision quality from outcome quality: a sound decision can still lose, a lucky outcome can still reward bad reasoning, and strategy requires reviewing the deck being stacked rather than only the last card drawn.
- That same source also adds a false-proxy warning: institutions often measure what is easy to count rather than what actually predicts strategic progress.
- The newer Seth brand source makes that warning more operational for small brands and creators: referrals, repeat engagement, and being genuinely missed are stronger signals than followers, likes, views, or other easy dashboards.
- The newest Seth brand-marketing source sharpens the same problem through metric mismatch: using direct-response scoreboards to judge brand work changes the work itself, pushing teams toward cheap attention, clickbait, and short-run optimization that can quietly destroy the trust brand building depends on.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], this page is narrower and more diagnostic. Strategy chooses direction; success metrics decide whether the chosen direction is actually being advanced in an honest way.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/innovation-accounting|Innovation Accounting]], this page is broader. Innovation accounting is one specialized answer for uncertain ventures; success metrics covers the wider design problem across mature firms, teams, and leadership systems.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/infinite-game-mindset|Infinite-Game Mindset]], this page is more operational. Infinite-game mindset asks what horizon matters; success metrics asks whether the scoreboard matches that horizon.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/trust-performance-matrix|Trust-Performance Matrix]], this page is more systemic. The trust-performance matrix evaluates people; success metrics evaluates the institutional scoreboards that decide which people and behaviors get rewarded.
- Compared with Seth Godin's framing, Simon Sinek is stronger on incomplete institutional scoreboards, while Godin is stronger on the probabilistic logic behind why bad scoreboards feel persuasive in the first place.
- The newer Seth source adds a more brand-specific metric lens than the rest of the page: its best question is whether trusted customers actually tell other people, not whether the marketer can display larger surface-level attention.
- The newest Seth brand-marketing source adds a mode-discipline comparison: some scoreboards are not merely incomplete, but category mistakes, because they evaluate long-horizon brand meaning as if it were short-horizon funnel performance.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Broadening metrics can improve judgment, but it can also create vagueness if everything is measured poorly.
- Hard numbers are tempting because they feel objective, yet they often hide the strongest behavioral distortions.
- Soft variables like trust or momentum matter, but they are easier to politicize if not anchored in observable behavior.
- The Seth source adds a neighboring tension: if teams overcorrect toward `ignore outcomes`, they can pretend to value decision quality while never confronting repeated evidence that the chosen strategy is weak.
- The newer Seth source adds another tension: referral depth is a useful truth signal, but some categories are structurally less talk-worthy, which means the vault should not flatten every business into a word-of-mouth model.
- The newest Seth brand-marketing source adds another tension: `brand` can become a refuge for vagueness and prestige theater unless teams still define what trust, fit, and long-run relevance should look like in observable terms.
- The main open question is how to keep success metrics rich enough to reflect reality without making them so complex that nobody can use them.
