# What It Is

Copywriting is the craft of writing language that makes a reader notice, understand, remember, trust, and act. In this vault, the current branch defines strong copy as concrete, fact-anchored, picture-forming, and specific enough that the message could not be lazily swapped between competitors.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a canonical home for written persuasion at scale.
- It sharpens the business branch by making messaging quality more operational.
- It connects brand strategy, audience understanding, and design execution rather than treating writing as isolated wordplay.
- It fills a real gap in the existing communication branch, which was stronger on spoken or interactive influence than on writing.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Harry Dry contributes the clearest governing heuristic: good copy should be visualizable, falsifiable, and unique.
- The source repeatedly pushes specificity over abstraction because readers remember what they can picture.
- Facts are treated as high-quality raw material for structure and credibility.
- The process is also part of the craft: write in the actual medium, revise aggressively, and use feedback and layout context early.
- Contrast, conflict, and parallel structure help make the message more legible and persuasive.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/advertising|Advertising]], this page is narrower and more text-centered; advertising is broader and includes the full creative system around the message.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]], this page is downstream and expressive: strategy defines what matters, copywriting decides how to make it land.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/effective-communication|Effective Communication]], this page specializes in written communication at scale rather than conversation, interviews, or negotiation.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/persuasion|Persuasion]], this page is more executional and language-level.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Great copy cannot compensate for weak differentiation forever; the source assumes there is something true and defensible to say.
- The current branch is strong on clarity and specificity, but it still needs more evidence on long-form sales writing, emotional storytelling, and offer construction.
- There is also an unresolved tension between strict falsifiability and the softer symbolic or aspirational language some brands still use effectively.
