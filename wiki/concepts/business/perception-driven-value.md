# What It Is

Perception-driven value is the idea that value is not determined only by objective product characteristics, but also by framing, context, aesthetics, symbolism, expectation, and the meaning attached to an experience. In the current vault, this concept sharpens the business branch by insisting that what people feel they are getting is often economically decisive.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why technical superiority alone often fails to command preference or price premiums.
- It gives marketing and design a strategic role rather than treating them as decorative wrappers.
- It widens the definition of innovation beyond functionality alone.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Rory Sutherland source argues that wording, positioning, and context can multiply value without equivalent physical change.
- The source treats reframing as a legitimate route to value creation when it helps people understand or experience something differently.
- It also presents aesthetics, waiting, location, and emotional resonance as economically meaningful rather than superficial.

# Source Comparisons

- This concept deepens [[wiki/concepts/business/advertising|Advertising]] by explaining why good messaging can change not only attention but willingness to value the offer.
- It supports [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]] by showing that strategy partly governs interpretation, not just product claims.
- It overlaps with [[wiki/concepts/communication/concrete-specificity|Concrete Specificity]], but the emphasis is different: specificity improves believability, while perception-driven value explains why belief and framing can alter the felt worth itself.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- If perception work outruns product reality, value creation can become deception.
- Some markets reward symbolic value heavily; others punish it unless functional value is also obvious.
- The vault still needs stronger contrast sources that distinguish ethical reframing from manipulative spin.
