# What It Is

Fat-tailed marketing is the view that marketing and creative-commercial discovery do not produce value in smooth, linear increments. Instead, most gains are modest while a small number of unusual ideas, reframings, or interventions create disproportionate and enduring returns. In this vault, the concept matters because it explains why systems built for average-case optimization often miss the actual economics of creativity.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the business branch a clearer reason to protect experimentation instead of funding only measurable incremental gains.
- It explains why agency billing, procurement logic, and quarterly dashboards can be structurally mismatched to real marketing value creation.
- It connects creative exploration to strategy, pricing, product design, and innovation rather than limiting it to campaign cleverness.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-10-rory-sutherland-marketing-psychology-and-perceived-value|Rory Sutherland Marketing Psychology and Perceived Value]] introduces the core claim that a few unusual ideas create disproportionate returns and that brands miss these gains when they optimize only for rational, legible improvements.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-psychology-hacks-of-the-worlds-best-advertisers|Psychology Hacks of the Worlds Best Advertisers]] strengthens this by contrasting thin-tailed optimization with fat-tailed discovery and arguing that testing is valuable partly because it produces surprising information, not only immediate revenue.
- That newer source also ties the concept to incentives: hourly billing, procurement filtering, and historical-data dependence all under-reward the rare idea that changes the category.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-rory-sutherland-soft-power-in-a-hard-world|Rory Sutherland Soft Power in a Hard World]] sharpens the complementarity rule: exploration and exploitation require different people, scoreboards, and tolerances, and the real failure is often trying to govern both by exploit-mode metrics alone.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/reverse-benchmarking|Reverse Benchmarking]], fat-tailed marketing is broader and more economic. Reverse benchmarking is one search method; fat-tailed marketing explains why such unusual searches are worth protecting in the first place.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/psychological-arbitrage|Psychological Arbitrage]], fat-tailed marketing is broader and less specifically perceptual. Psychological arbitrage focuses on underpriced framing and context asymmetries; fat-tailed marketing covers the overall return distribution of creative-commercial discovery.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/paradigm-shifts|Paradigm Shifts]], fat-tailed marketing is broader and more portfolio-oriented. Paradigm shifts are one recurrent source of outsized wins; fat-tailed marketing explains why protecting a portfolio of exploratory bets is rational in the first place.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/advertising|Advertising]], this concept is less about the craft of making work land and more about the economics of how rare high-upside ideas are found and funded.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- A fat-tailed model is useful only if teams still prune weak ideas ruthlessly enough to avoid romanticizing randomness.
- The vault still needs stronger operational guidance on how much exploration is enough in different company stages or category conditions.
- Teams can misuse the concept to excuse low discipline, poor evaluation, or creative theater with no real customer evidence.
