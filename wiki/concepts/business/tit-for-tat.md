# What It Is

Tit for tat is a repeated-game strategy that begins with cooperation and then mirrors the other side's previous move: cooperate after cooperation, retaliate after defection. In the vault, it matters less as a rigid rule than as a durable strategic profile for ongoing relationships: be cooperative first, respond proportionally to exploitation, forgive quickly when the other side repairs, and stay predictable enough that the interaction can stabilize. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-game-theory-a-simple-strategy-that-will-change-your-life-forever|Game Theory: A Simple Strategy That Will Change Your Life Forever]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the game-theory branch a practical repeated-games strategy instead of only static equilibrium analysis.
- It shows how cooperation can be defended without collapsing into naive niceness or permanent vengeance.
- It connects reputation, boundaries, and long-run payoff into one simple strategic pattern.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source presents `tit for tat` as the strongest durable lesson from Axelrod-style iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments.
- Its four operational traits are:
  - nice: cooperate first
  - retaliatory: punish defection promptly
  - forgiving: resume cooperation once the other side does
  - clear: make the rule understandable enough that others can adapt to it
- The source further argues that the strategy's strength comes from optimizing the whole repeated relationship rather than any single round.
- It also notes that more generous variants may perform better under noisy conditions, which keeps the concept from hardening into blind mechanical reciprocity.
- The newer source deepens that warning in three ways:
  - strict tit for tat can be too brittle when defections are accidental or misread
  - the surrounding ecology of strategies matters, so no repeated-game rule wins in every environment
  - generous tit for tat can outperform strict reciprocity when noise would otherwise lock both sides into punishment spirals
- The same source also sharpens why the strategy works socially:
  - it does not need to dominate counterparts in any single match
  - it wins by preserving access to high total payoff across many interactions
  - that makes its success easier to understand in non-zero-sum environments than in pure opponent-defeat framing

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/negotiation-and-mediation|Negotiation and Mediation]], tit for tat is less verbal and more structural. Negotiation focuses on dialogue and interest discovery; tit for tat focuses on repeated-response design across interactions.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/game-theory|Game Theory]], this concept is narrower. Game theory explains the field of strategic interaction; tit for tat is one especially durable response pattern inside repeated prisoner-style settings.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/zero-sum-games|Zero-Sum Games]], tit for tat makes most sense where the relationship is not purely fixed-pie. Its real advantage comes from sustaining total value over time, not from extracting losses from an opponent.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strict reciprocity can become too brittle in noisy or asymmetric environments where defections may be accidental, coerced, or misread.
- Immediate retaliation is not always safe or feasible when power is extremely unequal.
- The vault is now clearer on generous tit for tat as a noise correction, but it still needs stronger material on how much forgiveness is optimal under different error rates and power gaps.
