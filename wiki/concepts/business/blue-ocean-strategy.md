# What It Is

Blue ocean strategy is the attempt to create uncontested market space rather than compete head-to-head inside an already crowded category. In the current vault, the concept means redesigning the offer, the category logic, and often the target user set so demand is created instead of merely stolen. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-make-the-competition-irrelevant-blue-ocean-strategy|Make the Competition Irrelevant: Blue Ocean Strategy]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the business branch a cleaner answer to why some categories stay unattractive even when execution improves.
- It shifts strategy from rival comparison toward demand creation and game redesign.
- It helps separate real strategic differentiation from incremental improvement inside a structurally crowded field.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The source argues that red-ocean markets tend toward price pressure, imitation, and bloody share fights.
- It treats blue oceans as demand-creation moves where a company changes the category frame rather than only optimizing inside it.
- Non-customers matter because they reveal what current category assumptions make adoption too difficult, confusing, or unattractive.
- The branch now supports a category-first strategic question:
  - not only `how do we beat competitors?`
  - but `what category shape would make direct comparison less central or less relevant?`

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], blue ocean strategy is narrower and more market-creation specific. Business strategy covers the full allocation logic of a company; blue ocean strategy focuses on escaping crowded competition through category redesign.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/jobs-to-be-done|Jobs to Be Done]], blue ocean strategy is less diagnostic about customer progress and more generative about building a new category around under-served or non-consuming users.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/positioning-as-a-service|Positioning as a Service]], blue ocean strategy is upstream and more structural. Positioning clarifies a chosen corner; blue ocean strategy may redefine the whole map.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The concept is attractive enough that teams can label ordinary differentiation as `blue ocean` without having changed the economics of competition.
- Many markets really do demand competent red-ocean execution before category-creation experiments become viable.
- The current branch still needs stronger guidance on sequencing, validation, and how much proof is needed before a firm commits to a new category thesis.
