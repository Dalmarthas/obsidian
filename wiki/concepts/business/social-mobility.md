# What It Is

Social mobility is the degree to which people can move upward or downward across class positions rather than remaining locked near the status, wealth, and institutional access of their birth. In the current vault, the concept matters because it reframes many `success` traits as partly downstream of whether the surrounding game is open, trustworthy, and merit-rewarding or closed, volatile, and class-reproducing. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-14-game-theory-3-rich-dad-poor-dad|Game Theory 3 Rich Dad Poor Dad]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the business branch a structural counterweight to purely individual explanations of success.
- It clarifies why some environments train negotiation, confidence, and long-horizon thinking while others train obedience, caution, and survival behavior.
- It sharpens when motivational advice is useful versus when it is trying to solve a rigged-game problem with personality rhetoric.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source treats social mobility as the main governance-quality variable in the whole lecture:
  - open systems reward talent and ambition more credibly
  - blocked systems harden class reproduction and resentment
- Rich and poor families are described as training children for different institutional games:
  - rich-world children are trained for explanation, bargaining, and confident contact with authority
  - poor-world children are trained for compliance, risk avoidance, and survival inside more punitive conditions
- The source also reframes several familiar psychological constructs as mobility-adjacent:
  - delayed gratification depends partly on whether promises and institutions are reliable
  - resilience depends partly on whether help, fallback options, and recoverability are expected
  - self-reflection is easier when life is stable enough to examine rather than merely endure
- The source adds a systemic warning:
  - elites do not merely enjoy advantage
  - they often redesign schools, credentials, and gatekeeping systems to preserve advantage for their own children
  - this converts a once-open game into a more hereditary one
- The lecture also frames revolutions and populist surges as `game resets`:
  - debt, blocked advancement, and elite overproduction destabilize the system
  - frustrated near-elites then become the organizing layer that can mobilize broader discontent

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/growth-mindset|Growth Mindset]], social mobility is more structural and less interpretive. Growth mindset asks how a person reads difficulty; social mobility asks how open the larger field is for those efforts to matter.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/locus-of-control|Locus of Control]], this concept is more environmental and institutional. Locus of control explains agency beliefs; social mobility explains why those beliefs may be adaptive in one environment and unrealistic in another.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/game-theory|Game Theory]], social mobility is less formal. Game theory models strategic interdependence broadly; social mobility focuses on whether the game itself remains open enough for movement between positions.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/financial-confidence|Financial Confidence]], this concept is more societal than personal. Financial confidence is about planning a viable leap; social mobility is about whether the surrounding system actually offers viable ladders to climb.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Structural clarity can become defeatist if it is used to erase the real but uneven role of skill, agency, migration, networking, and strategic choice.
- The concept is useful, but the current source generalizes heavily and still needs stronger empirical depth on which institutions most affect mobility.
- The branch still needs sharper boundaries between valid critique of rigged games and overbroad narratives that flatten class variation, family variation, or individual outliers.
