# What It Is

Smallest viable audience is the strategy of aiming first at the smallest group of people who care enough to notice, adopt, talk, and organize around the work instead of diluting the offer for general acceptability. In the current source base, it is a defense against commodity competition and cultural invisibility.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the business branch a clearer answer to the problem of generic mass positioning.
- It links brand differentiation to audience selection instead of treating differentiation as message style alone.
- It clarifies that being specific is often a growth mechanism, not merely a branding preference.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Seth Godin source argues that average-for-the-masses work is vulnerable to price sorting and indifference.
- That source presents the smallest viable audience as the group most likely to care deeply, spread the story, and form a tribe around the work.
- Story, permission, and shared identity matter more when the audience is chosen for resonance rather than scale fantasy.
- The concept also implies that not everyone needs to be persuaded; strategic exclusion can strengthen meaning and memorability.
- The second Seth Godin source deepens the cost side of the concept: serving a smallest viable audience often requires tolerating peculiarity, criticism, and the loss of broad social approval rather than only making a cleaner targeting choice.
- The `This Is Strategy` source makes the concept more strategic than promotional:
  - the audience choice determines which systems the business enters
  - it influences what kind of customers, norms, and expectations the work will attract
  - forgiving everyone else is part of strategy because focus changes the future conditions of the work
- That same source also ties audience choice to empathy more tightly: the goal is not only finding people who can buy, but understanding whose status, affiliation, fear, and desired change the work is actually designed to address.
- The newer Seth marketing source makes the audience-definition layer more precise:
  - smallest viable audience should usually be defined psychographically, not only demographically
  - the useful question is what `people like us` believe, want, and normalize
  - saying `not for everyone` works better when the exclusion is tied to worldview rather than to shallow category labels
- The newest Seth marketing source adds an early proof test:
  - if the first ten right people would not miss the work or tell others, the likely problem is the offer, the audience, or both
  - early referral among the right few is more informative than vague interest from the wrong many
  - the concept is therefore not only about size, but about resonance strong enough to spread inside a tiny initial circle
- The newest Seth audience-building source makes the concept more sequenced and behaviorally concrete:
  - pick a group small enough to understand in worldview terms rather than only in market-segment abstractions
  - match the story to what that group already believes, fears, and desires
  - design the product and surrounding cues so the right customers naturally talk about it to one another

# Tensions / Open Questions

- A narrow audience can create intensity, but it can also become too small to support the business if the economics are weak.
- Specificity improves resonance, but it can harden into insularity if the maker starts protecting identity more than solving real problems.
- The newer Seth source adds another tension: audience specificity works best when it is anchored to generous contribution, not when it becomes niche vanity or performative weirdness.
- The strategy source adds a related tension: a sharply chosen audience can improve the future, but choosing an audience with bad incentives, low trust, or constant urgency can lock the business into permanently bad days.
- The branch still needs stronger evidence on how and when a smallest viable audience can expand without dissolving the original distinctiveness.
- The newest Seth source adds a related tension: even a well-chosen small audience may not be economically useful if the people who care cannot or will not fund the work enough to sustain quality.
- The newest Seth audience-building layer adds another tension: a narrowly chosen audience can still fail if the work fits the group's identity but lacks enough practical value or social visibility to trigger real buying and sharing behavior.
