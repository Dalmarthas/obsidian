# What It Is

Psychographic targeting is the practice of defining an audience by its beliefs, aspirations, fears, taste, status logic, and internal narratives rather than by demographic labels alone. In this vault, the concept matters because it explains why two people with similar age, income, or location can respond to radically different stories, products, and cultural signals.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the business branch a more behaviorally realistic way to define `who it's for`.
- It explains why smallest-viable-audience strategy depends on shared worldview, not just on narrow demographics.
- It protects the vault from reducing marketing insight to list-buying categories that are easy to measure but strategically shallow.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Seth Godin marketing source argues that modern marketing works better when it targets beliefs and self-stories instead of age brackets or zip-code stereotypes.
- That source also shows that strong offers often need to say `for you` and `not for you` in psychographic terms, because belonging and taste are part of what create demand.
- The same branch ties psychographics to status and culture: `people like us do things like this` is a compact way of describing the social script a marketer is either joining or trying to shift.
- The newer Seth brand-marketing source makes the concept more decision-relevant through the Nike example: the strategic question is whether a move resonates with the psychographic core that shapes brand meaning, not whether every possible demographic slice approves.
- That same source also shows that alienating non-core buyers can be acceptable when the move reinforces real fit with early adopters, cultural carriers, or other audience groups that actually define the brand's spread and margin structure.
- This overlaps with [[wiki/concepts/business/smallest-viable-audience|Smallest Viable Audience]], but the emphasis here is on how the audience is understood rather than how narrowly it is selected.
- It also overlaps with [[wiki/concepts/business/jobs-to-be-done|Jobs to Be Done]], but this page is more identity- and worldview-centered, while jobs-to-be-done is more situational and causal.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/smallest-viable-audience|Smallest Viable Audience]], this concept is upstream: psychographics help define the audience before the marketer decides how narrow the first audience should be.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/jobs-to-be-done|Jobs to Be Done]], this concept is less about the functional progress the customer seeks and more about the story, identity, and belonging wrapped around that behavior.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/perception-driven-value|Perception-Driven Value]], this page is more audience-diagnostic. Perception-driven value explains how meaning changes value; psychographic targeting explains which people are most likely to respond to which meanings.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Psychographics can produce better insight than demographics, but they can also encourage flattering storytelling if marketers infer motives too confidently from surface behavior.
- A psychographic segment can become strategically useful while still being too small or too weakly monetizable to support the business.
- The Nike-style case adds a neighboring risk: teams can convince themselves they are serving a psychographic core when they are really using audience mythology to justify stunts that do not fit their actual history or customer base.
- The main open question is how to validate psychographic hypotheses rigorously enough that they improve empathy instead of becoming elegant projection.
