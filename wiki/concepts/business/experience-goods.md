# What It Is

Experience goods are products, services, roles, or relationships whose value cannot be judged well from first glance and becomes legible only through repeated use, lived exposure, or familiarity. In the current vault, the concept matters because many markets still sort options through shallow visible proxies even when the real value is delayed, compounding, and experiential.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why strong options are often under-selected in markets built around photos, rankings, brands, or immediate surface impressions.
- It gives product, marketing, and onboarding work a more causal role: the job is often to help users reach the point where the value can actually be felt.
- It sharpens the distinction between selling what looks best instantly and designing for what proves best through use.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-how-your-brain-gets-tricked-by-clever-marketing|How Your Brain Gets Tricked By Clever Marketing]] argues that many worthwhile products and choices are mispriced because their benefits appear only after familiarity.
- The same source uses jobs, dating, and consumer products to show that first-glance filters often hide traits that matter more in lived experience than in initial sorting.
- It also suggests that trials, demonstrations, onboarding windows, and word of mouth work well partly because they give an experience good enough time to reveal itself.
- The newer Rory source sharpens this with a `no going back` effect:
  - some products look only modestly better before use
  - after trial, the old alternative feels absurd or clearly inferior
  - this means adoption strategy should often prioritize trials, demos, and experiential onboarding over explanation-heavy persuasion
- That same source also blurs the usual goods-versus-experiences split by arguing that many physical products matter mainly because of the recurring experiences they unlock.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/perception-driven-value|Perception-Driven Value]], experience goods are less about framing in the abstract and more about the timing of when value becomes visible.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/psychological-leverage|Psychological Leverage]], this concept is narrower. Psychological leverage is the broader design logic; experience goods explain one recurring reason why design, onboarding, and signaling matter so much.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/hidden-functions|Hidden Functions]], experience goods is less about tacit social roles and more about delayed realization. Hidden functions explains invisible value inside a system or touchpoint; experience goods explains why users often need lived exposure before any value becomes legible.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], experience goods are not a full strategic model, but they strongly affect go-to-market design, trial structure, pricing, and selection mechanisms.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Some offerings genuinely need strong first-glance clarity because people will never stay long enough for hidden value to emerge.
- Firms can misuse the concept as an excuse for poor usability or weak immediate communication.
- The vault still needs stronger contrast sources on search goods, credence goods, and when experience-good logic fails.
