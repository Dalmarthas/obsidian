# What It Is

Story propulsion is the set of techniques that keep a reader or viewer moving forward through a narrative rather than feeling that the work has stalled. In the current source base, propulsion comes from a mix of structural movement, scene or chapter transitions, unresolved questions, clear wants, serial engines, and the reliable feeling that the story is going somewhere. Main sources: [[wiki/sources/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|5 Simple Rules For Turning Your Cool Idea Into A Screenplay]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-dan-brown-masterclass-complete-summary|Dan Brown Masterclass Complete Summary]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-08-duffer-brothers-masterclass-complete-summary|Duffer Brothers Masterclass Complete Summary]], and [[wiki/sources/2026-04-08-david-baldacci-masterclass-summary|David Baldacci Masterclass Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It determines whether structure feels alive instead of merely correct.
- It connects macro planning to the lived experience of reading or watching moment by moment.
- It gives the vault a shared concept that bridges screenwriting and thriller-fiction craft.
- It externalizes one of the most reusable craft ideas across multiple writing sources.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The 2010 screenplay article emphasizes setup and payoff, beginning-middle-end clarity, and major turning points before drafting.
- Sorkin argues that if a scene is not moving the story forward, the script is standing still.
- Sorkin also treats exposition as something that must be hidden inside conflict or purpose so the audience never feels stranded in explanation.
- Brown defines suspense as a rhythm of promises and fulfillments that keeps generating fresh reasons to continue.
- The Duffer Brothers stress that propulsion begins with a simple central drive strong enough to carry multiple storylines and a season engine, not just an individual scene hook.
- Baldacci treats chapter length, chapter purpose, and the `big pop` opening as concrete propulsion tools that create repeated forward pressure.
- Brown designs chapters to begin with urgency and end with unresolved energy, twists, or deferred revelations.
- Across the sources, the reader or audience should feel guided rather than inert or confused.

# Source Comparisons

- The 2010 screenplay article talks about propulsion mainly at the structural level: acts, beats, setup, and payoff.
- Sorkin works at both structural and scene scale, stressing movement from point A to point B inside each scene.
- Brown works explicitly at the suspense scale, focusing on hooks, deferred answers, and serial momentum.
- The Duffer Brothers work at the series-engine scale, focusing on a simple core drive that can unify a pilot and an ongoing show.
- Baldacci works at the chapter-and-opening scale, focusing on a major opening disturbance and compact chapters that each earn their place.
- All five agree that momentum is not accidental, but they locate the main control point in different places: outline, scene construction, suspense rhythm, series engine, or chapter design.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much propulsion can flatten breathing room if the work never earns stillness.
- Too little propulsion makes even good ideas feel static or under-shaped.
- The right balance likely varies by medium, genre, and reader expectation.
- The vault now also shows that propulsion can fail for opposite reasons: weak engines at the macro level or weak scene and chapter execution at the micro level.
