# What It Is

Art making is the process of translating perception, memory, taste, and form into an object or experience that can carry meaning for other people. In the current source base, art making is neither pure self-expression nor pure technical display. It is a deliberate shaping of materials, scale, color, form, texture, and source material so that the work can create contact with a viewer.

# Why It Matters

- It opens a durable art branch in the vault rather than leaving visual creativity split across design pages.
- It captures the making side of art, not just the interpretive side.
- It creates a home for future sources on sculpture, painting, exhibition, curation, and visual composition.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Jeff Koons treats scale, color, form, and texture as the core tools of artistic meaning.
- Koons also argues that everyday objects, personal iconography, and art history can all become valid material for serious work.
- Koons balances intuition with technical exactness, showing that discovery and precision can coexist in one practice.
- David Carson contributes a neighboring visual principle: image, type, texture, and irregularity all communicate before formal explanation begins.

# Source Comparisons

- Koons is strongest on art as a full material language with philosophical claims about acceptance, meaning, and viewer participation.
- Carson is stronger on visual urgency and anti-safe expression, especially in typography and composition.
- Together they suggest that visual making is both emotional and structural: freedom matters, but so do material decisions.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Art making can be made too technical and lose life, or too anti-technical and lose control.
- The branch still needs sources that challenge Koons's generosity-centered philosophy from more adversarial or formally austere traditions.
