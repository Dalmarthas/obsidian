# What It Is

Visual hierarchy is the deliberate ordering of attention inside a design so the viewer can tell what to notice first, what supports it, and what can wait. In the current source base, hierarchy is built through focal points, contrast, separation, pattern, grouping, and selective emphasis rather than through text explanation alone.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the creativity branch a reusable concept for attention guidance rather than leaving these mechanics buried inside one source summary.
- It connects perceptual psychology to practical design choices such as scale, placement, contrast, spacing, and compartmentalization.
- It creates a bridge between expressive design and usable information design, where the problem is not only style but orientation cost.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source argues that viewers scan visuals in fragments, so the designer must provide a clear entry point instead of assuming full-scene comprehension.
- Focal points help establish that entry point by making the primary element visibly dominant.
- Contrast between foreground and background improves legibility and clarifies importance.
- Repeated patterns and consistent organization help viewers sort related information quickly.
- Simplification and grouping reduce cognitive clutter and make the intended order of reading easier to follow.
- Digital interactivity can strengthen hierarchy when motion is used sparingly to reinforce where attention should go.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strong hierarchy improves comprehension, but it can become manipulative if it forces attention without representing the underlying importance honestly.
- Simplification often clarifies, but dense material may require richer structure rather than aggressive reduction.
- Motion can strengthen hierarchy in digital media, but unnecessary animation competes with the focal point instead of supporting it.
