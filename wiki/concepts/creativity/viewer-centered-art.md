# What It Is

Viewer-centered art is the claim that a work does not fully exist as art until a viewer encounters it, interprets it, and completes its meaning. In the current source base, Jeff Koons states this most directly: the object matters, but the beholder's response is what activates the work.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a precise home for Koons's most distinctive philosophical claim.
- It links the art branch to broader questions of audience participation already present elsewhere in the vault.
- It helps explain why generosity, legibility, and emotional openness matter in visual work.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Koons says art resides in the viewer rather than in the object alone.
- The viewer's own history, associations, and emotional response complete the narrative of the work.
- No special expertise is required for the work to become meaningful.
- The artist's responsibility is partly to create a generous enough object that this encounter can happen.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- A strong viewer-centered model can democratize art, but it can also flatten differences in historical knowledge, power, and context.
- If meaning is too fully outsourced to the viewer, it becomes harder to say what the artist is responsible for shaping.
