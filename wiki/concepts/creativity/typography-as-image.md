# What It Is

Typography as image is the idea that type communicates visually and emotionally before or alongside the words it spells. In the current source base, letters are not neutral vessels. Their spacing, weight, texture, arrangement, and voice help create the meaning of the message.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a more precise concept than generic `typography`.
- It bridges the design branch and the advertising branch through the shared idea that visual voice matters.
- It helps explain why typographic choices can create tone, identity, and memorability even before semantic parsing finishes.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Carson explicitly says the word is an image and that typography should affect people emotionally.
- Font choice, spacing, capitalization, collision, and hand lettering all send messages before literal reading is complete.
- Carson argues that legibility is not the same as communicative power.
- Goodby Silverstein shows a brand-level version of the same principle through typographic and audio identity work like `Got Milk?`, where voice and visual treatment make a line memorable.

# Source Comparisons

- Carson treats typography as a primary expressive medium and is willing to bend readability when the message and audience justify it.
- Goodby Silverstein uses typography more strategically and identity-wise, as part of the total voice of a campaign or brand.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Typography can deepen meaning, but it can also become self-conscious style if it overwhelms the content.
- Experimental type raises real accessibility questions, especially outside the cultural contexts that made it legible in the first place.
