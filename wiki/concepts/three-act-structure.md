# What It Is

Three-act structure is the classical narrative frame that divides a screenplay into setup, conflict, and resolution. In the current source base, it is treated as a practical storytelling language that guides pacing and audience orientation rather than as a formula that eliminates originality. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|5 Simple Rules For Turning Your Cool Idea Into A Screenplay]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the audience a familiar sense of progression.
- It helps a writer locate turning points, escalation, and payoff.
- It supports planning because the writer can reason about structural functions before drafting scenes.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Act One: establish character, world, and story direction.
- Act Two: sustain conflict and obstacles.
- Act Three: resolve the dramatic question and climax.
- The source also pairs the three-act model with a nine-point beat heuristic: setup, dramatic question, inciting incident, break into act two, initial growth, midpoint, all-is-lost moment, second turning point, and resolution.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The source argues strongly for the model, but many strong films bend timing or emphasis.
- The nine-point sequence is presented as useful scaffolding, not as an inviolable page-by-page law.
