# What It Is

Writing process and revision is the larger workflow that governs how a writer prepares, drafts, evaluates, and reshapes a work over time. In the current source base, the strongest pattern is not mystical inspiration but disciplined preparation, tolerance for imperfect early material, and ruthless later shaping. Main sources: [[wiki/sources/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|5 Simple Rules For Turning Your Cool Idea Into A Screenplay]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]], and [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-dan-brown-masterclass-complete-summary|Dan Brown Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It determines whether craft knowledge becomes repeatable work rather than occasional luck.
- It connects pre-draft planning, draft generation, and revision into one system.
- It gives the vault a cross-source concept for process discipline rather than leaving that knowledge split across source-local pages.
- It helps separate source-specific habits from transferable process principles.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The 2010 screenplay article emphasizes outlining before drafting and learning formatting early to avoid preventable cleanup later.
- Sorkin emphasizes finishing the draft before trying to perfect it and then rewriting diagnostically against concrete problems.
- Brown emphasizes protecting the process through routine, draft-stage separation, placeholder use, and permission to write badly before distilling.
- Both Sorkin and Brown treat deletion and cutting as central rather than incidental to good writing.
- Across the sources, preparation is important, but so is avoiding paralysis or perfectionism before there is enough material to shape.

# Source Comparisons

- The 2010 screenplay article is strongest on pre-draft discipline: outline, structure, and format first.
- Sorkin is strongest on post-draft discipline: finish the block of marble, then cut and diagnose.
- Brown covers the whole arc most explicitly: process protection, side-by-side drafting modes, and later distillation.
- Together they suggest a layered workflow: prepare enough to know what you are making, draft far enough to have something real, then revise without sentimentality.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much planning can become avoidance; too little planning can create expensive chaos later.
- Some writers edit while drafting more than these sources recommend, so the right balance may vary by temperament and medium.
- Process discipline is transferable, but exact rituals are probably not.
