# What It Is

Visual literacy is the ability to read, build, and judge meaning through images, sequences, form, rhythm, spatial relationships, and visual contrast rather than through words alone. In the current source base, it is not ornamental taste. It is a real language capacity.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a shared concept between filmmaking and design rather than leaving image-thinking trapped inside medium-specific pages.
- It helps distinguish expressive visual choices from mere decoration or technical display.
- It sharpens the idea that images persuade, orient, unsettle, and narrate before they are verbally explained.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Scorsese argues that visual literacy is simply literacy, that moving images are a language, and that cinema creates meaning through juxtaposition, frame choice, and sequence rather than through single stills alone.
- Scorsese also insists that filmmakers should know cinema history because earlier directors built the grammar of cuts, movement, framing, and visual emphasis that later artists inherit or resist.
- Carson argues that graphic design communicates emotionally and structurally before literal reading catches up, which makes visual response part of the message rather than packaging around it.
- Lynch treats image, atmosphere, sound, and felt texture as primary meaning-carriers, not as decorative support for a script that already contains the whole film.

# Source Comparisons

- Scorsese is strongest on visual literacy as historical and grammatical knowledge: film meaning emerges from shots, cuts, sequence, color, and camera design.
- Carson is strongest on pre-verbal communication: type, layout, and surface already tell the viewer how to feel and where to look.
- Lynch is strongest on atmosphere and emotional image-logic: visual meaning can be intuitive, dreamlike, and difficult to paraphrase without becoming weaker.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Visual fluency can degenerate into style worship if the maker becomes more interested in citation or surface than in what the image is doing.
- Strong image-thinking can also become obscure if it loses contact with audience orientation, narrative legibility, or emotional stakes.
