# What It Is

Community-based conservation is a model that starts with local people as partners rather than obstacles and builds environmental recovery through solutions that also improve daily life. In the current source base, the TACARE approach is the clearest example: listen first, address real needs, build trust, and align livelihood with ecological protection. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-conversation-jane-goodall-masterclass-complete-summary|Conversation Jane Goodall Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It is more politically and socially durable than top-down conservation imposed from outside.
- It recognizes that people living near ecological crisis are not abstract variables but decision-makers with needs and knowledge.
- It links ethics and effectiveness by refusing to separate environmental care from human flourishing.
- It gives the vault a reusable applied-method concept for future environmental work.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Start with listening sessions rather than predetermined expert solutions.
- Use local staff who understand culture and language.
- Address multiple needs together: food, health, education, environment, and income.
- Support alternatives to destructive practices through training, microcredit, and sustainable agriculture.
- Add technology for mapping and monitoring only after trust and local structure are in place.
- Success is measured not only by restored land but also by stronger partnership and visible improvement in life.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Community partnership can be slowed or distorted by local power imbalances if participation is only nominal.
- Long-term trust-based work is often harder to fund than flashy short-term interventions.
