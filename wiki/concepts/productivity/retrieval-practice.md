# What It Is

Retrieval practice is the act of trying to recall knowledge or execution cues without immediately looking at the answer or score. In the current vault, it is more than a study trick. It is the point where learning proves it is actually available. The concept matters because recall attempts seem to strengthen access routes to memory more effectively than passive re-exposure alone.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the learning branch a sharper test for whether something is usable instead of merely familiar.
- It explains why `look away and reconstruct it` is often more valuable than one more reread or replay.
- It adds a pressure-performance implication: retrieval-based learning may produce memory that degrades less under acute stress.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-11-barbara-oakley-learning-how-to-learn|Barbara Oakley Learning How to Learn]] treats retrieval practice as one of the main correctives to illusions of competence.
- The Oakley branch links retrieval to chunking, spaced repetition, and the difference between exposure and usable recall.
- [[wiki/sources/2016-12-18-the-practice-strategy-that-could-bulletproof-your-memory-under-pressure|The Practice Strategy That Could "Bulletproof" Your Memory Under Pressure]] sharpens the concept by showing a specific difference between repeated study and repeated recall under later stress.
- That same source adds an executional translation: in performance domains, memory attempts should begin during learning, even when recall is partial, halting, or error-prone.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/chunking|Chunking]], retrieval practice is less about what knowledge becomes and more about how the learner tests and strengthens access to it. Chunking names the memory unit; retrieval practice pressures the system to make that unit available on demand.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/effective-practice|Effective Practice]], retrieval practice is narrower and more memory-specific. Effective practice covers session quality broadly; retrieval practice names one especially important method inside those sessions.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/focused-and-diffuse-learning-modes|Focused and Diffuse Learning Modes]], retrieval practice is less about mental state and more about task design. One concerns how the mind alternates between concentration and integration; the other concerns whether learning is being forced into recall.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/quiet-eye|Quiet Eye]], retrieval practice stabilizes memory access while quiet eye stabilizes perceptual attention at execution time. Both matter for performance under pressure, but they operate at different stages of the chain.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Retrieval practice is easy to oversimplify into `just test yourself more`, even though the right timing, difficulty, and feedback likely vary across domains.
- The current vault still needs stronger evidence on how retrieval practice transfers from list learning to embodied performance, creative work, or complex reasoning.
- Recall attempts can reveal weakness, but in some tasks they may also harden bad patterns unless paired with correction.
- The branch still needs a cleaner distinction between retrieval practice for symbolic information and retrieval practice for movement, interpretation, or sequencing.
