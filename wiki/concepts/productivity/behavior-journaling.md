# What It Is

Behavior journaling is the practice of measuring and recording daily alignment, sabotage, and repeated actions so behavior becomes visible enough to steer. In the current vault, it is framed less as expressive writing and more as an operational control tool.

# Why It Matters

- It makes invisible behavioral drift easier to notice.
- It gives goal systems a feedback loop instead of leaving them at the level of intention.
- It adds a durable measurement concept to the productivity branch.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Chase Hughes source calls journaling the closest thing to a `steering wheel` for behavior.
- It recommends daily ratings, such as how strongly a person prioritized the future self that day.
- It also recommends weekly review so routines, cues, and goals can be adjusted rather than merely wished for.

# Source Comparisons

- The current source is strongest on measurement-for-control: journaling matters because what is measured can be adjusted.
- It overlaps with the vault's broader framework language by turning self-change into something tracked, reviewed, and iterated rather than merely intended.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Tracking can become self-surveillance if it is too moralized or disconnected from useful behavior change.
- Ratings are only as good as the honesty and interpretation behind them.
- The branch still needs comparison sources on when journaling improves behavior and when it becomes performative bookkeeping.
