# What It Is

Self-disruption is the strategic discipline of changing, cannibalizing, or reinventing parts of your own business before competitors or market shifts do it for you. In the current source base, this is not novelty worship. It is controlled, values-aware renewal.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a reusable concept for the hard strategic move of attacking your own success before it decays into entitlement.
- It connects innovation to brand guardrails and execution capacity instead of treating disruption as free-floating hype.
- It sharpens the difference between offensive leadership and reactive defense.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Schultz states the principle directly: if you do not cannibalize your own business, competitors will do it for you.
- Schultz also ties self-disruption to premium differentiation, investment ahead of the curve, and the need to stay within categories and moves the brand has actually earned.
- Iger adds a related large-company version: strategy must keep evolving through technology shifts, changing distribution, and long-term brand protection rather than protecting yesterday's model for its own sake.

# Source Comparisons

- Schultz is strongest on entrepreneurial offensiveness: do not let the market force your hand, and do not confuse current success with future safety.
- Iger is strongest on scaled strategic adaptation: change has to be filtered through portfolio logic, technology shifts, and long-term value creation.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Self-disruption can keep a company alive, but it can also become self-harm if leaders chase novelty without operational readiness or brand permission.
- Protecting the core and changing the core are both necessary; the real problem is deciding which elements are essence and which are inertia.
