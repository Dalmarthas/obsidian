# What It Is

Franchise stewardship is the responsibility to manage legacy characters, worlds, and audience expectations in a way that preserves dramatic integrity rather than merely exploiting recognizable intellectual property. In the current vault, it is not the same as preserving a brand mechanically. It means protecting the narrative identity, emotional payoff, and coherent use of a property while still allowing renewal and risk.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a more precise name for a recurring failure mode inside blockbuster culture.
- It distinguishes healthy franchise continuation from cynical property maintenance.
- It clarifies why audience disappointment with reboots and sequels is often deeper than simple nostalgia resistance.
- It creates a usable bridge between story craft, audience trust, and industrial decision-making.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The first Chris Gore source argues that legacy properties fail when spectacle and nostalgia replace earned character payoff.
- The second Chris Gore source sharpens this into `studio malpractice`: major franchises can still be mishandled even with vast money, brand awareness, and infrastructure.
- The current branch implies that iconic characters require coherent struggle, consequence, and emotional logic rather than only visual recognition.
- Audience disappointment grows when studios market a continuation as meaningful stewardship but deliver a flattened, committee-managed product.
- The concept also overlaps with risk aversion: stewardship is weakened when executives treat the property as a box-checking machine instead of a living dramatic system.

# Source Comparisons

- Chris Gore is strongest on naming the industrial failure: stewardship collapses when executives and committees lose contact with what made the property meaningful.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/character-driven-storytelling|Character-Driven Storytelling]] overlaps at the narrative level: a franchise remains alive only if the characters still have real wants, conflicts, and arcs.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/audience-as-collaborator|Audience as Collaborator]] overlaps at the reception level: viewers notice when a property is being managed as a logo instead of as a story.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/factory-filmmaking|Factory Filmmaking]] overlaps at the system level: stewardship failures are one of the clearest symptoms of the factory logic Gore criticizes.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Stewardship can become too conservative if protecting identity turns into fear of change.
- Reinvention can also fail if novelty ignores the emotional and thematic logic that made the property matter.
- The vault still lacks strong counterexamples from franchise architects, producers, or studio executives explaining how difficult the balancing act is in practice.
