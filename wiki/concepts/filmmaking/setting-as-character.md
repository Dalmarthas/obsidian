# What It Is

Setting as character is the idea that place is not a neutral backdrop but an active force with texture, history, emotional charge, and narrative consequence. In the current source base, a writer often cannot get the story moving until the ground under the wheels is real enough to support it.

# Why It Matters

- It upgrades setting from scenery to narrative pressure.
- It links place, time, memory, and emotional truth inside one reusable concept.
- It gives the vault a durable home for Rushdie's strongest place-based fiction lessons.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Rushdie treats location as crucial enough that characters cannot really move until the world beneath them feels concrete.
- Place can be city, room, lost homeland, future world, or impossible invented place, as long as it is vivid and emotionally charged.
- Time matters as part of place: the same location in one era is not the same place in another era.
- Rushdie recommends writing place narratively rather than leaning on lazy adjective stacks.
- Strong settings create remembered images in readers' heads and can be loved, hated, mourned, or desired like people.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Overwritten setting can become static display if it does not interact with character or story.
- Too little setting leaves the work ungrounded, especially in expansive or surreal narratives.
