# What It Is

The `Hero Goal Sequence` is Eric Edson's micro-structural unit for feature storytelling: a short run of pages in which the protagonist pursues a concrete short-term goal, receives fresh news or a meaningful surprise, and is forced into a new immediate objective. In the current vault, it functions as a practical bridge between broad act structure and scene-by-scene momentum.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the writer a repeatable way to test whether the script is genuinely moving.
- It prevents large structure talk from becoming too abstract to diagnose page-level drift.
- It ties character activity directly to propulsion rather than treating plot movement as separate from the protagonist's behavior.
- It offers a simple audit tool for unfinished drafts: where the goal, fresh news, or directional change disappears, deadness often begins.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Each sequence is usually described as a 3 to 7 page unit with a visible short-term objective.
- The sequence pattern is goal -> pursuit -> fresh news -> transition to a new goal.
- The model assumes an active hero rather than a drifting or purely reactive protagonist.
- Fresh news matters because it produces real change rather than repetitive struggle.
- The source presents a full-feature heuristic of roughly 21 to 23 sequences, with strong concentration around act turns and the midpoint.
- The same branch also stresses that repetition is a warning sign: if successive units do not materially change the story state, structure has become mechanical rather than alive.

# Source Comparisons

- Edson is strongest on repeatable micro-structure: he names a portable unit that writers can use to outline or diagnose a script.
- Sorkin overlaps at the scene-function level, arguing that if the story is not moving, the script is standing still, but he is less prescriptive about sequence counts.
- Brown overlaps at the suspense-unit level: each chapter or section should renew question pressure, though his preferred vocabulary is hooks, promises, and answers rather than `fresh news`.
- Rhimes overlaps through act turns and A/B/C story motion, but she works more at episode-machine scale than at fixed feature-sequence scale.
- Truby overlaps in demanding deeper architecture and active character pressure, but he would likely resist turning the sequence count into shallow formula if premise and character design are weak.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The model is powerful as a diagnostic tool, but can become rigid if copied mechanically.
- Some narratives create momentum through atmosphere, rhythm, or fragmentation rather than through clearly bounded short-term goals.
- The heuristic may be most useful in mainstream feature storytelling and less universal than its strongest claims imply.
- Writers still need scene truth, subtext, and tonal distinctiveness; a functioning sequence pattern does not guarantee aliveness on its own.
