# What It Is

Three-act structure is the classical narrative frame that divides a story into setup, conflict, and resolution. In the current source base, it is treated as a useful orienting scaffold, but not as the whole story of structure. Main sources: [[wiki/sources/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|5 Simple Rules For Turning Your Cool Idea Into A Screenplay]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-dan-brown-masterclass-complete-summary|Dan Brown Masterclass Complete Summary]], and [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-truby-screenwriting-and-story-structure|John Truby Screenwriting and Story Structure]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the audience a familiar sense of progression.
- It helps a writer locate turning points, escalation, and payoff.
- It supports planning because the writer can reason about structural functions before drafting scenes.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Act One: establish character, world, and story direction.
- Act Two: sustain conflict and obstacles.
- Act Three: resolve the dramatic question and climax.
- Sorkin's compressed phrasing: chase the hero up a tree, throw rocks at them, then get them down or leave them there after the attempt.
- The escape or payoff mechanism should be prepared in Act One rather than appearing magically in Act Three.
- Exposition should orient the audience before the inciting action, but it should be hidden inside conflict or purpose.
- If the protagonist or inciting action still has not emerged by roughly page 20 to 25, the script is likely in trouble.
- Brown's thriller model does not foreground act terminology, but it still assumes a shaped arc with a legible dramatic question, escalation, and eventual payoff.
- The source also pairs the three-act model with a nine-point beat heuristic: setup, dramatic question, inciting incident, break into act two, initial growth, midpoint, all-is-lost moment, second turning point, and resolution.
- Edson adds a more granular classical reading: the three acts can be understood as `three worlds` of ordinary life, special-world pressure, and final maturity.
- He also frames the end of Act One and Act Two as `Stunning Surprises` that should hit as strong reversals, not merely as technical marker posts.
- Edson's model further implies that the act scaffold should be filled by smaller units of goal pursuit and fresh-news turns rather than by large undifferentiated stretches.
- Hauge sharpens the early-act diagnostic layer: Act One should establish everyday life, build empathy, and orient the viewer toward the hero's visible goal before the story fully accelerates.
- He also argues that rushing too quickly into the pursuit can weaken the screenplay later by burning structural fuel before the audience is properly invested.
- Truby adds the strongest challenge to shallow act thinking: reducing structure to visible three-act formulas can mislead writers if the deeper story anatomy, genre logic, and character-based transformation are underbuilt.
- In Truby's framing, the writer still needs structure, but the more important question is whether the story's internal architecture is strong enough to justify the beats.
- The second Truby source reinforces that critique from the professionalization angle: writers often cling to act formulas as shortcuts because they underestimate how much craft still lies beyond them.

# Source Comparisons

- The 2010 screenplay article is the most prescriptive about act proportions and beat placement.
- Edson is strongest on linking the act frame to emotionally sharp reversals and to a repeated sequence-level engine inside the acts.
- Hauge is strongest on first-act setup quality: empathy, ordinary-world context, and a visible outer goal should be in place before the screenplay makes its major launch.
- Sorkin defends three-act structure as deeply embedded audience language, but he emphasizes dramatic function over page-count obedience.
- Brown is the least theory-forward about acts, yet his thriller method still relies on equivalent structural movement through question, escalation, and payoff.
- Truby is the most suspicious of act formulas as teaching shorthand; he treats them as too shallow unless anchored in deeper character, genre, and premise work.
- Across the four, structure matters most as a delivery system for attention and payoff rather than as a formula to imitate mechanically.
- The second Truby source sharpens why formula persists: it offers psychological relief to undertrained writers, even when it does not solve the real story problem.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The source argues strongly for the model, but many strong films bend timing or emphasis.
- The nine-point sequence is presented as useful scaffolding, not as an inviolable page-by-page law.
- Dialogue-heavy scripts may distort page-count expectations, so page numbers are signals rather than laws.
- Edson's `three worlds` and sequence counts sharpen act structure into a more detailed planning tool, but that extra precision can invite false confidence if the emotional core is weak.
- Hauge sharpens a separate first-act tension: if the script delays empathy and orientation too long, viewers drift, but if it rushes too fast, the later story may feel exhausted or emotionally weightless.
- Truby introduces a live branch tension: some writers benefit from act scaffolds, while others may use them to avoid the harder work of designing deeper story anatomy.
