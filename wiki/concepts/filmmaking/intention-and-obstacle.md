# What It Is

Intention and obstacle is the claim that drama begins when somebody urgently wants something and something substantial stands in the way. In the current vault, this is the clearest foundational rule for screenwriting craft and the organizing principle for story, character, and scene design. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the story a driveshaft rather than a pile of interesting material.
- It turns facts or situations into drama by introducing pressure and resistance.
- It scales from the full script down to individual scenes.
- It helps test weak premises: if the want is casual or the obstacle is flimsy, the drama is underpowered.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The source defines it as the bedrock of story: somebody wants something, something is standing in the way.
- The want should be strong and urgent, not decorative.
- The obstacle must be formidable enough that easy fixes do not dissolve the story.
- The protagonist does not have to win, but must genuinely try.
- Character emerges from the tactics used against the obstacle.
- Hauge sharpens the same foundation from the audience-clarity side: the want should not only exist, it should be visible enough that the viewer can imagine the finish line.
- He also implies that this clarity must be prepared in Act One rather than left fuzzy while the script tries to compensate with theme, subplot, or atmosphere.

# Source Comparisons

- Sorkin is strongest on the dramatic bedrock: urgent want plus resistance generates drama.
- Hauge is strongest on early legibility: the audience must be able to see the protagonist's outer motivation clearly enough for the pursuit to matter.
- Together they imply that a screenplay can fail in two opposite ways: the want may be emotionally real but too vague to track, or it may be technically visible without enough pressure to feel urgent.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strong intention and obstacle are necessary, but not sufficient; tone, structure, and execution still matter.
- Some looser or more atmospheric forms may distribute pressure differently, even if they still contain hidden forms of want and resistance.
- Visible outer motivation improves clarity, but some stories may deliberately obscure the finish line for effect and still succeed if they compensate elsewhere.
