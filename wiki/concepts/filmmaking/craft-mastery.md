# What It Is

Craft mastery is the view that professional creative work requires a long-term relationship to technique, feedback, humility, and repeated skill expansion rather than a one-time idea or a shortcut formula. In this vault's current evidence, the concept includes two linked realities: the craft itself is technically deeper than beginners expect, and the person pursuing the craft must survive the emotional strain long enough to become genuinely good.

# Why It Matters

- It protects the vault from treating storytelling as only inspiration plus basic structure.
- It gives a home to the long-horizon professionalization layer behind screenwriting success.
- It connects technical learning and psychological endurance instead of treating them as separate problems.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Truby argues that most aspiring writers underestimate the complexity of storytelling, especially plotting and professional-level story design.
- He treats simplistic formulas as beginner crutches that do not replace mastery of deeper technique.
- He argues that many writers are undertrained rather than merely unlucky.
- He also argues that years of isolation, rejection, and weak external reinforcement eliminate many writers before skill has matured.
- In this framing, ongoing education, humility, and continued tool-building are professional requirements rather than optional extras.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Mastery language can become perfectionistic if it turns into an excuse never to ship work.
- Anti-shortcut rhetoric can be healthy, but it can also become vague unless the writer converts it into concrete learning habits.
- The vault still needs more sources that explicitly connect artistic vocation, technical training, and psychological endurance in one model.
