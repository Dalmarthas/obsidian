# What It Is

Premise testing is the pre-draft discipline of interrogating whether a story idea can actually carry a full narrative. In the current source base, the core test is whether the premise contains a compelling desire line, a credible and durable opponent, enough conflict to sustain the medium, and a protagonist whose weakness generates real transformation pressure.

# Why It Matters

- It catches structural weakness before months of drafting are spent on a premise that cannot support the work.
- It links character design to plot design at the earliest possible stage.
- It gives the vault a cleaner bridge between screenwriting structure and writing-process discipline.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Truby treats premise interrogation as a serious gate before drafting, not a casual brainstorm.
- He recommends testing whether the story has enough conflict to sustain roughly feature length rather than collapsing after the setup.
- He also frames protagonist weakness and desire as structural variables, not just character notes.
- Loglines and scene-sequence planning are presented as tools for testing the premise under pressure before pages are written.
- The current generic storytelling source strengthens the same layer with a simpler development stack: generate many ideas, choose the one that persists or genuinely interests people, distill it into a vivid logline, and keep that logline as a North Star through later development.
- It also adds a reusable self-questioning loop: who is this about, what do they want, why now, what stands in the way, and what happens if they fail?
- The roundtable source adds a sharper negative filter: a vivid arena, gimmick, or setting is not yet a premise if the writer has not built active conflict, stakes, and emotional pressure into it.
- It also adds ideation pressure through exercises like generating 100 ideas or 20 versions of a beat before trusting the first concept that appears.
- Simon Rich adds the strongest current abundance-before-filtering layer: premise testing works better when it has a large pool of candidates to reject rather than one precious idea being protected from scrutiny.
- Glenn Gers adds the cleanest explicit interrogation method in the branch: who is it about, what do they want, why can't they get it, what do they do, why doesn't that work, and how does it end.
- Gers also strengthens the idea that these questions can be reused across scales, so premise testing is not only a front-end filter but a continuing check on whether the story still has a living engine.

# Source Comparisons

- The 2010 screenplay article also stresses outlining before drafting, but Truby pushes the earlier question harder: whether the premise itself deserves a draft at all.
- Sorkin overlaps through objective and obstacle, but he is stronger on scene mechanics once the story is already moving.
- The Duffer Brothers overlap through layered development and not drafting too early; Truby sharpens that instinct into a more explicit stress test of premise strength.
- Brown overlaps through the warning that most ideas are not worth writing, but Truby expresses the test in more structural terms.
- The current generic source is strongest on beginner-accessible premise clarification: logline precision, idea filtering, and asking the right developmental questions before or during outlining.
- Simon Rich is strongest on premise supply: testing improves when generation is abundant, playful, and low-stakes before the stronger filtering questions arrive.
- Gers is strongest on portable question-driven clarity: he gives the branch a small repeatable framework that can expose premise weakness without requiring a larger architecture system.
- The roundtable source is strongest on the boredom-risk side: even ideas that sound attractive can still fail if they are mostly arena and too little story.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Premise rigor can save time, but it can also become a perfectionist gate that blocks exploratory discovery.
- Some stories reveal their deeper premise only through drafting, so the vault should not treat pre-draft testing as infallible.
- The right threshold for `enough conflict` varies by medium, form, and genre.
- There is also a tension between testing ideas on others and protecting a fragile idea long enough for it to become specific.
- Strong ideation exercises can push a writer past cliche, but they can also become productivity theater if they replace actual choice and commitment.
- Simon Rich sharpens a final upstream tension: abundance improves odds, but writers can still hide inside endless premise generation to avoid selecting a real direction.
- Gers sharpens a final tension: minimalist question frameworks are clarifying, but they can become superficial if the writer answers them in generic terms instead of discovering emotionally specific answers.
