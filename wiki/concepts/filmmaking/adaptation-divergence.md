# What It Is

Adaptation divergence is the principle that once a story moves from one medium to another, even small changes can trigger large downstream differences in structure, character, and causality. In this vault, Martin's strongest contribution is that the original work and its adaptation should be treated as distinct organisms rather than forced into one artificially synchronized narrative.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents false expectations of one-to-one fidelity between novel and screen version.
- It explains why small production changes can become major story changes later.
- It protects original-work integrity from being rewritten to match adaptation drift.
- It gives the vault a durable home for cross-medium storytelling pressure.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Martin's source is explicit that the novels and television adaptation are separate works with separate operational pressures.
- The same source sharpens the butterfly-effect rule:
  small changes in adaptation can create major later divergence.
- Production constraints such as budget, scheduling, legal rules, and platform standards can force adaptation decisions that the original novel never had to solve.
- Martin also models a boundary rule:
  adaptation influence can be acknowledged without retroactively altering the source novels to stay aligned.
- The current *Fight Club* source adds a more local screenplay-level version of the same principle:
  the adapter cannot preserve novel interiority by simple transfer, and must build external behavioral and visual equivalents instead.

# Source Comparisons

- Martin is currently the clearest source in the vault on adaptation as divergence rather than as degradation or fidelity scorekeeping.
- The current *Fight Club* source is strongest on how divergence happens inside scene construction:
  inward prose becomes action, props, ritual, environment, and contradiction.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/showrunning|Showrunning]] overlaps by showing that television decisions are shaped by production and institutional reality, not only by pure literary preference.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/filmmaking|Filmmaking]] overlaps by showing that every medium translates story through its own constraints and collaborative machinery.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/externalizing-interiority|Externalizing Interiority]] overlaps by turning the adaptation problem into a usable craft move rather than only a theory of medium difference.
- Together these pages suggest a useful compiled rule:
  the more complex the production context becomes, the less realistic total structural fidelity becomes.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much separation talk can become an excuse for shallow adaptation rather than a realistic acknowledgment of medium difference.
- Too much fidelity pressure can also produce dead adaptation choices when the new medium needs different compression, staging, or emphasis.
- The concept still needs more direct examples from other authors, adapters, and showrunners.
