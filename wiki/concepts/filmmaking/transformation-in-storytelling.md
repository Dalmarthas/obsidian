# What It Is

Transformation in storytelling is the idea that story matters because something real changes: in the protagonist, in a relationship, in the surrounding moral or emotional field, or in the audience's understanding of itself. In the current source base, transformation is not an optional emotional garnish. It is one of the reasons goal, flaw, stakes, and pressure need to be designed carefully in the first place.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents story from collapsing into event sequence without insight.
- It connects plot pressure to emotional meaning.
- It gives the vault a cleaner home for the question `what actually changes here, and why should anyone care?`

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source explicitly argues that stories exist to illuminate transformation, not merely to entertain.
- It connects transformation to flaw, goal, motivation, and stakes, implying that these are not separate checklist items but parts of one change system.
- The source also stresses emotional truth, suggesting that transformation lands when the character journey feels psychologically and morally believable rather than mechanically imposed.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Not every strong story depends on a clean protagonist arc; sometimes the transformation is relational, thematic, or audience-facing.
- Writers can overstate transformation and end up forcing false `lessons` onto characters.
- The vault still needs stronger primary-source grounding for different types of transformation across film, television, and fiction.
