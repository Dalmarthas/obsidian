# What It Is

Monologue preparation is the process of selecting, analyzing, testing, and reshaping a piece of acting material until it becomes specific, playable, and dramatically alive rather than literary, generic, or purely presentational. In the current source base, preparation is both textual and physical.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a durable home for one of the most explicit practical workflows in the current acting source.
- It turns vague `work on your piece` advice into concrete operations.
- It preserves actor-facing craft knowledge that is otherwise easy to lose in high-level acting talk.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The source emphasizes choosing monologues with real stakes, real movement, and enough substance to show what an actor can actually do.
- Actors should read the full play, identify who they are speaking to, what they want, and where the key shifts occur.
- Word-level work matters: carve out crucial words, avoid racing, and understand heightened language rather than bluffing through it.
- Grounding activities, opposite interpretations, masks, mirrors, and altered physicality are all presented as methods for breaking superficial patterns and discovering better truth.
- Monologue work is useful but incomplete because acting ultimately depends on reaction, not solitary display.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Monologues are necessary for auditions, but over-reliance on solo work can train habits that are less alive in real scene work.
- Detailed preparation helps, but overcontrol can flatten the surprise or danger that makes a performance compelling.
