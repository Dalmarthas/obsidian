# What It Is

`Outer Motivation` is Michael Hauge's term for the protagonist's visible external objective: the concrete finish line the audience can understand, track, and imagine being reached or lost. In the current vault, it matters because it sharpens several older ideas into one operational test: if the audience cannot picture what success looks like, the screenplay's engine is probably weak.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the story a legible direction early enough for viewers to invest.
- It keeps complexity from dissolving into confusion.
- It helps distinguish emotionally rich characterization from narratively unfocused wandering.
- It provides a clean diagnostic when a screenplay feels event-heavy but oddly shapeless.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Hauge argues that every compelling screenplay needs a visible finish line.
- He treats outer motivation as more foundational in early pages than thematic layering or psychological intricacy.
- The source implies that even complex narratives stay coherent because their protagonist goal can still be summarized simply.
- Weak or blurry outer motivation often explains why a script's structure later feels disjointed.
- The concept also connects to pace: if Act 1 has not oriented the audience toward the eventual pursuit, the screenplay risks either confusion or premature burnout.

# Source Comparisons

- Hauge is strongest on early legibility: the audience should know what the hero is moving toward before complexity expands.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/intention-and-obstacle|Intention and Obstacle]] overlaps closely, but Hauge emphasizes the visible finish line more explicitly than Sorkin's broader dramatic formula.
- Edson overlaps through the active hero and concrete short-term goals, though his emphasis is more on sequence movement than on the foundational finish line.
- Gers overlaps through story questions like `what do they want` and `how does it end`, but Hauge is more explicit that the goal must be visually and practically trackable.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Some stories rely on distributed desire, ensemble focus, or inward transformation, which can complicate the idea of one simple visible finish line.
- A clear outer motivation helps, but it does not automatically create emotional depth, surprise, or good scene work.
- Writers can also over-literalize the goal and flatten richer motives if they treat outer motivation as the whole story.
