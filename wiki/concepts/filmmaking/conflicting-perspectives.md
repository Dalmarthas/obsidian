# What It Is

Conflicting perspectives is a storytelling method that presents materially incompatible accounts of the same events without forcing the script to resolve them into one official version. In the current vault, the main use is not puzzle-box trickery. It is a way of turning disputed truth into dramatic energy, revealing character through self-justification, memory, and strategic narration.

# Why It Matters

- It lets the writer preserve ambiguity instead of flattening contested material into fake certainty.
- It can turn exposition into drama because every version of events also becomes a character move.
- It increases audience participation by making interpretation part of the experience.
- It gives research-heavy true-story adaptation a way to remain honest about uncertainty while still shaping a coherent film.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Sorkin source describes *The Social Network* as built from conflicting accounts in the Facebook lawsuits rather than from one authoritative narrator.
- The source explicitly compares the method to a Rashomon-style structure where subjectivity is part of the point.
- The same source also shows why the technique works best when grounded by strong intention and obstacle: the audience may not know whose story is truest, but they can still track what each person wants.
- The branch also shows that conflicting perspectives become more useful when paired with serious research and with audience trust rather than overexplanation.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Conflicting perspectives can create depth, but they can also dilute force if the audience loses the emotional through-line.
- Structural ambiguity is not automatically complexity; some stories become vaguer rather than richer when too much is left unresolved.
- The method is especially demanding when all the versions sound tonally or psychologically interchangeable.
