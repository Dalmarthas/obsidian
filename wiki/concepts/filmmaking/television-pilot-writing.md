# What It Is

Television pilot writing is the craft of building an opening episode that introduces a world, proves the series engine, launches character arcs, and makes a durable promise about what the show will keep being. In the current source base, the pilot is treated as a high-risk structural object that benefits from both deep development and strong clarity about character, audience expectation, and repeatable story design.

# Why It Matters

- A pilot has to do more than tell one story; it has to imply a continuing machine.
- It joins premise, character, world, season arc, and opening hook into one testable form.
- It gives the vault a clear home for future TV-specific writing sources instead of forcing them into feature-oriented pages.
- It externalizes the pilot-specific craft that was previously missing from the writing branch.
- It now also captures the difference between a pilot that merely starts and a pilot that teaches the audience how the series works.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Duffer Brothers prioritize a simple big idea and a central drive strong enough to organize multiple storylines.
- Characters need directional A-to-B arcs inside the pilot even if their larger journeys continue for a season.
- Worldbuilding should be legible but not overbuilt; mythology can stay partial if the engine works.
- Development should move through paragraph, story outline, scaffolding outline, and final outline before pages.
- If the pilot feels broken in script form, the repair usually belongs back at an earlier structural layer.
- Rhimes adds the premise discipline: a TV idea becomes real when it is specific enough to explain in a few sentences and broad enough to keep generating stories.
- Rhimes also frames the pilot as a promise to the audience, where episode 2 should reaffirm the show's identity rather than abruptly becoming a different machine.
- Rhimes sharpens practical pilot craft with five-act one-hour structure, A/B/C stories, strong act turns, and the `new person` device for natural exposition.
- The current Vince Gilligan source adds an anti-blandness rule: the pilot should not wait politely for interest, but can open on a flash-forward or other destabilizing image that immediately creates curiosity.
- It also sharpens the series-engine layer: the pilot's deeper promise may be not only world and premise, but the fact that this protagonist will truly change over time instead of resetting to baseline.

# Source Comparisons

- The Duffer Brothers are strongest on developmental architecture: layered outlines, series engine proof, and fixing problems at the right structural layer.
- Rhimes is strongest on operational pilot function: premise clarity, character-led promise, repeated series identity, act-turn design, and audience onboarding.
- The current Vince Gilligan source is strongest on opening violence and transformation promise: grab the audience early, then reveal that the real engine is the protagonist's moral and psychological mutation.
- Together they suggest a stronger pilot model than any one source alone: Duffer helps you not start too early, Rhimes sharpens what the finished pilot actually has to teach the audience and the network about the show, and Gilligan sharpens how an opening hook can be tied to long-horizon character change rather than mere spectacle.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Overplanning can make a pilot efficient but too airless if discovery never reenters the process.
- Underplanning can create an impressive opening premise without a durable season engine behind it.
- A pilot can also fail by proving only one episode's worth of drama; Rhimes makes the recurring promise problem more explicit than the earlier source base did.
- The current Vince Gilligan source sharpens a parallel risk: a pilot can hook aggressively and still fail if the protagonist's deeper motivation is too shallow to generate later escalation.
