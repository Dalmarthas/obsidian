# What It Is

Fiction writing is the craft of inventing narrative worlds, voices, people, and pressures compelling enough that readers experience imagined events as emotionally and aesthetically real. In the current source base, fiction is not merely plot delivery. It is the convergence of story engine, character life, formal choice, place, language, and the writer's worldview.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a broader narrative branch than screenwriting or thriller-writing alone.
- It creates a durable home for novel-specific and literary-fiction-specific craft lessons.
- It helps separate transferable fiction principles from medium-specific rules.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Rushdie provides the vault's broadest fiction architecture: six preparatory questions, flexible structure, living characters, setting as ground, strong openings, and freedom of form rooted in truth.
- Brown contributes the commercial-thriller end through urgency, suspense rhythm, and question-driven reader propulsion.
- Baldacci contributes practical novel-production discipline through chapter architecture, plausibility, research, and revision.
- Across these sources, fiction depends on both invention and discipline rather than on inspiration alone.

# Source Comparisons

- Rushdie is strongest on literary breadth: worldview, style, point of view, place, myth, and the global inheritance of fiction.
- Brown is strongest on suspense engineering and reader compulsion.
- Baldacci is strongest on workmanlike endurance, plausibility, and usable chapter craft.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Literary freedom can produce richness or shapelessness depending on the writer's control.
- Commercial pressure can sharpen narrative force or flatten stylistic risk.
- The branch still needs more sources on short fiction and minimalist fiction to balance Rushdie's expansive tendencies.
