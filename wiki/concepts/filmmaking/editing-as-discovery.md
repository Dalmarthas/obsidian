# What It Is

Editing as discovery is the idea that a film is not merely assembled after shooting but genuinely found there. In the current source base, editing is where structure, rhythm, emphasis, clarity, sound, and even meaning are re-seen under pressure from what was actually captured.

# Why It Matters

- It protects the vault against treating editing as mere cleanup or technical completion.
- It gives filmmaking pages a durable concept for the stage where plans meet reality and the work becomes legible or collapses.
- It connects film editing to the broader revision principle already present in the writing branch.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Scorsese emphasizes that cinema works through sequence and that a cut creates a third mental event in the audience, which means frame-accurate editorial judgment changes meaning.
- He treats the screenplay and even the shoot as incomplete stages: scenes continue to be rewritten through rehearsal, assembly, trimming, reshooting, test screenings, and sound or color decisions.
- Scorsese distinguishes between mathematically designed sequences and open scenes that only reveal their real shape in collaboration with the editor.
- He also insists that first assemblies should expose failure, that favorite scenes may need to die for pace, and that digital abundance creates a new need for discipline because infinite choices can blur intention.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Heavy predesign gives editors leverage, but overdesign can reduce the surprising value of footage that acquires a different life in the cut.
- Digital flexibility increases power and speed, but it can also weaken contemplation if the team reacts too quickly and keeps options open past the point of clarity.
