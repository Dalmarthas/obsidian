# What It Is

Telling details are small, precise observations that reveal more than their surface size: character, mood, pressure, social world, theme, or place. In the current vault, the idea comes from Michael Connelly's writing practice: a detail should open a window for the reader while preserving narrative momentum.

# Why It Matters

- It separates vividness from over-description.
- It gives research a sharper output target: not more facts, but the right fact.
- It helps writers make characters and places believable without pausing the story for explanation.
- It connects prose economy with reader imagination.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-23-how-to-write-vividly-well-michael-connelly|How to Write Vividly Well - Michael Connelly]] uses the example of a detective biting grooves into his glasses' earpiece at crime scenes.
- That detail implies tension, experience, and internal pressure without a paragraph of exposition.
- Connelly warns that too many details become speed bumps. The craft is choosing one detail that carries the most meaning.
- His daily and full-manuscript rewriting process functions partly as a telling-detail filter: remove the inert detail, replace the generic observation, and seed the detail that will matter later.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/concrete-specificity|Concrete Specificity]], this page is more narrative and literary. Concrete specificity makes a claim vivid and checkable; telling details make a fictional world and character feel alive.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/immersive-research|Immersive Research]], telling details are the distilled output of research. Research gathers contact with reality; telling details decide what survives on the page.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/screenplay-economy|Screenplay Economy]], this page applies a similar economy principle to fiction prose: the detail should carry story work, not merely decorate.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- A detail can feel clever but still fail if it does not reveal character, pressure, or place.
- Minimal detail can become thinness if the chosen detail is not strong enough to support imagination.
- The vault still needs more examples across literary fiction, memoir, and screenwriting to test how telling details change by medium.
