# What It Is

Screenwriting is the practice of building a dramatic blueprint for a visual medium. In this vault's current evidence, the screenplay is both a production document and a conflict engine: it must support actors, directors, rooms, and crew, while also giving the audience a clear path through idea, drive, resistance, scene movement, tone, and payoff.

# Why It Matters

- The medium places pressure on visual clarity, story economy, and scene-level functionality.
- Up-front preparation affects whether setups, payoffs, and character turns can land cleanly.
- Format matters because a screenplay is used operationally, not just interpretively.
- Conflict, audience trust, and revision discipline determine whether the script actually plays as drama rather than staying a collection of ideas.
- The branch now also shows that screenwriting sits between two pressures: structural clarity and atmospheric fidelity to the idea.
- The branch now also contains a stronger TV-specific tension: feature advice is not enough when the script has to prove a recurring series machine.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- How much of classical screenplay advice still holds for newer formats, shorter runtimes, and non-traditional narratives?
- Which parts of screenplay craft are medium-specific, and which transfer cleanly from fiction or essay writing?
- How much should a writer adopt a strong stylistic method, and how much should remain medium-agnostic craft?
- The branch now contains a live tension between outline-heavy development and intuition-led atmosphere-first creation; that tension should stay explicit rather than being flattened.
- Rhimes sharpens a separate live tension: some screenwriting rules belong to film, while television scripts must also prove recurring character and episode architecture.
