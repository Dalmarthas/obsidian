# What It Is

Screenwriting is the practice of building a dramatic blueprint for a visual medium. In this vault's current evidence, the screenplay is both a production document and a conflict engine: it must support actors, directors, rooms, and crew, while also giving the audience a clear path through idea, drive, resistance, scene movement, tone, and payoff.

# Why It Matters

- The medium places pressure on visual clarity, story economy, and scene-level functionality.
- Up-front preparation affects whether setups, payoffs, and character turns can land cleanly.
- Format matters because a screenplay is used operationally, not just interpretively.
- Conflict, audience trust, and revision discipline determine whether the script actually plays as drama rather than staying a collection of ideas.
- The branch now also shows that screenwriting sits between two pressures: structural clarity and atmospheric fidelity to the idea.
- The branch now also contains a stronger TV-specific tension: feature advice is not enough when the script has to prove a recurring series machine.
- Truby adds a third upstream pressure: before arguing about scenes or acts, the writer may need to ask whether the premise and genre design are strong enough to justify the draft at all.
- The second Truby source adds a fourth pressure: the writer may also fail long before success if they underestimate the technical and psychological demands of the craft itself.
- The current generic storytelling source adds a fifth pressure: even when the bigger architecture is in place, the writer still needs scene-by-scene clarity around goal, stakes, conflict, and transformation.
- Martell adds a sixth pressure: even in assignment work, the writer still needs a personal doorway into the material or the script risks becoming technically competent but spiritually dead.
- The current roundtable source adds a seventh pressure: scripts also fail when they confuse an interesting arena with a real story or settle for imitative, emotionally distant material.
- The current dialogue roundtable adds an eighth pressure: even strong structure can feel dead on screen if the dialogue over-explains, ignores objective, and forgets that cinema is primarily visual.
- Edson adds a ninth pressure: even a promising screenplay can sag if the hero is not moving through a visible chain of short-term goals, surprises, and renewed pursuit.
- Gers adds a tenth pressure: even before large architecture is solved, the writer may need a simple question-stack to prove that the story has a real character, desire line, obstacle pattern, failed tactics, and ending.
- Hauge adds an eleventh pressure: the first act can quietly sabotage the entire script if the hero's visible outer goal is blurry, the audience has not yet connected with ordinary life, or the story launches too hard before investment is formed.
- A second Lynch source adds a twelfth pressure: the writer often needs a light memory scaffold that keeps the film visible without overcommitting to full scenes too early.
- Simon Rich adds a thirteenth pressure: writers can cripple the process before structure even begins if they rely on rare inspiration instead of maintaining a repeatable system for generating many possible premises.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- How much of classical screenplay advice still holds for newer formats, shorter runtimes, and non-traditional narratives?
- Which parts of screenplay craft are medium-specific, and which transfer cleanly from fiction or essay writing?
- How much should a writer adopt a strong stylistic method, and how much should remain medium-agnostic craft?
- The branch now contains a live tension between outline-heavy development and intuition-led atmosphere-first creation; that tension should stay explicit rather than being flattened.
- Rhimes sharpens a separate live tension: some screenwriting rules belong to film, while television scripts must also prove recurring character and episode architecture.
- Truby sharpens a third live tension: practical act scaffolds may help, but shallow formula imitation can still produce dead scripts if genre, premise, and deeper story anatomy are weak.
- The second Truby source sharpens a fourth live tension: a writer can obsess over access or selling while neglecting the slower, lonelier work of actually becoming strong enough to use opportunity well.
- The current generic storytelling source sharpens a fifth live tension: some development advice is broad and universal, but it can become empty unless the writer converts it into hard questions and scene-level cuts.
- Martell sharpens a sixth live tension: professional assignment writing requires compromise and adaptation, but some compromises sever the writer from any living connection to the material.
- The current roundtable sharpens a seventh live tension: a project can sound vivid at the level of world or hook while still lacking the active story pressure needed to stay alive scene by scene.
- The dialogue roundtable sharpens an eighth live tension: speech can become sharper and more quotable while still weakening the screenplay if it begins replacing visual storytelling instead of serving it.
- Edson sharpens a ninth live tension: more detailed structural scaffolds can prevent drift and passivity, but they can also become rigid if the writer mistakes sequence math for the whole life of the story.
- Gers sharpens a tenth live tension: minimalist questions can rescue clarity and momentum, but they can also become superficial if the answers remain generic or emotionally unowned.
- Hauge sharpens an eleventh live tension: slow enough setup is needed for empathy and clarity, but too much pre-launch material can also become inertia if the script never coheres around the hero's visible aim.
- The second Lynch source sharpens a twelfth live tension: skeletal planning can preserve freshness and flexibility, but it can also hide undeveloped scene experience if the writer mistakes a card map for a finished dramatic design.
- Simon Rich sharpens a thirteenth live tension: abundant premise generation protects the writer from scarcity thinking, but it can also keep the work permanently upstream if no one idea is ever chosen and tested.
