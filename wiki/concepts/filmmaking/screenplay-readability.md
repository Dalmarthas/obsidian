# What It Is

Screenplay readability is the quality that makes a script rewarding to read before it is ever shot. In the current vault, that means lean prose, clear dramatic direction, forward pull, dialogue that survives read-aloud, and enough style or precision that the page itself creates trust and interest rather than feeling like a technical obstacle.

# Why It Matters

- A screenplay is usually judged as a reading experience before it becomes a production experience.
- Readability affects whether a reader believes the writer knows where the story is going.
- It bridges page-level craft with commercial reality: a script can succeed partly because people enjoy the read itself.
- It gives the vault a concept distinct from general structure or dialogue theory by focusing on how the screenplay feels as a document.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Max Landis source argues most directly that good scripts are sparse, clear, fast-moving, and `fun to read` on the page.
- The current Paul Thomas Anderson source overlaps by treating the screenplay as a blueprint that should give just enough and avoid literary over-description.
- The current dialogue roundtable overlaps by insisting on aggressive trimming, visual-first scenes, and spoken lines that justify their presence.
- The current `29 mistakes` source overlaps negatively: muddy goals, weak setup, flat escalation, and under-revised drafts damage the read even before production questions appear.
- The current image-derived studio-reader source sharpens the same branch through a reader's first-pass lens: openings need spark, action should read cleanly and directly, and dialogue should feel natural or sharp rather than expositional or generic.
- The same source also sharpens readability failure modes: flat talky openings, random/confusing plotting, repetition, and needless complexity make the script feel less controlled even when something is technically happening.

# Source Comparisons

- Max Landis is strongest on the commercial-reading test: if the script is not enjoyable as a document, it may die before anyone imagines the finished movie.
- Paul Thomas Anderson is strongest on blueprint discipline: readability improves when the page leaves room for actors, direction, and image instead of trying to become prose literature.
- The dialogue roundtable is strongest on line-level compression: readability improves when the screenplay stops explaining what the scene can already show.
- The current `29 mistakes` source is strongest on diagnostic failure patterns: readability collapses not only from wordiness, but from weak goals, vague conflict, and bad scene sequencing.
- The current studio-reader source is strongest on what a reader notices fastest:
  hook strength, clean action, vivid world cues, and the sense that the script knows what kind of reading experience it is delivering.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Readability can be misread as permission for generic simplicity or personality-free prose.
- Some dense or highly stylized scripts still read well because the density is controlled and dramatically useful.
- The branch still needs more material on how screenplay readability differs between feature specs, assignment work, and television pilots.
- Reader-friendly criteria can also favor immediate smoothness over productive difficulty, strangeness, or deliberately rough texture.
