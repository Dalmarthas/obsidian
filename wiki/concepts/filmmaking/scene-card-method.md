# What It Is

The scene card method is a lightweight pre-draft planning system that externalizes a story as movable scene units before the writer commits to full scene execution. In the current vault, Lynch presents the simplest version: early scripts can be skeletal memory tools, and the feature can be mapped through 3x5 cards that each hold a scene heading or core idea.

# Why It Matters

- It makes large story shape visible without forcing premature prose.
- It preserves fragile ideas by turning them into durable, reorderable units.
- It keeps revision cheap while the story is still fluid.
- It provides a practical bridge between intuition-led ideation and structural discipline.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Lynch presents the clearest minimalist version: catch the idea, translate it into words, treat early scripts as skeletons, and map the film through simple scene cards.
- The Duffer Brothers overlap through layered outlining: they keep development zoomed out and keep refining structural units before drafting expensive pages.
- Truby overlaps through front-loaded story engineering, sequence thinking, and the idea that major structural problems should be caught before dialogue polish.
- Edson overlaps through sequence-level architecture: stories stay alive when repeated short-term goals and reversals are made visible as modular units rather than as vague act labels.
- The current outlining-comparison source broadens the method family: cards are one option among beat sheets, notebooks, corkboards, and sequence pages whose shared job is to make pacing and macro-shape visible before scenes harden.

# Source Comparisons

- Lynch is strongest on minimal friction: the card is a memory device and a way to protect the living idea from overcomplication.
- The Duffer Brothers are strongest on recursive expansion: broad concept, paragraph, outline layers, and series-engine development before pages.
- Truby is strongest on architectural rigor: premise, genre, weakness, desire, and sequences all need design pressure before full drafting.
- Edson is strongest on propulsion: the sequence unit matters because it keeps the protagonist moving through visible goals, surprises, and changed circumstances.
- The current outlining-comparison source is strongest on pluralism: card systems matter not because they are universally superior, but because they belong to a larger family of visual scaffolds that can be chosen to fit the writer and project.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Cards can preserve flexibility, but they can also hide weak emotional logic if the writer never pressure-tests scene experience.
- Numeric heuristics like `70 scenes` can help with scale, but they can also become empty pseudo-rules if treated mechanically.
- A modular structure tool is useful only if the underlying story units actually contain conflict, change, and meaning.
- A beautiful board can create false confidence: visual order does not guarantee dramatic rhythm, emotional escalation, or scene necessity.
