# What It Is

Writing process and revision is the larger workflow that governs how a writer or director prepares, drafts, evaluates, and reshapes a work over time. In the current source base, the strongest pattern is not mystical inspiration but a managed cycle of idea capture, structural development, skeleton planning, drafting, diagnosis, cutting, and recommitment to the core engine of the work.

# Why It Matters

- It determines whether craft knowledge becomes repeatable work rather than occasional luck.
- It connects pre-draft planning, draft generation, and revision into one system.
- It gives the vault a cross-source concept for process discipline rather than leaving that knowledge split across source-local pages.
- It helps separate source-specific habits from transferable process principles.
- It now captures the full arc from fragile idea to finished narrative instead of only the drafting and rewriting stages.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The 2010 screenplay article emphasizes outlining before drafting and learning formatting early to avoid preventable cleanup later.
- Sorkin emphasizes finishing the draft before trying to perfect it and then rewriting diagnostically against concrete problems.
- Brown emphasizes protecting the process through routine, draft-stage separation, placeholder use, and permission to write badly before distilling.
- The Duffer Brothers emphasize refusing to draft too early, moving through increasingly specific outline layers, and backing out to the outline when pages break.
- Lynch emphasizes receptivity, daydreaming, and preserving psychic room for ideas before forcing them into rigid form.
- A second Lynch source adds a practical externalization step: translate ideas into words quickly, use early scripts as skeleton memory tools, and organize the feature through movable 3x5 scene cards before fleshing scenes out.
- Baldacci emphasizes the writer's prism, flexible but serious discipline, immersive research, and editorial collaboration that sharpens plausibility and pace.
- Scorsese adds the filmmaking version of revision: the script is only a starting architecture, rehearsal becomes writing, and the real work continues through assembly, screening, trimming, sound, and color.
- Rhimes adds TV-specific routine discipline: write daily, use rituals, trust deadlines, revise in small page batches, and use writers' table reads plus actor reads as verification layers.
- Rushdie adds a full literary-fiction process loop: answer the six governing questions, accept false starts, find the right entry point, finish the book, then revise through subtraction, addition, and nightly rereading.
- Truby adds a stronger pre-draft engineering layer: test the premise, build the logline, sketch scene sequences, seek structural criticism, and leave dialogue polish for later passes.
- The second Truby source adds the longer-horizon professional layer: keep studying, keep expanding technique, expect rejection and solitude, and do not confuse lack of sales with lack of work still to do.
- The roundtable source adds a practical anti-cliche loop: generate many alternatives, read the work aloud without performance polish, invite detached feedback, and treat rough early drafts as necessary material rather than personal failure.
- The dialogue roundtable adds a tighter oral-revision layer: cut lines aggressively, test dialogue by speaking it, watch for over-explanation, and trim scene entries and exits to where the dramatic pressure actually starts and ends.
- Edson adds a more engineering-heavy process move: know the ending before scene drafting and audit the script through `Hero Goal Sequences` so momentum failures become visible before revision turns vague.
- Glenn Gers adds a more adaptive process layer: learn your own rhythms, use rough placeholders without shame, and keep asking story questions rather than waiting for a perfect drafting state.
- Simon Rich adds an upstream ideation discipline: schedule premise-generation sessions, remove pressure for brilliance, generate in high volume, and separate raw idea abundance from later filtering.
- Both Sorkin and Brown treat deletion and cutting as central rather than incidental to good writing.
- Across the sources, preparation is important, but so is avoiding paralysis or perfectionism before there is enough material to shape.

# Source Comparisons

- The 2010 screenplay article is strongest on pre-draft discipline: outline, structure, and format first.
- Sorkin is strongest on post-draft discipline: finish the block of marble, then cut and diagnose.
- Brown covers the whole arc most explicitly: process protection, side-by-side drafting modes, and later distillation.
- The Duffer Brothers are strongest on layered development before pages exist: big idea, paragraph test, blue-sky ideation, and recursive outlines.
- Lynch is strongest on creative conditions plus skeletal planning: protect daydreaming, catch ideas immediately, and then hold the film lightly through headings, cards, and a movable scene map instead of forcing premature density.
- Baldacci is strongest on how working writers sustain output over long books: observation as lifestyle, practical research, and useful editor relationships.
- Scorsese is strongest on downstream revision pressure: films keep rewriting themselves after the pages are done, and the editor, actors, screenings, and post-production all reveal truths the script could not settle in advance.
- Rhimes is strongest on repeatable professional throughput: daily muscle memory, no-romance process discipline, page-level polishing, and social verification through reads and room practices.
- Rushdie is strongest on the full internal rhythm of long-form fiction making: false starts, discovery through writing, writer's-block reframing, nightly review, and the moment when the book can no longer be safely altered.
- Truby is strongest on upstream structural diligence: many problems should be caught before the draft through premise testing and scene-sequence thinking rather than repaired only after pages exist.
- The second Truby source is strongest on undertraining and attrition: many writers fail not because the field is unfair in the abstract, but because they stop learning or cannot withstand the path long enough to get good.
- The roundtable source is strongest on diagnostic workshop behavior: use read-alouds, scene-objective checks, alternative-generation drills, and plainspoken outside notes to locate where the script has gone dull.
- The dialogue roundtable is strongest on line-level compression: revision is not only about scene order or premise strength, but about deleting the spoken weight the visual scene no longer needs.
- Edson is strongest on mid-level structural auditing: use sequence chains, turning-point reversals, and active-goal logic to diagnose where a screenplay has stopped changing enough.
- Gers is strongest on lightweight iterative motion: small rough notes, repeated questioning, and process self-awareness can move a writer forward when heavier systems would trigger blockage.
- Simon Rich is strongest on anti-block abundance: use quotas, prompts, playful observation, and deliberate low stakes to keep the idea pipeline alive instead of waiting passively for inspiration.
- Together they suggest a layered workflow: catch a live idea, test whether it can carry real weight, develop structure before expensive pages, draft far enough to have something real, and then revise without sentimentality.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much planning can become avoidance; too little planning can create expensive chaos later.
- Some writers edit while drafting more than these sources recommend, so the right balance may vary by temperament and medium.
- Process discipline is transferable, but exact rituals are probably not.
- The main live tension is between receptivity and control: the vault now contains both outline-heavy and intuition-heavy models, and the right blend likely depends on medium and maker.
- Scorsese adds a medium-specific complication: in film, revision extends into shooting and post, so closure arrives later and under heavier collaborative pressure than it does on the page.
- Rhimes adds a second tension: some writers move forward through roughness, while her page-perfecting method tightens control early and may not scale equally well for every temperament.
- Rushdie also sharpens the final-stage tension: sometimes further revision helps, and sometimes the writer has simply lost the living web of the book and must stop cutting blind.
- Truby sharpens the front-end tension: aggressive preparation can prevent costly rewrites, but it can also become overengineering if it blocks discovery entirely.
- The second Truby source sharpens a social tension: writers may over-focus on access, validation, or status while underinvesting in the slower compounding of actual craft.
- The roundtable sharpens a workshop tension: feedback is essential, but the writer still has to distinguish between honest boredom signals and notes that merely push the script toward bland consensus.
- Edson sharpens a planning tension: detailed sequence architecture can prevent drift, but some writers may start treating the audit grid as the story itself rather than as scaffolding for living dramatic experience.
- Gers sharpens a workflow tension: personalization is healthy, but it can become self-excuse if `my process` is used to avoid finishing, testing, and revising the work.
- Lynch's second source sharpens a parallel tension: very light skeletons can preserve freshness, but if they remain too thin for too long they may postpone rather than solve dramatic diagnosis.
- Simon Rich sharpens an earlier-stage tension: prolific ideation can free the writer from perfectionism, but it can also delay commitment if abundance becomes a substitute for development.
