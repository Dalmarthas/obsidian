# What It Is

Narrative voice and perspective are the choices that determine who seems to be telling the story, from what distance, in what linguistic texture, and with what relation to the reader. In the current source base, these are not cosmetic flourishes. They are structural choices that govern intimacy, secrecy, rhythm, and the entire transaction between writer and audience.

# Why It Matters

- A good idea can fail if the point of view or telling mode is wrong.
- Voice is one of the clearest ways a work becomes unmistakably its own.
- It gives the vault a stable home for fiction craft that is not reducible to plot or revision.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Rushdie treats the `how` question as one of the hardest and most decisive in fiction writing.
- Rushdie distinguishes first person from third person in terms of interiority, distance, reader trust, and the management of revelation.
- Rushdie also separates style from voice: style is what you do with formal rules, while voice is the unmistakable authorial touch that emerges through repeated right decisions.
- Rushdie treats rhythm as part of meaning and recommends reading work aloud to hear whether the music carries the prose.
- Sorkin contributes a neighboring dramatic version of the same idea through dialogue judged by cadence, sound, interruption, and performability.

# Source Comparisons

- Rushdie is strongest on prose-level and narrator-level choice: first person, third person, multiple perspectives, rhythm, paragraphing, and the emergence of voice.
- Sorkin is strongest on spoken dramatic language: dialogue has to move like music in time.
- Together they suggest that voice is not an ornamental afterthought but a central delivery system for meaning.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Distinctive voice can become mannerism if it is no longer serving character or story.
- Formal experimentation expands possibility, but without clarity it can break the reader contract rather than deepen it.
