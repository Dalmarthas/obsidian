# What It Is

Spatial storytelling is the use of geography, architecture, entrances, exits, levels, concealment, and audience orientation as active parts of story construction. In the current vault, space is not only mood or scenery. It can be a suspense engine, a class diagram, a revelation system, and a constraint that shapes what characters can do.

# Why It Matters

- It turns location design into narrative design.
- It strengthens the bridge between screenwriting and filmmaking by making physical layout part of dramatic logic.
- It helps explain why some stories feel coherent and escalating even when much of the action happens in one place.
- It gives the vault a more precise concept than `setting matters`.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Bong Joon-Ho source shows the house in _Parasite_ being taught to the audience early so later concealment, reversals, and eruptions feel legible and inevitable.
- The same source treats architecture as both plot machine and metaphor, with vertical levels and hidden spaces carrying class meaning as well as suspense value.
- Spatial knowledge is presented as cumulative audience preparation: once the viewer knows the house, later movement through it becomes dramatic language rather than mere logistics.

# Source Comparisons

- The current Bong source is strongest on architecture as engineered escalation: space is a machine that releases conflict in stages.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/setting-as-character|Setting as Character]] is stronger on emotional charge, history, and felt world-presence than on precise suspense geography.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/visual-storytelling-first|Visual Storytelling First]] overlaps by insisting that narrative burden should move through image and staging instead of explanation.
- [[wiki/concepts/creativity/visual-literacy|Visual Literacy]] overlaps at the broader language level: spatial relationships already communicate meaning before anyone explains them.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Overexplaining geography can flatten surprise if the audience feels over-prepared.
- Underexplaining geography weakens later suspense because the viewer cannot track what is possible.
- Some stories need spatial precision; others benefit more from emotional or atmospheric place logic than architectural clarity.
