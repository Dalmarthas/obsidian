# What It Is

The `Six Essential Story Questions` are Glenn Gers's compact story-design framework: who is it about, what do they want, why can't they get it, what do they do about that, why doesn't that work, and how does it end. In the current vault, the framework matters because it turns vague story talk into an interrogation sequence that can be reused at outline, act, scene, and revision levels.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the writer a portable diagnostic tool that can be used before and during drafting.
- It keeps story development tied to character, obstacle, and consequence instead of dissolving into atmosphere or abstract theme.
- It is simple enough to use repeatedly without needing a large pre-draft architecture every time.
- It complements more elaborate systems by clarifying whether the basic dramatic engine is present at all.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The framework begins with character and desire rather than with worldbuilding or theme.
- It insists that obstacles are central, not decorative.
- The `what do they do` and `why doesn't that work` questions create movement, failed tactics, and escalation.
- The final question, `how does it end`, gives the writer a direction without demanding a mechanical beat map.
- Gers also implies that the same questions can help at scene scale, making them a flexible development and revision tool rather than only a premise test.

# Source Comparisons

- Gers is strongest on portable clarity: he offers a concise universal question-stack that can be used almost anywhere in the process.
- The generic storytelling source overlaps strongly, especially around goal, stakes, obstacles, and dramatic question, but Gers packages the logic more explicitly into a six-step interrogation method.
- Truby overlaps at the pre-draft level, but his method is broader, more engineered, and more concerned with premise, genre, and deeper architecture.
- Sorkin overlaps around objective and obstacle, but his center of gravity is scene pressure rather than a generalized development checklist.
- Edson overlaps on active pursuit and failure-driven movement, but Gers is looser and more diagnostic than Edson's sequence model.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The framework is powerful because it is simple, but that simplicity can hide medium-specific complexity.
- Some stories depend on distributed protagonists or unstable point of view, which can make the `who is it about` question less straightforward.
- The model clarifies basics well, but it does not by itself solve voice, tone, subtext, or thematic depth.
- Writers can also answer the questions mechanically without finding emotionally alive answers.
