# What It Is

Character interviews are a development technique where the writer informally questions a character as if that character were a real person. In the current vault, the goal is not trivia collection but discovery: voice, fear, contradiction, self-justification, hidden motive, and the limits of what the character understands about themselves.

# Why It Matters

- It helps move characters from label-based design toward psychologically usable specificity.
- It exposes weak motivations or generic backstory before they leak into scenes.
- It gives the writer a practical bridge between empathy and action.
- It can reveal when a supposedly clear character is actually too abstract to write believably.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current character-methodologies source recommends deep interviews with characters to surface fears, flaws, contradictions, and hidden drivers.
- It also implies that interviews are useful because characters often become false when the writer has not yet located an emotionally honest point of contact.
- Glenn Gers overlaps through repeated question-driven diagnostics: who is this, what do they want, why can't they get it, what do they do, why does that fail, and how does it end.
- The current *Fight Club* source adds a looser and more playful version of the tool:
  make characters argue, joke, react sentimentally, or reveal hypocrisy outside the strict main-script sequence so they stop behaving like plot machines.

# Source Comparisons

- The current character-methodologies source is strongest on psychological excavation: use questions to uncover hypocrisy, pain points, and inner logic.
- Gers is strongest on structural clarity: his questions are less confessional and more diagnostic, designed to expose whether the character can actually drive story.
- The current *Fight Club* source is strongest on activation:
  character interviews are useful not only for dossiers, but for generating surprising tonal behavior and emotionally charged scene material.
- Together they imply that character interviews work best when they combine inner-life discovery with story-useful pressure.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Interviews can produce helpful depth, but they can also become avoidance if the writer keeps generating dossier material without turning it into scenes.
- A character's spoken self-description is not the same as the truth of the character.
- Some hidden details matter only if they change how the character behaves under pressure.
