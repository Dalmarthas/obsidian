# What It Is

Screenplay economy is the discipline of giving the film only what it needs on the page and no more. In the current vault, that means writing with enough clarity, pressure, and suggestiveness for actors, directors, editors, and images to do their work, instead of trying to control every effect through over-described prose.

# Why It Matters

- It keeps the screenplay aligned with the medium rather than drifting toward novelistic explanation.
- It leaves interpretive room for actors, directors, and the set without collapsing into vagueness.
- It helps distinguish rich implication from heavy-handed overstatement.
- It gives the vault a reusable concept for the difference between a readable script and a playable one.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current Max Landis source adds the bluntest commercial-reading version of economy: the script should stay sparse, clear, and entertaining enough on the page that a reader keeps going instead of feeling slowed or over-managed.
- It also sharpens two specific economy tests: avoid over-direction, and cut dialogue that sounds embarrassing when read aloud.
- The current Paul Thomas Anderson source argues that scripts should give `just enough`, stay concise, and trust subtext and collaboration rather than padding the page with literary display.
- The dialogue roundtable overlaps by insisting that dialogue should be cut aggressively and that visual storytelling should carry more of the scene's burden.
- The acting source overlaps from the performer side: text works better when it is precise and playable rather than swollen with decorative writing around the actual dramatic action.
- Scorsese overlaps from the directing side: the script is a starting architecture that will continue evolving through rehearsal, shooting, and editing rather than a sealed literary artifact.
- The current `Sopranos` source adds an episode-writing version of the same discipline: every line and scene should justify itself through story movement, character pressure, or thematic weight rather than because it is merely amusing or well-observed.

# Source Comparisons

- The current Max Landis source is strongest on document-level readability: economy matters because the script has to survive as a pleasurable read before it can survive as a film.
- The current Paul Thomas Anderson source is strongest on screenplay-versus-prose distinction: film writing should stay lean enough for performance and direction to complete it.
- The dialogue roundtable is strongest on line-level reduction: if the scene still works after substantial cutting, the removed weight probably never belonged there.
- The acting source is strongest on performability: the actor needs clear playable language, not explanatory bulk that competes with the actual scene.
- Scorsese is strongest on mutability: once the work reaches rehearsal and set, the script should keep living rather than insisting on textual purity.
- The current `Sopranos` source is strongest on ruthless retention standards inside long-form drama: even symbolic, funny, or offbeat material still has to earn its place by serving the episode's trunk.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Economy can become emptiness if the writer strips away orientation, mood, or necessary dramatic clarity.
- Writers can also hide behind `leave room` as an excuse for underthinking the scene.
- Some voices and tones need more textual presence than others, so economy should not become generic flatness.
