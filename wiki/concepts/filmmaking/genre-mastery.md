# What It Is

Genre mastery is the view that a writer should understand genre as deep story logic rather than as a checklist of expected scenes. In this vault's current evidence, genre is both an external market language and an internal storytelling engine: it shapes desire, opposition, escalation, payoff, audience expectation, and the kinds of novelty a story can meaningfully deliver.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents writers from confusing generic familiarity with real originality.
- It connects commercial legibility to artistic invention instead of treating them as enemies.
- It gives the vault a clearer home for the upstream decision about what kind of story the writer is actually building.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Truby argues that Hollywood buys and sells genres, so writers need genre fluency before they can innovate successfully.
- He also argues that merely copying genre beats is weak craft; writers should combine, deepen, and transcend genres rather than deliver stale compliance.
- The source points to myth-based or hybrid blockbusters as examples of stories that work because they combine genre energies instead of staying trapped in one narrow template.
- Genre is therefore not only a marketing category but a structural design choice that influences the entire architecture of the script.

# Source Comparisons

- Truby is far more explicit about genre than Sorkin, who focuses more on conflict, objective, and scene movement than on genre taxonomy itself.
- The Duffer Brothers overlap indirectly: they also care about the big story engine and tonal mixture, but Truby sharpens that into a more formal genre-design claim.
- Lynch is the least genre-theoretical voice in the branch; he cares more about idea integrity and atmosphere than about genre as a market or structural framework.
- Together, the branch suggests that writers need both: living fidelity to the idea and enough genre intelligence to shape that idea into communicable dramatic form.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strong genre awareness can become mimicry if it turns into fear of breaking expectations.
- Anti-formula rhetoric can be misread as anti-structure, even when the real point is to go deeper than surface beats.
- The vault still needs more sources on genre from outside film and television before this concept becomes fully cross-medium.
