# What It Is

Acting craft is the disciplined practice of making a performance feel truthful, specific, alive, and narratively coherent rather than merely expressive on the surface. In the current source base, strong acting comes from reaction, stakes, textual precision, variation, and the willingness to fail in search of something more real.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a dedicated acting branch instead of leaving all performance knowledge inside directing pages.
- It links text, body, voice, stakes, and arc into one working model.
- It distinguishes genuine performance truth from affectation, cliche, or generic emotional display.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The source states that acting is reacting, not `acting`, and that performances live on what the character wants and how reality disappoints them.
- High stakes, contradiction, and variation matter more than playing one fixed emotional note.
- The actor should serve the writing rather than bend the writing down to familiar habits.
- Holding emotion under pressure can be more powerful than showing everything.
- Theater and screen require related but different craft: stage teaches sustained arc through repetition, while screen acting requires explicit mapping of knowledge and progression across non-linear shooting.
- The current Paul Thomas Anderson source adds the writer-director side of the same principle: scripts should leave enough room for actors to interpret, translate, and sometimes even improve the line in performance.
- It also sharpens a realism rule: if the actor's version reveals a truer or more natural moment, textual attachment should not automatically outrank performative life.

# Source Comparisons

- The acting source is strongest on the actor's internal labor: reacting truthfully, serving the text, and shaping playable beats.
- The current Paul Thomas Anderson source is strongest on actor-space in the writing: the page should not overfill the moment with literary control that leaves performance nothing living to do.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Strong inward containment can produce intensity, but it can also become unreadable if the audience is given too little.
- Serving the writing is a durable principle, but actors still face the live question of what to do when the material is thin or tonally unstable.
- Openness to actor improvement can deepen realism, but without strong taste it can also erode tone, pressure, or structural precision.
