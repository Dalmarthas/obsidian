# What It Is

Psychology-driven plot is the principle that story events should emerge from believable mental, emotional, and moral states rather than from writer convenience. In this vault, the strongest version is Gilligan's insistence that the room should ask what the protagonist would authentically do next given their current state, even when that answer makes the story darker, stranger, or less mechanically efficient.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents plot from feeling imposed from outside the character.
- It turns motivation, pride, fear, and self-delusion into structural forces rather than decorative psychology.
- It improves antagonists as well as protagonists because everyone keeps their intelligence and internal logic.
- It gives the vault a clearer bridge between character design and actual plotting decisions.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Gilligan's *Breaking Bad* source is the clearest current statement:
  plot should follow Walt's psychology, not a preplanned convenience.
- The same source makes pride and ego, not only financial need, the durable engine of Walt's choices.
- It also extends the rule to antagonists:
  dangerous opponents should not become stupid just to save the protagonist.
- Sorkin overlaps through objective and obstacle:
  plot movement should arise from what characters want and the tactics they use under pressure.
- The *Social Network* case study sharpens a neighboring truth:
  self-justifying accounts and conflicting perspectives can reveal character while still moving the plot.
- The current character-methodologies source overlaps by arguing that believable contradiction and personal logic create more credible scene behavior than trait lists ever can.
- The current Sam Esmail source adds a reverse-engineering version:
  big season turns can be planned early, but each step still has to be justified backward through emotional and psychological truth rather than through twist convenience.

# Source Comparisons

- Gilligan is strongest on room-level decision making:
  keep pressure-testing every turn against the protagonist's actual mental state.
- Sorkin is strongest on tactical behavior:
  plot emerges from pursuit, friction, and what someone tries when blocked.
- The current character-methodologies source is strongest on preparation for the same effect:
  empathy, contradiction, and point of view make later plot moves feel earned.
- The current Sam Esmail source is strongest on long-range season architecture:
  preplanned goalposts are acceptable if the route to them is rebuilt until it feels like a genuine consequence of the character's inner state.
- Together they suggest a stronger rule than `make the story exciting`:
  make the next event the emotionally and psychologically believable consequence of who these people are.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Pure psychological consistency can still produce inert drama if the character's wants are too passive or shapeless.
- Writer convenience is dangerous, but total submission to first-pass psychology can also trap the story inside repetition if the writer never increases pressure.
- The concept still needs more primary examples across genres beyond antihero television.
- Reverse engineering can preserve coherence, but it can also become retrofitting if the writer falls in love with the destination more than with the living character logic.
