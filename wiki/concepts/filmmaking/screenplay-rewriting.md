# What It Is

Screenplay rewriting is the disciplined process of diagnosing and solving problems after a full draft exists. In the current source base, rewriting is not framed as cosmetic polishing; it is the real sculptural phase where the writer clarifies conflict, cuts detours, sharpens intention, and makes the script behave like the story it is trying to be. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- Revision is easier than drafting because there is finally a concrete problem to solve.
- Finishing the draft gives the writer the full block of marble before cutting.
- Notes are useful when they identify pain points, not when they attempt to rewrite the script from the outside.
- Retyping or re-speaking the work can expose weak lines that visual scanning misses.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Get to the end first, even if the draft is visibly flawed.
- Cut anything the script can survive without.
- The most important notes often concern whether intention and obstacle are believable.
- Avoid characters performing the emotion the audience is supposed to feel.
- Trusted script editors should understand the writer's voice rather than force a different one.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Not every strong script editor gives notes in the same vocabulary, so collaboration quality is partly interpersonal.
- Some digressive or atmospheric material may deserve to survive revision if it is genuinely earned.
