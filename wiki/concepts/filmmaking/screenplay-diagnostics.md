# What It Is

Screenplay diagnostics is the practice of evaluating a script through recurring failure modes rather than through vague feelings of `it works` or `it doesn't`. In this vault, the point is to make revision and development more explicit: test concept strength, protagonist goal clarity, setup quality, genre fit, escalation, character vulnerability, emotional honesty, and professional readiness before assuming the script's problems are mysterious.

# Why It Matters

- It turns revision into a usable checklist instead of a fog of discouragement.
- It helps writers catch upstream structural weakness before polishing scenes that never had enough engine.
- It creates a bridge between concept testing, story movement, character design, and professional process.
- It gives the vault a reusable home for common screenwriting failure patterns.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current `29 mistakes` source is explicit that many weak scripts fail for repeatable reasons:
  no clear external goal, weak concept ownership, rushed setup, wrong genre fit, low stakes, flat conflict, thin characters, and under-revised submission behavior.
- The same source treats self-honesty as part of diagnosis:
  the writer must ask whether they are avoiding the more personal or more alive version of the story.
- It also sharpens a professional filter:
  feedback matters, but so does not circulating work before it has earned other people's time.
- The current image-derived studio-reader source adds a positive mirror to the same branch:
  readers repeatedly respond to strong premise, hook, protagonist, antagonist, dialogue, action clarity, world vividness, and third-act convergence.
- The same source also sharpens how reader trust collapses:
  late starts, passive protagonists, generic villains, stitched-together stories, repetition, coincidence, tonal whiplash, and anti-climactic endings all read like diagnostic signals rather than isolated mistakes.

# Source Comparisons

- The current `29 mistakes` source is strongest on failure-mode visibility:
  it names what usually goes wrong rather than only describing what excellence looks like.
- The current studio-reader source is strongest on first-read balance:
  it pairs the things readers reward with the things that make them lose confidence, which makes diagnostics feel closer to actual coverage logic.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/premise-testing|Premise Testing]] overlaps at the front end by asking whether the idea and genre logic deserve a draft at all.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/story-propulsion|Story Propulsion]] overlaps at the momentum layer by testing whether goals, stakes, and reversals keep the script alive.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/character-driven-storytelling|Character-Driven Storytelling]] overlaps at the character layer by showing that passive, invulnerable, or underbuilt protagonists often create diagnostic failure across the whole script.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/writing-process-and-revision|Writing Process and Revision]] overlaps at the process layer by turning diagnostic discovery into iterative rewriting rather than panic.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Diagnostic checklists can sharpen revision, but they can also become formula worship if the writer treats every script as identical.
- Some scripts fail because of deep taste or worldview issues that no checklist can fully solve.
- The vault still needs a stronger cross-source model for which diagnostics matter most at `idea`, `outline`, `draft`, and `pre-submission` stages.
- Reader-facing scorecards are practical, but they can also overvalue immediate polish or recognizability if they are treated as the only definition of quality.
