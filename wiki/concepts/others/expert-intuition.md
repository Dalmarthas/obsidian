# What It Is

Expert intuition is fast judgment that has become genuinely reliable because the environment contains stable patterns and gives frequent, accurate feedback. In the current vault, the key claim is not that intuition is mystical. It is that intuition is trained pattern recognition, and only some domains are structured well enough to produce it. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-10-daniel-kahneman-two-systems-thinking|Daniel Kahneman Two-Systems Thinking]].

# Why It Matters

- It separates deserved trust in skilled judgment from overconfident guessing.
- It helps the vault distinguish domains like chess from noisy domains like stock picking or long-range punditry.
- It gives a better rule for when to defer to experience and when to insist on slower procedure.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Daniel Kahneman source argues that intuition works well when a person repeatedly sees valid patterns and receives prompt feedback.
- The same source presents chess and anesthesiology as examples of domains where expertise can train fast pattern recognition.
- It also argues that intuition becomes unreliable in irregular, feedback-poor environments even when people feel extremely sure.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/two-systems-thinking|Two-Systems Thinking]], expert intuition is narrower and more conditional. It is the special case where fast judgment has earned credibility.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/chess/problem-first-chess-training|Problem-First Chess Training]], this concept is less about study method and more about the environmental conditions that allow strong pattern recognition to form in the first place.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/premortem|Premortem]], expert intuition answers when fast judgment can be trusted; premortem answers what to do when stakes are high enough that trust alone is not sufficient.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- People often assume years of exposure automatically create expertise, but the source implies that exposure without valid feedback may only create stronger illusion.
- The vault still needs more material on how organizations can test whether a domain truly supports expert intuition.
- Fast judgment can remain useful even when not fully reliable, which raises the practical question of how much evidence should be demanded before override.
