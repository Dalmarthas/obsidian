# What It Is

Inversion patterns are recurring personas where the visible trait is the inverse of the feared inner identity. In the current vault, the concept names patterns like exaggerated toughness, overhelpfulness, relentless achievement, or constant joking when these are used to defend against shame, helplessness, rejection, or emotional exposure.

# Why It Matters

- It turns a vague idea of `defensiveness` into a more usable pattern language.
- It helps the wiki preserve recurring archetypes that can be recognized across different people and situations.
- It also supports more accurate self-inquiry by asking what fear the overcompensation is trying to neutralize.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source gives several core examples:
  - toughness masking hurt or betrayal
  - people pleasing masking fear of rejection or being a burden
  - overachievement masking fear of not being enough
  - class-clown humor masking fear of emotional exposure
  - harsh judgment masking shame or guilt
- The source also offers a practical read-through:
  - over-assertiveness can point to fear of powerlessness
  - name-dropping can point to insecurity about worth
  - excessive politeness can point to fear of being disliked

# Source Comparisons

- This source treats inversion as a fast behavioral-reading heuristic rather than as a slow developmental psychology model.
- It differs from a hypocrisy frame because the point is not simply that people are inconsistent; the claim is that the inconsistency is structured by shame and defense.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Archetypal readings can become stereotypes if they are used lazily.
- One outward trait can arise from many different causes, so the mapping should stay probabilistic.
- The branch still needs better material on when inversion readings are useful and when they become projection by the observer.
