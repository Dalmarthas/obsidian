# What It Is

Status quo bias is the tendency to preserve the current state, not because it is best, but because change feels riskier and what is already owned feels more valuable than it objectively is. In the current vault, the concept combines loss aversion and the endowment effect into one practical trap: bad defaults often survive because giving them up feels like a loss, even when holding them is quietly more costly. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-thinking-fast-and-slow-by-daniel-kahneman|Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a clearer explanation for why people stay in failing jobs, projects, roles, and investments.
- It helps distinguish prudent patience from fear-shaped inertia.
- It strengthens the branch on decision quality by naming default-preservation as a specific error mode rather than a vague reluctance to change.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Kahneman source argues that losses loom larger than equivalent gains.
- It also argues that ownership inflates valuation, which makes current holdings harder to surrender.
- Together these mechanisms can keep people tied to losing stocks, disliked careers, and failing projects because exiting feels like crystallizing defeat.
- The source's practical correction is to ask what opportunities are being lost by preserving the current arrangement.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/two-systems-thinking|Two-Systems Thinking]], status quo bias is a specific downstream effect. Two-systems thinking explains why defaults feel natural; status quo bias names one durable way that natural feeling misleads.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/strategic-quitting|Strategic Quitting]], this concept is diagnostic rather than strategic. Strategic quitting concerns when leaving is wise; status quo bias explains why leaving often feels harder than the evidence justifies.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/wysiati|WYSIATI]], status quo bias is less about incomplete evidence and more about distorted valuation once a current state or owned asset is psychologically protected.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Many defaults really are good, so the concept can be overused if every form of patience gets pathologized as bias.
- The current branch still needs stronger decision rules for when persistence is a compounding advantage rather than a sunk-cost trap.
- The vault also needs better coverage of reversibility and switching costs so `change` is not implicitly treated as free.
