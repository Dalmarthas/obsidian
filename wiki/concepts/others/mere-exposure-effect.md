# What It Is

The mere-exposure effect is the tendency to like, trust, or choose something more simply because it has become familiar through repeated contact. In the current vault, the concept matters as one of the easiest ways `System 1` mistakes fluency for evidence: repetition can make an option feel truer, safer, or better without actually improving its quality. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-13-thinking-fast-and-slow-by-daniel-kahneman|Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman]].

# Why It Matters

- It explains how preference can be shaped before explicit reasoning begins.
- It gives the judgment branch a cleaner account of why repetition is persuasive even without argument quality.
- It helps separate evidence-based preference from conditioned familiarity in buying, voting, hiring, and attention markets.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Kahneman source argues that repeated exposure increases trust and liking even when understanding does not increase.
- It applies this to names, slogans, products, candidates, and neighborhoods.
- The concept implies that part of `free choice` is often environmental conditioning rather than neutral evaluation.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/two-systems-thinking|Two-Systems Thinking]], mere exposure is narrower and more specific. Two-systems thinking explains the architecture; mere exposure names one repeatable distortion inside it.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/wysiati|WYSIATI]], mere exposure is less about missing information and more about familiarity-generated comfort. WYSIATI makes the visible story feel complete; mere exposure makes the repeated option feel right.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/brand-strategy|Brand Strategy]], this concept is descriptive rather than strategic. Brand strategy may deliberately use repetition; mere exposure explains one reason repetition works.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Familiarity is not always irrational: sometimes repeated exposure really does reflect reliability, accessibility, or learned expertise.
- The vault still needs better boundaries for distinguishing healthy recognition from manipulative conditioning.
- The current branch is stronger on diagnosis than on how to redesign environments so familiarity does not quietly dominate major choices.
