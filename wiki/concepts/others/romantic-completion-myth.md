# What It Is

The romantic completion myth is the belief that a romantic relationship will solve inner emptiness, confirm personal worth, or complete a fundamentally incomplete self. In the current vault, this is treated as one of the more damaging Hollywood romance myths because it makes romance carry psychological work that should not depend on another person.

# Why It Matters

- It distorts partner choice by making romance feel like rescue rather than relationship.
- It can weaken self-respect, boundaries, and non-romantic bonds by treating romantic validation as the highest proof of value.
- The concept gives the vault a durable place to store the difference between companionship and salvation.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source argues that movies routinely frame romance as the cure for loneliness, purposelessness, or existential incompleteness.
- It counters this by stressing self-acceptance, inner wholeness, friendship, and family as the healthier foundation for romantic life.
- The same source also rejects the idea that someone should love a person `for who they really are` without that person doing any real growth or self-work.

# Source Comparisons

- The current source frames the issue culturally rather than clinically: the main target is a false script about fulfillment, not a detailed theory of dependency.
- It overlaps loosely with the vault's broader self-acceptance language by implying that healthier love starts from less shame and less hunger to be rescued.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Romance can genuinely deepen a life without needing to function as its cure-all center.
- The branch still needs better distinction between healthy interdependence and dependency disguised as destiny.
- The vault also lacks stronger material on how friendship, family, and self-worth protect against bad romantic decisions over time.
