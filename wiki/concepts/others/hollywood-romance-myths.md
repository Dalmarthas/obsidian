# What It Is

Hollywood romance myths are repeated story patterns that make unhealthy relationship dynamics feel romantic, noble, or destined. In the current vault, the concept names a media-shaped expectation stack: intensity is mistaken for quality, pursuit is confused with respect, fantasy is confused with compatibility, and romantic rescue is confused with emotional wholeness.

# Why It Matters

- Repeated tropes can quietly shape what people think love should feel like.
- The myths often reward drama, fantasy, and boundary violation over steadiness, repair, and mutual respect.
- The concept gives the vault a durable place to store media-conditioned relationship expectations instead of leaving them trapped in one source page.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source names eight recurring myths: love at first sight, hidden-worth rescue, persistence after rejection, grand-gesture repair, unavailable-partner pursuit, suffering as proof, emotional volatility as depth, and romance as life's solution.
- It argues that these tropes distort standards by making cinematic excitement feel more meaningful than mature connection.
- It also argues that the healthier alternative is less dramatic but more sustainable: boundaries, self-development, stability, and a life not organized around romantic salvation.

# Source Comparisons

- The current source is more cultural and corrective than therapeutic: it is diagnosing media-shaped expectations rather than mapping attachment theory or clinical relationship patterns.
- It is also more myth-focused than partner-specific: the main target is the inherited story about love, not one person's private pathology.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Some viewers consume romantic fiction without importing it into real expectations, so the cultural effect is not uniform.
- Fiction needs compression and intensity, which means some tropes may be dramatically useful even when they are interpersonally dangerous.
- The branch still needs stronger primary or research-based material if it is going to move from cultural diagnosis into relationship science.
