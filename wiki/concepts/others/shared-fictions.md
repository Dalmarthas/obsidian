# What It Is

Shared fictions are collectively believed stories, abstractions, and symbols that let large groups cooperate beyond the scale of intimate trust. In the current vault, the key examples are nations, money, corporations, and religions. They are not `false` in the trivial sense of useless fantasy. They are socially real because belief in them coordinates behavior, but they are dangerous when people forget that the fiction serves suffering beings rather than replacing them.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a way to explain how huge systems coordinate strangers without pretending those systems are natural objects.
- It helps distinguish moral reality from institutional mythology by asking who can actually suffer.
- It adds a narrative layer to the AI and governance branch by showing how power often operates through stories before it operates through code.

# Evidence In This Vault

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-11-yuval-noah-harari-21-lessons-for-the-21st-century]] argues that money, nations, and corporations depend on large-scale shared belief rather than on intrinsic biological reality.
- The same source uses suffering as a test for what deserves primary moral attention, claiming that people and animals can suffer while institutions and currencies cannot.
- It therefore warns that abstractions become dangerous when they justify cruelty toward real beings in their name.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/naturalistic-meaning|Naturalistic Meaning]], shared fictions is more collective and institutional. Naturalistic meaning asks how value exists in human life; shared fictions asks how societies organize value, loyalty, and legitimacy through stories.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/ai/ai-and-biotechnology-convergence|AI and Biotechnology Convergence]], shared fictions is less about technical power and more about the symbolic structures through which power is normalized and coordinated.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The concept clarifies coordination, but it can become too dismissive if every institution is treated as merely invented and therefore morally weightless.
- The suffering test is sharp, yet the branch still needs stronger treatment of how institutions indirectly affect suffering and why some abstractions deserve defense.
- The vault still lacks enough material on when shared fictions are constructive, when they become totalizing, and how they can be revised without social collapse.
