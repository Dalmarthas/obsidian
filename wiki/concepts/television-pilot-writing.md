# What It Is

Television pilot writing is the craft of building an opening episode that introduces a world, proves the series engine, launches character arcs, and leaves enough forward pressure for a season to exist. In the current source base, the pilot is treated as a high-risk structural object that benefits from unusually thorough development before scripting.

# Why It Matters

- A pilot has to do more than tell one story; it has to imply a continuing machine.
- It joins premise, character, world, season arc, and opening hook into one testable form.
- It gives the vault a clear home for future TV-specific writing sources instead of forcing them into feature-oriented pages.
- It externalizes the pilot-specific craft that was previously missing from the writing branch.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Duffer Brothers prioritize a simple big idea and a central drive strong enough to organize multiple storylines.
- Characters need directional A-to-B arcs inside the pilot even if their larger journeys continue for a season.
- Worldbuilding should be legible but not overbuilt; mythology can stay partial if the engine works.
- Development should move through paragraph, story outline, scaffolding outline, and final outline before pages.
- If the pilot feels broken in script form, the repair usually belongs back at an earlier structural layer.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Overplanning can make a pilot efficient but too airless if discovery never reenters the process.
- Underplanning can create an impressive opening premise without a durable season engine behind it.
