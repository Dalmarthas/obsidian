# What It Is

ADHD and anxiety overlap is the problem of similar outward symptoms arising from different underlying mechanisms. In the current vault, the key distinction is that ADHD is framed mainly through executive-function weakness, while anxiety is framed mainly through threat sensitivity and avoidance. The overlap matters because the same visible behavior can point to different intervention logic.

# Why It Matters

- It reduces the risk of treating all restlessness, racing thoughts, forgetfulness, or overwhelm as one undifferentiated problem.
- It explains why some people feel both undercontrolled and over-threatened at the same time rather than fitting neatly into one category.
- It shifts the conversation from character judgment toward mechanism, support needs, and more accurate self-observation.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source argues that ADHD and anxiety share many surface features, including worry, sleep disruption, fidgeting, and mental overactivity.
- It distinguishes them by root cause: ADHD is tied here to weak planning, inhibition, working memory, and task initiation, while anxiety is tied to fear loops and avoidance behavior.
- The same source argues that each condition can worsen the other: anxiety degrades executive functioning, and executive dysfunction raises overwhelm and uncertainty.
- It also adds a social consequence: uneven performance can be misread as laziness or lack of discipline, which increases shame and can intensify anxiety further.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The current branch rests on one secondary summary and still needs stronger evidence on differential diagnosis and comorbidity patterns.
- The vault does not yet have enough material on when anxiety should be treated primarily through exposure-based work versus executive-function supports versus medication.
- This concept can become too neat if it ignores that many people have both conditions, plus other drivers such as trauma, sleep deprivation, or depression.
