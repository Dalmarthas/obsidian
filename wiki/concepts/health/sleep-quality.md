# What It Is

Sleep quality is the combination of sleep depth, continuity, timing, and restorative effect rather than just the number of hours spent in bed. In this vault, the first health branch treats it as a systems outcome shaped by circadian cues, behavior timing, environment, stress, and underlying medical causes.

# Why It Matters

- It makes sleep improvement more precise than “try to sleep more.”
- It explains why some people can fall asleep but still wake repeatedly or feel poorly restored.
- It connects sleep to recovery, cognition, mood, and durable performance rather than treating it as a side concern.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Rhonda Patrick branch frames fragmented sleep as a multi-cause problem rather than a single hygiene failure.
- Sleep quality is linked to morning light exposure, evening darkness, a cooler room, lower noise, and predictable routines.
- Late food, alcohol, caffeine, and fluid intake are all treated as major disruptors of continuity and depth.
- The branch also makes room for supplements, but only as one layer inside a broader system.
- Medical conditions such as sleep apnea, nocturia, chronic insomnia, and menopause-related disruption are treated as serious drivers of poor sleep quality.
- The brain-health source adds a sharper long-horizon consequence: deep sleep supports glymphatic waste clearance, and poor chronic sleep is framed as a dementia-risk amplifier rather than only a next-day performance problem.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Sleep advice often collapses different problems into one generic checklist; this branch pushes against that, but the vault still has only one health source so far.
- The hardest open question is how to separate “mostly lifestyle” sleep problems from problems that are primarily medical or hormonal without underreacting or overmedicalizing.
- The branch also needs stronger evidence sorting on how much brain-aging protection comes specifically from deep sleep quantity, continuity, or broader sleep architecture.
