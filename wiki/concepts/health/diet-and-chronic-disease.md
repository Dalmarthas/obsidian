# What It Is

Diet and chronic disease is the claim that everyday food patterns are not peripheral to health outcomes but are major drivers of long-term disease burden. In the current branch, this includes both epidemiological framing and mechanism-level arguments about why some food patterns increase risk while others reduce it.

# Why It Matters

- It turns nutrition from a side topic into a central health variable.
- It explains why prevention can be more powerful than trying to medicate damage after it accumulates.
- It gives the vault a durable health concept that connects daily eating patterns to heart disease, diabetes, hypertension, kidney strain, and some cancer risk.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Michael Greger source argues that most premature deaths are preventable and that diet is one of the major reasons they occur.
- Animal-derived foods are associated in the current summary with `IGF-1`, endotoxin-driven inflammation, atherosclerotic load, and kidney stress.
- Plant-heavy eating is associated in the same source with lower cholesterol, better insulin sensitivity, stronger immune function, and greater cancer-cell suppression.
- The branch also treats modest dietary improvement as meaningful rather than pretending benefits only arrive through perfection.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/lifestyle-medicine|Lifestyle Medicine]], this page focuses on the disease link itself, while lifestyle medicine focuses on the treatment philosophy that follows from it.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/sleep-quality|Sleep Quality]], this page is more long-horizon and disease-oriented, while the sleep branch is more about recovery quality and day-to-day restoration. Together they suggest that health behaves like a system of daily levers rather than a set of isolated hacks.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/whole-food-plant-based-diet|Whole-Food Plant-Based Diet]], this page describes the risk-and-benefit logic underneath the dietary pattern rather than the pattern itself.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The current source is persuasive, but it likely compresses nuance around the relative strength of different mechanisms and the quality gradient within animal foods.
- Prevention language can slide into overconfidence if it underplays genetics, environment, or the role of non-diet health variables.
- The vault still needs more health sources to test how robust these claims remain across different expert perspectives.
