# What It Is
Premortem is a planning method that imagines a future failure in advance and works backward to identify what likely caused it so those risks can be reduced before action begins. In the current vault, it appears first as a timing principle in Daniel Pink and becomes much more explicit through Daniel Levitin as a decision-hygiene tool for stressful situations.
# Why It Matters
- It turns vague anxiety into concrete preparation.
- It improves judgment by moving part of the thinking process out of the crisis moment and into calmer conditions.
- It gives the vault a reusable bridge between stress, decision-making, planning, and prevention.
# Evidence In This Vault
- The Daniel Pink source names premortems as one of the strongest things to do at the beginning of an effort.
- The Daniel Levitin source makes the method concrete by tying it to travel, health, family planning, and everyday avoidable mistakes.
- The same Levitin branch also shows why premortems matter: stress narrows executive function, so advance design protects judgment when judgment will be weakest.
# Source Comparisons
- Daniel Pink uses premortems as one part of a broader timing-and-sequencing model: beginnings improve when failure modes are surfaced early.
- Daniel Levitin uses premortems more concretely and more health-adjacent: they are a practical defense against stress-impaired decision-making.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/stress-mindset|Stress Mindset]], premortem is less about reinterpreting stress and more about reducing the number of things that must be figured out while stressed.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/high-performance-mindset|High-Performance Mindset]], premortem is less psychological and more procedural: install foresight into the system, not only grit into the person.
# Tensions / Open Questions
- Premortems can become pessimistic rumination if they are not tied to actual preventive moves.
- Not every domain benefits equally from exhaustive failure anticipation; some contexts need speed, experimentation, and adaptation more than front-loaded scenario mapping.
- The vault still needs stronger overlap with checklists, crisis protocols, and evidence on how much premortems improve real outcomes.
