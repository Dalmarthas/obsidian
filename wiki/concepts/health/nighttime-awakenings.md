# What It Is

Nighttime awakenings are repeated interruptions of sleep continuity rather than simple difficulty falling asleep. In the current branch, they are treated as a diagnostic problem first: the question is not only “how do I stop waking up?” but “what is actually driving the awakenings?”

# Why It Matters

- It prevents the vault from collapsing different sleep problems into one vague insomnia bucket.
- It points attention toward treatable causes instead of endless trial-and-error hacks.
- It is often the key distinction between “more sleep tips” and “this needs medical follow-up.”

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Rhonda Patrick branch names chronic insomnia, sleep apnea, nocturia, stress, irregular schedules, age-related change, menopause, and environmental disturbance as common drivers of fragmented sleep.
- It also highlights timing mistakes such as late fluids, late meals, alcohol, and caffeine as common contributors.
- CBT-I is positioned as a stronger path for chronic insomnia than random self-experimentation alone.
- CPAP is named as a medically relevant intervention for obstructive sleep apnea.
- The branch makes it clear that some nighttime awakening problems will not be solved by supplements or generic sleep-hygiene checklists.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/circadian-alignment|Circadian Alignment]], this page is more diagnostic and cause-oriented.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/sleep-supplementation|Sleep Supplementation]], this page is upstream: before choosing a compound, identify whether the problem is behavioral, circadian, environmental, or medical.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Sleep self-diagnosis is noisy; people can underreact to medical causes or overreact to normal variation.
- One open question is how long someone should push behavioral changes before moving more decisively toward clinical investigation.
