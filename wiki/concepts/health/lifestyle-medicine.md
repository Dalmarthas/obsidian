# What It Is

Lifestyle medicine is the idea that many chronic-health problems should be addressed at the level of daily causes such as diet, movement, and routine rather than mainly through downstream symptom management. In the current branch, it appears as a critique of the gap between acute-care medicine and true prevention.

# Why It Matters

- It shifts the intervention point upstream.
- It reframes health from "what treatment do I need once damage is present?" to "what inputs are repeatedly creating or undoing the damage?"
- It gives the vault a stronger bridge between practical habit change and medical consequence.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Michael Greger source argues that medicine is effective at rescue and acute care but much weaker at preventing the chronic conditions filling modern life.
- Diet and exercise are presented as root-cause levers rather than nice add-ons to the real treatment.
- The branch also emphasizes that pharmaceuticals often manage symptoms or partial pathways, while food patterns can change the environment generating the pathology in the first place.
- Practical systems such as the `Daily Dozen` make the concept operational instead of moralistic.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/diet-and-chronic-disease|Diet and Chronic Disease]], this page is more about treatment philosophy than causation evidence.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/sleep-quality|Sleep Quality]], this page shows a parallel logic: the Rhonda Patrick branch also improves health through diagnosis, routine, environment, and repeated behavior rather than a single pill or hack.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/daily-dozen|Daily Dozen]], this page is strategic while the checklist is tactical.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Lifestyle medicine can become glib if it implies that all chronic illness is easily reversible through willpower or habit change alone.
- The strongest version of the concept needs to preserve room for medication, acute care, and cases where structural constraints make behavior change hard.
- The vault still needs richer primary evidence and broader health sources to calibrate which lifestyle interventions have the highest confidence.
