# What It Is

Information theory of aging is the claim that aging is driven primarily by loss of epigenetic information rather than by simple accumulation of irreversible genetic mutations. In the current branch, the genome is treated as comparatively stable while the epigenome is treated as the system that increasingly loses the ability to run the right cellular program.

# Why It Matters

- It offers a unifying explanation for multiple aging hallmarks.
- It makes aging sound more potentially reversible than a pure wear-and-tear model.
- It provides the logic behind why reprogramming and epigenetic clocks matter in the same branch.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The longevity composite source uses the analogy of scratches interfering with a reader rather than destroying the stored data itself.
- The branch frames epigenetic clocks as measurable readouts of biological age.
- It also links the theory to partial reprogramming experiments in mice, where some youthful function appears restorable without full dedifferentiation.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The theory is compelling, but the vault currently holds it through one composite secondary summary rather than through primary papers.
- Even if the mechanism is directionally right, the translational gap between mice and humans remains large.
- The safest current use of the concept is explanatory and hypothesis-generating rather than as a near-term promise of reversal.
