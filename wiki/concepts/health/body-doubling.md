# What It Is

Body doubling is the use of another person's presence to stabilize attention, task initiation, follow-through, or emotional regulation. In the current vault, it is treated as an external-regulation tool: another human's proximity, attention, or shared work period can act as borrowed structure when self-starting or self-steadying is unreliable.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a concrete social mechanism for executive support rather than treating support as only advice or encouragement.
- It is practical because it can be low-tech: another person, a coach, a friend, or even speaking thoughts aloud can make action more organized.
- It weakens the self-judging story that needing live support means incompetence rather than a specific regulation need.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current ADHD-anxiety source explicitly recommends working alongside someone else for accountability and structure.
- The same source treats saying thoughts out loud to another person, or even into an audio recording, as a way to organize anxiety and restore sequence.
- Body doubling appears here as one example of a broader `pit crew` model in which regulation can be distributed socially instead of carried alone.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/executive-function-scaffolding|Executive-Function Scaffolding]], body doubling is one specific social scaffold rather than the whole class of supports.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/stress-mindset|Stress Mindset]], body doubling changes the social environment more than the internal narrative.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/adhd-and-anxiety-overlap|ADHD and Anxiety Overlap]], this page is about one concrete intervention rather than the overall mechanism map.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The current branch does not yet specify when body doubling improves genuine skill transfer versus when it only works while the helper is present.
- The idea is intuitively strong and widely used in practice, but the vault still needs stronger evidence on what forms of shared presence work best for which problems.
- Social scaffolds can fail if they become surveillance, pressure, or dependency rather than co-regulation.
