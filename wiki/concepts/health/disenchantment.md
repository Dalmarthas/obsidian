# What It Is

Disenchantment is the moment when a habit loses its imagined reward value because its real experience becomes unmistakably less appealing than expected. In the current vault, this is the hinge between mindfulness and behavior change: the brain stops chasing the habit as strongly when the person feels, rather than merely knows intellectually, that the loop is unsatisfying.

# Why It Matters

- It explains why behavioral change can suddenly feel easier after enough honest observation.
- It offers a cleaner mechanism than `just use discipline`.
- It turns direct experience into the corrective force rather than relying on moral pressure.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Brewer source argues that curiosity reveals the actual unpleasantness or hollowness of a repeated habit.
- It contrasts intellectual knowledge that a behavior is bad with embodied recognition that the behavior is not truly rewarding.
- The same source frames this update as the reason craving weakens from the inside out instead of being overpowered from above.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/reward-based-habit-loops|Reward-Based Habit Loops]], disenchantment is the update that weakens the loop.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/mindful-curiosity|Mindful Curiosity]], disenchantment is the result that curiosity can produce.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/stress-mindset|Stress Mindset]], disenchantment is narrower and more behavior-specific. Stress mindset changes what arousal means; disenchantment changes what a habit is worth.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The current branch does not yet show how disenchantment behaves with substances or behaviors that still produce strong immediate physiological reward despite long-term cost.
- Some habits may remain attractive even when their cost is clear, which means disenchantment is not always enough on its own.
- The vault still needs stronger evidence on how quickly this process generalizes across different domains.
