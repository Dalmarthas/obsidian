# What It Is

Brain health and cognitive aging refers here to preserving memory, processing speed, structural brain integrity, and dementia resistance as people age. In this vault, the current branch makes the topic unusually practical by reducing it to repeatable lifestyle levers rather than treating cognitive decline as a purely mysterious late-life event.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the health domain a more specific cognitive endpoint instead of only general longevity language.
- It ties exercise, sleep, and prevention directly to brain function.
- It helps keep healthspan grounded in whether later life remains mentally usable, not just physically survivable.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source argues that aerobic exercise can protect or even improve hippocampal volume.
- It links deep sleep to glymphatic waste clearance and lower long-term Alzheimer's risk.
- It presents multivitamin use as a surprisingly simple but evidence-backed support for older-adult cognition.
- It also adds risk-reduction logic around alcohol, smoking, obesity, refined carbohydrates, traumatic brain injury, and genetic sensitivity such as `APOE4`.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/sleep-quality|Sleep Quality]], this page is broader and more cognitively targeted; sleep quality is one major lever inside the larger brain-health picture.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/longevity-fundamentals|Longevity Fundamentals]], this page is narrower and more brain-specific.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/healthspan|Healthspan]], this page turns one major aspect of healthspan into a concrete prevention target: staying mentally sharp enough to function well late in life.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- The multivitamin layer is promising in the current source, but likely lower-certainty than exercise and sleep.
- The branch currently rests on one compact secondary summary, so evidence ranking inside the branch is still immature.
- The vault still needs more brain-aging sources to distinguish the strongest causally supported interventions from the merely plausible ones.
