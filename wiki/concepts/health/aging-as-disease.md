# What It Is

Aging as disease is the claim that aging should be treated as a direct medical target rather than as an untouchable background condition that only expresses itself through downstream illnesses. In this vault, the idea matters less as a semantic trick and more as a shift in where medicine aims its effort: earlier, upstream, and more preventively.

# Why It Matters

- It changes the frame from managing age-related damage to trying to slow or reverse the processes creating the damage.
- It strengthens the case for preventive medicine, biomarkers, and earlier intervention.
- It gives the health branch a clearer bridge between daily habits and the larger question of biological decline.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The longevity composite source argues that aging already fits the functional logic of disease because it drives dysfunction, vulnerability, and loss of independence.
- The same branch suggests that formal recognition would change research funding, regulatory pathways, and the willingness to study interventions directly against aging.
- The idea also aligns with the vault's growing healthspan emphasis: if aging is targetable, more years of function become a practical goal rather than a vague aspiration.

# Source Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/lifestyle-medicine|Lifestyle Medicine]], this page is more medical and conceptual: lifestyle medicine is about changing causes through habits, while this page argues that the aging process itself should enter the category of treatable causes.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/healthspan|Healthspan]], this page changes the framing of the problem, while healthspan names the outcome we care about preserving.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/information-theory-of-aging|Information Theory of Aging]], this page is broader and normative; the information-theory page is one proposed explanation for why aging happens.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Calling aging a disease could unlock progress, but it could also medicalize ordinary aging too aggressively if not handled carefully.
- The strongest version of the idea still needs good biomarkers, intervention evidence, and regulatory clarity.
- The vault currently has this idea from a composite secondary source, so it remains a strong branch hypothesis rather than settled doctrine.
