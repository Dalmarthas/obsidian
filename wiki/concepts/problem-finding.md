# What It Is

Problem-finding is the practice of identifying the real issue underneath the stated issue before rushing into solution mode. In the current source base, value often comes less from answering the obvious question and more from clarifying what problem actually needs to be solved.

# Why It Matters

- It prevents wasted effort on surface symptoms.
- It upgrades persuasion from pitching answers to diagnosing reality.
- It links communication with strategy because the best intervention depends on framing the problem correctly.
- It gives the vault a reusable concept that can serve sales, management, consulting, and decision-making branches.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Pink makes the distinction explicit: in a world of information parity, problem-finding often matters more than problem-solving.
- Pink operationalizes the idea through the Five Whys and the move from stated wants to underlying needs.
- Pink's vacuum-cleaner example shows that the apparent product request may conceal a different underlying goal entirely.
- Iger contributes a strategic version of the same idea by repeatedly narrowing diffuse complexity into a few real priorities that actually matter.

# Source Comparisons

- Pink frames problem-finding as a persuasion and diagnosis advantage in everyday work.
- Iger frames it as strategic prioritization inside large organizations where bad framing wastes huge amounts of attention and capital.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Reframing the problem can feel clarifying or condescending depending on whether the other person feels genuinely heard first.
- Some environments reward fast answer-giving more than careful diagnosis, even when the diagnosis is what matters.
