# What It Is

Idea development is the process of noticing, catching, testing, and gradually refining raw creative signals until they can support a real piece of work. In the current source base, ideas are not treated as one-shot lightning bolts. They are built through receptivity, capture, incubation, intuitive trust, and progressively sharper refinement.

# Why It Matters

- Weak idea foundations create expensive downstream problems that craft alone often cannot rescue.
- It gives the vault a shared home for how writers and designers move from vague impulse to workable concept.
- It helps separate genuine development from fake progress that is really just premature execution.
- It preserves an overlap that now appears across film, television, fiction, and design.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The Duffer Brothers emphasize choosing the right big idea, testing it with a clear paragraph, and then keeping development zoomed out while blue-sky exploration matures.
- Lynch treats ideas as things to be caught, written down immediately, and protected through patience, desire, and daydreaming.
- Baldacci's `writer's prism` turns everyday life into story material and then lets ideas percolate before forcing them into manuscript form.
- Carson treats intuition and personal signal-detection as real creative guidance rather than as irrational noise to be suppressed.

# Source Comparisons

- The Duffer Brothers are strongest on staged refinement: big idea, paragraph, blue sky, outline, scaffold, script.
- Lynch is strongest on receptive conditions: make space, protect daydreaming, catch the idea before it sinks again.
- Baldacci is strongest on observational lifestyle: keep seeing the world as potential narrative material and let ideas combine over time.
- Carson is strongest on trusting the signal of what feels alive or provocative, especially when conventional rules would smooth it away.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much reverence for the initial spark can prevent necessary testing and revision.
- Too much early evaluation can kill a promising idea before it has enough form to prove itself.
