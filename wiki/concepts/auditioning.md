# What It Is

Auditioning is the professional practice of showing readiness, range, adaptability, and usefulness under compressed conditions. In the current source base, a good audition is not only a good performance. It is a prepared, directable, strategically framed encounter with a problem the room needs solved.

# Why It Matters

- It gives the vault a concrete acting-career concept rather than burying audition knowledge in source-specific notes.
- It links performance preparation to professional behavior.
- It makes room for the idea that career progress depends on selection and timing, not just hunger for every opportunity.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The source insists that audition preparation should include multiple ways of playing the material, close reading of the sides, and readiness to take direction fully.
- The room is framed as wanting you to succeed because you solve their casting problem if you are right.
- Actors can reset when a read goes badly instead of forcing a dead attempt to completion.
- Consistent craft work outside auditions matters because only working when stakes are immediate leaves the actor ungreased and undertrained.
- Career strategy appears here too: not every role is worth fighting for if the actor is not ready, and avoiding early pigeonholing can be worth short-term pain.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Bold choices help visibility, but choices that are too rigid make actors harder to direct.
- Strategic patience protects long-term career shape, but it can also become fear if used as cover for avoiding risk.
