# What It Is

Question-and-answer structure is the idea that narrative attention is sustained by raising meaningful questions, delaying answers credibly, and paying them off in ways that create further movement. In the current source base, Dan Brown states this most explicitly, but the same logic also appears in Sorkin's audience-first scene construction and the 2010 screenplay article's emphasis on setup and payoff. Main sources: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-dan-brown-masterclass-complete-summary|Dan Brown Masterclass Complete Summary]], [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]], and [[wiki/sources/2010-01-08-5-simple-rules-for-turning-your-cool-idea-into-a-screenplay|5 Simple Rules For Turning Your Cool Idea Into A Screenplay]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives suspense a concrete mechanism instead of treating it as mood alone.
- It supports cliffhangers, reveals, reversals, and chapter propulsion.
- It helps a writer pace answers so readers feel tension rather than confusion or betrayal.
- It creates a way to reason about narrative promises across an entire book.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Brown defines suspense as making promises and withholding fulfillment.
- Brown wants new questions to appear as old ones are answered so momentum never collapses.
- Sorkin warns against confusing the audience or telling them what they already know; both are failures of attention management.
- Sorkin's scene rule that story must move from point A to point B is another form of question-and-answer progression.
- The 2010 screenplay article frames the same logic in setup and payoff terms: you cannot pay off what you have not set up, and you should not set up what will not matter later.
- Across the sources, withholding must be believable rather than arbitrary.

# Source Comparisons

- Brown is the most explicit and systematic about question density, chapter hooks, and deferred answers.
- Sorkin is less hook-driven but equally focused on audience orientation and forward motion.
- The 2010 screenplay article is the most macro-structural, talking about setup and payoff across acts rather than chapter-by-chapter suspense.
- All three agree that narrative energy depends on managing curiosity, expectation, and release.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much withholding can feel manipulative if the reader senses the writer's hand too strongly.
- A high density of questions demands especially clear payoff tracking so the story does not become noisy.
