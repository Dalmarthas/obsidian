# What It Is

Question-and-answer structure is the idea that suspense fiction works by asking readers questions, delaying the answers credibly, and satisfying those answers while generating new questions. In the current source base, this is the core rhythm of a thriller's page-turning effect. Main source: [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-dan-brown-masterclass-complete-summary|Dan Brown Masterclass Complete Summary]].

# Why It Matters

- It gives suspense a concrete mechanism instead of treating it as mood alone.
- It supports cliffhangers, reveals, reversals, and chapter propulsion.
- It helps a writer pace answers so readers feel tension rather than confusion or betrayal.
- It creates a way to reason about narrative promises across an entire book.

# Evidence In This Vault

- Suspense is defined as making promises and withholding fulfillment.
- Early chapters should create as many meaningful questions as possible without becoming incoherent.
- New questions should appear as older ones are answered.
- Withholding must be believable; characters should not ignore obvious information just to protect the plot.
- Chapters often end at the moment of revelation, danger, or transition so the answer is deferred into the next chapter.

# Tensions / Open Questions

- Too much withholding can feel manipulative if the reader senses the writer's hand too strongly.
- A high density of questions demands especially clear payoff tracking so the story does not become noisy.
