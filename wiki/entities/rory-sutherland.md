# Overview

Rory Sutherland enters this vault as a marketing thinker focused on the irrational, emotional, perception-shaped, and constraint-shaped side of value. In the current source base, his role is not campaign craft alone but a broader challenge to optimization-heavy business logic: unusual ideas, framing shifts, psychologically meaningful design, better diagnosis of the real limiting factor, and more explicit attention to agency and uncertainty can all create more value than narrow efficiency improvements.

# Role In This Vault

- He deepens the advertising branch with a more behaviorally informed account of how brands become valuable.
- He adds a strategy-side warning that measurement-heavy systems can become blind to rare breakthrough ideas.
- He gives the business branch two reusable ideas that were previously underdeveloped here: `reverse benchmarking` and `perception-driven value`.
- He now also adds a systems-diagnosis layer through `bottleneck theory`, pushing the vault toward constraint-aware prioritization rather than diffuse optimization.
- The newest source adds a more operational experience-design layer through `psychological leverage`, especially around control, waiting, framing, and low-cost interventions that change how reality is navigated.
- The latest source adds a broken-market layer through first-glance proxy failure, `experience goods`, costly signaling, and Darwinian experimentation as an alternative to rigid top-down rationalism.
- The newest source adds a fuller brand-and-status layer through meaningful friction, scarcity, signaling versus counter-signaling, brand-building versus performance marketing, and customer service as part of marketing.
- The current source adds a clearer innovation-economics layer through `psychological arbitrage`, experienced-time design, and the claim that marketing and innovation are often the same process operating in opposite directions.
- The newer `Why Logic` source adds an institutional-critique layer: narrow proxy metrics, defensibility-driven reasoning, and seriousness theater can block both better talent selection and better psychological solutions.
- The current source adds a slower-feedback systems layer: many of the best loyalty, service, and brand moves work gradually, and they often depend on `hidden functions` leaders fail to notice until they are removed.
- The newest source adds a collective-behavior layer through `social heuristics`: named rules, visible scripts, and binary commitments can outperform precise but fragile guidance when the real system is social.
- The current source adds a sharper customer-value and service-design layer through `transaction utility`, `brand quakes`, and the claim that frontline human judgment often multiplies the whole brand more than dashboard culture admits.
- The current source adds a stronger discovery-economics layer through `fat-tailed marketing`, showing more explicitly why reverse benchmarking, anomaly-hunting, and protected exploration beat average-case optimization in creative markets.
- The current source adds a clearer frame-power layer through `paradigm shifts`, arguing that some of the biggest gains come from changing the map, category, or mental model rather than the underlying mechanics.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source treats breakthrough marketing as `fat-tailed`, with rare ideas producing outsized results.
- It argues that firms often win not by copying category best practice but by investing where the category is neglectful.
- It also insists that perceived value can be strategically redesigned through framing, context, aesthetics, and emotional meaning.
- The newer source adds that growth is often blocked by one real constraint at a time, and that the hard task is identifying whether the true limiter is awareness, availability, logistics, or another overlooked narrow point.
- The newest source adds that uncertainty, imposedness, and lack of felt control are often hidden experience costs, which means some of the highest-return interventions are not mechanical upgrades but psychological ones such as visibility, reframing, and milestone design.
- The latest source adds that markets for jobs, dating, housing, and products often fail because everyone sorts on the same shallow proxies, while real value may be delayed, experiential, or visible only through better signaling and experimentation.
- The newest source adds that perceived value is also shaped by helpful friction, story, packaging, recovery behavior, and status logic, which means brands often compete through meaning systems rather than through feature improvements alone.
- The current source adds that how time feels can matter more than how much time passes, and that weakly measured motives such as status, certainty, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness are often where underpriced opportunities hide.
- The newer `Why Logic` source adds that B2B buyers, hiring systems, and policymakers are also subject to framing effects, narrow proxy traps, and defensibility bias, which means Rory's branch applies beyond consumer advertising into selection systems and public interventions.
- The current source adds that products, service touches, workplace design, and human roles often carry tacit functions such as trust, bonding, recognition, and serendipity, which means reductionist optimization can quietly destroy compounding value.
- The newest source adds that some apparently irrational habits are actually robust social technologies: `designated driver`, `zipper merge`, and fixed-rule commitments work because they are memorable, observable, and easy to coordinate around.
- The current source adds that willingness to pay depends partly on the felt quality of the transaction itself, and that organizations often destroy brand trust when they optimize local service costs while ignoring hidden relationship value.
- The current source adds that marketing and innovation are governed by power-law upside, so the real managerial problem is often not cleverness alone but preserving room for uncommon ideas inside systems built to reward comparability.
- The current source adds that many bad business decisions are really framing failures: fake either/or constraints, thin-tailed scoreboards, and parameter-level thinking hide higher-leverage `do both` or paradigm-change options.
