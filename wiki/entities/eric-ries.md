# Overview

Eric Ries enters this vault as the main figure behind the Lean Startup method: an experimentation-first approach to building products under uncertainty. In the current source base, his role is not broad startup culture or founder biography, but a disciplined model for testing assumptions before teams overinvest in product, infrastructure, or certainty. The newer source in his branch widens that role from product-testing tactics into managerial design by defining startups through uncertainty and by adding innovation-accounting logic.

# Role In This Vault

- He sharpens the business branch's language for early-stage validation.
- He adds a cleaner general model behind ideas the vault already carried in more niche-specific form through Russian demand-testing pages.
- He gives the business branch a compact behavior-first decision rule: run an MVP, measure real action, then pivot or persevere.
- He also gives the branch a governance layer: learning should be tracked through metrics that reflect real behavioral movement rather than through dashboard theater.

# Evidence In This Vault

- The first source defines MVPs as learning instruments rather than miniaturized final products.
- It treats real customer behavior as more reliable than enthusiasm, compliments, or market-survey optimism.
- It highlights concierge and smokescreen MVPs as practical testing modes for reducing waste.
- The second source expands the branch by defining startups as institutions operating under extreme uncertainty, not only as small companies.
- That source also introduces innovation accounting as the mechanism that makes experimentation governable inside both startups and larger organizations.
