# Overview

Daniel Kahneman enters this vault as the clearest current source on how judgment actually misfires. In the present source base, his role is not generic psychology history. It is a practical model of fast versus slow thinking, the conditions under which intuition deserves trust, the mismatch between felt confidence and actual evidence, and the specific bias patterns that make familiarity, default-preservation, and incomplete stories look more rational than they are.

# Role In This Vault

- He gives the vault a canonical language for discussing bias, substitution, and effortful override.
- He sharpens the difference between real expertise and overconfident guesswork in noisy domains.
- He strengthens the existing decision-quality branch by explaining why procedural safeguards matter before stress or stakes rise further.
- The newer source also makes his branch more operational by naming three repeated traps:
  - familiarity mistaken for truth
  - current-state clinging through loss aversion
  - WYSIATI-style confidence from incomplete evidence

# Evidence In This Vault

- The current source frames judgment through `System 1` and `System 2`.
- It argues that intuition works best in regular environments with fast feedback and becomes unreliable in unpredictable ones.
- It also claims that confidence is often generated by story coherence rather than by evidential strength.
- The newer source adds canonical bias pages for [[wiki/concepts/others/mere-exposure-effect|Mere-Exposure Effect]], [[wiki/concepts/others/status-quo-bias|Status Quo Bias]], and [[wiki/concepts/others/wysiati|WYSIATI]].
