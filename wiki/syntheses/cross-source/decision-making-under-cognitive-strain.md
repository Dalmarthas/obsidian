# Question

What reusable model of better judgment emerges when Kahneman's two-systems thinking is read alongside the current Levitin decision-hygiene branch?

# Answer

Together, the two sources produce a five-part decision model:

1. Assume the first answer arrives automatically.
   Human judgment starts with fast pattern completion, not with detached analysis, so the first interpretation should not be mistaken for the best one.

2. Ask whether the domain deserves intuition.
   Fast judgment is most trustworthy where patterns are stable and feedback is real. In noisy, irregular domains, confident intuition may be mostly illusion.

3. Escalate to deliberate procedure when novelty, stakes, or ambiguity rise.
   Slow review, explicit criteria, checklists, and evidence review matter most where intuitive pattern recognition is least reliable.

4. Move important thinking upstream.
   Premortems, advance planning, and fixed systems reduce the amount of reasoning that must be done while stressed, rushed, or cognitively narrowed.

5. Distrust confidence and ask for better evidence.
   A coherent story can feel certain while still being weak. Better judgment therefore depends on concrete tradeoffs, explicit assumptions, and pressure-tested reasoning rather than on felt conviction.

# Basis

- Kahneman contributes the architecture problem: fast cognition is powerful, necessary, and systematically bias-prone.
- Levitin contributes the procedural answer: protect judgment before crisis through premortems, externalized systems, and concrete risk questions.
- Canonical concepts: [[wiki/concepts/others/two-systems-thinking|Two-Systems Thinking]], [[wiki/concepts/others/expert-intuition|Expert Intuition]], [[wiki/concepts/health/premortem|Premortem]]

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/health/daniel-levitin-decision-hygiene-framework|Daniel Levitin Decision Hygiene Framework]], this synthesis explains more clearly why those procedures are needed: they compensate for predictable cognitive failure modes, not only for generic stress.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/stress-mindset|Stress Mindset]], this synthesis is less about interpreting arousal constructively and more about preventing reasoning errors when arousal, confidence, or novelty distort judgment.
- Kahneman is stronger on diagnosis of judgment failure; Levitin is stronger on everyday preventive design.

# Gaps

- The current branch still needs stronger overlap with forecasting, checklists, aviation or surgery-style error reduction, and evidence-based debiasing methods.
- Both current sources are secondary summaries, so later ingest should test the practical claims against stronger primary material.
- The vault still lacks a clearer model for when fast heuristics remain good enough despite imperfect evidence.
