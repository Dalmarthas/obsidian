# Question

How do the screenwriting sources in this vault differ in their outlining approaches?

# Answer

The current vault now supports a clear compiled answer: strong screenwriters do not share one outline format, but they do share a commitment to making story shape visible before or during drafting. The main difference is not `outline versus no outline`. The difference is how much structure each writer needs to externalize, how early they do it, and how tightly they hold it once drafting begins.

The most useful shared model is:

1. Treat structure as non-optional.
2. Choose the lightest planning tool that still reveals macro-shape.
3. Expect the outline to evolve once the draft starts exposing real dramatic truth.
4. Use rules as diagnostics, not as orthodoxy.

# Comparisons

- The 2010 article is strongest on certainty:
  know the beginning, middle, end, midpoint, low point, and act turns before drafting.
- The Duffer Brothers are strongest on recursive layering:
  start broad, keep refining, and do not rush into expensive pages too early.
- Lynch is strongest on minimal friction:
  scene headings and cards can hold the film lightly without prematurely overdefining it.
- Truby is strongest on deep architecture:
  the outline should pressure-test premise, genre, weakness, and transformation, not just mark turning points.
- Edson is strongest on propulsion:
  a usable outline is one that keeps the protagonist moving through short-term goals, surprises, and new directions.
- Hauge is strongest on first-act orientation:
  the outline should make empathy, ordinary life, and visible outer motivation legible before the story fully accelerates.
- The current comparative source is strongest on pluralism:
  midpoint-only planning, late outlining, cards, beat sheets, sequence pages, and notebooks can all work if they reveal shape clearly enough.

Taken together, the branch now points toward outline flexibility rather than outline relativism. Not every method works equally well for every writer, but every durable method makes pacing, escalation, and story rhythm visible in some form.

# Gaps

- This comparison still rests mostly on secondary summaries rather than on full primary interviews, notes, or original craft books.
- The current source compresses many named writers into one summary, so some individual differences remain broader than ideal.
- The branch still needs more primary screenplay evidence showing how different outlining systems actually change finished scene quality.
