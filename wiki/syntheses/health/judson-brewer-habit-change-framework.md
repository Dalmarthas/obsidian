# Question

What reusable habit-change model does the current Judson Brewer source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a five-part model:

1. Map the habit as a reward loop.
   Start by identifying the trigger, the behavior, and the reward the brain expects from repeating it.

2. Stop betting on willpower alone.
   Under stress, cognitive control weakens, so brute-force resistance is unreliable precisely when the habit is strongest.

3. Meet urges with curiosity, not suppression.
   When a craving appears, study it closely: where it lands in the body, how it moves, and what emotional tone it carries.

4. Update the reward value through direct experience.
   Observe honestly what the habit actually feels like and whether it delivers what the brain predicted.

5. Let disenchantment loosen the loop.
   As the habit feels less rewarding in a concrete, embodied way, the brain stops pursuing it as automatically and change becomes less forced.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/health/adhd-anxiety-management-framework|ADHD Anxiety Management Framework]], this model is less about external supports and more about updating the internal reward logic of a habit loop.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/stress-mindset|Stress Mindset]], this framework is narrower and more behavior-specific. Stress mindset changes how pressure is interpreted; Brewer changes how cravings are examined and relearned.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/health/executive-function-scaffolding|Executive-Function Scaffolding]], this framework relies less on reminders and routines and more on real-time awareness inside the moment of urge.

# Gaps

- The current branch still needs stronger primary evidence coverage and clearer distinctions between everyday habits, addictions, and trauma-driven coping.
- The vault does not yet contain a dedicated page on relapse dynamics or on when curiosity-based methods should be paired with medical or social interventions.
- The mechanism is elegant, but the source does not fully sort when context change matters more than inner observation alone.
