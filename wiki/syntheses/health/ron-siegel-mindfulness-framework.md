# Question

What reusable mindfulness framework does the current Ron Siegel source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part mindfulness framework:

1. Start from human design, not personal defect.
   Distress is easier to understand when negativity bias, comparison, and mortality awareness are seen as built into the ordinary human mind.

2. Define mindfulness as awareness plus acceptance.
   Attention alone is not enough; the key ingredient is a kinder, nonjudgmental relationship to present experience.

3. Treat experiential avoidance as a core maintainer of suffering.
   Anxiety, depression, and reactive coping deepen when inner discomfort becomes something life must be organized around escaping.

4. Practice returning, not performing.
   Mind wandering is normal. The skill is the gentle repetition of noticing and returning rather than trying to force a perfect mental state.

5. Use mindfulness to change relationship, not to guarantee immediate relief.
   The aim is to become more able to be with discomfort, impermanence, and emotion without automatic shutdown or panic-driven control.

6. Keep the method evidence-based and bounded.
   Mindfulness can help meaningfully, but it should be tailored, practiced consistently, and integrated with broader care when needed.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/health/judson-brewer-habit-change-framework|Judson Brewer Habit Change Framework]], Siegel is broader and more foundational. Brewer explains how mindfulness can update cravings and reward learning; Siegel explains what mindfulness is, why distress arises, and why acceptance matters across anxiety and depression more generally.
- Brewer is more behavior-loop-specific and micro-level. Siegel is more clinically general, mechanism-oriented, and explicit about limits and evidence.

# Gaps

- The branch still needs stronger primary-source coverage and more direct treatment of trauma sensitivity, adverse effects, and who should not start with standard inward-attention practices.
- The vault also needs richer comparisons between mindfulness, CBT, exposure, self-compassion training, and medication-supported care.
