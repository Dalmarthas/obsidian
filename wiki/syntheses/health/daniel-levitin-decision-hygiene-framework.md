# Question
What reusable decision-hygiene model does the current Daniel Levitin source add to the vault?
# Answer
The source adds a five-part model:
1. Assume stress will degrade thinking.
   Do not build important decisions on the fantasy that you will be maximally rational in the hardest moment.
2. Run a premortem before the event.
   Imagine that the trip, treatment, or decision has already gone badly, then identify the likely causes while calm enough to act on them.
3. Externalize memory into systems.
   Fixed places, copies of documents, and stable routines reduce the number of preventable failures created by stressed cognition.
4. Ask for concrete risk numbers.
   Better decisions come from explicit tradeoffs, not from vague language about benefit or safety.
5. Move hard conversations earlier.
   Medical, financial, and quality-of-life choices are often better discussed before fear and urgency compress judgment.

# Comparisons
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/health/kelly-mcgonigal-stress-resilience-framework|Kelly McGonigal Stress Resilience Framework]], Levitin is less about healthier interpretation of stress and more about protecting decisions from stress-impaired cognition.
- Compared with Daniel Pink's timing branch, Levitin makes premortem more operational and more anchored in health, travel, and emergency-adjacent life management.
- Compared with broader prevention models in the health branch, this source is less about long-horizon biomarkers and more about avoiding avoidable mistakes when stakes suddenly rise.
# Gaps
- The current branch rests on one secondary summary and needs stronger primary evidence or adjacent sources on checklists, emergency cognition, and medical decision quality.
- Some cited medical numbers in the summary should be treated as prompts for verification, not as durable reference values.
- The branch still needs more explicit comparison between healthy preparation and anxiety-driven overplanning.
