# Question

What reusable leadership framework does the Simon Sinek source add to the vault?

# Answer

The current source base supports a seventeen-part model:

1. Evaluate people on trust as well as output.
   Performance matters, but trustworthiness is a separate leadership variable that changes team safety and cohesion.

2. Design the environment so people can tell the truth.
   Trust-centered leadership is not only about who gets selected. It is also about whether people can admit mistakes, ask for help, and show vulnerability without humiliation.

3. Prefer the reliable teammate over the toxic star.
   Medium-performance/high-trust people may strengthen the system more than higher-output people who damage the social environment.

4. Make invisible cultural labor visible.
   Reward the people who create safety, support, and team resilience rather than only those with the loudest measurable results.

5. Treat trust as a daily practice, not a one-off initiative.
   Culture changes through repeated support, not symbolic events or occasional speeches.

6. Coach behavior before cutting talent.
   High performers with character problems should be given a real chance to improve, but not indefinite protection.

7. Build systems that ask the team who they trust.
   If an organization does not know who truly has others' backs, it is flying blind on culture.

8. Model vulnerability without collapse.
   Trust rises when leaders can say `I need help` or `I don't know` with steadiness, because it teaches others that honesty is safe and competence does not require pretense.

9. Adapt feedback to the person.
   Trust-centered leadership does not assume one feedback style fits everyone; it learns how each person can best hear difficult truth.

10. Build culture through micro-behaviors.
   Phone-away presence, eye contact, truthful small moments, and real follow-up matter because teams infer the real culture from repeated signals, not from slogans.

11. Build a circle of safety.
   Trust-centered leadership begins when people believe they are protected inside the group and will not be casually sacrificed to numbers, ego, or convenience.

12. Keep threat outside the team when possible.
   The leader cannot remove market pressure or uncertainty, but can decide whether employees must also fear each other, their managers, or arbitrary institutional punishment.

13. Balance the chemistry of work.
   Goal pursuit matters, but systems built only around targets, bonuses, and status can reward selfishness and fear. Trust-centered leadership makes belonging, service, and mutual protection materially credible too.

14. Go first in sacrifice.
   The leader's moral job is to absorb discomfort, risk, or loss before asking others to do so, because protection and reciprocity grow when sacrifice flows downward instead of upward.

15. Treat leadership as behavior, not rank.
   Formal authority does not guarantee leadership; people at any level become leaders when they consistently care for those to their left and right.

16. Diagnose fear through hiding behavior.
   When people conceal mistakes, rigidly follow rules to avoid blame, or keep their heads down, the first diagnostic should be the environment leaders have built, not only the individual's character.

17. Maintain trust like fitness.
   Trusting teams are built through repeated small practices: asking what people need, making help-seeking safe, running learning-oriented postmortems, and responding to vulnerability with support. One-off events cannot substitute for the daily lifestyle of leadership.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/leadership/geno-auriemma-team-leadership-framework|Geno Auriemma Team Leadership Framework]], Sinek is less focused on standards and more focused on who should be trusted with membership and advancement.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/leadership/jurgen-klopp-leadership-framework|Jurgen Klopp Leadership Framework]], Sinek is less about emotional belonging and individualized handling, and more about trust as a hard personnel filter.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/schultz-values-driven-leadership-framework|Schultz Values-Driven Leadership Framework]], Sinek is less about large-scale company-building systems and more about the local moral logic of team selection and recognition.
- The newer Sinek source broadens the earlier one materially:
  - the first source emphasizes trust as a personnel and recognition filter
  - the newer source emphasizes trust as an environmental condition leaders must build and maintain every day
- The newest conversation broadens it again:
  - trust is also shaped by how leaders ask for help
  - how they tailor feedback
  - and whether their smallest repeated behaviors prove they care
- The newest TED talk broadens it one more step:
  - trust is grounded in felt safety before it becomes openness or feedback receptivity
  - leaders earn loyalty when they protect people under pressure
  - shared sacrifice reveals whether the culture is real
- The newest `Leaders Eat Last` source broadens it again:
  - reward systems and status structures can quietly undermine trust even when the rhetoric sounds humane
  - internal safety matters partly because it keeps effort aimed at external problems instead of internal defense
  - the chemistry framing gives the branch a more explicit explanation for why fear-based cultures get more selfish and short-term under pressure
- The new `Trusting Teams` source sharpens the earlier environment source:
  - the Noah example shows the same person performing differently under different management climates
  - the United/Virgin contrast makes rule rigidity a possible fear signal
  - the fitness analogy turns trust-building from a value into a maintenance practice

# Gaps

- This framework rests on secondary summaries rather than primary transcripts, direct implementation data, or measured culture-change studies.
- The branch is strong on diagnosis and principle but still lighter on implementation detail for trust measurement and fear-reduction routines.
- The branch is now stronger on vulnerable leadership and feedback practice, but it still needs better evidence on how to scale these behaviors in large systems without turning them into scripted performance.
- The branch is now stronger on safety and sacrificial leadership, but it still needs sharper guidance on when people-first coaching should give way to firm removal, restructuring, or other hard boundary decisions.
- The newest source makes the neurochemical explanation more vivid, but the vault still needs stronger support for how literally to treat those biology claims versus using them as memorable heuristics for incentive design.
- The vault still needs more business-side examples where trust-first promotion logic and leader-built safety were applied well or badly.
- The new `Trusting Teams` source is vivid on service anecdotes, but the vault still needs better tools for measuring trust, concealment, and judgment quality before a crisis exposes them.
