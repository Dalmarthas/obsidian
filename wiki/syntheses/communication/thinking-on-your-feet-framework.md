# Question

What durable framework does the current vault support for responding clearly under live speaking pressure?

# Answer

The current branch supports a six-part framework:

1. Prepare until spontaneity becomes easier.
   The cleanest hidden claim in the source is that many `off-the-cuff` answers are really pre-anticipated answers in disguise.

2. Enter the moment before trying to impress the room.
   Breathing, reducing self-talk, and re-centering attention matter because a cluttered mind cannot retrieve or sequence well.

3. Lead with the point, then add support.
   Under pressure, people often over-contextualize. A stronger move is to state the main answer first and only then elaborate.

4. Clarify broad questions before answering them.
   If the prompt is too wide, ask what specifically matters. Better question boundaries create better response boundaries.

5. Elaborate only enough to create understanding.
   A short example, application, or anecdote can sharpen meaning, but over-elaboration quickly turns into drift.

6. Speak from knowledge, not from panic-generated filler.
   Real-time credibility improves when the speaker answers from prepared knowledge, writes a few notes if needed, and admits when the answer belongs with someone else.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/communication/george-stephanopoulos-communication-framework|George Stephanopoulos Communication Framework]], this synthesis is narrower and more presentational. Stephanopoulos is stronger on interviewing, clarity, and silence under pressure; this framework is stronger on elaboration, point-first answering, and anticipating likely question classes.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/charismatic-presence|Charismatic Presence]], this framework is less about felt warmth and composure and more about retrieval, sequencing, and live answer construction.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/calibrated-questions|Calibrated Questions]], this branch uses clarification as a self-stabilizing response tool rather than as a negotiation tactic.

# Gaps

- The current framework is built from one practical article and still needs stronger support from debate, interviewing, teaching, or Q-and-A-heavy leadership sources.
- The vault still lacks a sharper distinction between productive pause and visible hesitation.
- More evidence is needed on when improvisation training helps versus when it rewards stylish rambling.
