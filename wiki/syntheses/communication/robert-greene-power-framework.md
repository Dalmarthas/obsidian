# Question

What reusable power framework does the current Robert Greene source add to the vault?

# Answer

The current source base supports a seven-part model:

1. Protect and weaponize reputation.
   Reputation is treated as compound leverage because it shapes what others assume before they verify.

2. Avoid unnecessary legibility.
   Concealment, selective silence, and not overexplaining reduce the amount of usable leverage others have against you.

3. Make leverage asymmetric.
   Stronger positions emerge when others rely more on your access, value, or permission than you rely on theirs.

4. Control the field, not just the argument.
   Power grows when you define options, create the context, and make others react inside boundaries you set.

5. Use timing and boldness together.
   Greene's model does not reward impulsivity; it rewards waiting well, then acting decisively enough that hesitation does not kill the advantage.

6. Manage visibility strategically.
   Attention, spectacle, absence, and apparent effortlessness all shape perceived rank and memorability.

7. Stay adaptive enough to avoid entrapment.
   `Formlessness` is the closing principle: rigid identities, routines, and public patterns make a person easier to predict and contain.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/persuasion|Persuasion]], Greene is less humane and less collaborative. The branch treats influence as contested terrain rather than mutual movement toward understanding.
- Compared with the Chase Hughes-derived [[wiki/concepts/communication/frame-control|Frame Control]] and [[wiki/concepts/communication/interactional-authority|Interactional Authority]] pages, Greene is more overtly political and less grounded in calm presence. He cares more about strategic opacity, status exposure, and dependence than about non-needy authenticity.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/cross-source/hidden-motives-and-social-behavior|Hidden Motives and Social Behavior]], this framework moves from diagnosis to action. The earlier Greene source explains why people behave irrationally; this one explains how a strategically minded actor might exploit, survive, or navigate that fact.
- The most useful vault-level reading may be mixed:
  - as an offensive model, Greene is ethically risky
  - as a defensive model, he sharpens awareness of manipulation, reputational games, and hidden option control

# Gaps

- The current source is a compressed secondary overview, so the branch still lacks deeper treatment of which laws are most central versus merely memorable.
- The vault still needs stronger countermodels from principled negotiation, institutional trust design, and moral philosophy.
- A future synthesis should compare Greene directly with Chris Voss, Daniel Pink, and leadership sources to make the ethics split more explicit.
