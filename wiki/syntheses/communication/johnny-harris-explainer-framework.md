# Question

What reusable explainer-storytelling model does the current Johnny Harris source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a five-part explainer model:

1. Start with experience, not abstraction.
   Let the audience meet the phenomenon through a concrete visual or sensory hook before asking them to process context.

2. Alternate anchors and bridges.
   Use striking evidence to earn curiosity, then use short explanation to orient the viewer, then repeat.

3. Keep context concise and timed.
   Explanation should arrive when the audience wants it, not as a front-loaded block that delays contact with the actual subject.

4. Choose anchors that are specific and intuitively legible.
   Generic graphics or abstract data are weaker than objects, scenes, or visuals that instantly imply stakes and texture.

5. Back the structure with real research.
   The format holds attention, but substance comes from enough investigation that the visuals mean something and the explanation can cash the check the hook writes.

# Comparisons

- Compared with generic information delivery, this branch prioritizes curiosity and sensory contact before abstract framing.
- Compared with purely cinematic visual-storytelling models, this branch keeps one foot in explanation: the bridge matters as much as the anchor, but it must stay short enough not to kill momentum.
- Compared with copywriting-style specificity, the emphasis is less on wording alone and more on how evidence is staged, sequenced, and rhythmically edited.

# Gaps

- This branch is still based on one secondary summary rather than a larger body of creator interviews or production breakdowns.
- The vault still needs stronger counterexamples showing when context-first explanation is the better move.
- The branch also needs more explicit evidence on how the method changes across video, writing, live speaking, and teaching.
