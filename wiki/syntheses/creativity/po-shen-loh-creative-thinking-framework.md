# Question

What creative-thinking framework does Po-Shen Loh add to the vault?

# Answer

The sources support a nine-part framework:

1. Move from answer production to answer judgment.
   In AI-rich environments, the scarce skill is not only producing a first draft. It is grading the draft: checking logic, assumptions, edge cases, and fit to context.

2. Preserve novelty in training.
   Creativity grows from unfamiliar problems that require invention. A system that pre-rehearses every pattern may raise short-term test performance while weakening flexible thought.

3. Use narrow domains for deep transfer.
   Loh's chosen math areas are narrow on purpose. Algebra, geometry, combinatorics, and number theory become gyms for general reasoning when the problems stay unfamiliar enough to require method-finding.

4. Replace passive AI use with critical integration.
   AI output should be treated like related code from StackOverflow: useful material to inspect, adapt, and integrate, not an answer to paste unexamined.

5. Learn through people, not only abstractions.
   Creative value depends on direct contact with real users, students, parents, schools, and constraints. Field interaction discovers pain points that desk ideation misses.

6. Design win-win-win systems.
   Loh's education model combines middle school learners, advanced high school math students, and drama-trained coaches so each group receives a real developmental or economic benefit.

7. Treat communication as part of creativity.
   Technical expertise compounds when the expert can teach, perform, improvise, and make complex ideas emotionally accessible.

8. Generate, destroy, and refine.
   The newer Loh source makes idea discipline more explicit: create many unconventional possibilities, attack their weaknesses, and scale only the rare robust idea that survives.

9. Aim creativity at human value.
   Loh's second source reframes creativity away from self-display and toward thoughtful value creation: the best ideas should improve or delight real people, not merely prove cleverness.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/ai/jeremy-utley-ai-collaboration-framework|Jeremy Utley AI Collaboration Framework]], Loh is less focused on prompt technique and more focused on the educational prerequisite for AI use: the human must be capable of judging generated output.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/ai/kasparov-human-machine-collaboration-framework|Kasparov Human-Machine Collaboration Framework]], Loh is less about process architecture between expert and machine and more about training people to remain flexible enough to use tools without becoming passive.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/effective-practice|Effective Practice]], Loh adds an anti-drill caveat: good practice should not eliminate the novelty that trains transfer and invention.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/creativity/idea-development|Idea Development]], Loh's creativity is more explicitly social and problem-driven. Ideas are not only caught and refined; they are discovered by contact with real constraints.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/ai/po-shen-loh-ai-era-thoughtfulness-framework|Po-Shen Loh AI-Era Thoughtfulness Framework]], this page stays centered on creative thinking and problem-solving, while the AI-era thoughtfulness page includes broader character, bias, education, and social-resilience claims.

# Gaps

- The source does not give a detailed classroom protocol for how each live class is structured.
- The vault still needs a clearer novelty-dosing model: how much repetition should precede unfamiliar problem work for beginners?
- The AI-judgment claim needs domain-specific treatment because grading generated answers is much easier for experts than for novices.
- The newer source makes the mission broader, but the vault still needs clearer evidence on whether Loh's program builds durable thoughtfulness beyond class engagement.
