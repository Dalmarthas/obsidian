# Question

What key craft principles overlap across the 2010 screenplay article, Aaron Sorkin, and Dan Brown?

# Answer

The current source base supports six recurring craft principles:

1. Structure is not optional.
   All three sources assume that narrative work needs shape, even if they talk about that shape differently.

2. Attention must be actively managed.
   Setup/payoff, scene movement, chapter hooks, and deferred answers are all ways of keeping the audience moving.

3. Preparation matters, but not as an excuse to avoid making pages.
   The 2010 article stresses outlining, Brown stresses blueprint-like planning and process protection, and Sorkin stresses the need to finish the draft before over-correcting it.

4. Revision is where much of the real craft appears.
   Sorkin and Brown in particular treat cutting, reshaping, and diagnostic thinking as central rather than secondary.

5. The audience or reader is active, not passive.
   Sorkin frames this as audience collaboration, Brown as question-and-answer tension, and the 2010 article as setup and payoff that orient viewers.

6. Strong work comes from disciplined systems, not waiting for inspiration.
   All three reject romantic drift in favor of repeatable craft behavior.

# Comparisons

- On structure:
  The 2010 article is most explicit about three-act planning and beat placement.
  Sorkin treats classical structure as a practical storytelling language.
  Brown cares less about named act theory and more about question density, urgency, and forward pull.

- On audience attention:
  Sorkin emphasizes clarity and collaboration.
  Brown emphasizes suspense via promises and answers.
  The 2010 article emphasizes setup and payoff as the backbone of coherence.

- On process:
  The 2010 article front-loads preparation.
  Sorkin emphasizes finishing and then rewriting.
  Brown emphasizes routine protection, side-by-side drafting modes, and later distillation.

- On medium:
  The 2010 article and Sorkin speak from screenwriting.
  Brown speaks from thriller fiction.
  The overlap suggests that many narrative principles travel across media even when their local implementation changes.

# Gaps

- This comparison is still built from secondary summaries rather than primary scripts or full transcripts.
- The vault still needs counterexamples from writers who reject classical structure more aggressively.
- The current overlap map is strongest on craft process and weaker on character philosophy, dialogue, and research comparison.
