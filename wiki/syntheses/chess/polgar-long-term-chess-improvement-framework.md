# Question

What long-term chess-improvement framework does this vault currently extract from the Polgar-style training source?

# Answer

The current source supports a six-part long-term framework:

1. Build improvement mainly on structured position-solving rather than opening consumption.
2. Progress through a broad curriculum of mates, miniatures, middlegames, and endgames instead of over-specializing early.
3. Solve slowly on a physical board and write answers before checking so the training resembles serious play.
4. Judge study by depth and quality of thinking, not by raw speed or puzzle count.
5. Keep opening study mostly at the level of principles until strength justifies deeper theory.
6. Recycle the material over years as a long-horizon practice rather than expecting a short burst of mastery.

This framework is less about instant practical tricks and more about building durable chess cognition.

# Basis

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-polgar-long-term-chess-improvement-plan]] frames the method around large-scale problem-solving with sparse explanation.
- The source also stresses physical-board work, written solutions, and reviewing mistakes as part of deliberate transfer to real games.
- It explicitly treats opening theory as secondary for most non-master players.

# Gaps

- The branch still lacks direct Polgar-primary-source material and stronger comparative evidence about how adult improvers should combine this method with game analysis, coaching, and opening work.
- It also still needs more direct sources on technical endgames and positional chess to know how complete this framework really is on its own.
