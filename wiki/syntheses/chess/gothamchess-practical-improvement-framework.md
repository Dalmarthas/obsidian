# Question

What practical framework does this vault currently use for improving and winning more chess games?

# Answer

The current branch supports an eleven-part practical framework:

1. Enter the opening with plans, not just moves.
2. Build board vision by seeing what every piece attacks, defends, and enables.
3. Ask why before committing to exchanges or captures.
4. Scan every move for tactical forcing ideas and tactical danger.
5. Use a repeatable anti-blunder checklist such as checks, captures, and attacks for both sides.
6. Read the position through its imbalances: files, squares, king placement, pawn structure, and piece-type differences should shape the plan.
7. Attack only when coordination and attacker count justify it rather than because the king "looks weak."
8. Use time in proportion to the position instead of rushing or freezing.
9. Reassess when the phase of the game changes rather than clinging to the old plan.
10. Simplify and convert cleanly when the position justifies it.
11. Use bots as a controlled training tool for patterns and conversion, but not as a replacement for human play and post-game analysis.

This framework is practical rather than theoretical. It is built for reducing common club-player mistakes: aimless exchanges, missed tactics, poor visual awareness, time misuse, "hope chess," structural self-damage, and failure to convert an edge.

# Basis

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-gothamchess-how-to-win-at-chess]] frames the game as a chain of practical decisions across openings, middlegames, and endgames.
- The source treats tactical awareness and purposeful exchanges as recurring error filters.
- The source also frames post-game mistake analysis as part of improvement, because explanation makes patterns reusable.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-gothamchess-beginner-chess-guide]] adds a more explicit beginner-operating system around opening principles, board vision, trade evaluation, time management, and pre-move checklists.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-gothamchess-essential-chess-tips]] adds the stronger planning layer: play from opening themes, read imbalances, punish mistakes with a checklist, respect attacker-versus-defender logic, and stay principled against unfamiliar moves.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-gothamchess-beating-beginner-chess-bots]] adds the most explicit Gotham training-context layer so far: steady principled play, simplification when ahead, basic mating technique, and skepticism toward over-reliance on artificial opponents.

# Gaps

- The branch still lacks deeper material on formal calculation training, positional play, and technical endgame theory.
- It still needs a dedicated source on attack evaluation and a stronger dedicated source on repertoire construction before those deserve their own canonical pages.
- It also still needs a richer comparison of bots, puzzles, engines, and human games as different training environments.
