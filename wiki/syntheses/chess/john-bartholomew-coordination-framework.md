# Question

What coordination framework does this vault extract from John Bartholomew's piece-and-pawn lesson?

# Answer

The source supports a six-part coordination model:

1. Develop pieces and pawns as one system rather than as isolated moves.
2. Spend early pawn moves only when they help development, center control, or a clear structural goal.
3. Avoid repeating piece moves in the opening unless the repetition wins something concrete or prevents immediate harm.
4. Treat undefended pieces as constant tactical liabilities that reveal coordination failure.
5. Do not trade pieces casually when behind in development; catch up first unless the exchange solves a concrete problem.
6. Use mini-goals to restore order when the position becomes messy instead of forcing a premature attack or simplification.

This framework strengthens the chess branch by connecting opening discipline, tactical safety, and structural judgment into one practical model of coordinated play.

# Basis

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-john-bartholomew-piece-and-pawn-coordination]] is the most direct current source in the vault on coordination as a unified relationship between pieces, pawns, and development pace.
- The source also adds a useful training layer: coordination can be practiced through simple operational checkpoints rather than only through abstract strategic vocabulary.

# Gaps

- The branch still lacks a deeper source on initiative races, prophylaxis, and when breaking coordination rules is positionally justified.
- It also needs more explicit comparison between John Bartholomew's classical beginner guidance and more flexible modern opening pedagogy.
