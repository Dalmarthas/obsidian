# Question

What practical anti-blunder framework does this vault currently extract from Ben Finegold's lecture on chess blunders?

# Answer

The current source supports a five-part blunder-management framework:

1. Assume the game is still alive until you have actually checked for saving resources.
2. Treat premature resignation as a practical blunder, not as a sign of sophistication.
3. Do not relax in winning or drawable positions, because complacency is often the final losing move.
4. Keep looking for tactical and defensive exceptions even when the position appears strategically hopeless.
5. Judge practical chess by results and resilience, not by how aesthetically good the earlier moves looked.

This framework is psychological without being vague. It turns resilience into a concrete chess skill: keep calculating, keep resisting, and keep converting until the board is finished with you.

# Basis

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-ben-finegold-blunders-in-chess]] centers the lecture on decisive blunders, practical resistance, and the danger of resigning or relaxing too early.
- The source repeatedly uses case studies to show that hidden tactical resources and return blunders exist even in positions that feel completely gone.
- It also treats rating and score as practical outputs of survival and non-collapse rather than of perfect style.

# Gaps

- The branch still needs more direct material on how stronger players should distinguish justified resignation from useful practical resistance.
- It also needs a richer bridge between psychological resilience and explicit defensive-calculation method.
