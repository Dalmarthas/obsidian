# Question

What shared model of change appears across the non-writing branches in this vault?

# Answer

The current source base supports a common six-part model:

1. Start from a framework.
   Each branch insists on a governing structure before action: strategy, leadership framework, blueprint, or interconnectedness model.

2. Speak like a human being.
   Real change depends on communication that sounds truthful, respectful, and legible to the audience rather than overperformed or manipulative.

3. Build trust before trying to move people.
   Durable change comes from partnership, safe disagreement, client empathy, advisory support, or community listening, not from treating people as obstacles.

4. Combine rigor with human feeling.
   Facts, strategy, and planning matter, but several sources argue that people only move when logic and lived stakes are connected.

5. Translate values into concrete action.
   The strongest branches do not stop at worldview. They turn it into pilot projects, blueprints, negotiation moves, campaign choices, or daily consumer decisions.

6. Keep change tied to durable identity.
   The newer business-strategy branch adds a missing layer: long-term change works better when the audience, institution, or brand can still recognize itself in the new direction.

The shared meta-lesson is that humane action is not the opposite of disciplined action. In these branches, the humane version is usually the more strategically competent one, especially when it stays legible over time.

# Comparisons

- Goodby Silverstein applies the model inside commerce: humane tone, strong strategy, and client empathy make bold advertising more likely to survive.
- Clinton applies it inside public leadership: frameworks, respectful argument, safe disagreement, and human storytelling make conflict more governable.
- Welteroth applies it inside career design: authentic voice, explicit architecture, and intentionally built support systems make reinvention more durable.
- Goodall applies it inside advocacy and conservation: interconnected thinking, dialogue, local partnership, and moral imagination make large problems more actionable.
- Iger applies it inside corporate transformation: narrow the priorities, communicate them constantly, negotiate candidly, and modernize without severing the brand relationship.

# Gaps

- These branches still rest on secondary summaries rather than primary transcripts or primary supporting material.
- The vault now has a stronger overlap map, but it still needs more counter-models from adversarial, highly quantitative, or explicitly top-down traditions.
- Some source-specific ideas remain branch-local even after this pass, especially detailed case studies and named exercises.
