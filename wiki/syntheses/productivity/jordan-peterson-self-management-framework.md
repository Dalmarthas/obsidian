# Question

What reusable self-management framework do the current Jordan Peterson productivity sources add to the vault?

# Answer

The Peterson productivity branch now adds a twelve-part self-management framework:

1. Stabilize physiology before demanding discipline.
   Treat food, sleep, wake regularity, and exercise as operating conditions for cognition rather than as optional lifestyle extras.

2. Protect the cognitive substrate first.
   Do not build a success plan on the assumption that raw intelligence can simply be upgraded at will. Preserve cognition through physical care and health-supporting routines.

3. Specify a future worth serving.
   Define in concrete terms what a good 3 to 5 year life would look like across relationships, work, health, leisure, and destructive-risk domains.

4. Align goals with temperament.
   Choose aims that fit actual motivational structure rather than abstract ideals borrowed from other people.

5. Break vision into causal micro-processes.
   Turn valued futures into small actions that can be repeated, scheduled, and visibly connected to the larger aim.

6. Negotiate with yourself instead of ruling by self-contempt.
   Build a schedule that balances obligation and reward well enough that the self you are managing will keep cooperating.

7. Improve the workflow, not only the effort.
   Before repeating meaningful work, redesign the process itself for leverage through elimination, delegation, rearrangement, and challenged assumptions.

8. Respect capacity ceilings on intense work.
   Peak output is not infinite. Protect the sharpest cognitive window, and admit that error-prone deep work may have a sustainable upper limit rather than an all-day maximum.

9. Size the challenge so growth stays sustainable.
   Seek enough pressure to reveal limitation and force correction, but pair it with deliberate recovery so the real target remains a meaningful multi-decade career rather than short-term heroic output.

10. Put immediate surroundings into directional order.
   Use room, desk, routines, and recurring interaction points as local embodiments of higher standards, especially when total life redesign is too abstract to act on.

11. Govern impulses through a ranked value structure.
   Discipline is not only resistance in the moment. It is the ongoing organization of competing short-term drives under a hierarchy that the person can live with, schedule around, and refine over time.

12. Measure progress locally and tell the truth early.
   Use yesterday's self rather than social comparison as the baseline, then audit where dishonesty, false agreement, or avoided conversations are preventing real forward movement.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/leadership/jordan-peterson-responsibility-framework|Jordan Peterson Responsibility Framework]], this model is more operational and less moral-existential. The responsibility framework is about burden, conscience, and avoiding nihilistic drift; this one is about how to convert valued aims into livable structure.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/productivity/kelly-mcgonigal-willpower-framework|Kelly McGonigal Willpower Framework]], Peterson is less physiological and lapse-recovery oriented. McGonigal is stronger on self-control under temptation; Peterson is stronger on designing a motivating architecture before the temptation battle arrives.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/productivity/tim-ferriss-self-management-framework|Tim Ferriss Self-Management Framework]], Peterson is less experimental and less systems-minimalist. Ferriss emphasizes fear reduction and externalized rules; Peterson emphasizes meaning-rich goals, trait shaping, and negotiated scheduling.
- The newer Peterson source also moves this branch closer to a `stress dosage` model: challenge should expose error and produce renewal, while planned recovery protects the longer horizon that makes productivity worth optimizing in the first place.
- The newest Peterson source adds a deeper motivational architecture beneath the calendar and routine layer: discipline works when a person can rank impulses under a value hierarchy and start the schedule simply enough that those impulses will keep cooperating.
- The daily-routine source adds a more concrete operating layer beneath all of that: stabilize wake time and fuel, respect the limited span of truly sharp intellectual work, and redesign repeated workflows for leverage before trying to outwork the bottleneck.
- The sorting-your-life source adds a local-environment layer: when abstraction is too big, order the room and the routine-level social moments first, because those mundane repetitions often dominate the lived feel of a life.
- The `12 Rules for Life` summary adds a day-to-day anti-comparison loop that the earlier productivity pages only implied: motivation stabilizes when the scoreboard is `better than yesterday` rather than `better than other people`, and course correction becomes more honest when avoided truths are surfaced quickly.
- The newer conscientiousness source adds a non-Peterson but compatible training hypothesis: some self-governance may be built not only by structure and value hierarchy, but also by repeated prosocial acts that rehearse setting aside immediate self-interest.

# Gaps

- The source is weaker on what to do when poor follow-through is driven by executive dysfunction, mood disorder, or structurally low control over schedule.
- The neuroscience and IQ claims are plausible as broad guidance, but they need stronger direct evidence if the vault wants to rely on them more heavily.
- The vault still needs stronger cross-source material on how conscientiousness is built without tipping into self-attack or chronic over-control.
- The newer conscientiousness source reduces that gap somewhat by adding altruistic rehearsal as one candidate training route, but the branch still lacks strong evidence on when that route beats simpler scheduling, environmental design, or clinical support.
- The `meaning as gauge` idea remains useful but underspecified; the branch still needs better practical signals for knowing when challenge is growth-producing versus overload-producing.
- The routine source adds two more open questions: how general the `three hours of intense work` ceiling really is, and how to distinguish real workflow redesign from endless optimization tinkering.
- The newest source adds another open question: which local environmental and interpersonal routines are most reliable as first repair points when life feels chaotic?
- The `12 Rules for Life` summary adds one more gap: the vault still lacks stronger sources on when self-scoring improves follow-through versus when it becomes compulsive self-monitoring.
