# Question

What reusable procrastination framework emerges when procrastination is treated as identification with an untrained mind rather than as a discipline problem?

# Answer

The current source base supports a six-part model:

1. Separate the observing self from mind activity.
   Thoughts, urges, bargaining, and boredom should be noticed as events in the mind rather than obeyed as the self's final command.

2. Diagnose fake struggle early.
   Procrastination often survives by staging inner conflict after non-action has already been chosen. The goal is to stop mistaking dramatized hesitation for genuine effort.

3. Distrust optimization-flavored avoidance.
   The mind can hide inside self-help, dopamine theory, efficiency logic, and endless planning just as easily as inside games or scrolling.

4. Act precisely when the mind says the action is too small or pointless.
   Tiny but real actions are useful here not because they are always sufficient, but because they reject the mind's `not enough` script.

5. Remove rewarded alternatives.
   `Do the task or do nothing` works by cutting off the hidden payoff structure that normally makes avoidance pleasant and work feel uniquely painful.

6. Build action on neutrality rather than on motivation.
   Waiting to want the task gives the mind too much leverage. A quieter stance that wants less can make appropriate action easier.

Together these moves treat procrastination less as a problem of missing inspiration and more as a problem of over-crediting mental narratives that are organized around escape.

# Gaps

- The main source is philosophically rich but secondary and only partly operationalized.
- The branch still needs stronger distinctions between healthy cognitive defusion and blanket distrust of thought.
- More evidence is needed on when strict boredom-based anti-avoidance rules help versus when they trigger rebound, shutdown, or unnecessary rigidity.
