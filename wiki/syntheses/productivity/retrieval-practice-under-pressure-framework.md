# Question

What durable framework does the current vault support for building memory that survives pressure better?

# Answer

The current branch supports a six-part framework:

1. Encode through recall, not exposure alone.
   Re-reading or re-playing can create familiarity, but recall attempts force the memory to become accessible without immediate support.

2. Start retrieval early instead of waiting for the polishing phase.
   Memory should be part of learning from day 1, even if the first retrieval attempts are partial, halting, and ugly.

3. Treat difficulty during recall as desirable evidence, not automatic failure.
   A hard recall attempt may mean the memory is being forced to organize itself into usable access routes rather than being propped up by cues.

4. Build chunks, then test whether the chunks are callable under load.
   Chunking explains how knowledge compresses into units; retrieval practice verifies whether those units can actually be brought back when needed.

5. Train for future pressure by making practice less cue-dependent.
   The strongest practical claim in the branch is that memories formed through repeated retrieval may be less vulnerable when acute stress later consumes working memory and composure.

6. Keep the transfer claim disciplined.
   The vault currently supports the principle strongly for learning and plausibly for performance, but the leap from laboratory recall tasks to complex artistic or professional execution still needs more evidence.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/productivity/barbara-oakley-learning-framework|Barbara Oakley Learning Framework]], this synthesis is narrower and more stress-oriented. Oakley explains why retrieval beats familiarity in general learning; this framework sharpens the claim toward pressure resistance.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/others/quiet-eye|Quiet Eye]], this branch is stronger on memory access while quiet eye is stronger on attentional stability at the moment of execution. Together they imply that performance under pressure can fail through either inaccessible memory or unstable attention.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/leadership/high-performance-mindset|High-Performance Mindset]], this framework is less about ambition, self-regulation, and identity under pressure, and more about how the material itself was encoded before the pressure arrived.

# Gaps

- The strongest direct evidence in the current branch comes from simple list-learning rather than complex real-world performance.
- The vault still needs better material on how retrieval practice should be dosed in music, public speaking, chess, and technical work.
- More evidence is needed on when retrieval without timely correction becomes counterproductive.
