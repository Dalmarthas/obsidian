# Question

What reusable strategy framework emerges from the current Blue Ocean source?

# Answer

The current source supports a seven-part framework:

1. Diagnose whether the category is structurally red.
   If the market is crowded, overcompared, and margin-compressed, better execution alone may not be enough.

2. Shift attention from rivals to non-customers.
   Study the people who avoid, underuse, or misunderstand the category to find the barriers incumbents normalize.

3. Look for demand unlocked by simplification, accessibility, and reframing.
   New categories often grow not by adding sophistication, but by removing intimidation, friction, and needless complexity.

4. Use value innovation as the design rule.
   Aim to increase buyer value while also lowering cost rather than assuming differentiation and efficiency must pull in opposite directions.

5. Apply ERRC to the offer.
   Eliminate, reduce, raise, and create until the product or service reflects a genuinely new category logic.

6. Define the category clearly and move early.
   Naming and occupying the new category matters because first meaningful presence can create a lead that later imitators struggle to erase.

7. Treat blue-ocean logic as a hypothesis, not a vanity label.
   The move is only real if the economics and buyer behavior actually change, not if the story simply sounds novel.

The distinctive contribution of this branch is strategic game redesign: do not begin with `how do we beat them?` Begin with `what category shape would make this comparison matter less?`

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/clayton-christensen-growth-framework|Clayton Christensen Growth Framework]], Blue Ocean is less diagnostic about innovation types and organizational incentives, and more explicit about category redesign through non-customer focus, value innovation, and ERRC.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/seth-godin-strategy-framework|Seth Godin Strategy Framework]], Blue Ocean is less philosophical about systems, time horizons, and empathy at the worldview level, and more concrete about escaping crowded competition through product-category redesign.
- Christensen is stronger on causal customer progress and why incumbents miss new growth.
- Godin is stronger on audience choice, game selection, and strategic posture.
- Blue Ocean is stronger on turning category escape into a specific redesign method.

# Gaps

- This framework currently rests on one secondary summary rather than a deeper cluster of Kim and Mauborgne sources.
- The branch is strong on strategic reframing, but still thinner on validation, go-to-market sequencing, and how to detect false-positive `blue ocean` enthusiasm.
- The current examples are consumer-facing and product-heavy; the vault still needs service, software, and B2B translations.
