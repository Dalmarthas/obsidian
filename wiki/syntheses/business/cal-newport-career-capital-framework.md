# Question

What reusable career framework emerges from the current Cal Newport source?

# Answer

The source supports a six-part framework:

1. Stop treating passion as the starting signal.
   Early excitement is weak evidence. Durable career satisfaction usually appears later, after competence and leverage grow.

2. Aim at the real rewards of good work.
   The source treats creativity, control, and impact as the core conditions most people actually want from a career.

3. Build career capital before demanding those rewards.
   Rare job conditions are usually purchased with rare skill, visible results, and trust.

4. Use a craftsman mindset as the daily operating posture.
   Focus on contribution, output quality, and becoming more useful rather than constantly checking whether the work feels magical.

5. Seek stretch work that creates differentiation.
   Under-owned, difficult, niche assignments accelerate distinctiveness faster than safe generic tasks.

6. Turn work into deliberate practice and then convert leverage into autonomy.
   Protect focused practice, get feedback, improve weaknesses, and use the resulting capital to negotiate for better scope, flexibility, and impact.

The distinctive Newport layer is economic realism: career design is not just about identity or alignment, but about what scarce value is being accumulated and what that value can later buy.

# Gaps

- This framework currently rests on one secondary summary rather than a wider Newport source cluster.
- The branch still needs stronger examples of how to apply the model when autonomy is low, management is weak, or the current field may be a temporary stepping stone rather than a long-term home.
- The current evidence is also stronger on building leverage than on knowing the right moment to spend it.
