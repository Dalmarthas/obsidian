# Question

What reusable business framework does the Jesse Cole source add to the vault?

# Answer

The Jesse Cole source base now adds a twenty-one-part model:

1. Build for the least privileged seat.
   The clearest test of a `fans-first` or `customers-first` claim is whether the cheapest, least prestigious, or easiest-to-ignore segment still gets real care and delight.

2. Treat the experience as the product.
   The offer is not just the core function. It is the full emotional arc around it, including entry, rituals, pacing, surprise, pricing feel, and exit memory.

3. Eliminate friction before trying to amaze people.
   Delight works only after the main annoyances, confusions, fees, and dead zones have been removed. You cannot out-entertain a broken journey.

4. Engineer the journey end to end.
   Memorable brands map what people see, hear, anticipate, and feel across the whole encounter instead of leaving the journey to operational drift.

5. Make remarkability operational.
   Word of mouth becomes reliable when the product contains visible moments, social scenes, and story-worthy generosity rather than merely a good slogan.

6. Entertain always, not occasionally.
   Every touchpoint can belong to the show: email, arrival, scanning, breaks, transitions, and exit. Entertainment is treated as a mindset for the whole organization, not as an isolated function.

7. Run high-volume creative experiments with disciplined debriefs.
   Constant novelty does not have to mean chaos if new ideas are tested in small batches and reviewed through a structure such as `Learn-Change-Plus`.

8. Compete on generosity where the category expects extraction.
   Hidden fees, price gouging, clutter, and nickel-and-diming can be reversed into trust-building strategic choices that strengthen loyalty and identity.

9. Engage deeply enough that customers stop feeling like an audience only.
   The strongest experience businesses create rituals, personal touches, and participation loops that make customers feel seen, included, and emotionally inside the brand.

10. Empower action inside and outside the company.
   Frontline staff need enough autonomy to create delight on the spot, and fans need enough stories, symbols, and shareable artifacts to spread the experience themselves.

11. Reframe overlooked talent around mission.
   People rejected by conventional systems can become unusually powerful contributors when the organization gives them a larger identity and purpose.

12. Use stories as management infrastructure.
   Cultural stories about delight, grief, belonging, and care should shape onboarding and judgment, not sit in the company as inspirational decoration.

13. Protect long-horizon quality from short-horizon capital logic.
   If investors, media partners, or monetization systems would force degradation of the customer experience, declining those incentives can be a strategic act rather than a romantic one.

14. Measure belonging, return, and referral depth.
   The strongest scoreboard is not only ticket revenue or audience size, but whether people come back, bring others, travel for the experience, and feel that the brand matters to them.

15. Replace the marketing budget with an attention plan.
   Instead of buying ordinary reach, engineer moments, formats, and choices that create headlines people retell without prompting.

16. Redesign the core product, not only the wrapper.
   Banana Ball shows that experience businesses may need to rework the underlying product rules themselves when dead time, weak pacing, or low stakes undermine delight.

17. Turn creativity into a scheduled operating system.
   Daily idea reps, weekly pitch meetings, rehearsals, and `LCP` reviews convert creative ambition into institutional rhythm rather than leaving it to inspiration.

18. Use founder mission to deepen belonging, not just branding.
   The personal layer matters here: loneliness, optimism, care, and family mission are translated into a business that tries to make people feel they matter, and then extends that logic into foster-care advocacy.

19. Let belief run ahead of visible proof.
   Founders sometimes need to hold and repeat a vision large enough to recruit people and momentum before objective evidence has caught up.

20. Expect a messy phase and design through it.
   Category-breaking experiences often launch with operational chaos, and the correct response is usually disciplined iteration rather than retreat into safe mediocrity.

21. Make employee experience mirror customer magic.
   If the backstage culture lacks care, recognition, flexibility, and gratitude, the public-facing wonder eventually becomes unsustainable theater.

The distinctive Jesse Cole layer is that brand, service design, pricing ethics, experimentation, culture, and live-show choreography are treated as one system. The newer sources make both the order of operations and the founder operating logic clearer: remove pain, design delight, keep testing, deepen participation, create attention instead of buying it, protect the emotional mission through explicit culture and product design, and survive the messy first versions long enough to make the experience real.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/seth-godin-remarkable-marketing-framework|Seth Godin Remarkable Marketing Framework]], Jesse Cole is less abstract about tribe, permission, and smallest viable audience and more operational about how to design the actual scene people will talk about.
- Seth Godin is stronger on strategic audience choice and cultural spread logic; Jesse Cole is stronger on how live rituals, generosity, and seat-level design turn that spread logic into reality.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/rory-sutherland-marketing-framework|Rory Sutherland Marketing Framework]], Jesse Cole is less focused on psychological framing in the abstract and more focused on running a repeatable experience machine with rituals, pricing rules, and debriefs.
- Rory is stronger on why perception and uncertainty matter; Jesse Cole is stronger on how to operationalize that insight across a full event journey.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/goodby-silverstein-advertising-framework|Goodby Silverstein Advertising Framework]], Jesse Cole is less campaign-oriented and more like a live-business operating system where the product itself becomes the ad.
- Compared with the earlier single Jesse Cole source, the new `5 E's` source is less rich in case-study feeling and anti-extraction specifics, but more useful as a transferable checklist for building and auditing experience systems.
- Compared with the newest interview source, the earlier Jesse Cole pages are stronger on experience shape than on founder system. The interview is stronger on attention as strategy, core-product redesign, creative cadence, and the standards-versus-belonging leadership tension inside the organization.
- Compared with the `Modern Day Disney` source, the earlier Jesse Cole pages are stronger on structural mechanisms, while the new source is stronger on belief, messy-first-version psychology, and employee-experience symmetry as conditions for sustaining the show.

# Gaps

- The framework now rests on three secondary summaries rather than on primary internal material, so it is stronger than before but still not a full operating manual.
- The source is strong on experience design and loyalty but lighter on the hard economics of staffing, margins, and quality control at scale.
- The branch still needs stronger evidence on which parts of this model transfer cleanly to software, services, or non-entertainment businesses.
- The source strongly celebrates founder-led experience quality, but the vault still lacks a clearer governance model for preserving this standard after founder centrality weakens.
- The newer `5 E's` source adds another unresolved tension: how much experimentation and theatricality can a business absorb before the cost of variance starts eroding the frictionless consistency that made the experience strong in the first place?
- The newest interview source adds another unresolved tension: when does founder mission create coherent culture, and when does it turn into overdependence on one person's psychology, pace, or need for meaning?
- The `Modern Day Disney` source adds another tension: belief before proof can create momentum, but it can also rationalize overreach or magical thinking if iteration quality and operational truth-telling are weak.
