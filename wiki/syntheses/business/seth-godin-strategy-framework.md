# Question

What reusable strategy framework does Seth Godin's `This Is Strategy` source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source base now contributes an eleven-part strategy model:

1. Define the change and the audience.
   Strategy begins with a clear answer to who the work is for and what change it seeks to produce.

2. Hold strategy steadier than tactics.
   Tactics should flex with conditions; the underlying philosophy should not be rewritten every week.

3. Read the system before trying to win inside it.
   Markets contain norms, incentives, defaults, gatekeepers, and status structures that shape what is possible.

4. Choose a real time horizon.
   Strategic advantage often comes from a longer `circle of now`, but only if the business can actually survive that horizon.

5. Treat markets as games with uncertainty, not as deterministic formulas.
   Good strategy stacks probabilities; it does not guarantee immediate outcomes.

6. Practice empathy precise enough to exclude.
   The strongest strategies know who they are for, who they are not for, and what emotional or identity shift matters to that audience.

7. Seek compounding loops.
   Feedback loops, network effects, status signaling, affiliation, and repeat participation create leverage that one-time awareness campaigns cannot.

8. Design scaffolding and productive tension.
   People need handholds at friction points and enough tension to move, but not so much chaos that they freeze or churn.

9. Separate decision reviews from outcome reviews.
   Strategy gets stronger when teams judge reasoning quality, proxy quality, and odds quality instead of worshipping the latest visible result.

10. Distinguish real dips from lottery traps.
   Persistence is strategic only when the hard phase belongs to a worthwhile game with visible paths, scaffolding, and improving odds.

11. Align with gravity.
   Better strategy follows the direction of incentives, network effects, and technological shifts instead of defending a familiar vehicle whose underlying pull is fading.

In the vault, this matters because it upgrades Godin from a niche-marketing thinker into a broader strategy thinker. The earlier Seth branch was strongest on remarkability, permission, and practice. This source connects those ideas to systems thinking, time horizon, competitive conditions, and probabilistic judgment.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/seth-godin-remarkable-marketing-framework|Seth Godin Remarkable Marketing Framework]], this page is more strategic and less creator-marketing-centered. The earlier synthesis is stronger on remarkability, permission, and practice; this one is stronger on systems, time, games, and decision quality.
- The newer perseverance source narrows the gap slightly by adding quitting discipline, dip diagnosis, and gravity-reading, which makes this synthesis more behaviorally usable than the earlier version of the page.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/simon-sinek-success-metrics-framework|Simon Sinek Success Metrics Framework]], Godin is less focused on institutional trust and culture metrics, and more focused on why probabilistic games and false proxies make leaders misread results.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/scalable-business-systems-framework|Scalable Business Systems Framework]], Godin is less operational about internal process design and more focused on the larger social and market systems that determine what internal systems are worth building.

# Gaps

- This framework is currently grounded in a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript or book chapter.
- The model is strongest on strategic framing, audience design, and cultural mechanics, but still lighter on finance, retention analysis, and sequencing under severe resource constraints.
- The branch is now stronger on quitting logic and path selection, but it still needs harder criteria for runway, capital limits, and when evidence is strong enough to abandon a long-horizon thesis.
- The branch would benefit from stronger counterexamples showing when standardization, broad-market clarity, or short-horizon execution are the correct strategy rather than failures of imagination.
