# Question

What reusable business framework does the new Steve Jobs Think Different source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part turnaround-and-brand framework:

1. Cut until the offer becomes legible.
   Focus is not only internal concentration. It is reducing portfolio sprawl until customers can understand what the company is trying to do.

2. Reset around product, marketing, and distribution basics.
   Turnarounds do not begin with endless abstraction. They begin by restoring competence in the few executive functions that determine whether the company can make, explain, and deliver what it sells.

3. Treat demand feedback as a strategic asset.
   Long inventory pipelines force the business to guess too far ahead. Faster distribution and lower inventory make real customer behavior more useful than internal forecasting.

4. Market values, not just features.
   In crowded categories, brand memory is built more by belief, symbolism, and identity than by technical recital alone.

5. Re-express enduring core beliefs.
   Products and markets can change sharply, but the company's deepest values should remain stable enough to organize the next chapter.

6. Use brand to honor a tribe larger than the product.
   `Think Different` works because Apple is framed as an ally of people who want to change the world, not merely as a seller of devices.

7. Link company philosophy and personal philosophy.
   The strategic layer and the Stanford layer point in the same direction: trust meaningful detours, keep searching for work that feels alive, and use mortality to strip away imitation.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/steve-jobs-focus-framework|Steve Jobs Focus Framework]], this source is less about the pain of cancellation and more about what focus is for: clearer products, clearer customer understanding, and a brand that can stand for something coherent.
- Compared with the earlier single-source version of this synthesis, the new `Get Much Simpler` page makes the turnaround more executive-operational: product, marketing, and distribution now read as one recovery checklist, and the branch gains a clearer claim that strategic clarity can restore energy after cuts.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/steve-jobs-stanford-framework|Steve Jobs Stanford Framework]], this source is less existential in isolation because it ties the life-philosophy layer directly to company identity, brand values, and strategic renewal.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/steve-jobs-next-framework|Steve Jobs NeXT Framework]], this source is less about platform leverage and ownership architecture, and more about feedback speed, portfolio simplification, and meaning-rich public positioning.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/simon-sinek-golden-circle-framework|Simon Sinek Golden Circle Framework]], Jobs is less abstract about `why` and more concrete about how belief, product clarity, and brand revival interact during an actual turnaround.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/iger-strategy-framework|Iger Strategy Framework]], Jobs is less portfolio-managerial and more founder-symbolic: he uses public narrative, product pruning, and operational compression as one integrated recovery move.

# Gaps

- The source still lacks a detailed primary account of the exact decision process behind Apple's cuts and distribution redesign.
- The morale claim that cancelled teams became more energized is useful but still thinly evidenced in the current source base.
- The branch needs more counterexamples showing when feature detail and technical proof should dominate values-heavy communication.
- The Stanford material here overlaps with an existing source, so future work should keep checking whether repeated Jobs clips add genuinely new structure or mostly restate the same life philosophy.
