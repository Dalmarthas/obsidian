# Question

What durable problem-solving framework can be extracted from this Elon Musk source without flattening it into generic optimization advice?

# Answer

The current source base supports a ten-part framework:

1. Challenge the question before solving the answer.
   Requirements should be treated as partially wrong until tested, so the team is less likely to solve the wrong problem elegantly.

2. Delete before improving.
   The first serious move is subtraction: remove parts, steps, and assumptions before polishing what remains.

3. Use reintroduction as evidence of real simplification.
   If nothing ever needs to be added back, the deletion pass was probably too timid to expose hidden waste.

4. Optimize and accelerate only what survives scrutiny.
   Speed and refinement matter, but only after the surviving structure is simple enough to deserve them.

5. Automate last.
   Automation should not become a multiplier on unnecessary complexity.

6. Lead close to the real work.
   Firsthand exposure to frontline tasks helps leaders detect dumb requirements, hidden fragilities, and material bottlenecks that abstraction tends to hide.

7. Intervene only on the current main constraint.
   Top-level attention is most valuable when it concentrates on the single biggest bottleneck, while nearly everything else remains delegated.

8. Reason from physics and material reality.
   The strongest assumptions are hard constraints such as physical law, material properties, and irreducible requirements, not inherited industry practice.

9. Think in the limit.
   Extreme-scale reasoning helps reveal whether cost and complexity are fundamental or merely artifacts of current design and process.

10. Define the ideal product before accepting the current toolset.
   Teams should imagine the best theoretically possible artifact first and then work backward to the manufacturing and process changes required to approach it.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/productivity/elon-musk-serial-focus-framework|Elon Musk Serial Focus Framework]], this branch is less about tempo and attentional sequencing and more about the order in which systems should be questioned, stripped down, and improved.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/bottleneck-theory|Bottleneck Theory]], this framework says more about intervention order: diagnose the limiter, but first make sure the constrained process deserves to exist in its current form.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/productivity/workflow-redesign|Workflow Redesign]], this framework is harsher and more engineering-centric: it puts requirement challenge and deletion ahead of the more general search for leverage.
- Compared with the Steve Jobs operational branch, Musk is less focused on integrated product architecture over long technology windows and more focused on forcing teams through a subtraction-first algorithm before optimization and automation.
- Compared with the newer Andreessen source, the direct Musk source is stronger on intervention order, while Andreessen is stronger on the weekly executive cadence of finding one bottleneck, micromanaging it briefly, and delegating everything else.
- Compared with the newest Lex clip, the existing Musk source is stronger on intervention order, while the new clip is stronger on asymptotic cost reasoning, ideal-product definition, and manufacturing-first innovation logic.

# Gaps

- The source is still anecdotal and personality-heavy, so the framework remains more heuristic than validated doctrine.
- The current vault still needs stronger counterexamples showing when redundancy, safety margins, or formal process should override deletion-first instincts.
- The branch needs broader evidence on how this algorithm performs outside high-control engineering environments.
- The Andreessen source adds a neighboring uncertainty: some organizations may not reduce to one dominant bottleneck cleanly enough for the weekly-rule framing to hold.
