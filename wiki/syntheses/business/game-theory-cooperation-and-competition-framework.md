# Question

What reusable decision framework emerges from the current game-theory source for competitive and cooperative situations?

# Answer

The current source base yields a thirteen-part framework:

1. Diagnose whether the problem is independent or interdependent.
   If your payoff depends heavily on what others choose, solo optimization logic is incomplete.

2. Separate collective desirability from strategic stability.
   The best total outcome may not be the one actors can hold without coordination, trust, or enforcement.

3. In competitive settings, ask what move remains defensible under plausible responses from others.
   Nash-style thinking is useful not because it finds the morally best outcome, but because it identifies where unilateral improvement is no longer available.

4. In cooperative settings, switch from win logic to allocation logic.
   Once people can form a coalition, the main problem becomes how to divide gains, costs, or credit without pretending that all contributions are identical.

5. Make fairness assumptions explicit before conflict hardens.
   Equal split, contribution-based split, and hybrid arrangements each imply different values, and hidden assumptions become resentment generators later.

6. Model the situation before moralizing or improvising.
   Start by naming the players, their strategies, and their payoffs. Many strategic mistakes begin with fuzzy actors, hidden options, or unexamined incentive structures.

7. Check whether the field is actually zero-sum.
   Some conflicts are fixed-pie contests, but many are not. If value can be created, coalitions can form, or trust can enlarge the outcome set, zero-sum thinking can make strategy unnecessarily crude.

8. Ask whether this is a one-shot move or a repeated relationship.
   Strategies that look rational in one isolated round can be self-defeating once future encounters, retaliation, and reputation matter.

9. In repeated settings, combine goodwill with enforceable reciprocity.
   Start cooperative, answer defection proportionally, forgive quickly after repair, and keep the rule legible enough that others can re-coordinate around it.

10. Ask whether the real variable is timing.
   In some strategic conflicts, everyone already knows the action class they want. The problem is deciding when to move, launch, attack, or reveal.

11. Anchor every negotiation against outside options before debating tactics.
   A deal only beats no deal if it beats the best credible alternative. BATNA logic prevents pressure or performance from disguising a strategically weak agreement.

12. In incomplete-information settings, use offers to learn, not only to claim value.
   Signaling and screening matter because many strategic problems hinge on hidden valuation, hidden constraints, or bluffing about willingness to walk away.

13. Design repeated cooperation for noise, not only for ideal conditions.
   Reciprocity works best when it can survive misreads, accidents, and one-off failures. Slight generosity can be strategically stronger than perfect score-keeping.

14. Distinguish robust conclusions from assumption-heavy ones.
   Dominance reasoning can remain useful even against unsophisticated opponents, while backward induction depends more heavily on shared rationality and clear finite endpoints.

# Basis

- The source's main competitive lesson is the Prisoner's Dilemma: individually rational defense can create a collectively inferior equilibrium.
- Its main cooperative lesson is the Shapley value: fairness can be defined through average marginal contribution rather than through sentiment or raw bargaining power.
- The newer introductory source adds the formal modeling layer: players, strategies, payoffs, dominant strategy versus Nash equilibrium, and the limit of purely zero-sum framing.
- The new repeated-games source adds the relational strategy layer: tit for tat, Axelrod-style tournament evidence, and the claim that cumulative payoff often favors reciprocal cooperation over single-round extraction.
- The Yale lecture adds the sequential-timing layer: dominance reasoning, backward induction, critical thresholds for acting, and the distinction between robust and fragile predictions.
- The negotiation source adds the bargaining-design layer: BATNA as outside-option floor, credible commitment as strategic self-binding, signaling and screening under incomplete information, and the claim that repeated dealing makes reputation economically rational rather than merely moral.
- The new repeated-games source deepens the ecological layer: nice, forgiving, retaliatory, and clear strategies can outperform clever exploiters over time; cooperation can spread from small clusters; and generous tit for tat can beat strict reciprocity in noisy environments.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/cross-source/decision-making-under-cognitive-strain|Decision-Making Under Cognitive Strain]], this synthesis explains a different class of failure. That earlier synthesis is about bad judgment under cognitive limits; this one is about structurally interdependent situations where even clear-headed actors can land in poor outcomes.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/communication/negotiation-and-mediation|Negotiation and Mediation]], this framework is less tactical and more architectural. It helps define the underlying game before anyone starts using persuasion techniques inside it.
- The new negotiation source narrows that gap slightly by showing where architecture and tactics meet: outside options, package design, signaling, and credible commitment are negotiation moves that only make sense once the game structure is explicit.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/business-strategy|Business Strategy]], this framework is narrower but sharper: strategy chooses the arena and tradeoffs, while game theory helps reveal whether the arena rewards defection, coordination, coalition-building, or redesign.
- Compared with the earlier game-theory source, the new introductory source is stronger on formal vocabulary and weaker on cooperative fairness details.
- Compared with the repeated-games source, the earlier game-theory sources are more static. The new source is stronger on reputation, retaliation, forgiveness, and why long-run payoff can reverse one-shot logic.
- Compared with the newest repeated-games source, the earlier tit-for-tat material is simpler and cleaner. The new source is stronger on strategy ecology, non-zero-sum `banker` framing, and why noise can make strict reciprocity underperform more generous variants.
- Compared with the Yale lecture, the earlier sources are stronger on static structure and repeated cooperation, while the Yale source is stronger on finite sequential timing and robustness-to-irrationality distinctions.

# Gaps

- The branch still needs repeated games, trust, reputation, commitment devices, and mechanism-design sources to become more operational.
- The current source gives only a light treatment of real-world fairness disputes, where precise marginal contribution is often hard to observe.
- The newer source adds another unresolved gap: classical game theory is analytically clear, but the branch still needs stronger behavioral material on when rational-agent predictions systematically fail.
- The repeated-games source adds a practical gap as well: proportional retaliation is easy to say and hard to calibrate when noise, power imbalance, or ambiguous defection are present.
- The newest repeated-games source reduces that gap by naming generous reciprocity, but not enough to make calibration easy; the branch still lacks stronger material on exactly when to forgive, verify, or escalate.
- The Yale lecture adds another open gap: the branch still needs stronger treatment of imperfect information, signaling, and Bayesian updating for cases where the endpoint exists but the relevant probabilities are uncertain.
- The negotiation source reduces that gap conceptually, but not fully: it names signaling and screening clearly without giving enough real cases for calibration under deception, asymmetric power, or multi-party bargaining.
- The vault still lacks a stronger bridge between game-theory logic and institution design for escaping bad equilibria.
