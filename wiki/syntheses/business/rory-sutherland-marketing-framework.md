# Question

What reusable marketing framework does the Rory Sutherland source add to the vault?

# Answer

The current source base supports a twenty-seven-part model:

1. Look for psychological leverage, not only logical proof.
   People often choose through framing, emotion, symbolism, and expectation before they justify the choice rationally.

2. Make room for fat-tailed ideas.
   A few strange, high-upside ideas can create more value than many rounds of safe optimization.

3. Use reverse benchmarking.
   Find the dimension the category neglects, then build distinctiveness there instead of copying standard excellence.

4. Treat perception as part of the product.
   Meaning, aesthetics, wording, waiting, and context can change value without equivalent technical change.

5. Design for control and uncertainty, not only throughput.
   Satisfaction often changes when people can see what is happening, anticipate what comes next, or feel some agency over the experience.

6. Evaluate solutions through three lenses.
   Technical feasibility, economic efficiency, and psychological experience should be considered together because the cheapest mechanical answer is not always the best human answer.

7. Generate ideas through anomalies, analogies, and trade-off reframing.
   Creative breakthroughs often come from odd observations, cross-domain borrowing, or dissolving a false either/or.

8. Redesign broken filters and proxy metrics.
   When markets sort through shallow first-glance cues, high-fit options disappear early, so better discovery paths and more varied signals can outperform more optimization of the existing filter.

9. Build around experience goods.
   Some offers reveal their value only through use, so trials, onboarding, demonstrations, and word of mouth matter more than instant specification-heavy comparison.

10. Use signaling and counter-signaling intelligently.
   Cost, scarcity, effort, packaging, and even selective simplicity can communicate seriousness, status, or confidence when they are hard to fake and fit the brand's meaning.

11. Separate helpful friction from harmful friction.
   Some effort increases trust, ownership, ritual value, or compliance, while other friction creates anxiety and destroys confidence; the goal is not zero friction but correct friction.

12. Protect exploration from institutional over-rationalization.
   Procurement, compensation models, and short-term incentives can kill the very experiments that produce uncommon value.

13. Diagnose the current bottleneck before optimizing the system.
   Growth is often constrained by one dominant limiter at a time, and the limiter may be awareness, distribution, logistics, or some other non-obvious narrow point.

14. Optimize for experienced time, not only elapsed time.
   Waiting, travel, and effort are lived psychologically, so reducing boredom, uncertainty, and imposedness can outperform modest speed gains.

15. Look for psychological arbitrage.
   Hidden upside often sits in reference-point shifts, context changes, and weakly measured motives such as certainty, status, autonomy, relatedness, and fairness.

16. Protect exploration from over-exploitation.
   Rational optimization should dominate the core, but some protected share of effort should be reserved for imaginative tests that standard models cannot justify in advance.

17. Prefer response feedback over defensibility theater.
   Big organizations often choose what can be justified in advance rather than what works in reality, so markets, experiments, and behavior change should outrank blame-avoidance logic when evidence is available.

18. Remember that B2B and hiring are psychological too.
   Technical buyers, recruiters, and institutions also respond to framing, labels, proxy metrics, reassurance, and personality, which means Rory's logic applies beyond consumer advertising.

19. Respect slow-feedback domains.
   Service, loyalty, retention, and brand often compound through delayed signals, so fast attribution systems systematically undervalue them.

20. For some offers, trial beats argument.
   When adoption flips after lived experience, the strategic job is to get people over the first-use barrier rather than to win a spreadsheet debate.

21. Audit hidden functions before simplifying.
   Many roles, perks, interfaces, and rituals carry tacit functions such as trust, serendipity, status, or bonding that disappear when leaders optimize only the explicit task.

22. Prefer named social heuristics when behavior is collective.
   In many real settings, visible binary rules, memorable labels, and shared scripts outperform precise numeric guidance because people copy, coordinate, and self-enforce socially.

23. Optimize the transaction, not only the product.
   Willingness to pay depends partly on fairness, context, seller identity, and what the act of buying seems to mean, not only on functional utility.

24. Protect multiplicative touchpoints.
   Some frontline interactions and recovery moments multiply the whole brand up or down, so service design and exception handling deserve investment beyond what local efficiency metrics justify.

25. Manage marketing as a fat-tailed search problem.
   Rare breakthroughs create disproportionate value, so strategy should protect anomaly-hunting, reverse benchmarking, and exploratory tests instead of judging everything by average-case optimization logic.

26. Separate explore and exploit governance.
   Exploratory work and exploitative work need different metrics, tolerances, and people; forcing both through one certainty-first scoreboard is a structural error.

27. Use paradigm shifts as soft power.
   Some of the biggest gains come from changing the map, label, category, or frame through which people interpret choices, because once the paradigm changes behavior can change with little mechanical redesign.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/business/goodby-silverstein-advertising-framework|Goodby Silverstein Advertising Framework]], Rory Sutherland is less focused on humane creative culture and more focused on the irrational economics of attention, value, and differentiation.
- Goodby Silverstein is stronger on making work people want to see; Sutherland is stronger on why strange framing and neglected dimensions can create disproportionate commercial value.
- The newer Rory source broadens the branch away from pure perception: it argues that unusual insight is also needed to find the real bottleneck, especially when teams keep polishing non-limiting parts of the business.
- The newer Rory `Why Logic` source broadens the branch from customer psychology into institutional psychology: it shows that proxy-heavy hiring, B2B seriousness norms, and policy defensibility can all suppress better options before the market ever gets to react.
- The newest Rory source broadens the branch again from `marketing psychology` into `experience design`: it shows that control, visibility, and uncertainty reduction can produce economic gains even when objective performance barely changes.
- The latest Rory source broadens the branch from experience design into market design: it shows how filters, signaling systems, and discovery paths can misprice real quality before anyone experiences it.
- The current Rory source broadens the branch from `psychological leverage` into `psychological arbitrage`: it clarifies that some of the biggest gains come from reframing comparison sets, improving experienced time, and exploiting weakly measured motives that standard models exclude.
- The current `Dirty Little Marketing Secrets` source broadens the branch from overt signaling into tacit system value: it shows that slow-feedback effects, no-going-back trials, and `hidden functions` inside roles or perks can create durable advantage precisely because spreadsheets struggle to count them.
- The newest Rory source broadens the branch from market design into brand-system design: it ties signaling, scarcity, service recovery, meaningful friction, and storytelling into one account of how brands create trust and pricing power.
- The current `Lost Genius of Irrationality` source broadens the branch from individual framing into collective behavior design: it argues that simple named heuristics, binary commitments, and socially contagious scripts can outperform more rational-looking guidance when norms and imitation drive the outcome.
- The current `Playbook Behind Every Great Campaign` source broadens the branch from campaign psychology into touchpoint economics: it adds `transaction utility`, explicit `Doorman Fallacy` logic, and a stronger claim that service moments can multiply the whole brand.
- The current `Psychology Hacks` source broadens the branch from general creativity advocacy into discovery economics: it sharpens fat-tailed return logic, adds stronger reverse-benchmarking cases, and makes explore-versus-exploit a more explicit operating distinction.
- The current `Soft Power in a Hard World` source broadens the branch from discovery economics into frame-level intervention design: it shows that `do both` reframing, paradigm shifts, and separate explore/exploit governance often matter more than optimizing within a bad frame.
- Together the two branches make the advertising layer broader: one emphasizes human truth and creative craft, the other emphasizes psychological leverage and non-linear value creation.

# Gaps

- This framework now rests on eight secondary summaries rather than a larger Rory branch.
- The vault still needs stronger contrast from more measurement-heavy marketing traditions.
- The bottleneck layer is conceptually strong but still needs more operational examples showing how firms distinguished awareness constraints from distribution, product, or operations constraints in practice.
- The AI claim in the earlier source is narrower and more speculative than the rest of the framework and should not be treated as the core durable lesson.
- The newest source is rich in examples but lighter on directly comparable evidence, so the exact effect sizes behind some interventions remain tentative here.
- The latest source is broad and idea-dense, but some of its political and social examples are more provocative than rigorously established.
- The newest source contains many high-signal anecdotes, but several famous examples are still better treated as directional illustrations than as settled causal proof.
- The current source adds a valuable experienced-time layer, but the vault still lacks stronger primary material on when certainty or boredom reduction beats genuine speed or reliability improvements in practice.
- The newer `Why Logic` source adds a useful institutional critique, but many of its claims about neurodiversity, conspiracy openness, and civic persuasion are more exploratory than firmly evidenced here.
- The current `Dirty Little Marketing Secrets` source adds strong heuristics around hidden functions and slow-feedback domains, but many examples remain anecdotal and still need better operational criteria for deciding when a tacit function is real enough to preserve.
- The current `Lost Genius of Irrationality` source adds a strong norm-design layer, but the vault still needs better criteria for deciding when binary social rules are genuinely superior to richer, more situational guidance.
- The current `Playbook Behind Every Great Campaign` source adds a strong service-economics layer, but the vault still needs better operating rules for measuring `brand quakes` and deciding when expensive human judgment truly outperforms automation.
- The current `Psychology Hacks` source strengthens the fat-tailed model, but the vault still needs clearer budgeting and governance rules for how much exploration different firms can justify without drifting into low-discipline experimentation.
- The current `Soft Power in a Hard World` source adds a strong paradigm-shift layer, but the vault still needs clearer tests for when a `do both` answer is genuinely available versus when trade-offs are real and costly.
