# Question

What practical reader-side scorecard emerges from the current image-derived studio script reader source?

# Answer

The source compiles into a six-part reader scorecard:

1. Premise and hook test.
   The script needs a real premise, not just a setup, circumstance, or mood board, and the opening has to hook quickly.

2. Character engine test.
   The protagonist must be able to carry the story, and the antagonist must be original and genuinely pressure-producing rather than stock obstruction.

3. Readability test.
   Dialogue should feel natural or sharp, action should read cleanly, and the page should move with confidence rather than drag through talk or clutter.

4. World and research test.
   The world should feel vivid, fitting, and enriched by relevant knowledge without turning into fact dumping or scenic tourism.

5. Propulsion and escalation test.
   The story should not drift, repeat itself, or reset to zero; conflict needs to compound and the third act should converge rather than unravel.

6. Ending and coherence test.
   The script should feel like one story choosing one lane, not several partial scripts stitched together, and the ending should feel cumulative rather than abrupt or evasive.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/filmmaking/screenwriting-mistakes-diagnostic-framework|Screenwriting Mistakes Diagnostic Framework]], this source is less abstract and more explicitly reader-facing: it mirrors strengths and weaknesses the way a spec-reader might feel them on first read.
- Compared with Truby-style premise work, this source is more practical and downstream: it asks whether the premise survives contact with pages, not only whether it looks strong in architecture form.
- Compared with Max Landis on readability, this source is broader and less voice-driven: it cares about enjoyable reading, but it also checks premise, villain, world, action, and third-act payoff in the same pass.

# Gaps

- The source is OCR-derived from screenshots rather than a fully contextualized article or lecture.
- It gives category-level reader heuristics, not a staged revision process.
- The vault still needs stronger sources from actual development notes, coverage reports, or reader memos to compare with this scorecard.
