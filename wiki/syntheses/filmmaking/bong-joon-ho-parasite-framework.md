# Question

What filmmaking and story-construction model does the Bong Joon-Ho _Parasite_ source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a five-part model:

1. Build people through interaction, not dossiers.
   Character is revealed by how people behave with one another, especially under unequal power, awkward proximity, or hidden motives.

2. Reject clean moral binaries.
   Drama gets richer when everyone is understandable, compromised, and partially trapped inside larger structures.

3. Use family as a pressure vessel.
   A family unit concentrates loyalty, shame, need, strategy, and emotional punishment in ways that accelerate story quickly.

4. Make architecture part of the plot.
   If a location matters, teach the audience its geography early so later suspense, concealment, and reversal can run through space rather than explanation.

5. Trust specificity to carry universality.
   Deep local detail does not block broad resonance; it often creates the texture that makes the story travel.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Scorsese, Bong is less focused here on cinema history and post-production discovery, and more focused on how social conflict, family units, and architecture can be fused from the start.
- Compared with Sorkin, the model is less verbal and more interactional: character is exposed through relational behavior and staging rather than primarily through scene rhetoric.
- Compared with Rushdie's place logic, Bong is more mechanical about geography: the house is not only alive, it is an engineered suspense device.
- Compared with the current character pages, Bong adds a stronger moral-ambiguity layer where comprehensibility matters more than innocence.
