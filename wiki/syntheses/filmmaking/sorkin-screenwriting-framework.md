# Question

What reusable screenwriting framework emerges from the Aaron Sorkin masterclass summary?

# Answer

The current source base now supports an eight-part operating model:

1. Start with intention and obstacle.
   Every story and every scene need a strong want and a credible resistance.

2. Build character from tactics, not biography.
   What matters most is how people pursue what they want under pressure, not what labels the writer assigns them.

3. Reframe topical material through timeless human conflict.
   A story that looks like it is `about tech` or `about the moment` lands harder when it is really about betrayal, ambition, status, jealousy, or friendship.

4. Use conflicting perspectives when disputed truth is part of the drama.
   Contradictory accounts can become structural fuel rather than a research problem, as long as the audience can still track what each participant wants.

5. Research hard, then dramatize without pretending certainty you do not have.
   Legal records, interviews, blogs, and public documents can provide a factual field robust enough to support ambiguity rather than flatten it.

6. Trust the audience with interpretation.
   The writer should guide clearly, but avoid forcing one official moral verdict when the stronger experience comes from letting viewers judge.

7. Write dialogue for performance, rhythm, and exact pressure.
   Cadence, interruption, syllabic precision, and rhetorical timing matter enough that some scripts benefit from rehearsal-heavy, low-improv execution.

8. Rewrite diagnostically.
   Finish the draft, identify the real failures, cut what the story can live without, and respond to notes by solving the underlying structural problem.

The distinctive Sorkin layer on top of this is not only speed or cleverness. It is the combination of dramatic pressure, interpretive trust, rhythmic language, and case-study rigor.

# Gaps

- This framework currently rests on a secondary summary rather than primary course materials.
- It reflects a strong authorial style and should later be compared against screenwriters with different methods.
- The `no-improv` lesson is partly case-sensitive and should not be universalized across all dialogue-heavy work.
- The vault still needs primary script examples to test how these principles behave on the page.
