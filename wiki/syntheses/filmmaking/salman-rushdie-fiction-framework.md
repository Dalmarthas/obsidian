# Question

What durable fiction-writing framework can be extracted from the Salman Rushdie masterclass summary?

# Answer

The current source base supports an eight-part framework:

1. Start from necessity.
   Write the book you cannot avoid writing, not the one that merely seems marketable or prestigious.

2. Answer the six governing questions.
   Know whose story, what story, why, when, where, and how before you commit heavily to pages.

3. Find the right door into the book.
   False starts are normal; the right opening and entry point unlock the form, voice, and movement of the story.

4. Build living characters and living ground.
   Characters need yearning, damage, idiosyncrasy, and speech, while place and time must feel concrete enough to carry them.

5. Choose the telling as carefully as the tale.
   Point of view, tense, narrator design, style, rhythm, and formal freedom determine whether the story can actually work.

6. Let imagination outrun mere imitation.
   Myth, surrealism, history, and invention are all available, but they must remain rooted in emotional truth.

7. Finish, then rewrite ruthlessly.
   Get something down, reach the end, and then judge what is missing, what is surplus, and what the whole book actually wants to be.

8. Develop a worldview, not just a technique.
   Read globally, observe carefully, know yourself deeply, and expand what you know so the work becomes unmistakably yours.

# Gaps

- The source is a secondary summary and some sections are richer on principle than on concrete textual examples.
- The framework is strongest on expansive fiction and literary ambition; the vault still needs counterweights from minimalist and short-form fiction traditions.
- Rushdie's breadth is a strength, but the branch still needs more explicit treatment of sentence-level editing examples and scene-by-scene emotional construction.
