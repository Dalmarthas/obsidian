# Question

What screenwriting model does the Paul Thomas Anderson source add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part model:

1. Build the routine around your real rhythm.
   Discipline matters, but borrowed rituals are weaker than a schedule your own mind and life can actually sustain.

2. Treat the screenplay as a blueprint, not prose.
   Write economically, leave interpretive room, and trust action, subtext, and performance more than literary over-description.

3. Use instinct before overcontrol.
   Early roughness, odd turns, and small mistakes can expose live material that perfectionism would erase.

4. Let the character push back.
   If a scene action feels false, the problem may not be technical execution but that the character would not truly do it.

5. Stay unprecious in collaboration.
   The page is not sacred if actors, direction, or set reality reveal a better version of the moment.

6. Let the project change you.
   A long creative process should alter the writer's own understanding, not merely produce pages.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Sorkin, the model is less rhetorical and less scene-argument-driven; it is more interested in restraint, actor room, and intuitive truth-testing.
- Compared with Lynch, it is less mystical about signal and more practical about routine, mutability, and non-precious execution.
- Compared with Scorsese, it is less centered on cinema language and post-production discovery, and more centered on how the page should behave before and during collaboration.
- Compared with the current writer's-block source, it is less about recovering motion and more about how to keep the writing alive enough that collaboration and instinct still matter.
