# Question

What durable dialogue-craft model does the new roundtable add to the filmmaking branch?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part dialogue model:

1. Let the visuals carry what they can.
   Dialogue should not bear information that action, staging, or image can communicate more cinematically.

2. Root every line in objective.
   Characters should speak from what they want in the moment, not from the writer's wish to explain the plot.

3. Cut harder than feels comfortable.
   Better dialogue is often the result of deleting repetition, softeners, and lines that arrive before or after the real dramatic beat.

4. Build around subtext and misunderstanding.
   What characters conceal, distort, avoid, or misread gives the exchange more dramatic life than literal statement does.

5. Differentiate voices subtly.
   Rhythm, word choice, aggression level, pet phrases, and class or background texture should make characters sound like different people under pressure.

6. Rewrite through the ear.
   Table reads, read-aloud passes, and performability checks reveal awkwardness, exposition drag, and dead rhythm faster than silent page reading.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Sorkin, this source is less centered on rhetorical musicality and more on cutting, scene objective, and anti-exposition discipline.
- Compared with Rhimes, it overlaps strongly on subtext and anti-therapy-speak logic, but adds more explicit advice on scene trimming and voice differentiation.
- Compared with the previous anti-boredom roundtable, this source moves from macro diagnosis to line-level and scene-level execution.

# Gaps

- This synthesis is built from a secondary summary rather than a fully attributed transcript.
- The vault still lacks a richer page on character voice as a standalone system across multiple dialogue sources.
- A strong future addition would be a primary screenplay or transcript-based study of exposition management in produced scenes.
