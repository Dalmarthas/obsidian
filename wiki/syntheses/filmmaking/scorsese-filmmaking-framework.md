# Question

What durable filmmaking framework can be extracted from the Martin Scorsese masterclass summary?

# Answer

The current source base supports an eight-part model:

1. Start from vocation, not career fantasy.
   The work is strongest when the director feels compelled to make this film rather than merely attracted to the profession.

2. Guard the individual way of seeing.
   Your background, obsessions, moral questions, and sense of what feels cinematic are the real roots of the work.

3. Learn the language and history of cinema.
   Film meaning comes from shots, cuts, sequence, color, sound, and visual design, and that language was built by earlier filmmakers whose work should be understood before it is copied or rejected.

4. Treat the script as architecture, not as a sacred final object.
   Draw, diagram, research, rewrite, rehearse, and prepare heavily, but remain willing to change the work once living reality appears.

5. Cast truthfully and direct through trust.
   Most of the film lives or dies with the actor choices, the chemistry between them, and the director's ability to orient people while still giving them room to explore.

6. Build the world through specific material decisions.
   Locations, production design, costume, light, lenses, and color should all answer to the period, place, character, and emotional logic of the story.

7. Use technology as servant, never as author.
   Steadicams, digital intermediates, pre-visualization, and modern editing systems are valuable only when they obey the dramatic intention of the film.

8. Find the film in the edit.
   Assembly, trimming, screening, sound, music, voiceover, pacing, and frame-level judgment are where the real shape and force of the movie become visible.

# Gaps

- The source is a secondary summary rather than a primary transcript.
- The framework is rich on philosophy and production craft, but the vault still lacks strong counterweights from documentary, animation, and more producer-led filmmaking cultures.
- Scorsese's model is especially strong on cinema tradition and director-centered authorship, which means later sources may need to challenge or rebalance parts of it.
