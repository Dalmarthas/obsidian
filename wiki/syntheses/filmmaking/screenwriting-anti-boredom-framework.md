# Question

What anti-boredom craft model does the roundtable source add to the filmmaking branch?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part anti-boredom model:

1. Start from emotional truth, not imitation.
   The work should come from what the writer genuinely fears, wants, obsesses over, or feels, not from trend mimicry.

2. Separate arena from story.
   A cool setup, profession, or environment is not enough until it is attached to goals, obstacles, stakes, and change.

3. Make character desire active and legible.
   Passive protagonists, vague wants, and hidden story engines drain attention quickly.

4. Build conflict at every scale.
   The overall narrative needs durable tension, and each scene needs a local struggle or shift rather than polite information exchange.

5. Use ideation drills to escape first-thought cliche.
   Methods like generating 100 ideas or 20 versions of a beat help the writer reach less obvious material.

6. Revise through exposure, not fantasy.
   Rough drafts, read-alouds, honest notes, and scene-by-scene diagnostic checks reveal boredom more reliably than private self-belief.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Truby, this source is less systematic about full architecture but sharper on boredom diagnosis and anti-cliche ideation.
- Compared with Sorkin, it is less scene-tactical and more explicit about emotional originality and the danger of dead imitation.
- Compared with Martell, it generalizes the authenticity problem beyond assignments: even original scripts can die if the writer is emotionally distant from the material.
- Compared with the generic storytelling source, it adds a more aggressive creativity layer through originality drills and the arena-versus-story distinction.
