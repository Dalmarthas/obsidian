# Question

What durable assignment-writing model does the Martell source add to the filmmaking branch?

# Answer

The source adds a five-part doorway model:

1. Start by asking `How is this my story?`
   Before accepting or developing the project, find the real point of personal contact.

2. Look for emotional equivalence, not literal sameness.
   The writer does not need to have lived the plot, but they do need access to the feeling, fear, desire, or contradiction underneath it.

3. Rewrite the assignment through your doorway.
   Bring your own angle, subtext, or moral pressure to the material rather than only executing the obvious generic version.

4. Use the doorway as a selection filter.
   If no honest doorway appears, the writer should consider declining the project rather than forcing empty work.

5. Protect long-term sustainability.
   Writing only for money or industry access without connection to the material weakens both the work and the writer's ability to keep going.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Lynch, Martell is more assignment-oriented and less origin-myth oriented.
- Compared with Rushdie, Martell is less concerned with full worldview and more concerned with the concrete access point into a specific project.
- Compared with Truby, Martell is less structural and more concerned with whether the writer can honestly inhabit the material in the first place.

# Gaps

- This source is better at project selection and reframing than at scene-level craft mechanics.
- The current layer still needs more evidence from writers working deep inside studio franchise systems.
- This remains a secondary-summary source rather than a direct Martell text.
