# Question

What recovery model for writer's block emerges from the current screenwriter-comparison source and adjacent writing pages?

# Answer

The current vault supports a six-part recovery model:

1. Normalize the blockage instead of dramatizing it.
   The block is not evidence that you are uniquely broken. It is often part of how creative work actually feels between usable breakthroughs.

2. Diagnose the blockage before treating it.
   The real problem may be:
   - a dead idea
   - a tired scene
   - a drafting mode mismatch
   - pressure overload
   - lack of emotional investment

3. Change the mode before increasing the force.
   Better moves include:
   - switching to another live project
   - editing earlier pages
   - writing notes, cards, or questions
   - returning to premise or structure instead of insisting on forward pages

4. Use incubation deliberately.
   Walks, music, driving, daydreaming, and unforced thinking can keep the work moving beneath the level of visible output.

5. Follow the living signal.
   If one thread still feels fun, curious, tense, or emotionally real, use that energy as the re-entry door rather than attacking the dead part head-on.

6. Return with patience and light re-entry.
   Come back through small, reversible moves:
   - revise a prior page
   - restate the problem
   - ask story questions
   - sketch a scene card
   - generate alternatives

# Comparisons

- Compared with Brown, this model is less routine-centered and more explicit about switching modes when the page goes dead.
- Compared with Lynch, it is less spiritual about signal-reception and more pragmatic about project rotation, music, and ordinary off-page activity.
- Compared with Sorkin, it is less about post-draft diagnosis and more about how to regain motion before or during the drafting stall itself.
- Compared with Simon Rich, it is more downstream: Rich is strongest on avoiding scarcity through premise abundance before blockage hardens around one stuck draft.

# Gaps

- The recovery model is built mostly from secondary summaries rather than primary interviews, journals, or process transcripts.
- The vault still needs more direct evidence on how different deadlines change the push-versus-incubate balance.
- The current branch is strongest on screenwriting and fiction; it would be useful to compare with composers, essayists, or designers dealing with similar blockage.
