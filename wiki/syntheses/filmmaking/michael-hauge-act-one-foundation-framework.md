# Question

What practical Act 1 framework does Michael Hauge add to the vault?

# Answer

Hauge adds a four-part Act 1 foundation model:

1. Give the protagonist a visible finish line.
   The audience should be able to picture what success looks like instead of swimming in vague character complexity.

2. Show ordinary life before major escalation.
   Status-quo context creates empathy and orients the viewer toward what the story is about to disrupt.

3. Build connection before psychological complication.
   Empathy should arrive before flaws, subplots, and thematic layering become dense.

4. Let Act 1 gather momentum rather than detonating too early.
   Rushing into the main pursuit can create short-term excitement but long-term structural burnout.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Sorkin, Hauge is more concerned with early empathy and visible goal setup than with scene-level verbal combat.
- Compared with Edson, Hauge is less sequence-engineering-focused and more concentrated on Act 1 orientation and audience readiness.
- Compared with Truby, Hauge is less architecturally deep but more directly practical for diagnosing first-act confusion.
- Compared with Gers, Hauge is more prescriptive about the opening quarter of the script and more insistent on visible outer motivation.
