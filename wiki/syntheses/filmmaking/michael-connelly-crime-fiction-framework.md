# Question

What reusable crime-fiction framework does Michael Connelly add to the vault?

# Answer

The Connelly source supports an eleven-part crime-fiction model:

1. Choose telling details over detail volume.
   A vivid page is not crowded with description. It contains the detail that reveals pressure, character, place, or theme while keeping momentum.

2. Rewrite as a discovery and compression system.
   Start the writing day by revising yesterday's pages, then do a full paper pass at the end. Revision is where details are upgraded, excess is cut, and early material is aligned with later payoffs.

3. Put character above puzzle.
   The investigation matters, but readers stay because they believe in the people moving through it. Plot, setting, and research should all make the fictional person more convincing.

4. Use the city as a story engine.
   A murder investigation gives the detective permission to move through neighborhoods, classes, institutions, and hidden social layers. Each case can become a tale of the city.

5. Give every page active desire.
   Large stakes are not enough. Characters should want something now, even if the want is small, private, or only indirectly related to the main case.

6. Layer internal conflict under procedural conflict.
   The case supplies external pressure, while addiction, age, love, guilt, doubt, fear, or moral corrosion supply the quieter pressure that keeps scenes human.

7. Compress dialogue until it earns its place.
   Dialogue should carry new information, reveal character, or expose subtext. Lines that repeat exposition or over-explain emotion weaken momentum.

8. Let subtext do adult work.
   The best exchanges may seem to be about logistics, jokes, or minor irritations while actually carrying insecurity, resentment, affection, or power.

9. Treat darkness as moral contact, not just genre atmosphere.
   Crime fiction should ask what happens to people who repeatedly confront murder, evil, and disorder. The hero's cost can matter more than the villain's pathology.

10. Use courtroom or investigation structures as debate containers.
   A trial can put a large question, such as AI, under dramatic pressure by forcing multiple sides into testimony, cross-examination, and verdict.

11. Respect medium differences.
   Novels can use interiority; television and film must externalize character through action, speech, casting, and repeated behavior. Adaptation gains reach but fixes what prose can leave private.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/filmmaking/dan-brown-thriller-framework|Dan Brown Thriller Framework]], Connelly is less focused on puzzle architecture, clocks, and crucibles, and more focused on character, city, moral cost, and one vivid detail that carries the page.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/filmmaking/baldacci-thriller-framework|Baldacci Thriller Framework]], Connelly shares professional discipline, research, and revision, but is more explicit about the city as a social map and the detective as a lens through which darkness is examined.
- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/filmmaking/salman-rushdie-fiction-framework|Salman Rushdie Fiction Framework]], Connelly is narrower and more procedural, but they overlap on place, world texture, reader imagination, and the need for concrete reality under invented people.

# Gaps

- This framework is based on a secondary video summary rather than direct transcript or page-level comparison of Connelly's novels.
