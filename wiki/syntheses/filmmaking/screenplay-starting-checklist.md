# Question

What should be in place before drafting a screenplay?

# Answer

Based on the current source base, the minimum pre-draft checklist is:

1. Read several produced screenplays to calibrate your sense of what a finished script looks like.
2. Choose an idea that keeps recurring or still feels alive after initial filtering.
3. Distill the story into a logline clear enough to act as a North Star.
4. Clarify protagonist, flaw, goal, motivation, and stakes before pages multiply.
5. Outline the story before drafting, at least enough to know the beginning, middle, end, midpoint shift, and lowest point.
6. Map the story against a structural model, usually three acts plus major turning points.
7. Learn screenplay formatting before you draft so you are not rewriting the same material into compliance later.
8. Use inexpensive or free tooling at the start; do not confuse buying software with making progress.

Operational interpretation:

- If the dramatic question is unclear, the outline is not ready.
- If the logline cannot hold the story in one vivid sentence, the premise is probably still blurry.
- If the protagonist's flaw, desire, and stakes are muddy, the emotional engine is not ready.
- If the key turns are missing, the structure is not ready.
- If the writer cannot say what transformation or insight the story is moving toward, the deeper story may not be ready.
- If the format is unfamiliar, drafting speed will be wasted on avoidable cleanup.

# Gaps

- This checklist currently rests on a single source and should later be stress-tested against stronger or more modern screenwriting references.
- Tool recommendations in the source are dated and should not be taken as current without verification.
- The checklist is stronger on pre-draft clarity than on scene-level execution and still needs more direct primary-script grounding.
