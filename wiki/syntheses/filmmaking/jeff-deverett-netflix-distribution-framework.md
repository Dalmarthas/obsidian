# Question

What practical distribution model does the Jeff Deverett source add to the filmmaking branch?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part commercial-delivery model:

1. Sell the film fast.
   Buyers hear many projects, so the pitch must arrive with the hook front-loaded and the explanation radically compressed.

2. Assume access usually runs through intermediaries.
   Even if a platform likes a project, approved distributors and delivery partners may still control the operational path in.

3. Budget for delivery, not just production.
   Subtitles, language versions, compliance packages, and technical handoff can meaningfully change the true cost of release.

4. Treat credibility as a real asset.
   On-time, on-budget execution and prior performance reduce buyer risk more than raw ambition does.

5. Borrow trust if you do not yet have it.
   New filmmakers often need experienced producers, distributors, or aggregators to clear access barriers they cannot solve alone.

6. Make persistence part of the pipeline.
   Rejection, silence, and delayed timing are normal, so professional follow-up is operational discipline rather than desperation.

# Comparisons

- Compared with the current filmmaking branch, this source adds the strongest explicit access-and-delivery layer after the film is creatively finished.
- Compared with [[wiki/concepts/business/creative-pitching|Creative Pitching]], the emphasis shifts from protecting bold creative work in the room to making a project intelligible and actionable inside a saturated buyer workflow.
- Compared with Lynch and Scorsese's filmmaking logic, Deverett makes the downstream constraint visible: a film can be artistically coherent and still fail commercially if packaging, delivery, and trust are weak.
