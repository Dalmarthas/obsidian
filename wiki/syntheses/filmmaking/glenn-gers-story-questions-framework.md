# Question

What practical story-development model does Glenn Gers add to the vault?

# Answer

Gers adds a four-part development model:

1. Start with interrogative clarity.
   Instead of relying on vague intuition alone, keep asking the six core questions until the story can withstand simple explanation.

2. Build story from desire and obstruction.
   Character, goal, obstacle, action, failed action, and ending are enough to expose whether a real dramatic engine exists.

3. Treat every character as central from their own perspective.
   Theory-of-mind characterization deepens scenes because minor characters stop behaving like furniture and start behaving like people with aims.

4. Respect the writer's actual process.
   Progress often comes from rough notes, iterative questioning, and self-awareness about working conditions rather than from forcing one allegedly correct workflow.

# Comparisons

- Compared with Truby, Gers is less architecturally dense and more usefully minimalist.
- Compared with Edson, Gers is less sequence-driven and more question-driven.
- Compared with the generic storytelling source, Gers offers a cleaner named method and a stronger emphasis on theory-of-mind characterization.
- Compared with Sorkin, Gers is less scene-musical and less performance-centered, but more directly reusable as a universal first-pass diagnostic.
