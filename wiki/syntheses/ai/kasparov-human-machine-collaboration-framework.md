# Question

What reusable human-machine collaboration framework does the Kasparov conversation add to the vault?

# Answer

The source adds a six-part collaboration framework:

1. Stop framing the problem as human versus machine.
   The stronger question is how to combine each side's advantages without forcing either one to imitate the other.

2. Optimize process before worshipping power.
   Better interface, handoff, and workflow can beat stronger isolated humans or stronger isolated machines.

3. Use the machine as calculator and critic, not as unquestioned oracle.
   Computational scale is powerful, but conceptual understanding and independent skepticism still have to remain human responsibilities.

4. Treat transparency and rules as part of the system design.
   Frontier contests and high-stakes collaborations fail when process fairness, data access, or accountability are vague.

5. Let AI compress learning, but do not outsource judgment.
   Faster feedback loops can accelerate skill growth, yet passive deference to machine outputs weakens the very reasoning the tool is supposed to augment.

6. Respond to technological change by moving toward new games.
   When tools automate old tasks, the strategic response is adaptation and frontier-seeking rather than nostalgia for protected difficulty.

# Comparisons

- Compared with [[wiki/syntheses/ai/jeremy-utley-ai-collaboration-framework|Jeremy Utley AI Collaboration Framework]], Kasparov is less about prompt craft and more about system architecture. Utley focuses on briefing and iteration; Kasparov focuses on interfaces, handoffs, skepticism, and preserving judgment under powerful machine assistance.
- Compared with the current chess-improvement branch, Kasparov is less about move quality in individual games and more about how engines reshape training, learning speed, and the meaning of expertise itself.

# Gaps

- The branch still needs more material on governance, labor impact, and institutional design before its technology optimism should be treated as complete.
- The vault also needs stronger non-chess case studies showing where `advanced chess` style collaboration transfers cleanly and where it breaks down.
