# Question

How many index cards make a full screenplay, and did Aaron Sorkin give a fixed number?

# Answer

The vault-backed answer is: Aaron Sorkin is recorded here as using index cards on a cork board, but the available Sorkin source pages do not preserve a fixed full-script card count. His method is described more as partial visibility: card the scenes you know, see roughly a few scenes ahead, and keep moving through intention, obstacle, and scene propulsion.

The fixed-number heuristic most likely being remembered is adjacent rather than clearly Sorkin-specific:

- `40` cards / major beats is a common Save the Cat-style screenwriting heuristic for mapping a feature screenplay.
- John August's public card advice is in the same range but looser: if a feature has fewer than about 30 cards it is probably too thin, and if it has more than about 50 cards it may be too dense.
- The local [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/scene-card-method|Scene Card Method]] page also records `70 scenes` as a numeric heuristic, but marks it as diagnostic rather than mechanical.

Practical translation: for a feature screenplay, expect roughly `40-50` index cards if each card is a major scene/beat unit. Treat that as a scale check, not a rule. A Sorkin-style card should still answer: what does someone want, what blocks them, what changes by the end, and how does the scene launch the next one?

# Basis

- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-07-aaron-sorkin-masterclass-complete-summary|Aaron Sorkin Masterclass Complete Summary]] says Sorkin uses index cards on a cork board, cards the first scenes he knows, and treats drafting as walking in the dark with a flashlight.
- [[wiki/sources/2026-04-09-aaron-sorkin-on-the-social-network|Aaron Sorkin on The Social Network]] says his process includes bad-idea generation, index cards, and partial structure rather than exhaustive outlining.
- [[wiki/concepts/filmmaking/scene-card-method|Scene Card Method]] compiles scene cards as a lightweight, movable pre-draft planning system and warns against treating numeric card counts as pseudo-rules.
- [[wiki/syntheses/cross-source/screenwriting-outlining-models|Screenwriting Outlining Models]] places cards inside a broader family of outlining tools: beat sheets, notebooks, sequence pages, corkboards, and flexible structural scaffolds.
